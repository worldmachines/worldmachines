# Prime Radiant

In Asimov's *Foundation* series, the Prime Radiant is the instrument through which psychohistory becomes visible. It projects the equations of the Seldon Plan as luminous symbols floating in space — not a display but an environment, something you inhabit rather than read. Hari Seldon uses it to encode the full mathematical structure of his civilizational forecast: the branching probability trees, the crisis points, the intervention windows.

The device is not a computer in the modern sense. It is closer to a navigational chart for time — one that shows not where you are but where history is going if nothing acts on it, and what the phase space of possible interventions looks like. Only the psychohistorian can read it, because the symbols are the mathematics itself, not a translation of it.

Two features matter for our purposes:

**It is a model, not a prediction.** The Plan does not say "event X will happen." It maps probability flows across the population of possible futures. The equations remain valid even as specific events deviate from expectation, so long as the aggregate trajectory stays within tolerances.

**It is revisable.** The Second Foundation's central work is updating the Prime Radiant as reality diverges from the original Plan. The equations are not scripture; they are a living document whose revision is itself a kind of civilizational intervention.

The World Machines project inherits both features: we are building a model (not a prophecy), and the model is meant to be iteratively corrected as history does or does not conform to it. The Oracle is our read-only interface to the current state of that model. The Prime Radiant is what it would become if the model were ever fully formalized — equations you could walk around inside.
