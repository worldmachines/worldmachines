# Dancing with Monsters: Four California Scenarios for 2042

*Series: 2042 — The Interregnum Deepens | Post 3 of 3*

---

There is a posture toward the Gramsci gap that I want to explore before laying out the scenarios: not the posture of the revolutionary waiting for the right moment, and not the posture of the technocrat trying to improve the existing system's throughput. Something between and beneath both.

Gramsci wrote from prison. He was watching fascism consolidate while the left fragmented and the old liberal order decayed. His diagnosis of the interregnum was not a counsel of despair. It was an attempt to understand what kinds of engagement with monstrous conditions create the conditions for something different, even when the transformative coalition does not yet exist.

Dancing with monsters is not the same as becoming one. It is not the same as defeating one. It is the specific skill of the interregnum: maintaining enough engagement with existing institutions to stay relevant and to create pockets of function, while not becoming so captured by their logic that you lose the capacity to imagine alternatives.

In California terms: working inside the 7,000-jurisdiction water patchwork well enough to actually get water to people, while holding in mind that the patchwork itself is a monster that needs to be unmade. Serving in a broken permitting process while documenting the workarounds that its own frontline staff have invented, because those workarounds contain the seeds of whatever comes next.

This is uncomfortable. It is morally impure. It is also the only way to get things done during an interregnum that may last longer than any individual career.

---

With that frame, here are four scenarios for California in 2042. These are not predictions. They are structured imaginations — each one asking what happens to the monster depending on the political energy brought to bear between now and then.

## Transformation

The constitutional convention that California has needed since roughly 1950 finally happens. Not in a single dramatic moment, but through an accumulation of crises that makes the dysfunction undeniable to constituencies that previously had enough insulation to ignore it: two catastrophic fire-flood cycles in the foothill communities, a budget implosion that exposes how much of the state's nominal governance is pure theater, a water failure in the Central Valley severe enough to make national news for months.

The convention is messy. The final document is imperfect. But it accomplishes what *A New California Dream* argued was necessary back in 2011: it rationalizes overlapping jurisdictions, creates genuine lines of accountability, gives California something it has never had — a real constitutional founding, rather than the 1849 rush job and five hundred amendments accreted on top.

The foothill communities that burned and flooded get rebuilt, but differently. Not back to the original pattern of subsidized risk in fire-prone chaparral, but forward to something new: fire-adapted architecture, managed retreat from the most dangerous parcels, aggregated water systems for the communities that remain. The La Canada Irrigation District and its hundreds of siblings finally get rationalized into regional water authorities with actual capacity.

Water and energy get managed as the coupled system they always were. A digital coordination layer makes the patchwork legible to itself for the first time — not replacing human judgment, but amplifying it across jurisdictions that previously could not see each other.

In 2042, it is early days. The transformation is not complete. But the direction is set, and the institutions being built are aimed at the right problems.

What this requires that we do not yet have: a cross-class coalition with organizational depth, a crisis severe enough to break open constituencies currently insulated from dysfunction, and political leadership willing to pursue institutional demolition rather than just reform.

## Growth

The Abundance coalition wins the policy debate without transforming the underlying governance structure. Housing construction accelerates dramatically through state preemption of local zoning. Permitting timelines collapse from seven years to eighteen months. Energy permitting gets streamlined enough to finally build out the renewable grid at the scale the climate requires.

California in 2042 is noticeably more affordable, more populated, more economically dynamic. More Californians can access the Dream the state promises. That is real and worth saying.

The constitutional scar tissue remains. The 7,000 jurisdictions are still there. The initiative-calcified budget mandates still eat the discretionary budget. The state can produce targeted, visible, politically legible reform — but it cannot govern itself as a coherent entity. The foothills burned again in 2038. The response was faster than 2025 but not faster than the fire.

Growth without transformation is not bad. It is genuinely better than the alternative. But it is also brittle. When the next major crisis hits — and it will hit — the underlying machinery for converting democratic will into collective action is still jammed. The monster is fed, not tamed.

## Constraint

Climate and fiscal tightening force consolidation without intentionality. The budget crisis of the early 2030s triggers automatic cuts that produce some de facto rationalization of the patchwork: there simply isn't money to keep all 7,000 jurisdictions staffed. The consolidation is chaotic, inequitable, politically ugly. The agencies that survive are the ones with the most aggressive lobbying, not the most functional governance.

This is managed decline, not collapse. California in 2042 is still California — the Dream still pulls immigrants, the tech economy still produces, the coast is still beautiful. But the public sector is thinner in the wrong places. Infrastructure gaps widen. The communities that needed the state most got the least from the consolidation.

The monstrous form doesn't get fixed; it just gets cheaper to run because there's less money to feed into it. The monster goes hungry. That is not the same as it being unmade.

What this scenario reveals is the cost of allowing the interregnum to drag on: the decisions that should have been made through deliberate reconstruction get made by fiscal crisis instead, and crisis-driven decisions are almost always regressive. They protect the organized and abandon the unorganized.

## Collapse

The Octopus wins. Not the Southern Pacific this time, but something more diffuse: the concentrated platform power of a handful of companies, the tribalism that Dan Walters warned about in the early 2000s, the initiative process finally captured so completely by competing billionaire interests that it produces nothing coherent.

California in 2042 has not collapsed in any dramatic sense. People are still there. The economy is still large. But the shared civic project has dissolved into a collection of tribes, each defending its own, nobody tending the whole. The water system creaks toward failure in slow motion. The schools in the foothills and the Central Valley have been effectively abandoned by state policy. The dream of a California in which anyone from anywhere might build a life is still present in the culture — it is still invoked at graduations and in campaign ads — but it has become what it always threatened to become: pure ideology, detached from the operational reality of what the state can actually do.

This is not Blade Runner. It is quieter than that. It is a civilization that has made its peace with being less than it could have been.

---

## On which scenario we get

I genuinely don't know. That is not a hedge. It is the honest position of someone who has spent enough time inside California's institutional machinery to know that it is neither as resilient as optimists believe nor as brittle as pessimists fear.

What I do know is this: the path to Transformation requires a coalition that does not yet exist, and the Gramsci gap is precisely the space in which such coalitions either form or fail to form. The work of the interregnum is not to pick the scenario and optimize for it. It is to understand what kinds of engagement with the current monstrous condition create the conditions for transformation — even from within constraint, even when the monster fights back.

The mugwumps were necessary. The Progressives who came after them were necessary too. And the thing that turned mugwump intellectual work into Progressive political power was not a better argument. It was organizational form: the cross-class coalition, the mass movement, the political party genuinely rooted in the communities bearing the costs of the Gilded Age.

That is what California 2042 is waiting for. The question, sixteen years in, is whether it arrived.
