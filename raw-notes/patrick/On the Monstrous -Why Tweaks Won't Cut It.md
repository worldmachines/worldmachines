# On the Monstrous: Why Tweaks Won't Cut It

*Series: 2042 — The Interregnum Deepens | Post 2 of 3*

---

Here is a question worth sitting with: what would it mean for a public institution to be genuinely monstrous?

Not merely inefficient. Not merely captured by interest groups. Not merely outdated. Monstrous in the way Frankenstein's creation was monstrous — assembled from good parts, animated by real purpose, and yet producing effects its creators would recoil from if they understood them clearly.

California's water governance is a 7,000-jurisdiction patchwork that nobody designed. It emerged from a century and a half of good-faith responses to specific crises: droughts, floods, legal disputes, development booms. Each agency made sense at the moment of its creation. Together they produce a system where, as my father once put it after spending a career inside it, "statewide water plans and projects are nearly impossible today." The whole is not just more than the sum of its parts. It is a fundamentally different kind of thing — a machine for absorbing change without producing it.

That is the monstrous condition. Not bad actors, though there are those. Not wrong values, though there are those too. An institutional form that converts democratic energy into procedural friction. That processes everything and produces nothing except its own perpetuation.

---

Both the Code for America crowd and the DOGE crowd have misunderstood this, in symmetrically opposite ways.

Code for America's diagnosis: government is old software running on old hardware; swap in modern technology and skilled practitioners and the programs will work better. They are right that the user experience of government is often gratuitously terrible, and they have done real work fixing it. But their prescription is operational. It assumes the underlying institutional structure is sound, or at least workable. It wants government to function better as government — faster, more legible, less hostile to the people it serves.

DOGE's diagnosis: government is bloated; cut it. The prescription is also operational, just in the opposite direction. Fewer people, fewer programs, fewer dollars. The underlying assumption is the same: the structure is fine, the scale is wrong.

Neither of them is asking the structural question: what if the institution itself is the problem? What if the form — this particular assemblage of agencies, jurisdictions, mandates, and political settlements — cannot be fixed by making it run better or by making it smaller?

You cannot fix a monster by making it more efficient. You cannot fix a monster by cutting it. You have to unmake it.

---

Genuine creative destruction in public institutions looks different from what either crowd describes. It looks like what the original Progressive reformers did in California between 1910 and 1920. They didn't improve the Southern Pacific's regulatory environment — they stripped the railroad's tentacles from every lever of state government and built a new apparatus in its place. The Public Utilities Commission, the direct primary, the initiative and recall, the civil service protections: none of these were improvements to what existed before. They were replacements. New institutional forms designed for different purposes.

What that required was a judgment that some institutions had to die and new ones had to be born. Not reformed. Not streamlined. Replaced.

That judgment is uncomfortable, because it requires being specific about which institutions are monsters and which are merely malfunctioning. It requires political power sufficient to actually close agencies, merge jurisdictions, eliminate the La Canada Irrigation District — which still exists, incidentally, serving a handful of parcels in the foothills, because nothing in California ever dies. It requires the organizational depth to survive the counterattack from the beneficiaries of every institution marked for demolition, and there are always beneficiaries.

None of this is impossible. California did it before. But it required a specific kind of political moment: a crisis deep enough that constituencies who had previously been insulated from the dysfunction became unable to ignore it, combined with a coalition organized enough to channel that energy into institutional reconstruction rather than mere protest.

We are not there yet. The morbid symptoms are clearly visible. The water system. The permitting apparatus. The school funding formula. The prison industrial complex that consumes what the schools should have. The overlapping local jurisdictions that make regional governance structurally impossible. Any honest accounting of California's public sector reveals institutions that are not merely inefficient but genuinely organized against the purposes they nominally serve.

Naming this clearly is not cynicism. It is a prerequisite for the work that comes next. You cannot unmake a monster you haven't identified as monstrous.

---

There is a version of this argument that tips into nihilism: everything is broken, nothing can be fixed, burn it all down. That is not what I'm saying. The destruction I'm describing is not random; it is purposive. It is demolition in service of reconstruction. The cathedral builders of the Progressive era didn't tear down for the sake of tearing down. They tore down so they could build something that would outlast them.

Californians have done this before. After the railroad came the progressive reform. After the water crisis of the early 20th century came the State Water Project — not elegant, not without its own contradictions, but a genuine attempt to build infrastructure equal to the state's scale and ambition. William Hammond Hall's vision of a neo-Mediterranean commonwealth took ninety years and multiple institutional cycles to partially realize.

The question for 2042 is whether the current interregnum produces a third such cycle, or whether the monstrous institutions outlast the political energy required to unmake them.
