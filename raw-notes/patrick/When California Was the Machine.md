When California Was the Machine
On the state that built a civilization and then forgot how
There's a photograph I keep thinking about. Pat Brown, 1960, standing at the groundbreaking for the State Water Project -- the largest water infrastructure undertaking in the history of any government anywhere. Behind him: the raw desert of the Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta, which would be turned into the hydraulic backbone of a civilization. The man is grinning. He has no idea the fight is already half-lost.
Not because the project would fail. It would mostly succeed. But because the coalition required to build it -- the administrative confidence, the political will, the sheer belief that the state could organize the physical world in service of a public vision -- had a shorter lifespan than the concrete they were pouring.
I've been thinking about this in the context of Venkatesh Rao's World Machines framework, which I've been working through in a series of posts here. The core argument: civilizational history is organized not by nations or ideologies but by successive "world machines," large-scale coordination systems that define what's possible, what's legible, what's normal. We are living through the late Dusk of the Modernity Machine -- the system built on nation-state power, industrial infrastructure, and mass institutional coordination -- while two successor machines struggle to Dawn around it.
California built itself on the Modernity Machine's purest logic. And California is now the clearest case study of what that machine's Dusk looks like from the inside.

The postwar decade in California was a machine in full Day. Pat Brown's administration alone produced the State Water Project, the Master Plan for Higher Education, and 1,000 miles of new freeway. Not sequentially. Simultaneously. The UC system went from five campuses to nine in a decade. The California Aqueduct, at 444 miles, remains the longest water conveyance structure in the world. These were not incremental improvements on existing systems. They were the physical instantiation of a coordinating vision: a state that could harness expertise, capital, and democratic mandate to transform the landscape in service of human flourishing.
What made this possible was not just Pat Brown's considerable political gifts. It was the administrative apparatus underneath him. The New York Bureau of Municipal Research model, which had been spreading through American government since the Progressive Era, had by 1960 produced something genuinely capable in Sacramento: engineers who had been trained to study actual operations, budget analysts who knew where the money was and what it could do, project managers who understood construction schedules were not suggestions. The machinery of government matched the ambition of the politicians running it.
Venkat describes the Modernity Machine as fundamentally a legibility engine. Its core logic is the compression of variety into canonical order, making the world manageable by simplifying it into forms that can be counted, planned, and acted upon. Water flows as acre-feet. Miles of highway are standardized. University admissions become a scoring system. The compression has costs -- James Scott has written the book on what gets lost when a complex living system gets flattened into a legible map. But the Modernity Machine's great genius was that the legibility created real coordination. You could actually build things.

The decline is not a mystery, exactly. Each protective layer added to the system made the next layer easier to add and the underlying capacity harder to exercise. CEQA, passed in 1970, was a genuine and necessary environmental protection. But a law designed to prevent bad projects also, mathematically, slows good ones. Prevailing wage requirements protected workers. They also raised costs. Community engagement protocols were democratically legitimate. They also created infinite veto opportunities. NEPA, the ESA, successive layers of water quality regulation, the explosion of consultant ecosystems organized around compliance rather than construction -- none of these were wrong, exactly. They were each defensible. The aggregate effect is what I once described to a colleague as a state that has become extraordinarily capable at administering complexity rather than reducing it.
The Modernity Machine's institutions don't fail because of bad people. They fail because of what VGR calls zemblanity: compounding bad luck that befalls those who act dead. Each small act of institutional deadness -- the department that won't approve a permit because approval would require a precedent, the environmental review extended for the third time because the consultants found a new subspecies, the financing structure that collapsed because three agencies couldn't agree on jurisdiction -- each of these seems defensible in isolation. The compound effect is a civilization that cannot build.
High-speed rail was supposed to be California's generational infrastructure achievement. It is instead the case study that gets cited in every policy school in the country as an example of execution failure. Not because the vision was wrong. Because the administrative substrate required to deliver it had been hollowed out over fifty years. The soft costs of procedural compliance now routinely exceed the hard costs of construction. The people who once would have joined the state and learned to move large projects through complex environments are at Andreessen Horowitz or a climate startup instead. The public sector got good at hiring people comfortable with process. It stopped being able to attract people made uncomfortable by it.
This is not a California-specific story. The EU is here. The UK is arriving. But California got there first, and California got there furthest, because California led the procedural swing of the 1970s with the same energy it had once brought to the construction swing of the 1960s. West of the west, as I've put it before. California as leading indicator.

There's a harder version of this argument that I find myself reluctant to make but unable to avoid.
The Modernity Machine's legibility logic produces institutions that are fundamentally oriented toward the past. They are organized around the last set of problems they successfully solved. The water rights system in California was brilliant for allocating a shared resource in a world of relatively stable hydrology. It is an abstraction that is now leaking badly -- the Colorado River Compact, which allocated 17.5 million acre-feet of water that the river no longer reliably produces, is the textbook case. The institutions built to manage that system are oriented around legal interpretations of a 1922 document rather than the physical reality of what comes down the river. Abstraction leaks are how Dusk announces itself.
The UC system, which was one of the most audacious experiments in mass meritocracy the world had ever seen, is now primarily organized around its own reproduction. Not education as a Modernity Machine delivery system for a capable citizenry, but the university as an ecosystem with its own tenure structures, endowment imperatives, and credentialing rituals largely decoupled from the skills the next economy needs. Pat Brown's Master Plan offered a place in the system to every Californian who earned it. The current UC system is primarily an engine for producing people who can administer the current UC system.
This sounds unfair. It is partly unfair. But the pattern is legible. The institutions of the Modernity Machine were built to build. In Dusk, they build mainly themselves.

I am not, by temperament, a declinist. The bones remain. The aqueduct still runs. The UC campuses still graduate engineers and doctors and policy analysts who do remarkable things. The administrative capacity to do large things is not gone; it is encrusted, in some places, in ways that a capable governor with a real coalition and a genuine theory of reform might still clear.
The question the World Machines framework poses is not whether the institutions can be saved. It is whether the next coordination logic will emerge from within them or around them. The Divergence Machine -- the successor logic, which organizes around variety and experimentation and plural coexistence rather than canonical order -- does not necessarily require fixing CEQA. It may simply route around it, the way the internet routed around broadcast media without reforming NBC.
What California could be, in that framing, is not the Modernity Machine's best remaining practitioner but the Divergence Machine's first significant laboratory. A state that stopped insisting everything pass through a canonical process and started creating a genuine ecosystem of pilot programs, data collaboratives, financing innovations, and institutional experiments that demonstrate what works before asking the whole machine to replicate it.
That is a different kind of pioneer. Not the canal-building, freeway-paving, university-founding titan of 1960. Something more nimble, more comfortable with uncertainty, more interested in what's actually working than in what the plan said would work.
Pat Brown grinned at the groundbreaking because he knew what he was going to build. The next version of that grin will belong to someone who knows which experiments to run.
