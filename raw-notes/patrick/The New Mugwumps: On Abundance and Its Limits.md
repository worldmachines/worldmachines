# The New Mugwumps: On Abundance and Its Limits

*Series: 2042 — The Interregnum Deepens | Post 1 of 3*

---

Ezra Klein, Derek Thompson, the expanding herd of wonks who have rallied around the idea that the central failure of American liberalism is a failure to build — housing, transit, energy, public institutions — these people are 60% in the right direction. The diagnosis is largely correct. America like many Western Countries has become bogged down in arcane procedures that frustrate the ability to actually implement laudable policy goals. 

Now for the comparison that will irritate some of them.

---

The mugwumps were Republicans in the 1880s who crossed party lines to support Grover Cleveland because they found their own party's corruption intolerable. They were largely professionals, journalists, academics, reformers. They cared about civil service reform, good government, anti-machine politics. They were, in the terminology of their moment, the serious people. The Atlantic wrote favorable things about them. (Note the echoes with the never Trumpers…) 

They also had essentially no mass base. They could not mobilize workers. They had no organic connection to the populations actually bearing the costs of the Gilded Age. Their reforms were real but procedural — better management of existing institutions, not transformation of the social order.

Two decades later, the original Progressives arrived. Hiram Johnson in California didn't improve the Southern Pacific's regulatory environment — he broke the railroad's grip on Sacramento by organizing a cross-class coalition that included labor, farmers, and small businesspeople who were tired of paying tribute to the Octopus. Robert LaFollette in Wisconsin built a genuine mass movement. Theodore Roosevelt, for all his aristocratic bluster, understood that conservation and trust-busting required mobilizing publics, not just persuading intellectuals.

The difference was not rhetorical sophistication. The mugwumps were sophisticated. The difference was organizational form — which is another way of saying: who is with you when the machine fights back?

---

The Abundance crowd has the magazine writers and the policy entrepreneurs. It has some mayors and some state legislators. It has an emerging think-tank ecosystem and a vocabulary that has genuinely shifted what is speakable in liberal policy circles.

What it does not yet have is labor. Organized tenant movements. The communities most directly burdened by the infrastructure deficit it correctly names. That is not yet a fatal flaw. The mugwumps were a necessary precondition for the Progressives — they changed what was sayable, softened the intellectual ground, made the problem legible. But they confused articulation with implementation, and the distance between those two things is where most reform efforts go to die.

California is instructive here. The Abundance conversation has genuinely opened space to discuss housing construction and permitting reform that was, five years ago, effectively outside the Overton window. Real progress, genuinely important. But the state's deeper dysfunction — the constitutional scar tissue accumulated since 1879, the overlapping 7,000 jurisdictions, the initiative-calcified budget mandates that make coherent statewide governance essentially impossible — that is largely untouched.

The wonks got the diagnosis right. What my old book, *A New California Dream*, argued in 2011 still stands: California never had a real founding. Its 1849 constitutional convention was a forty-three-day rush job, its 1879 revision a railroad-era kludge, and the five hundred-plus amendments since have not fixed the underlying problem but accreted around it. The system isn't broken in the sense of malfunctioning. It's functioning exactly as the accumulated political settlements of a century and a half demand. That is a much harder problem than inefficiency.

The Abundance movement is doing mugwump-level work. That is not an insult. The mugwumps were necessary. But if the pattern holds, what comes after them won't look like a more sophisticated version of wonkery. It will look like a cross-class coalition with enough organizational depth to survive a real counterattack from the interests that benefit from dysfunction.

We are not there yet. The question is whether we get there before 2042, or after.
