# Networks vs. Hierarchies Change in History

## Reference

- *The Square and the Tower: Networks and Power, from the Freemasons to Facebook* by Niall Ferguson (2018)

## Book Summary

Niall Ferguson argues that historians have over-told history as a story of hierarchies — states, churches, armies, corporations (the tower) — while neglecting the informal social networks (the square) that often actually drive change. Drawing on modern network science, he reinterprets episodes from the printing revolution and the Reformation through the Enlightenment, the American Revolution, and the twentieth century as contests between networks and hierarchical orders. Networks excel at innovation and can overthrow ossified hierarchies with startling speed, but they are fragile, poor at governing, and vulnerable when a hierarchy attacks them directly. Totalitarian regimes succeeded precisely by delegitimizing or destroying every social network outside the party-state, while informal networks operate through reciprocity and mutual support, following neither market logic nor hierarchical command. Ferguson reads our own era, like the age of the printing press, as a networked age disrupting established hierarchies, with the outcome genuinely undecided.
