# Institutional Capacity and Internet Transition

## Reference

- [The Internet Transition](https://berjon.com/internet-transition/) by Robin Berjon

## Article Summary

Robin Berjon argues that the Internet is not merely a new technology but the trigger of a major evolutionary transition, on the order of single-celled life becoming multicellular. The core problem is an institutional capacity gap: the Internet made greater institutional capacity possible, raised the complexity our institutions must handle, and broke existing institutions, while we have not yet used the new governance capabilities it enabled. Collapse is no escape, since it simplifies social organization rather than individual lives and forecloses any climate response. Top-down governance also fails, for the legibility reasons James C. Scott identified, and because technical architecture is itself politics — choices like client-server versus peer-to-peer decide who can change the rules. Berjon's way through is Cambrian Governance: a polycentric, networked ecosystem modeled on Rodney Brooks's subsumption architecture, where many overlapping decision centers operate under shared rules and subsidiarity runs horizontally rather than down a hierarchy.

## My Notes

- The institutional capacity gap is a dynamic that is strongly related to the divergence world machine.  In fact, I think it might be one of the key dynamics driving Divergent Logics. This suggests things to pay attention to or study further:  where is the internet increasing complexity? How are institutions losing capacity? What dynamics are created when those two dynamics meet?
- The author argues that complexity is good in society and that the way to deal with this increasing complexity is through improving and advancing both hard technologies and social technologies. This is an implicit rebuke of the degrowth types, and I strongly believe that this is the correct posture to take
- The author mentions that top-down governance cannot work in a world with increasing complexity (a divergent world). The issue here is that top-down approaches require reducing complexity, like the idea of Seeing Like a State. The legibility required by modern bureaucracies will not work in a divergent world. To successfully govern in a divergent world, we will need systematized or protocolized approaches rather than Modern bureaucratic ones 
-  One interesting idea of this article is that architecture is politics. Design choices, architectural design choices made at the beginning of the internet, have governed the shape of the internet all the way up the technological and social stack. If protocolized approaches to governance are adopted, this dynamic will be important to anticipate. Architectural rules are generative, and constraints are generative. We should understand that protocolized approaches to governance will create second- and third-order effects, and we should be aware of this as we design protocolized approaches to governance
