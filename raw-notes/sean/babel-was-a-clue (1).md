# Babel Was a Clue

*Reading an AI encyclical for the small signal of a large, slow change*

[*Magnifica Humanitas*](https://www.vatican.va/content/leo-xiv/en/encyclicals/documents/20260515-magnifica-humanitas.html) presents itself plainly enough. Leo XIV, May 2026: a long encyclical "on safeguarding the human person in the time of artificial intelligence," threaded through 135 years of Catholic social teaching from [*Rerum Novarum*](https://www.vatican.va/content/leo-xiii/en/encyclicals/documents/hf_l-xiii_enc_15051891_rerum-novarum.html) forward. Dignity, the common good, subsidiarity, solidarity, social justice. And a clue.

What is this clue? He organizes the whole document around two biblical scenes: the Tower of Babel and Nehemiah's rebuilding of the walls of Jerusalem. Babel is "a single language, a single technology, a single direction" (§7), a bid to "make a name" by reaching heaven without God, and it ends in scattering. Nehemiah is a ruined city rebuilt family by family, each given "a section of the wall," recovering a common language that is "not one of uniformity, but one of communion" (§8) — one that "rebuilds relationships before rebuilding with stones."

If you've been sitting in the [Contraptions](https://contraptions.venkateshrao.com/) book club this year, that pairing trips a wire.

Here's the wire. Our [worldmachines.org](https://worldmachines.org/) framework reads modern history as the work of two engines pulling against each other. The **Modernity Machine** is a *pull* system: it drags everyone toward a single imagined future — universal development, scientific mastery, one best way to live — and calls the pull Progress. The **Divergence Machine** is a *push* system: it produces radically different outcomes depending on local conditions and admits no single destination, no one place we're all headed. Convergence versus divergence; one future versus many. Once you have the two in hand, Leo's opening is not a good-versus-evil contrast at all. Babel is the Modernity Machine — one language, one direction, the pull to the single future, ending (note this) in scattering. Nehemiah is the Divergence Machine — many hands, many sections, plurality that somehow coheres without being flattened. And the metaphor sides *against* convergence. Which is the last thing you'd expect from the most convergent institution on Earth: the one that runs on a single universal truth radiating from one centerline in Rome.

So: why would the Pope open an AI encyclical with a diagram out of our book club?

Here's the working rule that makes the question worth chasing. Metaphors come first. A writer reaches for an image before the argument is settled, and then, across a long text, the argument drifts to fill the space the metaphor opens. The metaphor is the seed crystal; the prose grows into its lattice. And here's why a book club bothers chasing a single image in a single document: we aren't cataloguing divergence the way birdwatchers tick off species. We're hunting the small early signals of changes that are big and slow — the kind that take centuries to fully arrive, and that are legible at the start only as a clue. Babel/Nehemiah might be one. So pull the string.

The easy half first. The "technocratic paradigm" — the document's central villain — is pure Modernity Machine: "the logic of efficiency, control and profit" (§92) made the measure of all things, "reducing creation to an object of exploitation and human beings to mere cogs" (§92). That's Babel in 2026 dress, a single digital language that promises to translate everything "into data and performance" (§10). The Babel pole is real, and the text knows it is Babel.

Now the harder half. One divergentist metaphor at the front, or a whole vein?

A whole vein. Truth is a "multifaceted polyhedron," in which "the one truth of the Gospel is reflected from different angles" (§25) — plurality that refuses to flatten into a sphere. "No single model of change or universal solution exists" (§153) — an institution built on universal truth renouncing the one-best-way at the level of solutions. The "rejected stones — the poor, the sick, the migrants" become "the cornerstone" (§16): the places the old machine filed as error are where the future gets built. In the book club's shorthand, the bugspace of the Modernity Machine is the feature space of the Divergence Machine — and Leo keeps building on the bugspace.

Worth a correction here, because it tightens rather than weakens the read. Leo isn't blessing divergence as *scattering* — scattering is Babel's curse, not its cure. His word is communion: many sections of wall, one city; one truth "reflected from different angles" (§25) rather than flattened. But that's the same thing our framework keeps straining to name — a Divergence Machine isn't fragmentation either; it's plurality that coheres without being flattened. Two vocabularies, one structure, one enemy: homogenization.

Then the strangest strand. Describing AI itself, Leo says the systems are "more 'cultivated' than 'built'" (§98) — grown inside a framework rather than designed part by part, so that even their makers "do not understand what they produce" (§99). That's a growing metaphor, not an engineering one, and *does it smell like evolution?* is one of our own divergence probes. He reaches for evolution at the exact moment he is most afraid of the thing. And then he says it outright: his verb for what to do with AI is "to disarm," which means "restoring it to the plurality of human cultures and ways of life" (§110). Not convergence in disguise — an instruction to *de-converge* a technology.

How far does the string go? Look at what Nehemiah actually does. He doesn't overthrow Persia. He is "in the service of the Persian King Artaxerxes" (§8), asks permission, and rebuilds *alongside* the empire — which is, almost exactly, the book club's principle that the divergence machine never fights the old order but brackets it and builds past it. Leo chose a parable whose hero brackets the empire. I doubt he chose it because he'd read the book club. The metaphor chose for him.

Someone will object before you finish the sentence: *this is just metamodernism.* In the framework's telling, metamodernism is modernity that cannot mourn itself — a backward-looking structure of feeling that reaches for re-enchantment and sincerity precisely because the Progress it still wants has stopped arriving, oscillating between irony and earnestness over the ruins.[^1]None On that reading the encyclical looks like a textbook case: re-enchantment, the meaning crisis, the doleful gravitas that wants what modernity used to deliver and can't have back. Subsidiarity isn't divergence; it's decentralized modernity, federalism with incense. Isn't this Dusk-Modernity necromancy in a Dawn costume?

The tell the objection can't absorb is that the metaphor was a *choice*. The convergent default was right there on the shelf — the Pauline body in Christ, one organism, one head; it even surfaces in the conclusion, under the heading "One body in Christ" (§234). Leo didn't open with it. He opened with a building site: scaffolding, distributed labor, a wall going up while the city is still half-rubble. Necromancy picks ruins; he picked a construction site. The metaphors are the part of this document that is *alive*[^2]None — and they are doing more work than the document quite needs them to.[^3]None

So what is the live metaphor pointing at? Liveness, in the encyclical, isn't a vague mood of presence-over-optimization. It is two specific and rather hard claims. First, humanity as **embodied incarnation** — the "wounded yet beloved flesh" (§231), the body as "a dwelling place of God and a temple of the Holy Spirit" (§239). Second, humanity as **being made in the likeness of God** — dignity as a gift that "precedes and transcends each person" (§50), not earned, not computed, not revocable. You are a body, and you are an image.[^4]None That is what the document is defending when it defends the human.

And here is the genuinely strange thing, the thing worth stopping on. *It is weird that this seems weird.* An institution stands up and says: you are embodied, you are made in the image of God, and that is the ground that no optimizer can touch — and in 2026 this reads as a contrarian, almost eccentric position rather than a banal one. Hold that reaction up to the light, because the reaction is itself the clue. The water we swim in has shifted far enough that *you are a sacred body* now lands as a provocation. The encyclical's anti-"Promethean dreams" stance (§128) is aimed at exactly the current that did the shifting — but I'll leave that thread for another day. What I want logged here is narrower and stranger: not the claim, but our *surprise* at the claim. Watch that surprise over time. If what counts as "weird" in this region keeps moving, that drift is a machine doing its slow work, and you'll have seen it early.

Now zoom out, because the deepest tell isn't in this document at all. It's in the shape of the centuries behind it.

For the entire run of the Modernity Machine, the Catholic Church has been on its back foot — not in two or three discrete moves but in one long, continuous adjustment, reacting and re-steadying against an engine that kept moving the ground. It opens with the Counter-Reformation: a convergence clamp (Trent, the Index, centralization). Trent was nothing if not propositional — but the Church also answered the printing press in marble, light, and ceiling. Bernini, the Baroque, the room that swallows you whole: beauty as counter-modern media strategy, not decoration. And from there the response never really stopped — a centuries-long sequence of resistances and accommodations, all of them made from inside the pull system, all of them trying to find footing on its terms.

What changes the picture is that, late in this long response, the Church gave formal doctrinal shape to an instinct it had carried unevenly for centuries but never named. Local, monastic, diocesan, guild-shaped pluralism is old; *subsidiarity as a systematic principle of social teaching* is not. The word is barely a hundred years old: the lineage runs through Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum* (1891), and it was only "systematically formulated" by Pius XI in 1931 (§31).[^5]None A *push*-shaped principle, in other words, named and made a pillar of the doctrine of a pull-shaped institution less than a century ago — and then mostly left as a quiet sub-clause rather than a lead instinct.[^6]

What Babel/Nehemiah lets us glimpse — no more than glimpse — is that quiet sub-clause moving toward the front. Not the clamp and not the accommodation, but the push-shaped instinct surfacing in the *metaphors* of an AI encyclical, paired with a liveness the rest of the room isn't arguing for — the labs optimize, the states make legible, the critics mourn, and almost no one with real power says *you are a body and an image.*

I'm not claiming this is the future of the Catholic Church. Things this size start far too small for anyone to claim that. The response to modernity has run for four centuries and is still running; if something new is beginning inside it, the new thing would be invisible now except as exactly this — a choice of metaphor, a vein of liveness-talk, a Pope opening a document about machines with a half-built city instead of a single body.

A metaphor can take an institution somewhere it never meant to go. Whether this one does, we won't know for a long time — which is the whole point. It is precisely the kind of small, slow clue this book club exists to catch. Now it's logged.

[^1]: I'm taking this characterization of metamodernism from the *Divergence Machine* essays ([part I](https://contraptions.venkateshrao.com/p/the-divergence-machine), [part II](https://contraptions.venkateshrao.com/p/the-divergence-machine-ii)), where it figures as a late, backward-facing structure rather than a forward one — modernity's afterglow, not its successor. Stating the objection in the framework's own terms is the point: a strawman metamodernism would be cheap to knock down and prove nothing.

[^2]: The early criticism has mostly come from the other direction — that the encyclical is too socialist, or reads like a European bureaucrat's wishlist (procedural safeguards, GDP-beyond metrics, "transparency and accountability" by the paragraph). On our terms that criticism is half right, but mislocates the problem. Those passages *are* the weakest in the document — but not because they lean left or technocratic. They're weak because they're *dead*: convergence-flavored proceduralism, the machine's own language borrowed to police the machine. The life in this encyclical isn't in its policy. It's in its metaphors. The critics and I are pointing at the same paragraphs and drawing opposite conclusions about why they fall flat.

[^3]: Worth being precise about what "alive" is doing here. In the framework it isn't a compliment, it's a diagnostic: a live element is generative — it opens moves, recombines, points past itself — where a dead one merely administers or preserves. By that test most of the document's *prose* is administrative and much of its *imagery* is generative, which is the whole tension this essay is pulling on.

[^4]: The pairing is older than it looks. In the Aristotelian-Thomist grammar the Church reasons in, body and image aren't two attributes bolted onto the human; they are *matter* and *form* — the body the *materia*, the *imago Dei* something like the *forma* that makes the matter a person rather than a mechanism. Hylomorphism is the technical name. It bears on the AI question in a way I'll only gesture at here: the live issue isn't whether a system is clever but whether it is the *form of* anything — a substantial form actualizing a body, or only very fast reasoning with no one home. Under its plain language, the encyclical is making a hylomorphic claim about what a human is. (A thread for another essay.)

[^5]: Pius XI, *Quadragesimo Anno* (1931), is where subsidiarity receives its canonical formulation; the deeper root is Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum* (1891), the document this encyclical is consciously written in the lineage of. The dating carries weight in the argument: a roughly hundred-year-old principle surfacing now is a far more legible clue than some timeless Catholic preference for the local, which is what a looser telling would imply.

[^6]: The encyclical shows you the tempo itself. Its section on slavery calls the Church's long complicity "a wound in Christian memory" and, "in the name of the Church," asks pardon (§176), noting that slavery's "full incompatibility" with the faith took eighteen centuries "to be explicitly recognized." Same slow-motion pattern: not new doctrine replacing old, but a truth formally held becoming a social reality the institution could finally see. If it can take eighteen centuries to fully see one of its own commitments, a hundred-year-old instinct surfacing in a metaphor is exactly the size and speed of clue to expect.
