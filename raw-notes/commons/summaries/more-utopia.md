# Utopia (Thomas More, 1516)

**Author**: [[thomas-more]] · **Narrator**: [[raphael-hythloday]] · **Founder-legislator of the island**: [[utopus]]
**Edition read**: Gilbert Burnet's 1684 English translation, edited by Henry Morley

Thomas More's *Utopia* is a two-book Latin dialogue that first diagnoses the social evils of enclosure-era England and then answers them with a fictional traveller's report of a communal, standardized island commonwealth. Beneath what Morley calls its "veil of a playful fiction" lies "a noble English argument."

## What the book is

Per [[more-utopia-introduction]]: written in Latin in 1515–16 and composed back-to-front — Book 2, the description of the place ("Nusquama," or "Nowhere"), was drafted first in late 1515, and the Book 1 frame was added early in 1516. It was printed at Louvain (1516, edited by Erasmus and Peter Giles), Basle (1518, revised by More), Paris, and Vienna, yet "was not printed in England during More's lifetime"; English readers waited for Ralph Robinson's 1551 translation and then Burnet's more skilful 1684 version. The fictional frame grows out of a real 1515 diplomatic embassy to Flanders, where documented fact "slides into fiction with the finding of Raphael Hythloday." Morley reads the whole as protective satire — England displaced onto France, "ironical praise of the good faith of Christian kings" doing double duty as censorship insurance.

## Book 1 — the dialogue: whether to serve, and what ails England

- **Meeting Hythloday** ([[more-utopia-book1-01-meeting-hythloday]]): the weathered Portuguese philosopher who sailed with three of Vespucci's four voyages; the traveller as an alternative epistemic authority; the book's governing question is institutions, not marvels — "states that are well and wisely governed."
- **Serving princes** ([[more-utopia-book1-02-serving-princes]]): Hythloday refuses to counsel a king; courts are closed flattery-loops, and appeal to ancestral custom is an unfalsifiable veto. More's persona answers with the fountain metaphor — good and evil "flow from the prince... as from a lasting fountain."
- **Cardinal Morton's table** ([[more-utopia-book1-03-cardinal-morton-theft]]): the book's most famous social criticism — idle retainers, standing armies, and enclosure ("sheep devour men") manufacture the thieves the law then hangs. "You first make thieves and then punish them."
- **The Polylerits** ([[more-utopia-book1-04-polylerits-punishment]]): restitution and marked, unchained penal labour instead of the gallows — and the satire that the same proposal is scorned from Hythloday but applauded when the Cardinal repeats it.
- **The jester and the Achorians** ([[more-utopia-book1-05-jester-and-mock-counsel]]): court flattery snaps to whatever power has already blessed; the Achorian fable argues that conquest is a losing trade.
- **Corrupt counsel and the Macarian cap** ([[more-utopia-book1-06-treasure-and-corrupt-counsel]]): five arts of fiscal extraction and judicial capture, answered by the Macarian kings' oath never to hold more than a thousand pounds of gold.
- **Civil philosophy vs. withdrawal** ([[more-utopia-book1-07-philosophy-in-courts]]): the Plautus-comedy analogy for pliable engagement against Plato's rain-simile for principled withdrawal.
- **Against private property** ([[more-utopia-book1-08-common-ownership]]): no nation with property and money can be governed "justly or happily"; Peter Giles's incentive-and-order objection; the pivot from argument to eyewitness — "five years among the Utopians" — that turns the rest of the book into travel report.

## Book 2 — the description of the island

- **Geography and founding** ([[more-utopia-book1-09-island-of-utopia]]): the crescent island and its hidden, native-piloted channel; [[utopus]]'s fifteen-mile cut severing the land from the continent; fifty-four identical towns a day's walk apart; rotating two-year farm labour so the island is never ignorant of agriculture.
- **Amaurot** ([[more-utopia-book2-01-towns-amaurot]]): the exemplary town; uniform three-storey houses owned by no one and redistributed by lot every ten years; a founder who laid the frame and left the finish to successors.
- **Trades and the six-hour day** ([[more-utopia-book2-03-trades]]): every citizen trained in agriculture plus one craft; uniform undyed dress; an audited six-hour day; an anti-luxury theory of abundance in which labour is the means and improvement of the mind the end.
- **Traffic** ([[more-utopia-book2-04-traffic]]): demographic quotas rebalanced by moving bodies between households and colonies; colonization licensed by a natural-law claim on "idle" land; moneyless storehouses, communal dining halls, and public hospitals.
- **Travel and the contempt for gold** ([[more-utopia-book2-05-travelling-01-trade-and-gold]]): passported, tracked mobility; life organized for total visibility; gold and silver deliberately debased into chamber-pots and slaves' chains, dramatized by the humiliated Anemolian ambassadors.
- **The ethics of pleasure** ([[more-utopia-book2-05-travelling-02-pleasure-ethics]], [[more-utopia-book2-05-travelling-03-true-pleasures]]): a religiously warranted hedonism bounded by a rational calculus; true pleasures of mind and body sorted from false ones, with health as the foundation.
- **Learning and printing** ([[more-utopia-book2-05-travelling-04-greek-learning]]): tireless land-improvement, a three-year mastery of Greek, and the independent reinvention of papermaking and the press.
- **Slaves, marriage, and plain law** ([[more-utopia-book2-06-slaves-marriages]]): slavery as a calibrated penal category; priest-sanctioned voluntary death; the mutual nude pre-marital inspection; plainly worded laws and the abolition of lawyers.
- **Military discipline** ([[more-utopia-book2-07-military-discipline]]): war held in contempt yet waged as an administered economy of assassination bounties, expendable Zapolet mercenaries, and tribute.
- **Religion** ([[more-utopia-book2-08-religions-01-toleration]], [[more-utopia-book2-08-religions-02-priests]], [[more-utopia-book2-08-religions-03-worship]], [[more-utopia-book2-08-religions-04-conclusion]]): toleration under Utopus's founding law; the ascetic Brutheskas and a tiny elected priesthood; imageless worship of one Divine Essence called Mithras; and the closing "conspiracy of the rich" peroration, undercut by the narrator's famous refusal to fully assent.

## Throughlines

- **The frame's irony.** The dialogue keeps both positions live: the reader gets Hythloday's argument at full force, then watches "More" the narrator decline to endorse it, wishing "rather than hope" to see Utopian laws followed at home.
- **Common ownership as structural cure**, with pride — not scarcity — named as the load-bearing vice that keeps unjust systems standing.
- **Legibility as a design principle.** From the fifty-four identical towns to the audited day to the abolition of private "corners," Utopia imagines a total, readable order. This cross-cutting move is developed at [[the-legible-commonwealth]].

## In the World Machines frame

*Utopia* (1516) sits at the **Dawn** of the [[modernity-machine]] (constructed ~1200–1600), before cadastres, timetables, and double-entry books had actually built the legible world. The corpus reads it as that machine's "last sincere architectural drawing." The argument that its cure deploys the very legibility logic Book 1 shows destroying English commoners is set out in the synthesis [[the-legible-commonwealth]].
