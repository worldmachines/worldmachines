# The Relentless Revolution (Joyce Appleby, 2010)

*The Relentless Revolution: A History of Capitalism* (Norton, 2010) is [[joyce-appleby]]'s
history of capitalism from premodern scarcity to the 2008 financial crisis and its aftermath.
Its organizing move, made in the opening pages, is to invert the usual question. The puzzle is
not capitalism's triumph but its four-thousand-year **delay** — why "brilliant minds penetrated
some of the secrets of the cosmos but couldn't imagine how to combat hunger" for millennia
beforehand ([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch01-puzzle-of-capitalism]]).

## Thesis

Capitalism is "a cultural system rooted in economic practices" — not a law of human nature, not a
freestanding self-correcting mechanism, and not a destination history was travelling toward. Its
history therefore has to be told with attention to "culture, contingency, and coercion" together
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch01-puzzle-of-capitalism]]). Appleby states the methodological
corollary flatly midway through the book: "I want readers to resist the temptation to bring
capitalism onto the historic stage as something inevitable, because it was not"
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch04b-currency-crisis-and-new-economic-thinking]]).

The book's title is cashed out in its final line: capitalism is "relentless," but "not mindless" —
because market participants' experience and expectations shift across generations, societies keep
modifying and monitoring their economies rather than simply repeating past failures
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch13b-capitalisms-legacy-poverty-and-the-green-economy]]).

## The shape of the argument

**I. The trap and the break (chs. 1–4).** Traditional societies worldwide ran on scarcity and
inherited status rather than incentive, with upwards of 80 percent of labour committed to food that
still often fell short. Two centuries of global trade after 1500 did not by themselves break this —
Iberian bullion, Dutch commercial brilliance, and French *derogation* all produced commerce without
capitalism. What broke it was an agricultural transformation in the Netherlands and England, then a
two-century public argument that gave the market a vocabulary and a theory of human nature
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch02-trading-in-new-directions]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch03a-basket-of-consumables-and-population]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch03b-dutch-and-english-farming-improvements]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch04a-market-discourse-and-usury]]). See
[[agricultural-revolution]].

**II. Industry, slavery, and transfer (chs. 5–7).** Sugar plantations and steam-powered factories
are treated as "twin responses to the capitalist genie" — both capital-hungry breaks from inherited
practice, both imposing routinized labour at unprecedented scale. Science and craft fused into the
steam engine and the disciplined factory; Germany and the United States then acquired British
technique by espionage and emigration and overtook Britain within a generation of the 1851 Crystal
Palace, proving capitalism transferable rather than an English peculiarity
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch05a-slavery-labor-and-industrial-inventions]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch05b-science-enlightenment-and-technology-culture]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch06a-german-and-american-industrialization]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch06b-banks-incorporation-and-the-1873-depression]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch07-industrial-leviathans-and-their-opponents]]).

**III. Empire, corporate form, and catastrophe (chs. 8–9).** Surplus capital turned states into
"venture capitalists," producing the Scramble for Africa and Leopold's Congo, while Meiji Japan
borrowed the tools without submitting to the borrowing. Firms answered ruinous competition three
different ways — American merger and bureaucracy, German cartels, British family continuity. Then
the wealth industry had generated armed the alliance system, and war, punitive peace, and a
leaderless world economy produced the Great Depression and a second, deadlier war
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch08a-european-imperialism-in-africa-and-asia]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch08b-corporate-restructuring-and-american-industry]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch09-war-and-depression]]).

**IV. Reordering and diffusion (chs. 10–12).** Postwar recovery ran along three coordinating modes
Appleby labels *indicative*, *imperative*, and *informative*; the Marshall Plan and a
deliberately anti-nationalist European integration rebuilt the West; Cold War science built the
computer state; 1973 ended the golden age. Then Friedman-era deregulation, Japan's lean production,
the personal computer, the Four Little Tigers, Walmart's logistics, China's "bird cage economy,"
Russia's failed shock therapy, and India's dismantled License Raj carried capitalism outward along
paths that refused to converge
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch10a-marshall-plan-and-postwar-consumer-economy]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch10b-cold-war-science-computers-and-labor]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch11a-japan-and-the-rise-of-personal-computing]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch11b-internet-asian-tigers-and-walmart]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch12a-wto-and-chinas-economic-transformation]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch12b-eastern-europe-and-indias-transformation]]).

**Coda (ch. 13).** The 2008 crisis, a survey of practical anti-poverty innovators (Yunus, Sen,
De Soto, Barnes) and a nascent green-technology wave, and a closing judgment on capitalism's
incompatible partnership with democracy
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch13a-the-2008-financial-crisis]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch13b-capitalisms-legacy-poverty-and-the-green-economy]]).

## Throughlines

**"A succession is not a process."** The preconditions historians cite — printing, clocks, the New
World, papal law — were present in places that never became capitalist, so no single-cause account
survives. Appleby applies the same scepticism to her own century: America's industrial supremacy
carries a misleading "aura of an inexorable and optimal development generated from within the very
logic of capitalist enterprise," and postwar European integration "came from individuals like Monet
and Schuman, not from any economic laws"
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch01-puzzle-of-capitalism]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch08b-corporate-restructuring-and-american-industry]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch10a-marshall-plan-and-postwar-consumer-economy]]).

**Trade is a precondition, not a cause.** Commerce had flourished for millennia and would have gone
on doing so; merchants got rich buying low and selling high, but the wealth distinctive to
capitalism came from producing things more efficiently. Hence the Dutch puzzle — the era's best
commercial and financial institutions, yet no industrialization, which Appleby attributes to a food
dependency that "prized security above risk taking"
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch02-trading-in-new-directions]]).

**Agriculture came before industry.** The unglamorous switch from fallow to four-part rotation "did
more to make capitalism possible than trade in spices or the parade of industrial machines that
gets the credit," and the resulting collapse of the farm workforce from 80 to 40 percent is what
made mechanization rational in England specifically
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch03b-dutch-and-english-farming-improvements]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch05a-slavery-labor-and-industrial-inventions]]).

**Capitalism had to be argued into legitimacy.** England's unusually open press let pamphleteers,
moralists and "hired pens" redescribe human beings as self-interested, calculating and rational,
displacing an older picture of impulsive, sin-prone humanity — and the calico craze showed ordinary
consumers enacting the proposition before any economist theorized it. Two centuries later Appleby
reads the same pattern in reverse: capitalism's early proponents had to argue it into acceptance as
"natural, liberating, progressive, and rewarding" before it could be naturalized
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch04a-market-discourse-and-usury]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch13b-capitalisms-legacy-poverty-and-the-green-economy]]).

**Coercion is inside the story, not an aberration beside it.** Plantation slavery is treated as a
capitalist invention rather than a pre-capitalist survival, with racial ideology as retrospective
alibi ("circular reasoning kicked in"); the cotton gin made the domestic slave trade the American
South's dominant entrepreneurial activity; late-century imperialism was capital's overflow, not its
opposite; and Britain's deindustrialization of India turned a manufacturer into a raw-materials
supplier
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch05a-slavery-labor-and-industrial-inventions]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch06a-german-and-american-industrialization]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch08a-european-imperialism-in-africa-and-asia]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch12b-eastern-europe-and-indias-transformation]]).

**There is no single capitalism, and no convergence.** American risk-loving individualism and
German disciplined tenacity produced near-identical outcomes; three nations answered destructive
competition three different ways; capitalism proved able to "take hold in diverse social contexts
under government supervision and within communitarian cultures," and China and India's rise
undercut the assumed link between markets and democracy
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch06b-banks-incorporation-and-the-1873-depression]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch08b-corporate-restructuring-and-american-industry]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch11a-japan-and-the-rise-of-personal-computing]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch12a-wto-and-chinas-economic-transformation]]).

**No one is in charge.** Dispersed private decisions generate prices that "deliver information"
nobody centrally interprets until crisis forces the question; in 2008, locally rational choices —
Asian household saving, homeownership policy, bonus structures — converged into a collapse no actor
intended. Capitalism is "not a unified, coordinated system," which is at once its inventiveness and
the reason no one is accountable for its damage
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch06b-banks-incorporation-and-the-1873-depression]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch13a-the-2008-financial-crisis]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch13b-capitalisms-legacy-poverty-and-the-green-economy]]).

**Capitalism cannot stabilize itself.** Its health depends on an external legal and civic substrate
— courts, clean politics, transparent finance — that the deregulatory decades eroded; and democracy
and capitalism remain "the couple that can neither live with nor live without each other," since
they answer to different values
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch13a-the-2008-financial-crisis]],
[[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch13b-capitalisms-legacy-poverty-and-the-green-economy]]).

## Where it meets the World Machines frame

Appleby's anti-teleology is the book's strongest point of contact with WMP. Her "many elements,
some fortuitous" account of capitalism's emergence — a paradigm assembling out of thousands of
independent, uncoordinated decisions and only visible as a "revolution" in retrospect — is the
[[history-machine]] pattern stated in a historian's own idiom, and her explicit refusal of both
great-man and single-logic explanations makes the book unusually easy to read alongside
[[world-machines]] and [[psychohistory-and-world-machines]]. Her closing insistence that capitalism
has "no coordination from any center" sits close to [[Configurancy]]'s non-teleological
co-emergence.

Three further resonances recur across the notes. The book supplies a long series of measuring,
standardizing and record-keeping installations — Dutch bookkeeping schools, English fiscal
transparency, the Zollverein, IBM's punch card, Walmart's item-level inventory — that map onto
[[legibility-as-machine-core]], though Appleby consistently declines to give them causal primacy;
see [[legibility-as-precondition-not-cause]]. Her repeated finding that capitalism proliferates
institutional forms rather than converging on one is the [[divergence-machine]]'s signature. And
several of her transition moments — Versailles, the 1970s unravelling of the big-business /
big-labour / big-government consensus, the post-2008 vacuum — have the shape of a [[gramsci-gap]]:
an old settlement's legitimacy gone before a replacement exists.

The book also runs against parts of the frame in useful ways. Appleby's account of the Enlightenment
"conjectural history" that replaced cyclical rise-and-decay with cumulative, irreversible progress
is a direct genealogy of the assumption WMP's Dawn–Day–Dusk cycles reject
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch05b-science-enlightenment-and-technology-culture]]), and her
treatment of Smith's "uniform, constant, and uninterrupted effort of every man to better his
condition" is a conatus-like premise made load-bearing for an entire discipline (compare
[[Conatus]], [[adam-smith]]).

## Interlocutors

Appleby positions herself against Smith's naturalized "truck and barter," Marx's coercive class
formation, and — with the most credit — Weber's insistence that pre-capitalist Europeans had none of
the psychology later economists assumed. She also dismisses Diamond's biogeography, Landes's
blended-factors narrative "with no interactive mechanism," Crosby's purely intellectual account, and
Pomeranz's global-parity thesis ([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch01-puzzle-of-capitalism]]; on
Landes see [[david-s-landes]]). Later chapters take up Malthus, Locke, Hume, Schumpeter, Keynes,
Friedman, Rostow's modernization theory and the dependency theorists in turn.
