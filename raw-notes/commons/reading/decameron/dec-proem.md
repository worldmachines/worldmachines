# DEC 01 — Proem & Author's Introduction

The Proem is Boccaccio's dedicatory preface, written in his own voice, before the fictional frame of the *Decameron* proper begins. There is no narrator-king or narrator-queen here: the authorial persona speaks directly. He situates the book's origin in personal biography — a youthful, intense, and largely unrequited passion that nearly consumed him — and in the debt of gratitude he owes to those whose conversation and consolation helped him survive it. The project that follows (a hundred stories told by seven ladies and three young men during the 1348 plague) is presented not as literary ambition but as medicine dispensed to those who need it most.

## Themes

**Consolation as ethical obligation.** The opening logic is tight: Boccaccio was once afflicted and was helped; gratitude demands that he help others in turn; the appropriate vehicle is *pleasurable discourse* (stories). Storytelling is recast from entertainment into a form of care — a moral economy of reciprocated comfort.

**Gendered constraint and the asymmetry of relief.** The Proem's most analytically striking move is its argument for why women need this medicine more than men. Men who are lovesick can ride, hunt, fish, game, travel, trade — distraction is mobile and structurally available to them. Women of the period are "enmewed" (caged) in the "narrow compass of their chambers," constrained by fathers, mothers, brothers, and husbands, with no socially sanctioned way to externalize or discharge melancholy. Their fire burns hotter *because* it is hidden. This asymmetry of access to remedy is Boccaccio's justification for a female dedication.

**Love as passion that runs its own course.** Boccaccio's description of his youthful love is notable for its stress on involuntariness: neither reason, counsel, shame, nor fear of consequences could break or bend it. It abated only "of its own motion, in process of time." This is not romantic triumph but a natural subsidence — and importantly, it leaves behind only pleasance, not the earlier grievous ardour. Love is treated as a weather system that passes, not a choice or a virtue.

**Fortune's uneven distribution of endurance.** Fortune gives "less strength to endure" to those who are "delicate" — i.e., the gently-reared women of Boccaccio's imagined readership. The book is explicitly framed as a partial correction of Fortune's niggardliness: the author steps in where Fortune has been stingy with support.

**Utility alongside pleasure.** The stories are to provide not only "solace from the delectable things therein shown forth" but also "useful counsel" — teaching what is to be eschewed and what is to be pursued. The didactic and the pleasurable are co-equal goals, not ranked.

## Quotables

> "A kindly thing it is to have compassion of the afflicted and albeit it well beseemeth every one, yet of those is it more particularly required who have erst had need of comfort and have found it in any."
— Opening sentence; the moral axiom grounding the whole enterprise.

> "…constrained by the wishes, the pleasures, the commandments of fathers, mothers, brothers and husbands, abide most time enmewed in the narrow compass of their chambers and sitting in a manner idle, willing and willing not in one breath, revolve in themselves various thoughts which it is not possible should still be merry."
— The key gender-constraint passage; the word "enmewed" (caged like a hawk) is the most precise image in the Proem.

> "I purpose, for the succour and solace of ladies in love (unto others the needle and the spindle and the reel suffice) to recount an hundred stories…"
— The book's official dedicatory proposition; the parenthetical is a mild barb at women not in love, or alternatively a concession that other reading matter serves the unafflicted.

> "…nor shall it ever pass away, save for death. And for that gratitude, to my thinking, is, among the other virtues, especially commendable and its contrary blameworthy…"
— Boccaccio's grounding of the project in a virtue ethics of memory and obligation.

## WM resonances

**[[The Thicket]]** — The ladies' "narrow compass of their chambers" is structurally the inverse of the thicket: it is enclosure without interior Space, without portal-choice, a habitat that traps rather than shelters. Boccaccio's book is offered as a substitute thicket-in-text — a curated interior world the enclosed woman can enter. The brigata's actual retreat to the countryside (introduced at Day the First) makes this concrete, but the Proem already maps the logic: the afflicted need a bordered space with room to move inside it.

**[[capture-resistance]]** — The gender argument turns on the structural unavailability of self-directed movement for women: their conatus (the drive to seek relief, to discharge inner pressure) is suppressed by family and custom. The Proem names this suppression explicitly without using that vocabulary. Stories are positioned as the one form of "going about" open to the immobilized.
