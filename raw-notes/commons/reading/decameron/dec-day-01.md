# DEC 02 — Day the First

The unit opens with Boccaccio's Author-Prologue to the ladies (justifying the grim beginning as a "rugged mountain" one must cross for the plain beyond), moves into the celebrated plague description (1348 Florence), then narrates the formation of the *brigata*—seven ladies and three men who meet by chance at Santa Maria Novella and withdraw together to a hilltop palace two miles outside the city. Their first act in the country is to constitute a monarchy: Pampinea elected queen, a full household ordered, a daily schedule imposed. The stories of Day One are free-topic (each storyteller chooses what to tell); at day's end Pampinea passes the crown to Filomena, who announces the theme for Day Two: people who, after adversity, attain an unexpectedly joyful outcome.

## Themes

**Social order in collapse.** The plague section is a meticulous anatomy of what happens to institutions—law, custom, kinship, burial rite, sexual modesty, religious observance—when mortality breaks the enforcement mechanisms of civilization. Boccaccio distinguishes three response strategies (withdrawal and temperance; licentiousness; middle course with aromatics) and pronounces all of them equally ineffective. The breakdown is not moral but structural: the "ministers and executors" are dead, so "every one had license to do whatsoever pleased him."

**Flight and the curated enclave.** Pampinea's speech is a rational argument for self-preservation: remaining in the city means witnessing death or suffering dissolution of manners; withdrawing does harm to no one because one is already abandoned. The country villa is emphatically not a castle but a place of pleasure—gardens, music, wine, storytelling. The brigata enacts a carefully designed social form: rotating sovereignty (a king or queen per day), fixed schedule (canonical hours repurposed), divided household roles for servants, and a single explicit rule against outside news.

**Wit as the instrument of survival.** The Day One stories, though free-topic, cohere around a set of propositions: God's grace operates through the faith of petitioners regardless of the worthiness of the saint invoked (Ciappelletto); divine institutions survive their corrupt stewards (Abraham the Jew); a skilled debater can turn a forced question into a parable of mutual legitimacy (Melchizedek); an abbot who pursues a monk for carnal sin can be checkmated by the monk's observation of the abbot's own act (the monk and the abbot); a woman can deflect a king's advances with a feast of hens (the Marchioness of Monferrato); a sharp jab in plain speech can reform what formal reproaches cannot (the King of Cyprus; Guglielmo Borsiere and Ermino Avarizia). Storytelling is thus proposed as an instrument of moral and social management.

**Sacred fraud and the permissiveness of divine grace.** The Ciappelletto story, which opens the day, is the most philosophically ambitious. Boccaccio plants a real theological problem: if Master Ciappelletto fabricated an exemplary confession and was venerated as a saint, and if God nonetheless heard the prayers of those who prayed to him, what does that imply about the mechanism of intercession? Pamfilo's conclusion is careful: God regards "the purity of the suppliant's intent" rather than the worthiness of the intermediary.

## Notable stories

**Story 1 — Master Ciappelletto.** The worst man ever born—forger, perjurer, murderer, blasphemer, sodomite—falls mortally ill in Burgundy and performs a virtuoso false confession so devout that the friar absolvs him and subsequently preaches his holiness to the city. Ciappelletto is canonized by popular devotion. The frame question is whether God's grace can route through a fraudulent vessel; Pamfilo says yes, given the sincerity of the worshippers. The story also supplies a dark comedy of bureaucratic religion: the two merchant brothers worry less about Ciappelletto's soul than about a potential anti-Lombard riot if he dies unshriven.

**Story 2 — Abraham the Jew.** A Parisian merchant, Jehannot, tries to convert his Jewish friend Abraham. Abraham insists on going to Rome first to judge Christianity by its clergy. Jehannot dreads this will end the project forever. Abraham returns: he found the papal court a "forgingplace of things diabolical"—lust, avarice, simony under euphemistic names. But precisely because the Church survives its own officials, Abraham concludes the Holy Spirit must be its true foundation and converts. The story converts anti-clerical satire into paradoxical proof of divine superintendence.

**Story 3 — The Three Rings (Melchizedek).** Saladin traps the Jewish moneylender Melchizedek with the question "which of the three Laws is the true one?"—expecting an answer that will give grounds for seizure. Melchizedek tells the parable of a man with one precious ring who has three sons; he secretly commissions two perfect copies; each son receives one; no one can now determine which is the original. The question of which faith is true likewise "yet pendeth." Saladin admires the escape, discloses his financial need, and Melchizedek funds him freely. The parable is the most formally elegant piece of wit in the day.

**Story 5 — The Marchioness of Monferrato.** King Philip of France, inflamed by rumor of the marchioness's beauty, engineers a visit during her husband's absence. She serves a banquet of nothing but hen dishes. When the king asks whether only hens are born in these parts, she replies: "women, albeit in apparel and dignities they may differ somewhat from others, are natheless all of the same fashion here as elsewhere." He grasps the message, stifles his passion, and departs. A single prepared sentence, reinforced by a structural metaphor in the food, accomplishes what no direct refusal could have.

**Story 7 — Bergamino and Messer Cane della Scala.** A court minstrel, stranded when the magnate Cane withdraws his promised patronage and left paying out his wardrobe to his innkeeper, tells Cane a story—within the story—of Primasso the grammarian who arrives at the Abbot of Cluny's open table only to have the Abbot refuse him on an inexplicable impulse of stinginess; the Abbot, when he identifies his visitor, is ashamed and compensates lavishly. Cane recognizes himself in the Abbot and immediately makes Bergamino whole. The story illustrates how narration can enact the critique it describes, forcing the patron to become his own corrector.

## Quotables

"I say, then, that the years of the fruitful Incarnation of the Son of God had attained to the number of one thousand three hundred and forty-eight, when into the notable city of Florence, fair over every other of Italy, there came the death-dealing pestilence..." (plague opening)

"…brother forsook brother, uncle nephew and sister brother and oftentimes wife husband; nay (what is yet more extraordinary and well nigh incredible) fathers and mothers refused to visit or tend their very children, as they had not been theirs." (plague description)

"folk recked no more of men that died than nowadays they would of goats" (plague mortality)

"It is manifest that, like as things temporal are all transitory and mortal, even so both within and without are they full of annoy and anguish and travail and subject to infinite perils, against which it is indubitable that we, who live enmingled therein and who are indeed part and parcel thereof, might avail neither to endure nor to defend ourselves, except God's especial grace lent us strength and foresight" (Pamfilo, Story 1 opening)

"…the question which was the father's very heir abode pending and yet pendeth. And so say I to you, my lord, of the three Laws to the three peoples given of God the Father" (Melchizedek's ring parable)

"a sin that's hidden is half forgiven" (the Abbot, Story 4—Boccaccio noting the adage with irony)

"Cause Liberality to be here depictured." (Guglielmo Borsiere to Ermino Avarizia, Story 8)

## WM resonances

**[[The Thicket]]** — The brigata's retreat is a textbook thicket construction: curated membership, portal-bordered (they leave the city together, servants handled separately), interior regulated by its own rules (rotating sovereignty, no news from outside, schedule by canonical hours), defended by terroir (the country villa two miles from city walls, physically withdrawn "from the high way"). The contrast with the disintegrating city is explicit: the brigata is a habitat for a way of being when the ambient social order has collapsed. Pampinea's founding speech articulates the thicket rationale directly—withdrawal is not abandonment of the social, but creation of a better-regulated alternative space.

**[[gramsci-gap]]** — The plague section describes an interregnum with unusual precision: "the reverend authority of the laws, both human and divine, was all in a manner dissolved and fallen into decay, for [lack of] the ministers and executors thereof." Old legitimacy (civic law, canonical custom, kin obligation) is dead; the new form (the brigata, storytelling-governance) cannot yet be born at scale. The brigata is a small-scale solution inside the gap, not a resolution of it.

**[[legibility-as-machine-core]]** — Boccaccio's plague narrative is partly a story about what happens when legibility infrastructure fails: burial records, household census, neighborhood ties, institutional roll-calls—all break down. The pickmen (*becchini*) who replace the civic burial system are illegible operators, hired labor without names or functions that the old social map can assign. Abraham's reading of Rome is itself a legibility audit: he goes to "enquire into the manners and fashions of the Pope and Cardinals" and finds that the labels ("procuration," "sustentation") have been decoupled from their referents by those who administer them.
