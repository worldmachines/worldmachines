# DEC 06 — Day the Fifth

Fiammetta presides over Day Five and sets its governing theme: stories of lovers who, after grievous or ill-starred trials, arrive at happiness. The day spans ten novels whose geography is vast — Cyprus, Sicily, Rhodes, Tunis, Armenia, Ischia, Ravenna, Florence, Perugia — and whose social register runs from ruling kings to a cuckolded cobbler. The common thread is not luck but the vindication of love's worth: in story after story a social barrier, a captor's power, or a family's refusal collapses precisely because the love in question is proved — by courage, sacrifice, or a reversal of recognition — to have been deserved.

## Themes

**Love as civilizing force.** The Day opens with Cimon (Story 1), an aristocratic idiot literally called "brute beast," transformed by the sight of Iphigenia sleeping into first a man of culture and then a warrior. Pamfilo's framing rhetoric insists love can "uplift those souls that are subject unto it" from the lowest point. The motif recurs with Pietro/Teodoro (Story 7): a slave raised as a son, discovered to be a nobleman's son only when the noose is around his neck. The Day repeatedly treats social classification as provisional and love as a force that reclassifies.

**Fortune as antagonist and instrument.** "Fortune the unstable" is named explicitly in at least four stories. Fortune hands Cimon his prize then snatches it back via a storm; Fortune keeps Martuccio alive in a Tunisian prison long enough for a war to need his advice; Fortune drives Costanza's unmanned boat to Susa rather than letting her drown. Boccaccio's Fortune here is not random: it is a testing mechanism. Lovers who survive its reversals earn their ends; the Day's ethical point is that persistence in worthy love outruns bad luck.

**Recognition, identity, and hidden nobility.** Three stories (5, 7, and 1) turn on a discovery of true parentage or true rank. Agnesa turns out to be Bernabuccio's daughter, not a Lombard orphan; Pietro turns out to be the Armenian ambassador's son. These anagnorises are not arbitrary sentimentality — they resolve social impossibility by revealing that the obstacle (class mismatch) was always illusory. Identity, in Day Five, is something that fortune conceals and love's extremity finally discloses.

**Institutional authority vs. erotic right.** Several stories stage a direct confrontation between a sovereign's anger (a king, a captain, a father) and the claim that love constrained by long devotion deserves clemency. Ruggieri's appeal to King Frederick in Story 6 articulates the principle most cleanly: "defaults merit punishment, even so do good offices merit recompense." Authority that kills devoted lovers is consistently coded as an error; authority that corrects itself is coded as wisdom.

**The final two stories as counterpoint.** Story 9 (Federigo and his falcon) and Story 10 (Pietro di Vinciolo) function as deliberate tonal pivots. Story 9 is Day Five's moral jewel: no violence, no abduction — just courtly sacrifice in poverty, met by a widow's recognition of worth over wealth. Story 10 (Dioneo's privilege story) is almost an anti-novel: adultery ratified by a husband whose own desires are homosexual, ending with a three-way supper and no moral resolution. Boccaccio uses the contrast to frame the Day's range from idealism to comedy.

## Notable stories

**Story 1 — Cimon and Iphigenia.** A man of "brute" intellect is awakened to reason, culture, and martial skill purely through beholding a sleeping girl. He abducts her at sea, is storm-wrecked back to Rhodes, imprisoned, freed by the magistrate Lysimachus to help in a counter-abduction, and ultimately marries Iphigenia in Crete. The story is at once a hymn to love's civilizing power and an unflinching account of love-as-seizure, with two brides snatched from their wedding feast and their bridegrooms killed. Boccaccio does not reconcile these; he lets both valences stand.

**Story 2 — Costanza and Martuccio Gomito.** Costanza, believing her exiled lover dead, sets herself adrift in an unmanned boat to drown; wind carries her to Barbary where she lives for years in a Saracen household. Martuccio survives imprisonment by advising a king on a military ruse (narrow bowstring notches, incompatible with the enemy's arrows) and rises to wealth. The pair reunite through reputation spreading across the Mediterranean. The story is notable for its female agency — Costanza survives and recovers not through rescue but through competence and patience — and for the military-intelligence subplot, an anomaly in the Day's erotic texture.

**Story 8 — Nastagio degli Onesti.** The most formally strange story of the Day. Nastagio witnesses, in a pine-forest, a weekly supernatural re-enactment: a knight slays a disdainful woman, feeds her heart to dogs, and she resurrects to run again — punishment for cruelty to her lover in life. Nastagio stages a banquet at the same spot; his cruel beloved witnesses the vision, recognizes its application to herself, and capitulates to marriage that evening. The mechanism is fear, not love — cruelty is cured by terror of cosmic retribution. Boccaccio frames this as "Divine justice," but the machinery is closer to a coercive spectacle.

**Story 9 — Federigo degli Alberighi and the falcon.** The Day's centrepiece and one of the most celebrated stories in the Decameron. Federigo impoverishes himself courting Giovanna, who never returns his love; reduced to a farm and a single falcon, he kills the bird as the only meal fit for her when she unexpectedly visits to ask for it as medicine for her dying son. The son dies, the bird is gone, and Giovanna — moved precisely by the magnitude of what was sacrificed — chooses Federigo over all other men when her brothers press her to remarry. "I would liefer have a man that lacketh of riches than riches that lack of a man." No violence, no abduction: the resolution is entirely moral.

**Story 10 — Pietro di Vinciolo.** Dioneo's privilege story, broadly farcical. A Perugian husband who married only to conceal his homosexuality leaves his wife to pursue his own affairs; she takes a lover; the lover's presence is betrayed by an ass treading on his fingers under a hen-coop. Pietro recognizes the young man as someone he had long desired; the story ends with supper for three and an arrangement that satisfies all parties. The story's point is less titillation than structural symmetry: it replies to the Day's idealism with a reminder that "love" covers arrangements far outside the chivalric frame.

## Quotables

> "Love's arrow having, then, through Iphigenia's beauty, penetrated into Cimon's heart, whereinto no teaching had ever availed to win an entrance, in a very brief time, proceeding from one idea to another, he made his father marvel and all his kinsfolk and every other that knew him."
> — Story 1 (Pamfilo on Cimon's transformation)

> "The lofty virtues implanted by heaven in his generous soul had been bounden with exceeding strong bonds of jealous fortune and shut in some straitest corner of his heart, all which bonds Love, as a mightier than fortune, broke and burst in sunder."
> — Story 1 (Pamfilo's eulogy of Love as liberation)

> "The default of them committed, being bruited about everywhere, came to the ears of Ruggieri dell' Oria... 'Like as defaults merit punishment, even so do good offices merit recompense, let alone grace and clemency.'"
> — Story 6 (Ruggieri rebuking King Frederick)

> "Madam, since it pleased God that I should set my love on you, I have in many things reputed fortune contrary to me and have complained of her; but all the ill turns she hath done me have been a light matter in comparison with that which she doth me at this present."
> — Story 9 (Federigo on the falcon, discovering the cost of hospitality)

> "I would liefer have a man that lacketh of riches than riches that lack of a man."
> — Story 9 (Giovanna to her brothers, choosing Federigo)

## WM resonances

**[[The Thicket]]** — The brigata's daily management of narrative is a sustained thicket-construction: they have physically withdrawn to an estate, curated a rule of social intercourse, and designed each Day's topic as a portal through which only certain kinds of experience enter. Day Five's insistence that love finds its way through every obstacle — prison, storm, poverty, royal wrath — is itself a thicket argument: interiority and worth cannot finally be made legible or capturable by external power. The pine-forest in Story 8 functions literally as a thicket, both refuge and terror: it is the space outside the social order where supernatural justice operates, and where Nastagio's recognition of his own situation becomes possible precisely because he is alone, outside legible civil space.

**[[capture-resistance]]** — The stories repeatedly stage capture — literal imprisonment (Cimon, Martuccio, Pietro/Teodoro), abduction (Restituta, Iphigenia), enslavement (Pietro/Teodoro) — as the condition love must survive. What makes Day Five's resolutions satisfying is that the lovers' conatus — their striving to persist and expand into their chosen attachment — cannot be extinguished by any of these capture events. Martuccio advises his captor king rather than disappear into the prison. Gianni di Procida infiltrates a royal garden barefoot. Federigo maintains dignity in absolute poverty. The Day's moral is precisely that worthy desire is capture-resistant.
