# DEC 09 — Day the Eighth

Day the Eighth is governed by Lauretta, who sets the topic as tricks (*beffe*) played in every direction — men on women, women on men, men on each other. It is the most systematically comic day so far, yet it contains the collection's single most morally uncomfortable story: the scholar's revenge (VIII.7), which the other characters find troubling enough to require the reassurance of the lighter VIII.8. The frame opens on a Sunday morning, the company visiting a neighboring church before settling by the fountain for storytelling, a detail that underlines the Day's holiday mood — which the revenge tale then deliberately disrupts.

## Themes

**Wit as social weapon.** The Day's central claim, articulated in various registers, is that intelligence (or its absence) determines one's fate in the game of beffe. Gulfardo outsmarts a mercenary woman by using her own greed against her. The Varlungo priest out-maneuvers Belcolore with a fake pledge. Bruno and Buffalmacco repeatedly victimize Calandrino not through force but through his irrepressible credulity. The day's closing story (VIII.10) is the most elegant: a Florentine merchant cheated by a Palermo courtesan reverses the fraud so precisely that she loses twice what she extracted from him.

**The costs and limits of the trick.** VIII.7, the scholar's revenge, is the day's moral fulcrum. Pampinea frames it as an instruction: "craft is put to scorn by craft," and those who mock scholars had better beware. But the execution — a widow left naked on a tower all midsummer's day, blistered, parched, and stung by flies — appalls the women listeners, and the narrator herself calls the scholar "terribly stern and obdurate, nay, cruel." The story refuses to resolve whether the punishment fits the prank. The scholar's extended speech refusing mercy is one of the longest and most rhetorically charged passages in the Decameron.

**Legibility and performance.** Several stories hinge on the marks and instruments that make value visible: the Customs book that tells the Palermo courtesan exactly what a merchant is worth; the ginger-bolus ordeal that makes guilt legible on Calandrino's face; the doctor's scarlet gown that announces learning he does not possess. Status is costume.

**The credulity of vanity.** Calandrino (VIII.3, VIII.6) and Master Simone the physician (VIII.9) are comic studies in the same pathology: extreme susceptibility to flattery and fantasy produces a man who can be led almost anywhere. The fool believes in the heliotrope because he wants invisible power; the physician believes in the nocturnal orgy society because he wants to be recognized as worthy of it.

## Notable stories

**VIII.1 (Gulfardo and Guasparruolo's wife).** The Day's curtain-raiser: a German soldier, disgusted that a married woman will sleep with him only for two hundred florins, borrows the money from her own husband, pays her, then — with the husband present — reclaims the "loan" in front of him. The woman is trapped by her own greed; she must hand the money back and say nothing. Short, cold, paradigmatic.

**VIII.3 (Calandrino and the heliotrope).** The first Calandrino story is a masterpiece of comic escalation. Maso del Saggio plants a story about a magic invisibility stone in the Mugnone riverbed; Calandrino collects stones all morning, believes he has the heliotrope when his friends pretend not to see him, and walks home loaded with rubble — only for his wife's greeting to shatter the illusion. He beats her savagely for "causing him to lose" the stone's power. Bruno and Buffalmacco arrive feigning ignorance, hear the whole story from him, and depart, leaving him with a roomful of useless stones. The story's richness lies in the logic of self-betrayal: Calandrino, like a good Ptolemaic thinker, fits every event into the magic-stone theory right up until he can't.

**VIII.7 (The scholar's revenge on the widow Elena).** The Day's longest and most ambivalent story. Elena, a beautiful Florentine widow, keeps the scholar Rinieri standing in a snow-filled courtyard on Christmas night as a joke for her actual lover — then dismisses him as a fool. Rinieri, who "knew where the devil keepeth his tail," feigns continued devotion and waits months for opportunity. It arrives when Elena's lover abandons her and she naively asks the scholar to perform necromancy to win him back. Rinieri instructs her to stand naked on a remote tower at night; once up, he removes the ladder. She spends the next day — midsummer, cloudless — naked on the burning platform, bitten by flies and gads, blistered by sun, refused water. The narrator's aside ("she knew not aright, ladies mine, what it is to try conclusions with scholars") is the pivot between comedy and cruelty. The scholar's two refusals of mercy — both speeches — are meticulously argued, even beautiful in their pitilessness. The story ends with the widow recovering, her maid breaking a thigh on the descent, and the scholar "passing on, content."

**VIII.9 (Bruno and Buffalmacco dupe Master Simone).** The physicians' credulity story takes the longest build-up in the Day: Bruno and Buffalmacco (now appearing for the third time) cultivate a pompous returned-from-Bologna doctor by inventing an exclusive nocturnal "roving society" with magical banquets, queens from every country, and necromantic pleasures. The doctor feeds them for months in hopes of admission. The culminating trick — instructing him to wait on a tomb outside Santa Maria Novella at night, where Buffalmacco in a devil-mask "mounts" him and deposits him headfirst into a manure dyke — is less memorable than the build-up: Bruno's invention of the society, with its preposterous roster of imported queens, is one of the Day's comic high-points.

**VIII.10 (Salabaetto and Biancofiore).** Dioneo's story is a perfect structural reversal of VIII.1: a Florentine merchant is fleeced by a Palermo courtesan, then engineers his revenge by creating fake merchandise in the Customs register, borrowing a thousand florins against it, and vanishing — leaving the courtesan's broker to open casks full of seawater and bales full of tow. "Who with a Tuscan hath to do, must nor be blind nor see askew," she laments. The story is also the Day's clearest meditation on the Customs book as an instrument of trust and deception: the same legibility that made Salabaetto visible to Biancofiore in the first place is what he exploits to dupe her in return.

## Quotables

> "She knew not aright, ladies mine, what it is to try conclusions with scholars." — Pampinea, framing VIII.7 before the revenge unfolds.

> "And albeit I am no eagle, knowing thee to be no dove, but a venomous serpent, I mean to pursue thee, as an immemorial enemy, with every hate and all my might." — Rinieri to Elena on the tower (VIII.7); the moment where "chastisement" is explicitly distinguished from vengeance.

> "I swear to God that I would have written such things of thee that, being ashamed, not to say before other folk, but before thine own self, thou shouldst have put out thine own eyes, not to see thyself in the glass; wherefore let not the little rivulet twit the sea." — Rinieri on the power of the pen (VIII.7); a rare direct statement of the author-scholar's ultimate weapon.

> "Who with a Tuscan hath to do, must nor be blind nor see askew." — Biancofiore's epitaph on being outwitted by Salabaetto (VIII.10).

> "Thus, then, as you have heard, is sense taught to whoso hath learned no great store thereof at Bologna." — Closing sentence of VIII.9, the physician story; the Day's comic formula in miniature.

## WM resonances

**[[legibility-as-machine-core]]** — The Customs register of Palermo (VIII.10) is a small-scale legibility instrument: it makes merchant wealth visible, measurable, and actionable to strangers. Biancofiore uses it as a targeting mechanism; Salabaetto weaponizes it against her by writing false values into the same registry. The story shows both sides of legibility's double edge: it enables trust and coordination but also predatory extraction, and whoever controls what gets written controls the game.

**[[The Thicket]]** — The brigata's daily retreat to gardens, fountains, and "little neighboring churches" before storytelling is, as in earlier Days, a curated refuge from the plague and from undifferentiated social chaos. Day the Eighth's comedy depends on this enclosure: the stories are told from inside a space where wit is valued and cruelty can be examined without being experienced.
