# DEC 08 — Day the Seventh

Day the Seventh is set in the Ladies' Valley (Val delle Donne) — a locus amoenus apart from the city, ringed by birdsong, laurels, and a clear lakelet. Dioneo, the company's licensed libertine, governs; his chosen theme is tricks (*beffe*) that wives have played on their husbands, whether for love or self-preservation. The narrators explicitly frame the theme as both entertainment and practical instruction: women learn craft, and men are warned. The Day runs ten stories plus a frame conclusion. Every story ends with the husband thoroughly fooled, the wife's position either secured or improved, and the narrator's approval unmistakable.

## Themes

**Wit as the feminine sovereign.** The Day's core argument is that female intelligence, far from being limited by domestic confinement, is sharpened by it. Resourcefulness grows precisely in constraint. Every protagonist — Tessa, Peronella, Agnesa, Ghita, the wife from Arimino, Isabella, Beatrice, Sismonda, Lydia — improvises a deception under real time pressure, and every one succeeds. The narrators Fiammetta and others explicitly endorse this: jealous husbands *deserve* to be tricked; the law, if just, would excuse wives who act in self-defence.

**Jealousy as a self-fulfilling disorder.** Husbands in this Day divide into two types: the credulous simpleton (Gianni Lotteringhi, the Arezzo Tofano, Egano) and the jealous watchman (the husband from Arimino, Arriguccio, Nicostratus). Both types fail. The simpleton is too credulous to imagine deception; the jealous watcher is too consumed by his suspicion to read evidence correctly. In Story 5 the jealous husband literally confesses as a priest to his wife, hears her mock his surveillance, and is still outwitted. In Story 8, Arriguccio beats the wrong woman and cannot prove a thing.

**The household as theatre of improvisation.** The beffe in this Day are not pre-planned conspiracies but lightning-fast on-the-spot solutions. Peronella (Story 2) invents the vat-sale within seconds of her husband appearing. Isabella (Story 6) turns a second lover's armed exit into an alibi for the first. These scenes stage what we might call **conatus** under maximum pressure: the self's insistence on persisting — here through desire — finding unexpected channels when direct routes are closed.

**Ecclesiastical hypocrisy as a recurring note.** Stories 1 and 3 both mock clerical pretension. Gianni Lotteringhi is a pious wool-comber who collects "goodly orisons" from clergy in exchange for food and clothing; Fra Rinaldo uses scholastic logic to seduce his own gossip. The Church's supposed tools — exorcism, confession, godparent bonds — are repurposed as instruments of adultery or cover.

**The unreliability of the senses.** Story 9 (Lydia and the pear-tree) makes this thematic. When Nicostratus climbs the enchanted pear-tree he sees his wife and servant in flagrante; both deny it; he doubts his own eyes. The gag runs both ways: everyone in the tree sees "marvels," making the whole incident stand as a comedy of epistemological helplessness. The wife then has the tree cut down — destroying the "evidence" and ending the comedy on her terms.

## Notable Stories

**Story 1 (Gianni Lotteringhi and the "phantom").** Tessa's lover Federigo arrives on a night Gianni unexpectedly returns. She invents a phantom that has been frightening her and leads Gianni in a "conjuration" to the garden-door — the incantation is in fact a coded message directing Federigo to find two capons, eggs, and wine hidden at the foot of the peach-tree. The husband spits on cue; the lover eats well. The story is framed as dual-use practical lore: "get them by heart and they may yet avail you."

**Story 2 (Peronella and the vat).** The most physically bold of the Day. Peronella hides her lover Giannello inside a large vat moments before her husband returns — then claims she has already sold it to this same "buyer" for seven florins. Giannello demands the husband scrape it clean before he takes it; while the husband scrapes inside the vat, Giannello uses Peronella as a pretext for completing what was interrupted — the husband works obliviously below. The scene condenses physical farce, commercial language, and bawdy double-entendre into a single tableau.

**Story 5 (the jealous husband as confessor).** The most structurally elegant. A jealous Arimino merchant disguises himself as a priest to hear his wife's confession. She recognises him at once and uses the confession booth to feed him a tale of a priestly lover who visits nightly. Every detail — locked doors, sleep-spells, nightly visits — is literally true (her husband does all these things) and simultaneously framed as the lover's supernatural deed. After she unmasks his trick to his face, she walks him through the logic: the "priest" was him; the sleep-spells were his own sleep; the nightly visits were his own conjugal habits. He is left philosophically defeated. This is Day Seven's sharpest piece of wit.

**Story 7 (Lodovico/Anichino cudgels Egano).** Lodovico, disguised as servant "Anichino," gains access to Madam Beatrice by posing as the most loyal of servants. After Beatrice stages a false accusation to send Egano to the garden dressed as her, Anichino sleeps with her, then goes out to beat Egano with a stick while scolding "the wicked woman" who tried to seduce him. Egano returns convinced his servant proved his virtue by beating off his wife's nocturnal temptress; Beatrice tells him that since Anichino suffered "words" patiently and Egano took the cudgel for it, he should honour the servant. The physical comedy and the domestic logic dovetail perfectly.

**Story 9 (Lydia, Pyrrhus, and the enchanted pear-tree).** The most elaborate and structurally daring. Lydia must pass three proofs of love to win Pyrrhus: killing Nicostratus's favourite hawk in company, plucking a lock of his beard while playing, extracting one of his teeth with false medical pretence. She performs all three. Then in the garden she and Pyrrhus enact their liaison in Nicostratus's presence, and the "enchanted pear-tree" alibi is used to make him distrust his own sight. She ends by having the tree cut down as "the enemy of my honour." Pamfilo prefaces this as a story of Fortune rather than reason — the luck required is even higher than in the other tales.

**Story 10 (Tingoccio's ghost and the gossips of heaven).** Dioneo's own tale, anomalous in form: the ghost of Tingoccio returns from the afterlife to report, with apparent seriousness, that the sin of lying with one's gossip carries *no* special punishment there — "here is no manner of account taken of gossips." Meuccio, hearing this, immediately repents his earlier restraint and resolves to be "wiser in that respect for the future." The tale satirises both clerical moral taxonomy and the credulity required to believe in ranked afterlife punishments for specific social-category infractions.

## Quotables

"Phantom, phantom that goest by night, with tail upright thou cam'st to us; now get thee gone with tail upright. Begone into the garden to the foot of the great peach tree; there shalt thou find an anointed twice-anointed one and an hundred turds of my sitting hen." — Tessa's improvised "conjuration," Story 1; the entire double-meaning is packed into a formal ritual register.

"O Love, how great and how various is thy might! How many thy resources and thy devices! What philosopher, what craftsman could ever have availed or might avail to teach those shifts, those feints, those subterfuges which thou on the spur of the moment suggestest to whoso ensueth in thy traces!" — Lauretta's invocation opening Story 4; the clearest statement of the Day's thesis that erotic necessity is the mother of invention.

"It rejoiceth me mightily to see a wise man led by the nose by a woman, even as one leadeth a ram by the horns to the shambles." — the wife from Arimino, unmasking her jealous husband in Story 5; one of the Day's most direct feminist speeches.

"Here is no manner of account taken of gossips." — Tingoccio's ghost, Story 10; the Day's final punchline, collapsing both the ecclesiastical framework and the sexual taboo of gossipship into a single bathetic phrase.

## WM resonances

**[[The Thicket]] (as domestic opacity).** The women in Day Seven operate from what the context pack calls interior Space defended by opacity: the household, nominally under the husband's surveillance, becomes a habitat whose internal complexity the husband cannot read. Tessa's coded incantation, Sismonda's reordered bedroom, the wife from Arimino's rerouted access through the roof — all are exercises in sustaining an invisible pocket of autonomy within a formally legible domestic enclosure. The thicket here is the marriage bed itself: opaque to the husband's gaze precisely because its signals mean two things at once.

**[[legibility-as-machine-core]] (surveillance and its failure).** Multiple husbands in Day Seven attempt to impose legibility — the string-on-toe trap (Story 8), the disguised confessional (Story 5), the nightly watch at the door (Stories 4, 5) — and every attempt collapses. The jealous husband as surveillance agent is the Modernity Machine in miniature, trying to make domestic life readable and controllable; the wife's wit produces variety and opacity faster than the system can track. Story 5 is the cruelest version: the husband's surveillance mechanism becomes the instrument by which she feeds him disinformation.
