# DEC 12 — Conclusion of the Author

The Conclusion follows immediately after the close of the Tenth Day and the brigata's dispersal. There is no narrative frame — the storytelling society dissolves off-page — and Boccaccio steps forward as author-in-his-own-person to address "most noble damsels" directly. The unit is an extended rhetorical apologia, anticipating five classes of objection and disposing of each in turn before a brief envoi.

## Themes

**The reader as ultimate moral agent.** Boccaccio's controlling argument is that a text's harm or benefit is determined not by its content but by the disposition of whoever encounters it. Wine, fire, and arms each serve this analogy: all are useful, all can destroy, none is therefore "naught." The corollary is that blame for misuse attaches to the reader's "perversity," not to the instrument. This argument is structurally important because it pre-empts censorship by relocating moral responsibility entirely into the recipient.

**Contextual fitness.** A secondary line of defence insists on context: these stories were told "amidst gardens, in a place of pleasance and diversion," not in church or a school of philosophy, to people "of mature wit and not to be led astray by stories." The setting (the brigata itself) is the licence; remove the context and the question changes.

**The imperfection of all made things.** Against the charge that not every tale is equally good, Boccaccio invokes Charlemagne who could not make enough Paladins to fill an army, and the well-tilled field that still harbours nettles. No maker, God excepted, executes every piece equally well. The argument accepts the criticism and neutralises it.

**Authority of the painter and the preacher.** Boccaccio claims parity with the visual arts (painters depict Adam and Eve naked without censure) and with contemporary preaching (friars' sermons are "for the most part" full of "quips and cranks and gibes" today). The comparison is pointed: if licence is already practised in sacred contexts, the storyteller's latitude is modest indeed.

**Satirising the friars.** Boccaccio addresses the charge that he has been "venomous" about the friars with elaborate mock-courtesy: they are "a good sort of folk" who "eschew unease for the love of God" and "grind with a full head of water and tell no tales" — every clause a barb — but who "savour somewhat of the buck-goat." This is the one objection he refuses to retract, converting his defence into a renewal of the attack.

**Tone and the nutgall jest.** When accused of "quips and cranks and quodlibets" unbefitting a man of "weight and gravity," Boccaccio concedes he is "a man of weight" but says he is "so light that I abide like a nutgall in water." The self-deprecating wit is itself an enactment of the quality charged against him.

## Notable stories

This unit contains no tales; it is a pure authorial statement. The section heading in early manuscripts and the Payne edition calls it "Conclusion of the Author" precisely to mark it as outside the fictional frame entirely.

## Quotables

> "Corrupt mind never understood word healthily, and even as seemly words profit not depraved minds, so those which are not altogether seemly avail not to contaminate the well-disposed, any more than mire can sully the rays of the sun or earthly foulness the beauties of the sky."

— The book's central moral axiom: virtue and vice inhere in the reader, not the text.

> "Everything in itself is good unto somewhat and ill used, may be in many things harmful; and so say I of my stories."

— The compressed formula of the wine/fire/arms argument.

> "No field was ever so well tilled but therein or nettles or thistles or somewhat of briers or other weeds might be found mingled with the better herbs."

— The apology for uneven quality; also a quiet admission that the weeds are there.

> "They all, not to lead any one into error, bear branded upon the forefront that which they hold hidden within their bosoms."

— The rubrics (each tale's heading) as a legibility device: the book flags its own contents so the reader can navigate or abstain.

## WM resonances

**[[legibility-as-machine-core]]** — The closing remark that every tale "bears branded upon the forefront that which they hold hidden within their bosoms" (i.e. the summary rubrics) is a micro-instance of legibility as design principle: the book makes itself readable and classifiable so that individual readers can exercise informed selection. The Modernity Machine's legibility impulse appears here at the level of the book-as-object rather than the state.

**[[capture-resistance]]** — The entire argument for reader-sovereignty is a form of capture-resistance operating in the opposite direction: Boccaccio resists the capture of his text by censorious external interpretation by insisting the text cannot be made to *mean* harm to a well-disposed mind. The locus of authority is returned to the reader as autonomous agent, not to any supervising institution.

**[[The Thicket]]** — The brigata and its garden setting function as a thicket (curated refuge, interior space, portal-bordered) throughout the Decameron; the Conclusion ratifies this retroactively by insisting the tales were always already context-bound: their licence is coextensive with the garden's borders. Remove the thicket, and the argument for licence collapses.
