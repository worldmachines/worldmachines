# DEC 03 — Day the Second

Filomena governs the Second Day. The theme she sets is explicit: those who, "after being baffled by divers chances, have won at last to a joyful issue beyond their hope." The ten stories form the most structurally coherent day of the early Decameron: every tale deploys a long arc of catastrophe followed by a reversal that the protagonist could not have engineered from the start. The frame audience — seven ladies and three young men in a villa outside plague-struck Florence — listens to these restorations as implicit argument: fortune is real but not final.

## Themes

**Fortune as the operating system of the world.** Multiple narrators open with explicit meditations on Fortune. Pampinea (Story 3) gives the sharpest philosophical gloss: "all the things which we foolishly style ours are in her hands and are consequently, according to her hidden ordinance, transmuted by her without cease from one to another and back again, without any method known unto us." Fortune is not merely bad luck; she is a systemic force that strips identity, property, and rank — and sometimes restores them.

**Wit and resourcefulness as the only reliable counter.** The protagonists who survive and prosper are the ones who adapt to their degraded circumstances without collapsing into pure passivity. Alessandro accepts the disguised princess on her terms. Landolfo, twice ruined, recovers by never releasing the floating chest. Ginevra (Story 9) cross-dresses for six years and engineers her own vindication from inside the Soldan's court. Beritola (Story 6) nurses fawns in a sea-cave — hardly strategic, but she survives long enough for providence to act.

**Disguise, concealment of identity, and the gap between surface and truth.** Nearly every story turns on a character who is not what they appear: Alessandro mistaken for a monk's servant, Andreuccio taken for a cutpurse, the Count of Antwerp reduced to a horseboy, Ginevra living as "Sicurano da Finale." The Day is quietly fascinated by how easily identity is stripped and how it can be re-asserted only by accumulating new evidence from scratch.

**Women's agency within structural constraint.** The princess in Story 7 (Alatiel) is the extreme case: she is violently transferred nine times, never once in control of the transaction, yet survives by pragmatic accommodation and ultimately deceives her father and the King of Algarve about her past. Ginevra (Story 9) takes far more active vengeance. Bartolomea (Story 10) refuses return to her inadequate husband and stays with the pirate. The Day consistently portrays women navigating around, rather than through, the structures that constrain them.

**Fidelity, slander, and defamation.** Stories 8 and 9 are explicitly about false accusation: Gautier condemned on the queen's lie, Ginevra condemned on Ambrogiuolo's manufactured evidence. Both restorations require the original accuser to confess publicly. Boccaccio seems interested in how defamation travels faster than reputation and how long innocent people must wait for institutions to correct themselves.

## Notable stories

**Story 2 — Rinaldo d'Asti.** Robbed, stripped to his shirt in winter, Rinaldo shelters against a town gate and is taken in by a widow whose noble patron has cancelled. She gives him her dead husband's clothes, her bath, her supper, and her bed; his robbers are caught the next morning and everything is returned. The economy of providential symmetry is almost comic: the saint Rinaldo prays to each morning delivers compensation on an overnight cycle. The story also works as a dry joke about religious devotion as traveller's insurance.

**Story 5 — Andreuccio of Perugia.** The most technically accomplished story on the Day. A naïve horse-trader from Perugia arrives in Naples and in a single night falls through a privy trap, is locked out stinking, lowered into a well by thieves, shut inside an archbishop's tomb by the same thieves, and terrifies a second set of grave-robbers out of the church — emerging at dawn with a valuable ruby. Three catastrophes, each inadvertently followed by a partial recovery; the final gain comes only because Andreuccio has already been stripped of everything worth stealing. Fiammetta notes this arc compresses into one night what most Day-2 stories spread over years.

**Story 6 — Madam Beritola.** The most emotionally expansive story of the Day. A Neapolitan noblewoman loses husband, sons, and rank in the Sicilian political upheaval, lives feral on a desert island nursing baby fawns at her breast, is rescued by a Pisan pilgrim, spends fifteen years as a servant under a false name, and slowly — through her son's love affair and imprisonment, the Sicilian Vespers, and Guasparrino's belated honorable conduct — recovers her family. The goats, the nurse's prudent deception, and the scene of mother-son recognition by "maternal odour" are the Day's most quietly powerful images.

**Story 7 — Alatiel.** The Day's most audacious structural experiment: a princess sent from Cairo to marry the King of Portugal passes through nine men (shipwreck survivor, Majorcan lord, his brother, two Genoese sailors, the Prince of Morea, the Duke of Athens, the Emperor's son, a Turkish king's servant, a Cypriot merchant) over four years, returns to her father as a "maid" thanks to a brilliant coached narrative, and marries the Portuguese king after all. Pamfilo's frame — that beauty is dangerous because it causes men to do nefarious things — is ironic: the real argument is that Alatiel's survival is a function of structural vulnerability, not her choices.

**Story 9 — Bernabo and Ginevra / "Sicurano."** A merchant wagers on his wife's fidelity; his opponent cheats by hiding in a chest and memorising a bedroom detail plus a mole. The wife, nearly killed, cross-dresses for six years, rises in a foreign court to a position of authority, engineers her traducer's confession before the Soldan, and reveals herself. The deceiver is smeared in honey and staked in the sun to be eaten by insects — the Day's most gruesome punishment, and its most satisfying vindication.

## WM resonances

**[[The Thicket]] — the brigata as curated refuge.** The plague frame is still operative: the company has withdrawn to a hillside villa, carefully chosen, to insulate themselves from social disorder. Day 2's stories dramatise what happens when people are expelled from their thickets (noble rank, household, trade network) by force. Restoration is always back to a recognisable thicket, not to some novel form of life. The Day implicitly argues that thickets are fragile but reconstructible.

**[[Conatus]] — striving to persist under pressure.** Ginevra's six-year cross-dressed career, Beritola nursing fawns to stay alive, Andreuccio clinging to his floating plank — the Day is full of figures whose only virtue is the refusal to stop. This is Spinoza's conatus in narrative form: persistence as the foundational act, prior to any strategy.

**[[legibility-as-machine-core]] — identity as readable document.** The Count of Antwerp's restoration depends entirely on the queen's deathbed confession restoring the official record. Ginevra's vindication requires a public tribunal before the Soldan. Andreuccio's alibi is the inn-register of foreign arrivals. The stories reveal how much personal identity in the late-medieval social order was a matter of legibility — inscription in recognized records — and how catastrophically its absence operates. This is not a forced WM connection: Boccaccio's world already runs on proto-legibility infrastructure (guild registries, church records, noble lineage), and Day 2 shows what it feels like when that infrastructure fails an individual.
