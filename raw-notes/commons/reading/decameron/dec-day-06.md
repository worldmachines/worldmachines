# DEC 07 — Day the Sixth

## Orientation

Elisa presides over the Sixth Day. Her announced theme — those who, assailed by jibing speech, have vindicated themselves or escaped loss, peril, or shame with a ready word — shifts the *Decameron*'s register from plot-driven intrigue to rhetoric itself as the subject of study. Before the first story even begins, the cook Licisca bursts in with an earthy dispute about bridal virginity, winning Dioneo's instant verdict and setting the comic pitch. The Day closes with Dioneo crowned king for Day Seven and the ladies slipping away to bathe in the enclosed "Ladies' Valley" — a curated natural retreat — while he proposes the new theme: tricks women play on their husbands. Ten stories follow the announced frame; all turn on the moment of speech.

## Themes

**Wit as equalizer.** Social rank is repeatedly undone by the well-aimed sentence. Cisti the baker schools a patrician; Chichibio the cook escapes a flogging; Guido Cavalcanti — the philosopher — leaps a tomb and delivers an insult so compressed only one listener can decode it. The Day insists that intelligence is not indexed to station.

**The word as action.** Several stories make explicit that a saying can do what force cannot: Madam Filippa's courtroom speech not only saves her from death but actually changes the law of Prato. Fra Cipolla's improvised sermon turns a trap into a greater hoax than the one he had planned. Speech here is performative, not merely decorative.

**Fortune versus ready wit.** The Day's prologue (Filomena's meta-story about Madam Oretta) establishes a taxonomy: some responses are prepared ingenuity, others are sheer fortune dropping words into the mouth of the fearful (Neifile's note on Chichibio). Boccaccio explicitly holds both open.

**Class, body, and representation.** Story five on Giotto and Forese; story six on the Cadgers; the Licisca frame — all circle the comedy of bodies and lineage. Noble souls in mean vessels, and the reverse.

**Licence under plague.** Dioneo's defense of the Day Seven theme is the most direct speech in the entire *Decameron* about why permissive storytelling is appropriate: the judges have left the benches, the laws are silent, and survival licenses freedom of discourse. This is the book's own poetics stated aloud.

## Notable stories

**Story 1 — Madam Oretta and the horse metaphor.** A gentleman offers to carry Oretta "a-horseback" with a fine story, then delivers it so badly she begs to be set down. The tale-within-tale frame makes meta-commentary on narrative quality; Filomena uses it to open the Day as a statement about what good wit looks like.

**Story 2 — Cisti the baker.** Nature and fortune hide noble spirits in mean conditions; Cisti, a wealthy baker, stages a ritual of wine-offering that nudges a patrician (Messer Geri Spina) into requesting rather than demanding hospitality. When Geri's servant brings an enormous flagon, Cisti's "send him to the Arno" forces Geri to recognize his own social error. The finest node in the Day's class-wit theme.

**Story 7 — Madam Filippa of Prato.** Caught in adultery and hauled before a magistrate under a statute calling for her death, Filippa confesses openly, then argues — on grounds of consent and distributive justice — that the law is naught, that she gave her husband all he asked and had lawful surplus remaining. The crowd laughs and agrees; the statute is amended on the spot. The most radical story of the Day: speech reshapes civic law.

**Story 9 — Guido Cavalcanti at the tombs.** The historical Guido Cavalcanti, ringed by Florentine cavaliers who mock his Epicurean speculations, springs over a marble tomb and says, "Gentlemen, you may say what you will to me in your own house." Only Messer Betto decodes the compressed insult: the tombs are the house of the dead; men of ignorance and no learning are worse than dead. A five-word rebuke that requires a learned interlocutor to translate it to its audience.

**Story 10 — Fra Cipolla and the coals.** The longest and most baroque story: a mendicant friar's preachment on fake relics, a pranksters' switch of the Angel Gabriel's feather for coals, and Fra Cipolla's real-time improvisation of an absurdist travelogue through "Truffia, Buffia, Menzogna" (Nonsense-land, Cozen-land, Lie-land) ending in a new theological rationale for the coals being the very coals that roasted St. Lawrence. The trap becomes a more profitable confidence scheme than the original. Dioneo's showcase piece; the Day's most elaborate demonstration that improvisational intelligence tops prepared fraud.

## Quotables

> "Sir, this horse of yours hath too hard a trot; wherefore I pray you be pleased to set me down." — Madam Oretta (Story 1)

> "To the Arno" — Cisti the baker's answer when Geri's servant brings an oversized flagon (Story 2)

> "Should I cast it to the dogs? Was it not far better to gratify withal a gentleman who loveth me more than himself, than to leave it waste or spoil?" — Madam Filippa before the provost (Story 7)

> "Gentlemen, you may say what you will to me in your own house." — Guido Cavalcanti leaping the tomb (Story 9)

> "Passing the straits of San Giorgio, I came into Truffia and Buffia, countries much inhabited and with great populations, and thence into the land of Menzogna." — Fra Cipolla's improvised travelogue (Story 10)

> "Know you not that, for the malignity of the season, the judges have forsaken the tribunals, that the laws, as well Divine as human, are silent and full licence is conceded unto every one for the preservation of his life?" — Dioneo defending the Day Seven theme (Conclusion)

## WM resonances

**[[legibility-as-machine-core]]** — Madam Filippa's argument implicitly attacks a legibility instrument: a statute that imposes a single classification ("adulteress = death") without attending to consent, surplus, or the parties' actual arrangements. Her counter-speech insists on complexity that the law's blunt rule cannot hold. The crowd's laughing assent — and the law's amendment — models how lived complexity can, momentarily, force legibility structures to revise themselves.

**[[The Thicket]]** — The "Ladies' Valley" episode in the Conclusion is a brief but exact figure of a thicket: a round, naturally enclosed space entered by a single narrow way, interior meadows invisible to the outside, curated by its own miniature hydrology, a deliberately sought refuge from the afternoon heat of the plague-time. The ladies enter alone, bathe, and return. The geometry of a portal-bounded interior space with its own conditions is rendered almost topographically by Boccaccio.

**[[capture-resistance]]** — Fra Cipolla's set-piece improvisation is a working model of capture-resistance: a trap is set (the feather replaced), the target detects the trap mid-performance, and rather than collapsing, he converts the hostile intervention into a new and more profitable scheme. His conatus — his drive to persist and expand in the very moment of exposure — is the comic core of the story.
