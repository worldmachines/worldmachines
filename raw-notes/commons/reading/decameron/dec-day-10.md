# DEC 11 — Day the Tenth

Day the Tenth is governed by Pamfilo, who sets the theme as generous and magnificent deeds — whether in love or any other sphere. It is the collection's final and most overtly ethical Day, designed as a culmination: the brigata has survived the plague at a remove, told stories of wit and folly and eros, and now arrives at a considered account of human excellence. Pamfilo frames magnificence ("the light and lustre of every other virtue") in the opening story's frame, and the Day proceeds as a sequence of escalating demonstrations — each story inviting the company to debate which magnanimous act is the greatest — before closing on Griselda, whose extreme patience Dioneo, with characteristic irony, explicitly labels a "monstrous folly" that he counsels no one to imitate. The frame's conclusion is the brigata's dissolution: Pamfilo dissolves the company the next morning and they return to Florence, the plague awaiting them.

## Themes

**Magnificence as competitive virtue.** Each story is framed as a bid in a tournament of generosity. Can a churchman outdo a king? Can a man who guides an assassin to his own door outdo a churchman? Can a lover who rescues a buried woman and then freely returns her to her husband outdo all prior acts? The frame dialogue between stories makes explicit what is usually left implicit in the Decameron: narrators argue with each other's rankings. This turns magnanimity into a kind of social performance and reveals its paradox — genuine liberality, like Nathan's willingness to die for a stranger, may exceed all ordinary human calculation.

**The erotic renunciation as the supreme generous act.** Stories 3 (Nathan/Mithridanes), 4 (Messer Gentile), 5 (Ansaldo/Dianora), 6 (King Charles), 7 (King Pedro), and 8 (Titus/Gisippus) all involve relinquishing a beloved person or a sexual claim. Boccaccio repeatedly stages the moment when desire meets honour and honour wins — not by suppression but by a freely chosen gift. This is magnanimity as erotic mastery of the self, a harder and more praiseworthy act than mere material largesse.

**Friendship as the highest social bond.** Story 8 (Titus and Gisippus) is the Day's longest and most discursive tale and contains a formal eulogy of friendship: "A most sacred thing, then, is friendship… the most discreet mother of magnanimity and honour." Gisippus gives Titus his betrothed bride; Titus later risks death to save Gisippus. The story's coda laments that such friendship is now exiled by "wretched cupidity."

**Patience as magnificence — and its discontents.** Griselda (Story 10) recasts the Day's theme: if magnificence is giving more than reason requires, patient endurance is its passive correlate. But Dioneo's frame — calling Gualtieri's conduct a "monstrous folly" and explicitly advising against imitation — unsettles any straightforward reading. Griselda is saintly; Gualtieri is, by the story's own admission, a tyrant testing an impossible ideal. Whether her patience is virtue or something more disturbing is left deliberately open.

**Fortune, merit, and legibility.** Story 1 (King Alfonso / Messer Ruggieri) turns on the problem of unrecognized desert: fortune rather than the king has withheld the reward. This is the Day's quieter opening theme — the gap between a person's worth and what external circumstances allow others to see. The king's proof (the coffer of earth versus the coffer of jewels, chosen by bad luck) exonerates him while acknowledging that social legibility of merit depends on contingencies beyond any patron's control.

## Notable stories

**Story 1 — Ruggieri and the King of Spain.** A Florentine knight serves Alfonso magnificently but receives nothing, concluding it is the king's fault. The king proves it is Ruggieri's bad fortune by the coffer-test; Ruggieri draws the earth-filled chest, and the king gives him the jewel-chest anyway in defiance of fate. A compact illustration of the gap between merit and social recognition, and of princely magnificence that corrects Fortune rather than imitating her.

**Story 3 — Nathan and Mithridanes.** The richest fable of the Day. Mithridanes, unable to surpass Nathan's generosity, decides to kill him, but Nathan himself guides his would-be assassin to the place of ambush — offering his life as the supreme generous gift. When Mithridanes realizes the old man is Nathan, shame transforms into friendship. The story's logic is that generosity, taken to its limit, obliterates the distinction between gift and sacrifice, between self and other.

**Story 4 — Messer Gentile and Madam Catalina.** Gentile opens a tomb to steal a final kiss from the woman he loved in life and finds her still alive — and pregnant. He nurses her back to health, delivers the child, then stages an elaborate banquet to restore her and the boy to her husband Niccoluccio. The story's moral question — does the husband have any claim on what he abandoned? — is answered by the company's earlier discussion of a servant cast into the street; Gentile inverts the logic to argue the gift is the more magnificent for being freely chosen rather than legally compelled.

**Story 8 — Titus and Gisippus.** The Day's most sustained narrative, tracing a Greek-Roman friendship through the donation of a bride, family scandal, Roman exile, destitution, false confession to murder, and final mutual rescue. Boccaccio uses it to deliver a formal apology for friendship as the ground of all virtue. The story's length and its rhetorical set-piece — Titus's address to the Athenian families — reflect the humanist ambition of the Decameron's conclusion.

**Story 9 — Messer Torello and Saladin.** A Christian nobleman unwittingly entertains the Sultan of Babylon in disguise; when Torello is later captured in the Crusades, Saladin recognizes and honors him. A magic bed transports Torello home in time to prevent his wife's remarriage. The story pairs Christian and Muslim magnificence across religious boundaries and is the collection's most openly fantastical tale. Saladin's parting gifts — crown, ring, jewels — are repayments of hospitality, not ransoms; magnificence, Boccaccio implies, belongs to no single faith.

**Story 10 — Griselda.** A marquess takes a peasant girl to wife, then strips away children, honor, and clothing in a serial test of her obedience, finally restoring everything. Boccaccio (through Dioneo) explicitly frames Gualtieri's behavior as "monstrous folly" even as the story celebrates Griselda's indestructible patience. It became one of the most famous stories in European literature — Petrarch translated it into Latin, Chaucer adapted it as the Clerk's Tale — precisely because of its irreducible moral difficulty. Is patient endurance of injustice a virtue or a symptom?

## Quotables

"Magnificence … is the light and lustre of every other virtue." — Neifile, opening Story 1, staking the Day's claim.

"To give an hundred years is no great boon; how much less, then, is it to give the six or eight I have yet to abide here? Take it, then, an it like thee." — Nathan to Mithridanes, offering his own life (Story 3).

"A most sacred thing, then, is friendship and worthy not only of especial reverence, but to be commended with perpetual praise, as the most discreet mother of magnanimity and honour, the sister of gratitude and charity and the enemy of hatred and avarice." — Narrator's coda to Story 8.

"My lord, I ever knew my mean estate to be nowise sortable with your nobility… Here is your ring wherewith you espoused me; take it." — Griselda's reply when dismissed in her shift (Story 10).

"What more can here be said save that even in poor cottages there rain down divine spirits from heaven, like as in princely palaces there be those who were worthier to tend swine than to have lordship over men?" — Narrator's final word on Griselda.

## WM resonances

**[[legibility-as-machine-core]].** The coffer-test in Story 1 is a small parable about the problem of social legibility: Ruggieri's merit is real but invisible to institutional reward. The king must invent a test precisely because ordinary legibility (court assignments, gift-giving records) cannot surface it. This is not the Modernity Machine's cadastral legibility but its precursor anxiety — can worth be made readable to power?

**[[The Thicket]].** The brigata as a form is most explicit in the Tenth Day's conclusion: Pamfilo dissolves it because "our company, already known to several others of the neighbourhood, may multiply after a fashion that will deprive us of our every commodity." The refuge is defined by controlled access; once it becomes legible to the outside, its interior space collapses. The brigata is a thicket with a deliberate closure date.

**[[Conatus]].** Griselda's patience under Gualtieri's stripping-away is not passivity but a specific form of conatus: the drive to persist as herself despite systematic erosion of her social markers (rank, children, clothing, house). She maintains interior continuity when every external attribute is removed. Dioneo's discomfort with the story may register exactly this: conatus under suppression looks indistinguishable from submission, and that ambiguity is the story's real subject.
