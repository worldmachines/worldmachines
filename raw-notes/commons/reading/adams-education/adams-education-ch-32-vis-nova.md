# Chapter XXXII: Vis Nova (1903-1904)

Across a single year spanning the concealed run-up to the Russo-Japanese War, a proxy supper at Roosevelt's White House, John Hay's last great diplomatic success, the St. Louis World's Fair, and a summer automobile tour of Norman and French Gothic cathedrals, Adams keeps applying his private "scales of force" to whatever spectacle is in front of him — until the assassination of Plehve, glimpsed on a street placard in Troyes, jolts him toward resolving to build his own formula of history rather than borrow anyone else's.

## Themes

**Force measured against inertia** organizes the chapter's opening movement. Europe's "conspiracy of silence" around the coming war with Japan, Cassini's transparent bluffs, and the general opacity of "a government by inertia" are set against Roosevelt's blunt table talk and Japan's aggression, which proceeds "as mathematically — as a demonstration of Euclid." Adams explicitly treats each day of the unfolding war as "a measure of relative energy on the historical scale," and calls the whole affair "a Grammar of new Science quite as instructive as that of Pearson" — the vocabulary of force-measurement that will crystallize, by the chapter's end, into the book's coming Dynamic Theory.

**The dynamo-born "new American"** emerges from the St. Louis Exposition, which Adams values not for its exhibits (he finds the economics a "complete" failure and prefers the fair emptied of visitors) but as evidence of a new social organism: a population of mixed European migrants "squeezed and welded" by steam power into a population with "no distinctive marks but that of its pressure." Adams states the analogy outright — the new American is "the servant of the powerhouse, as the European of the twelfth century was the servant of the Church" — restating his dynamo/Virgin dualism as a claim about the total, era-defining infrastructure of an age, a formulation that resonates strongly with the World Machines idea of a [[world-machines]] as the deep, all-organizing apparatus of a period.

**The Virgin as a still-quantifiable force** drives the second half of the chapter. Adams buys an automobile — "the form of force which Adams most abominated" — expressly to chase the Virgin's monuments across Normandy and France, framing his devotion as a literal physics problem ("a formula as precise as s = gt^2/2, if he could but experimentally prove it"). Touring the stained glass from Chartres (c. 1150) to Beauvais (c. 1570-80), he reads the shift from the unified Tree of Jesse to a glass thick with secular royal portraiture and ornamental incident as a kind of decline he can almost formalize — "Complexity, Multiplicity, even a step towards Anarchy, it might suggest, but what step towards perfection?" — a movement that echoes the World Machines [[decay-sequence]] of infrastructure sliding from functional to ornamental.

**Plehve's assassination as rupture.** In Troyes, mid-reverie among the glass and the memory of Thibaut of Champagne, Adams reads a street bulletin announcing the murder of the Russian interior minister. The collision of medieval sanctity and contemporary violence — "Martyrs, murderers, Caesars, saints and assassins" — gives him vertigo and forces the unanswerable question of whether "assassination forever [is] to be the last word of Progress." The stupendous failure of the Virgin's nineteen hundred years of unity-building, set against a world "bloodier than when she was born," is what finally drives Adams, back in his garret that autumn, to stop measuring other people's formulas and build his own — his own attempt at what World Machines would call a [[history-machine]], the Dynamic Theory of History that the book now turns to construct.

## Notable passages

The Russian-inertia paragraphs (c1) establish the chapter's evidentiary method: diplomatic silence as itself a measurable quantity. Roosevelt's table talk (c2), including his unguarded "I could do it myself; I could stop it to-morrow!", supplies the contrasting case of visible, undisguised force. The St. Louis passage (c3) generalizes from a single fair to a civilizational claim about steam as the new organizing power, explicitly parallel to the Church's medieval role. The automobile/Virgin passage (c4) states Adams's governing metaphor — devotion to the Virgin as literally a force to be measured, like gravitation. The Plehve vertigo passage (c5) is the chapter's turning point, where accumulated force-measurement collapses into moral horror. The closing paragraph (c6) is the chapter's stated payoff: the decision, at last, "to shape after his own needs the values of a Dynamic Theory of History."
