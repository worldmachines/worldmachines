# Darwinism, Deep Time, and the Failure of Uniformity

In London between 1867 and 1868, with his diplomatic career offering no further outlet, Henry Adams takes up Darwinism as his next intellectual amusement, befriends the geologist Sir Charles Lyell, and even ghostwrites American notices of Lyell's *Principles of Geology*. Testing Natural Selection against real fossils on Shropshire's Wenlock Edge — tracing his own ancestry back through Pteraspis and Limulus to the Cambrian — he finds only proof of change, never proof of selection or of uniform, ascending evolution. The chapter ends with a comic detour into currency theory for the *North American Review*, a return to Rome and London, and Adams's ironic verdict that seven years of intense, fashionable self-education left him exactly where he started.

## Themes

- **Darwinism as social contagion, not conviction.** Adams becomes "a Darwinist before the letter" not because he has weighed the evidence — "he was hardly trained to follow Darwin's evidences" — but because refusal costs more effort than assent: "Henry Adams was Darwinist because it was easier than not." The chapter groups Natural Selection with the atomic theory, the conservation of energy, and the kinetic theory of gases as one item in a cluster of "vast theories on such narrow foundations" absorbed on trust — a paradigm assembling itself across disconnected technical fields before suddenly "convulsing society," in a manner the [[history-machine]] framework would recognize.

- **Geology tested against Natural Selection — and found wanting.** Ghostwriting for Lyell gives Adams a private laboratory for Darwinism. He presses Lyell on the glacial epoch, on species like *Terebratula* that appear "identical from the beginning to the end of geological time," and on the abrupt, unheralded appearance of the first vertebrate, *Pteraspis*, in the Ludlow shale. Each case undercuts the doctrine it was meant to support, and Adams concludes that Lyell's uniformitarian geology rests on "pure inference, precisely like the inference of Paley, that, if one found a watch, one inferred a maker" — leaving him able to prove only "Evolution that did not evolve; Uniformity that was not uniform; and Selection that did not select."

- **Wenlock Edge collapses deep time into kinship.** Lying in the grass above the Abbey ruins, Adams feels "time-sequences became interchangeable" and claims Pteraspis — "a cousin of the sturgeon" — as his own "earliest ancestor and nearest relative." The passage is less geology than autobiography: an American in search of ancestry finds it not in family or nation but in Silurian rock, where "one's instinct abhors time."

- **Unity as psychological necessity dressed as scientific law.** Adams's Darwinism ultimately serves a need larger than science: a "safe, conservative, practical, thoroughly Common-Law deity" to replace the Church, and a justification for continued political consolidation after the Civil War. He is explicit that for his generation "Law should be Evolution from lower to higher, aggregation of the atom in the mass, concentration of multiplicity in unity, compulsion of anarchy in order" — a telos worth paying for again in blood and treasure. The passage reads as a personal creed for what World Machines calls [[legibility-as-machine-core]], the [[modernity-machine]]'s pull toward a single ordering attractor.

- **A Darwinian "for fun": irony as method.** Adams's real discovery on Wenlock Edge is psychological, not paleontological: he is "the first in an infinite series" of his ancestors "to discover and admit to himself that he really did not care whether truth was, or was not, true." Belief becomes aesthetic and provisional — "he should surely drop off from Darwinism like a monkey from a perch" — anticipating the detached, multiplicity-seeking historian of the book's later chapters.

## Notable passages

- The opening embrace of Darwin (c1) sets the chapter's ironic key: enthusiasm outrunning competence, in a London drawing-room where "Darwin was convulsing society."
- The Wenlock Edge meditation (c2) is the chapter's imaginative center, where deep time becomes personal genealogy.
- The Paley's-watch indictment (c3) is Adams's most explicit statement of geology's evidentiary failure, delivered even as he keeps helping Lyell "heap up the evidences of evolution."
- The "Darwinian for fun" passage (c4) names the book's recurring stance toward inherited systems of belief: adopted for amusement, discarded without loss.
- The Unity-as-Law passage (c5) is the chapter's most consequential sentence, converting a scientific fad into a program willing to spend "five thousand millions more in money, and a million more lives."
- The closing return to London (c6) delivers the chapter's punchline: for all the science and society absorbed, Adams judges himself no better educated in 1868 than when he landed in 1858.
