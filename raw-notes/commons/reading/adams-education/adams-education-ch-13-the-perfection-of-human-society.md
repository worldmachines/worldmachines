# The Perfection of Human Society

Covering roughly 1860 to 1865, the chapter turns from Adams's official duties as private secretary to his parallel, unofficial "education" in English society, which he judges a near-total failure: London's social world never resolves into anything he can name, map, or use, and its most celebrated spectacles (Mme. de Castiglione mobbed at Devonshire House, Garibaldi worshipped at Stafford House) teach him nothing he can put into a system. The chapter's title comes from Lothrop Motley's offhand remark that the London dinner and the English country house were "the perfection of human society," a claim Adams spends much of the chapter turning over and mostly disproving, before ending on the one real social gain of the period: a chance introduction, over breakfast at Sir Henry Holland's, to Charles Milnes Gaskell, which draws him into a durable Yorkshire friendship with the Milnes Gaskell family.

## Themes

**Social "education" that never converges.** Adams frames his London years as a second, parallel curriculum to his diplomatic one, and judges it just as much a failure: he "professed himself ignorant of society; he never knew whether he had been in it or not," and after eight or ten seasons claims to know less about how to enter society than at his first appearance in 1861. The chapter's self-mocking irony (its title is a quotation Adams spends the whole chapter dismantling) is of a piece with his recurring device of narrating his own miseducation.

**London society as a set of mutually illegible circles.** Rather than one legible social order, Adams finds "a score of societies which seemed quite independent of each other" — legal, ecclesiastical, military, naval, sporting, artistic, county — each as strange to the others as to him, with no synoptic view available to any single observer. His own formulation, that society had "no unity" and one wandered in it "like a maggot in cheese," describes a social field that resists exactly the kind of ordering, mapping, and standardization a [[modernity-machine]] observer would expect to find; it reads as a small-scale instance of proliferation outrunning any single legible frame, closer to [[divergence-machine]] terrain than to any capturable system.

**Spectacle without interpretation.** The two social "events" Adams claims actually meant something to him — the mobbing of Mme. de Castiglione and the duchesses' worship of Garibaldi — are presented as scandals that exceed any framework he has for reading them: "their curves were too complex for mere arithmetic." Both scenes stage society performing an intensity of feeling (toward beauty, toward revolutionary charisma) that outstrips its own ordinary decorum, and Adams explicitly declines to supply the meaning, leaving it to poets rather than sociologists.

**Interrogating Motley's paradox.** Adams treats "the perfection of human society" almost as a thesis to be tested against evidence: not the food (bad, and everyone complains of it), not the look of the table (worse), not the manners (worse still), until he narrows it down to a small, semi-literary dinner circle — Grote, Jowett, Milman, Froude, Arnold, Swinburne, Gladstone — perfect only "in the eyes of a Harvard College historian," and useless training for anywhere but London itself.

**A rehearsed, aging performance of belonging.** Adams's account of the "ideal" social pose — stranger on the hearth-rug, "an air of expectant benevolence, without curiosity" — describes a costume that reads as insolent patronage in youth and only becomes charming around sixty, a timeline of decades he cannot afford since "he had his living to earn." It is a wry anatomy of social performance calibrated entirely to elapsed time rather than skill.

**Chance intimacy versus wasted duty.** The chapter closes by distinguishing the "life wasted" of official social duty — escorting Americans to drawing-rooms, bowing to titles at Court — from the few "personal intimacies" that actually mattered, arrived at by an unplanned chain of chance (a breakfast invitation, a fellow guest at the door, a cousin's cousin at Cambridge) that Adams calls predestined: "Intimates are predestined." The passage's insistence that a meaningful bond emerges from accident rather than design, and could not have been planned toward, resonates with [[Configurancy]]'s picture of non-teleological co-emergence between agents.

## Notable passages

The opening paragraph fixes Adams's odd social rank after his father's diplomatic triumph — an "American Peer of the Realm" with no actual diplomatic function left to perform, which sets up the chapter's premise that what follows is purely social, not professional, education.

The "score of societies" passage is the chapter's most systematic sociological observation: London's social world is not one hierarchy to be climbed but many disconnected worlds running in parallel, a structure Adams captures in the memorable image of wandering "like a maggot in cheese."

The Devonshire House and Stafford House vignettes are the two moments Adams singles out as meaningful in seven years of attendance, and both are staged as excesses of feeling that overwhelm any explanatory frame he has available.

The Motley paragraph states the chapter's organizing paradox directly and sets up the extended, paragraph-by-paragraph disproof that follows — food, then looks, then manners, before landing on a genuine but narrow answer.

The hearth-rug passage is Adams's most concentrated self-portrait of studied social performance, complete with its own decades-long timeline for the pose to mature from awkwardness into charm.

The closing passage on Milnes Gaskell reframes the entire preceding catalogue of official, empty social duty against the one chance encounter that actually shaped the rest of Adams's life, ending on the aphorism that intimacy is not built but found.
