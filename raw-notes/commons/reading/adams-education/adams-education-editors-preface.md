# Editor's Preface: The Making and Withholding of The Education of Henry Adams

Henry Cabot Lodge's preface to the 1918 Massachusetts Historical Society edition tells the publication history of The Education of Henry Adams: how it was conceived as the deliberate companion piece to Mont-Saint-Michel and Chartres, why Adams himself refused to publish it during his lifetime, and why the Society now brings it into print after his death.

## Themes

**A paired experiment in measuring historical "force."** Lodge quotes Adams's own explanation, appended to Chapter XXIX, of the two-book scheme: "man as a force must be measured by motion from a fixed point," and Adams chose the century 1150-1250 -- embodied in Amiens Cathedral and the works of Thomas Aquinas -- as that fixed unit, letting him "project his lines forward and backward indefinitely." Chartres, written first, was pitched as "a Study of Thirteenth-Century Unity"; the Education was its deliberate counterweight, "a Study of Twentieth-Century Multiplicity." The pairing itself is Adams's method: two dated reference points bracketing a measurable span of civilizational change, an approach with some kinship to how [[history-machine]] paradigms are read off distributed, dated evidence rather than declared by fiat -- though Lodge's preface stays at the level of publication history, not theory, and the resonance should not be overstated.

**A confessed reversal of Augustine's method.** Adams told friends, half in jest, that his ambition was to complete St. Augustine's Confessions, but where Augustine "had worked from multiplicity to unity," Adams, "like a small one, had to reverse the method and work back from unity to multiplicity." Lodge presents this as both a joke and a real diagnosis of why the book's "literary form" defeated its author -- the scheme "became unmanageable as he approached his end."

**Adams's own theory of history blocked his own publication of it.** The book's final chapters (per Lodge, "the last three or four chapters," per the text elsewhere Chapters XXXIII and XXXIV) carry Adams's "favorite theory of history," which he tried again to clarify in the 1910 pamphlet A Letter to American Teachers before illness in 1912 ended his writing. Lodge notes the irony directly: Adams's own theory held "the teacher was at best helpless, and, in the immediate future, silence next to good-temper was the mark of sense" -- so Adams practiced what his theory preached, choosing not to publish and hoping the book would "quietly fade from memory."

**Posthumous publication as custodianship, not authorial intent.** Because the privately printed 1906/1907 edition (100 copies) had already circulated and been "freely quoted by every book whose author requested it," Adams could neither withdraw nor finish it to his satisfaction. Lodge frames the Massachusetts Historical Society's 1918 edition explicitly as serving readers' access rather than overriding the author: it publishes the text "not in opposition to the author's judgment, but only to put both volumes equally within reach of students."

## Notable passages

The Chapter XXIX excerpt on measuring "man as a force" is the intellectual core of the preface: it is Adams's own account of why the two books exist and what unit of historical "motion" he chose to measure them by.

The Augustine-reversal passage is the preface's most memorable and self-deprecating image, explaining in a single sentence why the Education's structure resisted Adams's own attempts to finish it.

The passage on Adams's theory of history turning back on his own silence ("the teacher was at best helpless... silence next to good-temper was the mark of sense") is the preface's emotional and thematic hinge, dated pointedly to "After midsummer, 1914" -- the eve of the First World War -- as the moment Adams's withholding became final.

The closing passage on the Massachusetts Historical Society's rationale explains, in the editor's own voice, why this posthumous edition exists at all: access for students, not a verdict on the author's unresolved intentions.
