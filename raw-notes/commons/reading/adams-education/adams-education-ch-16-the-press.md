# The Press (1868): Return to a Machine-Made America

Landing at a North River pier in July 1868 with the Motley family, Henry Adams finds a native country stranger to him than a Phoenician trader beaching a galley in 1000 B.C.: a decade of civil war and industrial buildout has reorganized American life around "the great mechanical energies--coal, iron, steam," leaving his European-tuned diplomatic and literary education useless. Unfit, by his own account, for the railroad-building his brother Charles takes up, and finding no opening in Boston's press or Newport's society, Adams settles on journalism as his one remaining path and follows Attorney General William Evarts to a still-rural Washington in October 1868. There a village-sized political society hands him, as raw material for the *North American Review*, the *Nation*, and newspaper correspondence, close-range studies of President Andrew Johnson, Secretary of State Seward, Treasury Secretary Hugh McCulloch, Chief Justice Salmon Chase, and Senator Charles Sumner.

## Themes

- **Return as obsolescence.** Adams casts his homecoming as a man arriving after his own world has already ended. The chapter's controlling image is an earthworm "twisted about, in vain, to recover his starting-point," unable even to see its own trail, now that coal, iron, and steam have given "a distinct superiority in power over the old industrial elements--agriculture, handwork, and learning." This is close to what World Machines calls a [[gramsci-gap]]: the old legitimacy (Puritan lineage, classical education, diplomatic service) has visibly died, and Adams has no place yet in whatever is being built to replace it. It is in staging this obsolescence that Adams draws an invidious contrast with recently arrived Eastern European Jewish immigrants, describing them in terms that are openly antisemitic and judging them better suited than his own storied ancestry to the new economic order; the attitude is Adams's own, reported here rather than endorsed, and the passage is excluded from quotation in this note because of its content.

- **The press as the only market left.** Boston offers "no market for educated labor," Newport offers gaiety without education, and Adams's wide acquaintance yields no help; he describes the young men of his class as being "for sale, in the open market" at the price of mechanics, with no one troubling to ask their fitness. Washington journalism becomes the fallback almost by elimination, and the chapter maps the New York press as a closed field: Henry Raymond dead, Greeley's *Tribune* held by Whitelaw Reid, Dana's *Sun* built on a "blackguardism" Adams cannot supply, Bennett's *Herald* a one-man "despotic empire," leaving only Bryant's *Evening Post* and Godkin's *Nation* — the same narrow readership he already had through the *North American Review*.

- **Railway capital as the era's infrastructure.** Adams is explicit that the postwar generation is "already mortgaged to the railways": having spent its force settling the question of the Union by "sheer force," the country turns to building "capital, banks, mines, furnaces, shops, power-houses, technical knowledge, mechanical population, together with a steady remodelling of social and political habits, ideas, and institutions to fit the new scale." Read against World Machines vocabulary, this is a first-person eyewitness account of a construction burst inside the [[modernity-machine]] — the interlocking technologies and institutions of an era being laid down within a single generation, with everyone alive at the time "mortgaged" to a project none of them individually chose.

- **Washington as a miniature laboratory of power.** Because 1868 Washington is still a village of a few hundred yards around La Fayette Square, Adams can study its principal actors almost as specimens. Andrew Johnson's "moral certainty" as a Southern statesman impresses Adams more than the man's judgment; Seward, waiting out his "discharge," offers only the dry courtesy of a nightly game of whist ("A very sensible entertainment!"); McCulloch is prized as the rare official willing to treat young men as equals, against Adams's aphorism that "every friend in power is a friend lost"; and Sumner is dissected as a mind with "the calm of water which receives and reflects images without absorbing them" — useful to a newspaperman precisely because, like the Silurian fossils of the previous chapter, he has become part of "the accumulated capital of a Silurian age," a survival mined for copy rather than consulted for judgment. Adams also notes in passing that "the world, after 1865, became a bankers' world," foreshadowing the financiers who dominate his later chapters.

## Notable passages

- The arrival at the North River pier (c1) sets the chapter's frame: a returning American finds his own country as strange as an ancient trader would.
- The earthworm-and-caravan passage (c2) states the chapter's central diagnosis — a society blindly groping toward its own head while coal, iron, and steam eclipse the old order — immediately before the excluded antisemitic aside.
- The catalogue of New York editors and papers (c3) is the concrete map of "the press" Adams surveys and is shut out of, driving him to Washington correspondence instead.
- The Andrew Johnson passage (c4) crystallizes Adams's theory of Southern political charisma as a self-certainty that "actually made him" a President, independent of judgment or ability.
- The McCulloch passage (c5) delivers the chapter's aphorism on power and flattery, framing McCulloch as the rare official who is the exception to it.
- The Sumner passage (c6) is Adams's most extended character study in the chapter, picturing a mind that reflects the world without ever absorbing it.
