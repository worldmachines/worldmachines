# Failure (1871): The Harvard Professorship, the North American Review, and Clarence King

Chapter XX closes the opening movement of *The Education*. Adams narrates his seven years (1870-1877) as Harvard's Assistant Professor of History under President Eliot's reform agenda, running in parallel with his editorship of the North American Review, and pronounces both careers failures by his own reckoning — before the chapter pivots, in its final third, to his 1871 summer trip West with the Fortieth Parallel Survey, where a chance overnight at a cabin in Estes Park introduces him to Clarence King, the friendship that will recur through the rest of the book. A twenty-year narrative gap follows this unit.

## Themes

**Teaching as a moral impossibility, not a technical one.** Adams frames the crisis of his professorship as philosophical before it is practical: a teacher "affects eternity" and cannot know where influence stops, yet any coherent account of history forces the teacher into unwanted metaphysics — treating history "as a catalogue, a record, a romance, or as an evolution," each choice quietly making of one's students "either priests or atheists, plutocrats or socialists, judges or anarchists." He locates the discipline's own failure here too, noting history had "nowhere broken down so pitiably" as in the Middle Ages he was assigned to fill between Gurney's classical courses and Torrey's modern ones.

**The "tenth mind" and the doomed elitism of method.** Barred from philosophy by his own uncertainty and bored by mere facts, Adams settles on cultivating only the ablest student in ten — "nine minds in ten take polish passively, like a hard surface; only the tenth sensibly reacts" — explicitly at the expense of the rest. He calls the resulting technique "historical method" half-mockingly, and diagnoses its failure structurally: the method, once learned, has "little use," since the Middle Ages offer no true beginning and no textbooks, only a "tangled skein" to be taken up anywhere.

**Institutional kindness as the counterweight to self-assessed failure.** Adams insists on his own failure — "He had tried a great many experiments, and wholly succeeded in none" — even as Harvard, through President Eliot and John Fiske, treats him as a success. He reads this discrepancy as characteristic of the college's persistent, almost willful impartiality, and credits Harvard, despite his having "persistently criticised, abused, abandoned, and neglected" it, as the only institution that "redeemed America, since it was true to its own."

**Editing as an even thinner education than teaching.** The North American Review sharpens Adams's self-diagnosis: an editor "could help others, but could do nothing for himself," and success is measured not in literary merit but in "ten pages of advertising." He treats the seven years as lost on both fronts, preferring in the end his "attic in Washington" and the fifty dollars a month of frank ignorance.

**Clarence King as the counter-image of Adams's own broken life.** The chapter's final movement reverses its tone entirely. Where Adams's life is "a succession of violent breaks or waves, with no base at all," King has "moulded and directed his life logically, scientifically" — organizing the Fortieth Parallel Survey as a civil rather than military measure, "paralleling the Continental Railway in Geology." Adams casts King as the fulfillment of the American type he himself failed to become, meeting him not through design but by getting lost on muleback at nightfall in Estes Park.

**A deliberate close to the first movement.** The chapter's last lines seal off the young-Adams narrative: "No more education was possible for either man," and Adams returns to Cambridge "harnessed to his cart," submissive to the systems he has just spent the chapter indicting.

## Notable passages

The "teacher affects eternity" passage is the chapter's most quotable statement of the moral stakes Adams assigns to pedagogy, escalating from parent to murderer to teacher to argue that historical instruction cannot avoid making ethical claims it cannot honestly defend.

The "tenth mind" passage is the clearest statement of Adams's actual, if failed, pedagogical experiment — an elitist method he later concedes led "nowhere."

The self-assessed-failure passage, opening "The uses of adversity are beyond measure strange," is Adams's own verdict on the professorship, dated precisely to his 1877 departure and qualified by his gratitude for Harvard's courtesy even in defeat.

The Estes Park meeting and the description of King as an "avatar" and the fulfilled American type are curated together because they form a single narrative beat: the chance encounter, then Adams's retrospective elevation of King into the friendship's larger meaning, converging "the sunshine of the Sierras" with "the fogs of London."

The closing lines — "No more education was possible for either man" — mark the deliberate hinge the unit metadata flags: the book's first movement ends here, before a twenty-year gap in the narrative.
