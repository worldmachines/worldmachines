# Free Fight (1869-70)

Adams opens on the sensual pleasures of a Washington spring, then narrates his and his brother Charles's plunge into investigating Jay Gould's September 1869 attempt to corner gold and the Erie Railway machine behind it. From there the chapter widens into a portrait of Grant's administration disintegrating into a set of paired personal wars — Boutwell locking himself alone in a silent Treasury, Fish and Sumner splitting over annexation schemes in the Caribbean and Canada, and Attorney General Hoar colliding with Chief Justice Chase over the Legal Tender Act — that leave Adams convinced the political system inherited from 1789 has broken down and can only be patched, never repaired.

## Themes

**Washington as sensual counterweight to Boston.** The chapter opens by contrasting the "solitary animal" chill of New England with Washington's "brooding heat of the profligate vegetation," a landscape Adams loves "too much, as though it were Greek and half human." This unclouded pleasure functions as the one grace note against which the chapter's institutional collapse reads as loss rather than mere farce.

**The Gold Conspiracy as an impenetrable mystery.** Adams and Charles descend on the gold corner as journalists — Charles on the Erie Railway history, Henry on the "so-called Gold Conspiracy" itself — but the deeper Henry digs into Congressional testimony, the more the trail "faded and died out at the point where any member of the Administration became visible." The episode teaches him less about Gould than about the limits of government-furnished evidence, and about his own fear of finding out too much.

**Free fight: the Cabinet as a set of paired duels.** The chapter's title names its real subject: not one scandal but an administration dissolving into private wars. Boutwell empties the Treasury of anyone who could interfere with his repose and lets it die of neglect; Fish and Sumner split over St. Thomas, Samana Bay, and a scheme to trade Alabama Claims concessions for Canada; Hoar and Chase collide over the Legal Tender Act after Adams, trying only to defend the Court, is dragged by Frank Walker's polemic into open opposition. Adams sums up the wreckage bluntly: his four most powerful friends had "matched themselves, two and two, and were fighting in pairs to a finish."

**The political machine has broken down.** The chapter's most consequential claim is structural rather than personal. Adams declares that "the system of 1789 had broken down, and with it the eighteenth-century fabric of a priori, or moral, principles," leaving politics reduced to endless expedients to "patch" or "tinker" "the political machine as often as it broke down." He explicitly frames this as a century-scale diagnosis — "the political dilemma was as clear in 1870 as it was likely to be in 1970" — and adds that "the moral law had expired--like the Constitution." This reads as a direct ancestor of the [[decay-sequence]] and [[gramsci-gap]]: an old legitimating order dead in substance while its forms are kept limping along by improvisation, with no replacement yet able to take its place.

**From reformer to pirate.** The chapter closes on Adams's own transformation. Having failed to reform anything through the press, he ships his Erie article to the Edinburgh Review specifically to strike the Erie management through their exposure in London, and takes pleasure in the prospect of being "pirated and advertised free of charge" by American papers reprinting it without paying him — a small but telling pivot from earnest civil-service reform toward cynical mastery of publicity mechanics.

## Notable passages

The opening Washington-spring passage sets a tone of unguarded pleasure that makes the chapter's subsequent institutional collapse land as tragedy rather than mere farce. The Gold Conspiracy paragraph shows Adams's method and its limits: "material furnished by a government seldom satisfies critics or historians, for it lies always under suspicion." The "four friends... fighting in pairs" passage is the chapter's structural key, naming Sumner-Fish and Chase-Hoar as the operative duels behind the chapter's title. The "system of 1789 had broken down" passage is the chapter's thesis statement and its clearest resonance with World Machines' vocabulary of decay and interregnum. The closing "pirate" passage shows what this environment does to Adams's own character — the last stage of a reformer's disillusionment.
