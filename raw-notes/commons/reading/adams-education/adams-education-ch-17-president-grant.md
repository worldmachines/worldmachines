# President Grant (1869)

Settled into bachelor quarters in Washington through the winter of 1868-69, Henry Adams drifts among office-seekers and reform journalists while Congress waits for Grant to take charge, and gives himself over to the city's undemanding social life and to two ambitious literary projects — an anonymous article on American finance for the Edinburgh Review and a self-invented annual political "Session" review for the North American Review. Like most of Washington, Adams pins his hopes on Grant as a second Washington, a soldier who will restore order after the chaos of Andrew Johnson's presidency; the Cabinet announcements and, still more, an intimate acquaintance with Grant himself through his housemate Adam Badeau, dismantle that hope and leave Adams diagnosing Grant not as a statesman but as a pre-intellectual anomaly that makes nonsense of evolutionary progress and dooms Adams's own reforming ambitions to drift.

## Themes

**Washington as an idle, ten-years-delayed apprenticeship.** Adams frames his first Washington winter as a release after the constraint of London society — "Adams found himself a young duke in Washington" — where the town's poverty and simplicity make it "easiest society he had ever seen" and where, by his own account, he had "ten years of youth to make up." This is explicitly non-educational: "in the amusements of Washington, education had no part," and the chapter treats the social apprenticeship as background rather than substance, a holding pattern before the real test — Grant — arrives.

**Journalism as an improvised bid for political authority.** Unable to secure any paid or official role, Adams manufactures influence through print: an anonymous Edinburgh Review article for the prestige of standing "on the staff... under the vast shadow of Lord Macaulay," then a stolen format (Lord Robert Cecil's "Session") repurposed for the North American Review, meant to become, "in time, a political authority." The ambition is naive but calculated — "if he can select the five hundred, he reaches the five hundred thousand" — and it is this same appetite for a lever of influence that later curdles into his admission that he was, plainly, "for sale."

**The Grant-as-Washington illusion and its collapse.** "At least four-fifths of the American people" project onto Grant the parallel with the first President — a soldier who must know how to "organize a government" and restore "moral and mechanical order." Adams shares the illusion fully, even planning to help the Executive strip the Senate's confirmation power. The Cabinet announcements puncture it within minutes: Grant's nominations "betrayed his intent as plainly as they betrayed his incompetence," and Adams is left ashamed not of Grant but of himself, for "another total misconception of life."

**Grant as a pre-intellectual force that breaks the theory of evolution.** Through his intimate, garrulous housemate Adam Badeau — Grant's biographer and staff officer — Adams builds a portrait of Grant's mind as "an intermittent energy," torpid for long stretches while his staff talk ideas into him by indirection, until he "announce[s] the idea as his own" and acts on it with total conviction; "they were not sure that he did think." Adams generalizes this into a type shared with Garibaldi — men whose energies are "the greater, the less they wasted on thought," "forces of nature" rather than reasoners. That such a man could be, by consensus, "the highest product of the most advanced evolution" is, for Adams, not a compliment to Grant but an indictment of the theory: "the progress of evolution from President Washington to President Grant, was alone evidence enough to upset Darwin."

**Drift as the final verdict, and Adams left unbought.** Despite personally liking several of Grant's cabinet (Fish, Cox, Hoar), Adams concludes the administration's real character is inertia: "Grant avowed from the start a policy of drift; and a policy of drift attaches only barnacles." "Let us have peace!" — Grant's recurring commonplace — turns out to mean simply being left alone, with no policy for reformers like Adams to attach themselves to. The chapter ends on Adams's own commodification of himself — "He was for sale. He wanted to be bought" — and the flat verdict that "luck went dead against him," closing off, for a generation, the reforming career he had planned.

This dynamic — an old, personally-embodied tradition of statesman-virtue (Adams's own eighteenth-century inheritance, the Washington archetype) finding no successor built to receive it, only an administration organized around instinct and drift rather than any legible program — reads as a small-scale instance of the [[gramsci-gap]]: the old legitimacy (reasoned, educated government) has visibly died in Adams's own estimation, while nothing capable of replacing it, in a way he can recognize, has yet been born. Badeau's account of how Grant's staff "talk their ideas into" him indirectly until he "announce[s] the idea as his own" is also a striking miniature of the [[history-machine]] pattern WMP describes at civilizational scale — a decision assembling silently and distributedly among independent actors until it lurches into visible, attributed form.
