# Preface: The Manikin in Place of Confession

Dated February 16, 1907, this preface to *The Education of Henry Adams* opens with an extended quotation from Rousseau's *Confessions*, then explains why Adams will not write in that confessional mode: lacking any American model for "high education" beyond Benjamin Franklin, he proposes instead to write of "Henry Adams" as an impersonal manikin — a model figure — onto which the "garment" of his education can be draped and inspected for fit, so that young men might learn the economy of force rather than witness a moral confession.

## Themes

**Confession versus the manikin.** Adams opens by quoting Rousseau's vehement, God-addressed self-exposure at length, then pointedly declines to follow it: nineteenth-century educators, he notes, "have declined to show themselves before their scholars as objects more vile or contemptible than necessary." In place of Rousseau's Ego, Adams substitutes a manikin — "a monument of warning against the Ego" that has "steadily tended to efface itself, and, for purposes of model, to become a manikin on which the toilet of education is to be draped."

**Absence of an American model.** Adams laments that the era offers "few recent guides to avoid, or to follow" and that American literature has "scarcely one working model for high education," forcing the student back past Rousseau to Franklin for any model of self-teaching — the gap this volume claims to fill.

**Education as economy of force.** The young man is recast not as a moral subject but as a physical one: "a certain form of energy," whose education is training in "economy of his force," partly "clearing away of obstacles," partly "direct application of effort" — tools and models to be discarded once mastered. This is a mechanical, instrumental theory of pedagogy set explicitly against Rousseau's confessional self-cultivation.

**The manikin as a half-believed fiction.** Adams defends the manikin as a necessary abstraction — "the only measure of motion, of proportion, of human condition" — that nonetheless "must have the air of reality; must be taken for real; must be treated as though it had life." The closing, arch "Who knows? Possibly it had!" signals the ironic, self-aware narrational stance the book will use throughout in referring to its subject in the third person.

## Notable passages

The Rousseau epigraph sets up exactly what Adams will not do, by contrast. The "no American model" paragraph supplies the stated occasion for the book. The garment/tailor imagery and the closing "must be treated as though it had life" passage are the methodological core: they explain, before a single chapter of narrative begins, the famous device by which the book will speak of its own author in the third person, as "Henry Adams" rather than "I."
