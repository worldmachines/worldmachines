# Foes or Friends: London Diplomacy and the Discovery of Swinburne (1862)

Serving as his father's private secretary at the American Legation during the darkest year of Anglo-American Civil War tension, Henry Adams spends 1862 learning two overlapping lessons: how to keep a composed face in a London society that has turned Lincoln and Seward into demons and treats every Union setback as an occasion for gloating, and, when the diplomatic terror briefly lifts at year's end, how small a private secretary's cultivated taste can feel next to the overwhelming, uncategorizable genius of Algernon Swinburne, met at Monckton Milnes's Yorkshire house party.

## Themes

**Civility conceals hostility more effectively than it reveals loyalty.** Adams's hatred of England is total — he "wanted nothing so much as to wipe England off the earth" — yet it is a private hatred he must never show, since the Legation's survival depends on treating Lord Russell's discourteous, evasive replies as ordinary diplomatic friction rather than what young Adams reads as "intent to kill." The chapter's title names this exactly: London's supposed friends require more careful self-management than its open foes, because "the average mind sees quickest through a bluff" and any crack in candor among friends does more damage than open hostility ever could.

**Grief and rage must be performed away, not merely felt.** After catastrophes like the second Bull Run, Adams describes a private discipline of concealment — reading the *Times* over breakfast without reading aloud, smiling at a Cabinet Minister's gloating remark in the throne-room, weeping only "on one's pillow" — that costs him more than facing enemies ever would. The emotional labor of the private secretary is inseparable from the political stakes: any lapse could become a diplomatic incident.

**Diplomacy is read for motive, never taken at its word.** Adams generalizes from his father's experience that "no professional diplomatists worried about falsehoods" since some measure of lying was assumed of everyone; the real skill was inferring "the motive that lay beyond the expression." Lord Palmerston — "a peau de rhinocere," in Baron Brunnow's phrase — is profiled as the Legation's central puzzle: universally distrusted, willing to sacrifice any foreign minister to momentary popularity, yet impossible to out-manoeuvre directly.

**Even insiders cannot fully reconstruct why history turned out as it did.** The Butler "woman-order" crisis brings the Legation to what the narrator calls its most critical moment, as Palmerston appears set to manufacture a public quarrel in the Commons — and then, inexplicably, backs down, apologizes by silence, and never renews hostility. Minister Adams credits Russell's intervention, but the text insists the true cause was never settled, "though afterwards he felt less sure" — a deliberate refusal to resolve the episode into a tidy causal story, consistent with the chapter's earlier aside that "the story belongs to history, not to education" and its verdict that "great men were the worst blunderers," gesturing toward a [[history-machine]]-style skepticism of any single actor's design as the real engine of events.

**Genius humbles rather than instructs.** The chapter pivots sharply from diplomatic terror to Monckton Milnes's Fryston, where Adams meets the wounded diplomat-adventurer Lawrence Oliphant, the reserved Stirling of Keir, and, above all, the young Algernon Swinburne, whose torrential, encyclopedic talk overturns Adams's Boston-trained standards of taste. Tested against Victor Hugo and Walter Savage Landor and found wanting, Adams accepts the verdict against himself without appeal, casting his own presence next to Swinburne as that of "a worm" who "could no more interest Algernon Swinburne than he could interest Encke's comet" — an education not in facts but in the felt limits of one's own mind.

## Notable passages

The opening paragraph sets the emotional register for the whole unit: Adams's wish to "wipe England off the earth" collides with the enforced calm of legation life, and the Trent Affair and Russell's "discourteous" replies read to a twenty-four-year-old private secretary as evidence of intent to kill rather than ordinary diplomatic coldness.

The passage on facing friends versus enemies states the chapter's thesis directly: after disasters like the second Bull Run, "the effort of facing a hostile society was bad enough, but that of facing friends was worse," because candor must be affected even when one is "exasperated, furious, bitter, and choking with tears" — dramatized by the private secretary's controlled smile at a Cabinet Minister's gloating aside in the throne-room.

The portrait of diplomatic motive-reading explains the interpretive discipline the Legation lives by: since no one expects literal truth from statesmen, the only useful skill is inferring intent, illustrated by Palmerston's reputation as a trap-layer who could "go down to the House of Commons and betray or misrepresent a foreign Minister" for momentary advantage, tossing rival diplomats "on the horns of this jovial, slashing, devil-may-care British bull."

The Butler crisis passage is the diplomatic climax: Minister Adams's curt "Palmerston wants a quarrel!" sets up a confrontation over General Butler's New Orleans order that the Legation narrowly survives, ending — mysteriously, even to the participants — with Palmerston himself backing down and behaving, against all expectation, "like a well-bred gentleman who felt himself in the wrong."

The Fryston passages mark the chapter's turn: "Then, at last, if never before, Adams acquired education" as Swinburne's talk astonishes even Milnes's other seasoned guests, and the encounter culminates in Adams's own verdict on his negligibility before genius — that he "could no more interest Algernon Swinburne than he could interest Encke's comet" and had, in the end, "nothing even to offer."
