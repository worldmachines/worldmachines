# A Dynamic Theory of History (1904)

In this chapter Adams sets aside narrative autobiography and states, in his own voice, the mechanics he thinks underlies all of history: Progress is "the development and economy of Forces," and man is not the agent who captures energy but the particle captured by it — a spider in a web, an animal educated by fire, running water, and grain, whose "body and thought are alike their product" of whatever forces impinge on him. From that axiom Adams runs the whole span of Western history as one continuous physics problem: the primal worship of an unnameable unity later called God; Rome's economizing of the gods into the Cross, which Constantine wields as literal military and political force; the Cross's failure at the sack of Rome and its retreat into St. Augustine's purely spiritual Civitas Dei; the mysterious, simultaneous ninth-to-thirteenth-century arrival of the compass, Greek fire, and gunpowder as forces of a wholly new order; and finally Bacon's reversal of the relation between mind and nature, after which "thought did not evolve nature, but nature evolved thought," carrying Europe through Newton, Franklin, and Watt to a nineteenth-century acceleration that by 1900 produces forces beyond the physicist's comprehension. It is Adams's most explicit attempt to write a general theory of historical causation, and the chapter that most directly proposes history as the study of forces acting on and through human institutions rather than the reverse.

## Themes

- **Man as captured particle, not free agent.** The chapter's founding move inverts the common-sense picture: rather than man capturing the forces of nature, "a dynamic theory... takes for granted that the forces of nature capture man." Susceptibility to force, not mastery of it, is what Adams calls "the highest genius." This is a strikingly literal use of *capture* — man as the captured party — which sits in productive tension with WMP's [[capture-resistance]] framing, where liveness is defined by resisting exactly this kind of absorption into an attracting mass.

- **Progress as economy, not addition, of force.** Adams is emphatic that most of recorded history before 1500 shows no growth in the volume of force available to man, only "economies of energy" — better roads, coinage, law — applied to forces already known since deep antiquity (fire, muscle, wind). Real historical rupture, in his scheme, requires a genuinely new force entering the system, not merely a more efficient use of an old one.

- **Institutions as trusts that overcapitalize a force.** Constantine's Edict of Milan is read not as piety but as speculation: he "merged all uncertain forces into a single trust, which he enormously overcapitalized, and forced on the market," using the Cross as literal ordnance ("In hoc signo vinces!... a train of artillery"). Religion here is capital structure, not belief — an institution built to concentrate and monetize an attractive force, which resonates with how a [[world-machines]] machine consolidates diffuse practices into a single legible apparatus of control.

- **Symbols decay from weapon to ornament.** The Cross's career across the chapter is a legible decay curve: literal battlefield force under Constantine, contested physical protector at Alaric's sack, defended as purely spiritual wealth by Augustine once its mechanical claims fail, and finally an object that "great masses of idle and ignorant tourists" visit at Ravenna or Chartres "with vague notions about the force that created them." That arc — functional weapon to allegorical faith to ornamental tourist relic — tracks WMP's decay sequence almost exactly.

- **The Baconian inversion as the hinge of modern history.** Adams treats Bacon's demand to "try evolving thought from the universe" instead of "evolving the universe from a thought" as the pivot on which "modern history" turns, and pointedly denies agency to the great names that follow: Newton, Franklin, and Watt are not inventors but passive conductors — "certain men merely held out their hands... and great forces of nature stuck to them as though she were playing ball." This is a striking counter-model to great-man historiography, closer to WMP's [[history-machine]]: the paradigm assembles itself from forces already in motion and the named individuals are where it happens to surface.

- **Civilizational rise and exhaustion as one mechanical curve.** Rome's four centuries from Diocletian's abdication back to its Pax Romana peak, and its collapse under "excess... of action and reaction" rather than deficiency, reads as a self-contained Dawn-to-Dusk cycle centuries before the [[world-machines]] framework's own ~1000-year model — a civilization solving its problems so completely that the solution itself, over-extended, tears the system apart.

- **Acceleration as the terminal law.** The chapter closes by naming what the next chapter will formalize: force gaining mass so quickly after 1800 that by 1900 it produces a "new class of supersensual forces" before which even the scientist stands as helpless as a priest of Isis before the Cross — Adams's own attempt at the kind of dated, extrapolable claim WMP calls a forecasting Spine.

## Notable passages

The opening paragraph states the theory's premise as bluntly as Adams ever states anything: "A dynamic theory, like most theories, begins by begging the question: it defines Progress as the development and economy of Forces... Man is a force; so is the sun; so is a mathematical point, though without dimensions or known existence." Everything downstream in the chapter is an application of this single redefinition.

The spider-in-the-web passage supplies Adams's controlling image for how force educates without effort or consent: fire, water, animals, and grain are all described as forces that "formed his thought, induced his action, and even shaped his figure" long before man had any faculty to resist or select among them — education as something done *to* man, not achieved *by* him.

The Roman dynamic-law passage is Adams's clearest statement that decline is not the absence of force but its excess: the empire "should, as a problem of mechanics, have been torn to pieces by acceleration," and its turn to Christianity is read as the forced economizing of a system that had generated "too much energy, and too fast."

The Constantine passage is the chapter's most audacious metaphor: the Edict of Milan recast as a stock prospectus, the Cross as an overcapitalized trust and simultaneously as literal ordnance at the Milvian Bridge — force, capital, and religion collapsed into a single mechanism of institutional capture.

The Bacon passage marks the chapter's hinge, where Adams credits the entire trajectory of modern science to a reversal of method rather than a discovery of fact — the moment thought agrees to be evolved by nature instead of the reverse.

The closing passage on the acceleration after 1800 is the chapter's forward-looking payoff, naming the "supersensual forces" of 1900 as the point at which even trained scientific observers lose their bearings — the same bewilderment, Adams insists, that a priest of Isis felt before the Cross of Christ in the fourth century.
