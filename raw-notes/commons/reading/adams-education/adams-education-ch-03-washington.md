# Washington and the Boy's Political Education, 1850–1854

Chapter III of *The Education of Henry Adams* follows the author's boyhood self from Boston, where lack of a fixed social standard let blackguard violence and antislavery mob politics do the real teaching school never managed, through a formative 1850 journey to Washington and Mount Vernon that fixes George Washington as an unresolvable moral paradox, to the Free Soil coalition's 1851 election of Charles Sumner, which drags the fourteen-year-old boy into corrupt machine politics and leaves him, by his own account, permanently morally compromised — all narrated decades later as evidence that his entire eighteenth-century-flavored upbringing was already obsolete by 1900.

## Themes

**Boston taught by absence of standard, and blackguardism did what school could not.** Adams frames Boston's peculiar freedom — "he escaped the evils of other standards by having no standard at all" — as leaving the boy-mind supple but also exposed to "Blackguard Boston," embodied in the Boston Common snowball wars against Conky Daniels's slum toughs, which the older narrator retroactively reads as training ground for the boys who would later die on Virginia battlefields.

**Slavery is first encountered as visceral, contradictory sensation, not doctrine.** Crossing into Maryland in May 1850, the twelve-year-old is simultaneously repelled ("Slavery struck him in the face; it was a nightmare") and seduced by "the freedom, openness, swagger, of nature and man" — a split response Adams attributes to something in his own "Johnson blood" rather than to any coherent moral reasoning.

**George Washington becomes a fixed point that cannot be reconciled with the slavery that produced him.** The road to Mount Vernon is Adams's proof text: bad roads prove bad morals, slavery is the wickedness behind the road — "and yet, at the end of the road and product of the crime stood Mount Vernon and George Washington." Adams treats the refusal to resolve this contradiction as a survival mechanism ("any attempt to deal with them seriously as education is fatal"), one his father modeled by simply never preaching about it.

**The Free Soil coalition inducts the boy into machine politics and permanent moral confusion.** The 1850–51 bargain that traded the Massachusetts governorship to Democrats for Sumner's Senate seat disgusts the "statesman class" around Adams's father in principle while they take its benefit in practice; the boy, helping elect his father's friend Sumner, concludes he was "already corrupt" and never finds a way out of the resulting "state of moral confusion." Underneath this sits a generational shift Adams senses but cannot yet name — statesman politics giving way to operative-run party machinery ("the subordinates ousted their employers and created a machine which no one but themselves could run"), a felt but unnamed changeover not unlike a [[gramsci-gap]] between a dying mode of legitimacy and one not yet born.

**The chapter's real argument is retrospective: this whole education was already worthless.** Writing as an "American of 1900," Adams closes by judging that the boy of 1854, in everything but mathematics, "stood nearer the year 1 than to the year 1900" — the chapter's antebellum incidents are offered less as nostalgia than as evidence for the book's larger thesis of educational failure.

## Notable passages

The Conky Daniels snowball-fight passage is the chapter's clearest instance of the book's retrospective irony: a boys' game read forward into the Civil War casualties it unknowingly rehearsed. The Fugitive Slave Law passage shows how Boston's own antislavery violence, not abstract instruction, "wrought frenzy" in the boy. The Mount Vernon passage states the book's central unresolved contradiction in miniature. The Sumner-election passage is the boy's induction into "practical politics" and the moral compromise Adams says he never escaped. The closing passage delivers the chapter's real thesis, reframing all of it as evidence of obsolete education.
