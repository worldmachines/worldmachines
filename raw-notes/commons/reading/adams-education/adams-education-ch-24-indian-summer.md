# Indian Summer: Hay's Diplomatic Harvest and the Turn to a Dynamic Theory of History (1898–1899)

At sixty, Henry Adams watches the Spanish-American War deliver, almost by accident, the Anglo-American rapprochement his family had angled for since the Stamp Act, engineered not by Adams doctrine but by Germany's sudden menace and by the diplomatic tact of his friend John Hay — first as Ambassador, then as a reluctant Secretary of State ground down by a paralyzed Senate; meanwhile Adams himself drifts through Sicily, Rome, and Assisi with the Lodges, resumes his twelfth-century studies at the return of John La Farge, recoils in inherited personal outrage at the Boer War, and ends the unit setting out — half earnest, half ironic — to build a mathematical "law in history" modeled on Kepler and Newton.

## Themes

**Vicarious triumph, not personal education.** Adams reads Hay's diplomatic success — England, terrified by the "grizzly terror" of a rising Germany, finally falling into "America's arms" after "two hundred years of stupid and greedy blundering" — as the fulfillment of five generations of Adams-family effort, giving him, for the first time, "a sense of possible purpose working itself out in history." But he is careful to separate the sensation from any teachable method: "This was history, not education." His own decades of failure to find "the working of law in history" while a Harvard professor are explained by a single maxim that will govern the rest of the book's search for a science of history: chaos cannot be taught.

**Office as poison.** Adams's horror at watching Hay accept the State Department is rendered almost medically — office as a toxin that works "not ambition" but "the distortion of sight—the warping of mind—the degradation of tissue—the coarsening of taste—the narrowing of sympathy to the emotions of a caged rat." The friends at Surrenden Dering treat Hay's promotion as a sentence, not an honor, and Mrs. Hay is left engineering daily walks and tea just to keep her husband's spirit from being consumed by the "malarious marsh."

**La Farge's nuance versus Adams's Bostonian reason.** The reunion with John La Farge in Paris supplies the chapter's aesthetic counterweight: where the "American mind" walks "straight up to its object" and argues, La Farge moves "round an object" and "abhorred dispute," treating even contradiction as "only a shade of difference." His standing reproach — "Adams, you reason too much!" — reframes Adams's whole intellectual method as a provincial tic ("He should have blamed Adams for being born in Boston") and sets up the Whistler dinner anecdote as a demonstration: Whistler's talk sought eccentricity for effect, while La Farge's silence was the deeper art.

**Inherited grievance as personal politics.** The Boer War becomes, for Adams, not a matter of policy but of family right: a hundred and fifty years after John Adams "began the task," he finds himself needing to prove, "by appeal to law and fact, that George Washington was not a felon, whatever might be the case with George III" — a vivid instance of the book's recurring move of collapsing personal ancestry into constitutional argument.

**The Senate as bad machinery.** Hay's own words supply the chapter's most concrete political complaint: a "malcontent third" can "dish" any treaty regardless of merit, reducing the Secretary of State's real duties to fending off claims, pressing fraudulent ones, and finding offices for Senators' friends. Adams generalizes the diagnosis coolly — "bad machinery merely added to friction" — treating the Constitution's checks not as principle but as engineering defect.

**The turn to a dynamic theory of history.** The unit closes with Adams's decisive pivot: having "swept his mind clear of knowledge," he resolves to correlate twelfth- and twentieth-century political, social, and scientific values "in some relation of movement that could be expressed in mathematics," explicitly modeling himself on Kepler and Newton and reaching for a "historical formula that should satisfy the conditions of the stellar universe." He finds no teacher — Newcomb too orthodox, Gibbs unreachable, Langley sympathetic but suspicious of philosophy — and is left with Judge Stallo's neglected "Concepts of Modern Science" and his own isolation. This lonely, self-taught, decades-long assembly of a private historical paradigm — visible to no one, credentialed by no institution, arrived at essentially alone — is the book's own version of what World Machines calls a [[history-machine]]: a paradigm built silently and distributedly until it lurches into visibility (here, in the chapters that follow). The ambition itself — a quantitative science of history that does not collapse into either determinism or noise — is also a direct ancestor of the [[world-machines]] project's own psychohistory, "corrected for chaos" in exactly the sense Adams means when he calls chaos untaught and unteachable.

## Notable passages

The opening paragraph sets the chapter's elegiac register — Adams as "the last survivor of the Legation of 1861," savoring a victory decades in the making. The "chaos cannot be taught" passage is the chapter's epistemological hinge, distinguishing lived historical satisfaction from any transmissible method. Hay's Senate speech, quoted at length, is the book's clearest first-hand account of why treaty-making had become unworkable by 1899. The La Farge exchange ("Adams, you reason too much!") crystallizes a recurring dialectic in the book between Bostonian argument and an aesthetic, indirect mode of knowing. The Boer War passage shows Adams's politics as inseparable from inherited family grievance. And the closing Kepler/Newton passage — reaching for "a value, even infinitesimal," for the "attraction" of Congress — is the germ of the dynamic theory of history that dominates the book's final chapters.
