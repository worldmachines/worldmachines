# Accidental Education: Dresden, Rome, and Garibaldi's Palermo (1859–1860)

Having abandoned Berlin, Adams drifts through a second, equally sterile year of nominal Civil Law study at Dresden, then is pulled out of Germany altogether by his newly married sister, Mrs. Kuhn, whose Italian sympathies carry him through the armistice lines of the Franco-Austrian war into Milan and the Stelvio Pass; the chapter's emotional center is the month of May 1860 he then spends in a still-mediaeval, unrestored Rome that flatly refuses to fit any theory of historical progress, followed by an errand as a despatch-bearer to Palermo that puts him, for five minutes, face to face with Garibaldi in the middle of the Sicilian revolution. It closes with a deliberately unserious retreat into Paris and Adams's own verdict on two years abroad: no profession, no society, "no education," only the identity of a tourist.

## Themes

**"Accidental education" hardens into Adams's working theory of how he actually learns.** Nothing planned — not the Civil Law, not German literature, not two winters of German society — leaves a mark; what does land (a glimpse of Italy, a night at the barricade above the Stelvio, Rome itself) arrives sideways, unsought, and is prized precisely because it was not aimed at him.

**Germany is caught mid-collapse, and the war exposes it.** Adams loved a mediaeval, eighteenth-century Germany "the Germans were ashamed of, and were destroying as fast as they could"; Napoleon III's declaration of war on Austria over Italy catches him at exactly the seam between that dying world and the unified, coal-and-railway Germany to come — a passage that reads as a personal-scale [[gramsci-gap]], the interregnum where the old order is already discredited and the new one has not yet arrived, and where (in Adams's own words) men who "stayed too long at the points of contact" get crushed.

**Progressive politics needs a moral alibi for violence, and Adams watches himself construct one.** Working out whether to be "Guelph or Ghibelline" over Napoleon III's war, he concludes that his own liberal principles — freedom, education, economic development — require alliance with "allies as doubtful as Napoleon III, and robberies with violence on a very extensive scale," so that "Education insisted on finding a moral foundation for robbery." He names the result flatly: self-interest dressed as principle is merely medieval faction politics again, "Machiavelli translated into American."

**Mediaeval Rome is offered as living proof against any law of progress.** Because it was still unexcavated and unrestored in 1860 — "No sand-blast of science had yet skinned off the epidermis of history" — Rome cannot be arranged into an "orderly, middle-class, Bostonian, systematic scheme of evolution"; Forum, Vatican, Rienzi, Garibaldi, and Tiberius Gracchus refuse to fall into sequence. This is close to a direct statement of the [[world-machines]] premise that historical infrastructure does not evolve smoothly toward a telos, and it anticipates the decay sequence by reading Rome's ruins as the visible residue of institutions that outlived their function.

**Gibbon's Rome becomes a graveyard of failed "world machines," with a third failure not yet ruled out.** Adams sits on the same steps where Gibbon conceived the *Decline and Fall*, and generalizes the scene: Rome holds "the chief monuments" of the failure of two great experiments of Western civilization, with "nothing proved that the city might not still survive to express the failure of a third" — a civilizational-cycle image that speaks directly to decay sequence and [[history-machine]] thinking about how eras assemble, dominate, and dissolve.

**Garibaldi is read as an instrument of a process bigger than his own intentions, not a great man directing it.** In the eyewitness encounter at Palermo, Adams frames Garibaldi as a "sphinx" whose "extreme simplicity" concealed "extreme complexity," and reports — citing Garibaldi's own later autobiography — that he "had not understood his own acts; that he had been an instrument; that he had served the purposes of the class he least wanted to help." This undercuts great-man historiography in favor of something closer to WMP's [[history-machine]] view (a paradigm assembling from forces the participants don't control) and to [[Configurancy]]'s non-teleological co-emergence of agent and outcome.

**Ceding the reins and choosing Paris as deliberate non-education bookend the chapter's drift.** Mrs. Kuhn's Italian tour begins with Adams's first "experiment in giving the reins to a woman" — he draws from it a lifelong axiom, "no woman had ever driven him wrong; no man had ever driven him right" — and the chapter ends with the mirror-image choice to hand himself over to Paris precisely because he disapproves of it, extracting a season of pleasure from a place he had resolved to take no education from at all.

## Notable passages

The opening statement of the "accidental education" doctrine (c1) sets the chapter's frame before anything happens: for eighteen months nothing intended will teach him, and only chance will. The Germany-at-the-seam passage (c2) catches Adams between a dying mediaeval world and Napoleon III's war for Italy, the clearest Gramsci-gap image in the unit. The Guelph-Ghibelline reasoning (c3) is Adams building, in real time, the moral cover story that lets a self-styled reformer support "robberies with violence." The Rome-resists-evolution passage (c4) and the Gibbon/two-experiments passage (c5) together carry the chapter's real argument: mediaeval Rome, unrestored and unexcavated, refuses the Bostonian faith in progress and instead reads as the wreckage of prior civilizational failures, with a third not foreclosed. The Garibaldi passage (c6) closes the chapter's political education with the same lesson turned on a living man: even the era's most consequential actor did not, by his own later account, understand the acts he was serving.
