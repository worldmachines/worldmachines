# Chaos (1870)

Returning to a London he finds unchanged and complacent in the spring of 1870, Adams believes himself settled into a life of drift and amusement — until, within a single summer, his sister's agonizing death from tetanus and the sudden eruption of the Franco-Prussian War strip away, in quick succession, his last illusions about steady "education," Nature's benevolence, and Europe's stability, leaving him unmoored enough to accept, against his own judgment, an Assistant Professorship of history at Harvard.

## Themes

**Nature revealed as blind, purposeless force.** The chapter's real turning point is not political but personal: watching his sister die slowly of lockjaw after a trivial cab accident, Adams for the first time feels "the shell of custom broken" and Nature not as a benign teacher but as indifferent violence — she "played with it," "liked the torture," and "smothered her victim with caresses." The experience produces a wholly new, non-rational register of understanding ("Impressions like these are not reasoned or catalogued in the mind") that collapses the "stage-scenery of the senses" and leaves the mind "stripped naked, vibrating in a void of shapeless energies." Even Mont Blanc, restored days later to its usual sublime beauty, had shown itself for a moment as "a chaos of anarchic and purposeless forces" — the chapter's title made literal in landscape.

**History as sudden eruption, not steady progress.** The Franco-Prussian War breaks the same way grief did: without warning, and outrunning anyone's capacity to govern or even narrate it. "On July 4, all Europe had been in peace; on July 14, Europe was in full chaos of war," and Adams stresses that no participant — not Gladstone, not Napoleon III, not even Bismarck — understood the mechanism they were caught in ("Bismarck: himself hardly knew how he did it"). This is close to what World Machines calls a [[history-machine]]: a process that assembles invisibly out of independent causes and only "lurches into visibility" as catastrophe, resisting any "great men" account of who caused it. The chapter's insistence on unmasterable chaos, political and natural alike, anticipates the corrective to teleological history that projects like [[world-machines]] are explicitly built to formalize.

**"Education" exposed as illusion, reward as anticlimax.** Adams frames the chapter around the collapse of his belief that experience accumulates into competence. The Gold Conspiracy article is refused by editor after editor for fear of Erie's power, then finally published and instantly pirated — earning him no money and only the epithet "begonia" from a hostile Senator. The Harvard appointment arrives on the same illogical footing: pressed by his family and by President Eliot's airtight non-argument ("If you will point out to me any one who knows more, Mr. Adams, I will appoint him"), Adams takes a post he explicitly disclaims competence for, "on lines he had not chosen, in subjects for which he cared less than nothing."

**Washington's barrenness.** The chapter closes on a long, bitter survey of Gilded-Age Washington — Hewitt, Pendleton, Grant, Sumner — concluding that almost no one who passed through public life there left "permanent result," and that the American character on display in the capital was not the pushing, ingenious type of national myth but "bored, patient, helpless" men who "could not face a new thought."

## Notable passages

The sister's death is the chapter's emotional center: "The last lesson--the sum and term of education--began then," Adams writes, before describing ten days in which "the muscles grew rigid, while the mind remained bright," ending in convulsion. Out of that vigil comes the chapter's most philosophically loaded passage, where Nature's "gesture" appears as "a phantasm, a nightmare, an insanity of force," and Adams concludes flatly that God "might be... a Substance, but He could not be a Person." Days later, recovering at Lake Geneva, the same vision briefly overtakes the Alps themselves. Then the frame shifts from the personal to the continental: the ten-day collapse of European peace in July 1870, narrated in the same vocabulary of sudden, ungovernable chaos. The chapter's final movement is comic relief by contrast — Eliot's unanswerable logic drafting Adams into teaching, and the deflating image of the "begonia," "showy but not useful," as the only public response Adams's most ambitious journalism ever earned.
