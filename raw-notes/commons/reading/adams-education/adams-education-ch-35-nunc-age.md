# Nunc Age: The Trusts, the New Man, and the Death of John Hay

Adams returns to New York in November 1904, nearly forty years after his first postwar homecoming, and finds the city "frantic" with a power that "defied meaning" - the raw energy of Trusts and corporations. He travels to Washington to watch Roosevelt do battle with the Trusts, reading the contest not as politics but as physics: a test of whether the new economic forces can breed a "new man" fit to master them, since the old type of man has plainly failed. Adams restates his dynamic theory of history as an explicit, dated forecast, then turns from public history to private grief as his lifelong friend John Hay's health collapses; the chapter, and the book, end with Hay's death in Paris and Adams's meditation on the close of "education" for the last of the three friends.

## Themes

**Power outrunning meaning.** The 1904 New York skyline "became frantic in its effort to explain something that defied meaning"; the same power that built it has made the world "irritable, nervous, querulous, unreasonable and afraid," and Adams, watching from a club window, feels himself a traveller in Rome under Diocletian, sensing an anarchy for which "no Constantine the Great was in sight."

**The Trusts as the era's true infrastructure.** Adams reads Roosevelt's antitrust campaign as "a measure of motion" rather than a moral crusade: the Trusts "stood for the larger part of the new power that had been created since 1840," a genuinely revolutionary force that "tore society to pieces and trampled it under foot."

**Searching for the "new man."** The book's education metaphor culminates in an open question: whether the new energies can produce a human type equal to them, since the old type "had plainly reached the end of his strength, and his failure had become catastrophic." Adams's language - an old order visibly spent, a new one not yet born, only a hoped-for "child born of contact between the new and the old energies" - reads as a near-literal description of an interregnum, close to what this project calls a [[gramsci-gap]].

**The dynamic theory as forecast.** Adams extends his mechanical theory of history into a dated, checkable claim: that acceleration measured since 1800 would let "the mathematician of 1950" plot the human race's future "as accurately as that of the November meteoroids" - a proto-psychohistorical wager on lawlike historical motion that anticipates the kind of oracular, falsifiable forecasting this project (see [[world-machines]]) attempts.

**Nature's mercy toward death, then Hay's.** Before turning to Hay, Adams lingers on creatures that withdraw to die alone - Scott's antarctic seals, the statesman Purun Dass - finding in them "a singular sympathy for death" that quietly rehearses the chapter's real subject.

**The end of education.** Hay's last exchange with Adams, Shakespeare's "The rest is silence," and the news of his death in Paris dissolve into the book's closing meditation: the shared "education" of the three friends is over, with only a wistful, half-ironic hope of a centenary reunion in 1938.

## Notable passages

**New York's hysteria.** The harbor view that once showed "American society as a long caravan stretching out towards the plains" (1868) has become, in 1904, a city whose outline "became frantic in its effort to explain something that defied meaning" - the chapter's opening image of power that has outgrown its own meaning.

**The Trusts as physics.** Adams frames the antitrust fight mechanically: the Trusts "stood for the larger part of the new power that had been created since 1840," and the real question is whether "the conflict of forces would produce the new man," since "no other energies seemed left on earth to breed."

**A dated forecast.** Adams's dynamic theory becomes an explicit, falsifiable prediction - that acceleration since 1800 would let "the mathematician of 1950" plot "the past and future orbit of the human race as accurately as that of the November meteoroids."

**Nature's sympathy for death.** Before Hay's decline, Adams meditates on animals that withdraw to die alone, quoting Scott's antarctic journal on seals that "crawl to the shore or the ice to die, probably from its instinctive dread of its marine enemies."

**"The rest is silence."** Hay's last exchange with Adams - "You must hold out for the peace negotiations" / "I've not time!" / "You'll need little time!" - resolves weeks later into Shakespeare's line and the news of Hay's death, received as "only the quiet summons to follow--the assent to dismissal."

**The book's close.** The memoir ends by dissolving the three friends' shared education into a wistful hope of an afterlife reunion at their 1938 centenary, when they might finally "find a world that sensitive and timid natures could regard without a shudder."
