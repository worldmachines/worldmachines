# Dilettantism: The Failure of Art and Antiquarianism as Careers (1865-1866)

With Lincoln reelected and the Civil War ending, the pressure that had defined Henry Adams's years as his father's private secretary in London lifts, and leaves him with nothing to replace it. A winter escort trip to Italy delivers luxury without education; Lincoln's assassination reaches the party in Rome and produces only numbness, not insight. Returning to a Legation routine that has hardened from habit into vice, Adams concludes that both law and diplomacy are closed to him — the first foreclosed by his diplomatic training, the second by knowing too much to accept a junior post. Adrift, he lets himself be pulled into two substitute pursuits that structure the rest of the chapter: English art-collecting, learned under the critics Palgrave and Woolner and tested in a farcical episode of expert attribution at Sotheby's and the British Museum; and antiquarian history, tried on for size in a magazine article on Pocahontas urged by Dr. Palfrey. He ends the chapter judging both a false start.

## Themes

**Career paths close as fast as Adams can name them.** The chapter opens by walking through his options and eliminating them one by one — law, diplomacy, a State Department recall blocked by Andrew Johnson's feud with the Senate, the fates of friends (Hay, Barlow, Bartlett, Higginson, Charles Adams) scattering into law, business, or the army. Only the press remains, and even that is diagnosed as "an inferior pulpit; an anonymous schoolmaster; a cheap boarding-school" rather than a real vocation — the last resort of "the educated poor."

**Art, as practiced by the English, is diagnosed as an anti-education.** Adams's verdict on the pursuit is blunt: unlike a discipline with an endpoint, art appreciation as the English practiced it "had no centre or circumference, no beginning, middle, or end, no origin, no object, and no conceivable result as education." The chapter frames this as a symptom of English character generally — a mind, like an English drawing-room, full of incoherent furniture never meant to cohere into a whole.

**Expert authority collapses under its own test.** The extended set-piece — Adams buying a disputed drawing for twelve shillings, then walking it through Palgrave, the dealer Holloway, the British Museum's Curator Reed, the manuscript keeper, and finally the scholar Libri — is the chapter's real argument. Each expert overturns or hedges the last: Reed first rejects it as not Rafael, then reverses himself on the strength of a watermark while still calling it "much overdrawn"; Woolner dismisses Reed outright ("Reed's a fool!"); even Libri, consulted to read the handwriting, cannot identify the language it is written in. Adams keeps the drawing on his mantelpiece for forty years afterward as a standing joke on the whole apparatus of connoisseurship, refusing ever to settle the question of its authenticity.

**Antiquarianism offers no better exit.** Turning from British art to British history yields the same diagnosis — a field of "traps and pitfalls," scrappy and directionless except in the hands of a Macaulay whom no one else could safely imitate. Dr. Palfrey's suggestion that Adams write on Captain John Smith and Pocahontas for the *North American Review* gives Adams his first published historical work (January 1867), but the chapter explicitly refuses to credit it as a real education — merely a second dead end alongside the first.

## Notable passages

The closing of Adams's professional options is stated with a lawyer's precision: **"Any one who had held, during the four most difficult years of American diplomacy, a position at the centre of action... could not beg a post of Secretary at Vienna or Madrid."** This is less self-pity than a structural observation — his qualifications are too specific to redeploy.

The Rome passage on Lincoln's death is the chapter's quietest and strangest moment: the assassination lands in a city already saturated with political murder, provoking not grief but a kind of historical fatigue — **"as though America were also getting educated"** — followed immediately by the flat notation that nothing about the party's travel plans changed.

The drawing anecdote's coda is the chapter's thesis in miniature: Adams's decision to never resolve the drawing's authenticity is presented as the only intellectually honest response available to him, given that the entire chain of experts consulted had already contradicted one another. The passage functions as Adams's retrospective judgment on the whole enterprise of connoisseurship as a claimed form of knowledge — worth flagging for readers interested in how legitimating expertise can decay into pure performance without any of the participants (least of all the experts) noticing; see [[decay-sequence]].

The chapter's final paragraph is its explicit verdict, naming the two-pronged trap directly: Adams finds himself "dragged on one side into English dilettantism, which of all dilettantism he held the most futile; and, on the other, into American antiquarianism, which of all antiquarianism he held the most foolish" — a double false start that the next chapter must undo. The five-years-long limbo between a finished war identity and an unfound peacetime one reads as a personal instance of an institutional interregnum — compare [[gramsci-gap]], though Adams frames it here purely as private failure, not epochal transition.
