# A Law of Acceleration

Adams closes his historiographical experiment by proposing a literal "dynamic theory" of history: civilization behaves like a mass accelerating toward a new centre of attraction, and the rate of that acceleration can be measured — imperfectly but consistently — through proxies like coal-output, steam-horsepower, and the compounding complexity of scientific knowledge. Tracking a roughly ten-year doubling from 1840 to 1900, and extrapolating backward with decreasing confidence through the histories of mathematics, architecture, and prehistoric tool-making, Adams arrives at a "law of acceleration" as unyielding as any law of mechanics — one that, if it holds, will soon force the human mind past its historical carrying capacity and into "a new phase subject to new laws."

## Themes

**A dynamic theory built on the mechanics of attraction.** Adams opens by discarding argument for image: history is a mass drawn toward "a new centre, or preponderating mass, artificially introduced on earth," accelerating until it reaches a new equilibrium, like Newton's comet. This is an explicit bid to make history quantifiable the way physics is quantifiable — an early ancestor of WMP's own attempt to fuse qualitative historiography with quantitative, model-driven method (`[[world-machines]]`).

**Coal-output as a dynamometer.** Lacking a direct instrument for measuring "social movement," Adams settles for what nineteenth-century society itself measured — coal-power — and treats its doubling roughly every decade as a stand-in for the growth of total historical force. This is legibility-making turned on history itself: an attempt to render an otherwise inchoate mass of change into a single commensurable ratio (`[[legibility-as-machine-core]]`).

**Retrocasting the ratio through centuries of proxies.** Unsatisfied with a single century of data, Adams tries to carry the same doubling ratio backward — through the mathematicians (Laplace, Newton, Descartes), the architects (St. Peter's, Amiens, Hagia Sophia), and finally prehistoric stone tools — conceding at each step that the further back he goes, the less the numbers can be trusted, though the underlying "sequence" seems undeniable. This is history read as detective work: inferring an invisible governing process from many independent, non-narrative traces rather than from any single documented cause (`[[history-machine]]`).

**The failure of the "stationary period" prophecy.** Adams recalls that mid-century science, following John Stuart Mill, expected force to plateau — a conviction that "lasted through the eighties" until radium disproved it. The passage is a case study in institutional slowness to recognize an ongoing acceleration already visible in the data.

**The vertiginous, ironic self-portrait.** Adams dramatizes the felt cost of acceleration by casting himself as "an elderly and timid single gentleman in Paris" flung about by forces "as though he had hold of a live wire or a runaway automobile" — bombs, automobiles, accidents as the lived texture of runaway progress, not just its statistics.

**Teacher becomes pupil; the new American as a "sort of God."** Adams renounces the historian's traditional pedagogical authority over the coming generation, predicting that anyone who lived to see the year 2000 would know how to control unlimited power and stand to the nineteenth century as something like a god compared with any former creation of nature — an ambivalent, quasi-oracular forecast rather than a triumphalist one.

**The mind must "jump."** The chapter's closing image — thought as "common salt in indefinite solution" approaching a phase change — treats the acceleration not as endless smooth growth but as a system approaching a discontinuity: multiplicity outrunning the mind's capacity to unify it, forcing a break into "a new phase subject to new laws." This reads as a direct precursor to WMP's own transition dynamics, where a world machine's Day cannot extend indefinitely and the crossing into what comes next is neither continuous nor guaranteed peaceful (`[[gramsci-gap]]`).

## Notable passages

The chapter's argumentative spine is the dynamic-theory image itself — "a new centre, or preponderating mass...accelerate its motion till it shall establish a new equilibrium" — followed immediately by its empirical anchor, the coal-output doubling "every ten years between 1840 and 1900." Adams then generalizes this into a near-physical law: "A law of acceleration, definite and constant as any law of mechanics, cannot be supposed to relax its energy to suit the convenience of man." The human cost of that law appears in the comic-terrified self-portrait of forces that "grasped his wrists and flung him about as though he had hold of a live wire or a runaway automobile." The chapter's forecasting culminates in the claim that the post-1900 American would know how to "control unlimited power" and be "a sort of God" by comparison with prior humanity — before closing on the image of mind as salt in solution that "would need to jump" into a new phase.
