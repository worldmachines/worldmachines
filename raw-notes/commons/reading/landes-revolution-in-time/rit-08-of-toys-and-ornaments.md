# RIT 08 — Of Toys and Ornaments and Serious Things (ch. 6)

Spring drive and fusee created *l'horlogerie en petit volume*, and for the first time the trade ramified by country. Landes's chapter is a sorting argument: of the three paths now open — miniaturized complexity (the German toy), the timepiece as jewel (the French ornament), and the precision instrument — only the third mattered "in the long run," and the first two were "magnificent dead ends" that nonetheless bankrolled the industry and kept automation alive. The chapter then pivots to the demand side of precision: Puritan consumers who wanted a monitor not a bauble, astronomers who needed to seize time in flight, and the longitude problem, which made accurate timekeeping a matter of state prizes, drowned fleets, and the largest mobilization of scientific talent Europe had yet seen.

## Key arguments

- **Ornament and complexity were diversions, not failures of skill.** German tabernacle clocks "went against functional logic": fortune-priced, mediocre rate, unmaintainable — the purchase committed the buyer to an annuity for upkeep, and orphaned pieces ended in treasuries like the Topkapi. Their lasting contribution was keeping the principle of automation alive for a later age.
- **Institutions chose the dead end.** German guilds responded to collapsing demand (end of Ottoman tribute, Thirty Years War) by doubling down on complex *chefs-d'oeuvre*, blocking innovation, and persecuting non-guild *Landmeister* as *Pfuscher* (botchers). Closure killed recruitment: by the mid-18th century the trade had shrunk to a shadow, and "the best way to become a clock- or watchmaker was to be the son of one or marry his widow."
- **Demand shaped design ideology.** French makers' peak effort went to the case because their richest customers wanted ornament; craftsmen-artists saw luxury work as most dignified and profitable. The effect was to confine demand to a fortunate few and "diminish awareness of the watch as a tool, an instrument of planning and self-discipline." The English 'puritan' watch is the counter-case: plain, reliable, adopted by Calvinists as monitor — and the makers, often Puritans themselves, competed on precision because unadorned products could be distinguished no other way.
- **The earth is "the original clock."** Longitude is just a time difference, so the navigation problem reduced to either timing a celestial event against tables or carrying reference time aboard; the chronometer (second solution) eventually prevailed.
- **Science and craft were not yet ruptured.** The longitude effort joined Galileo, Huygens, Hooke, Leibniz and Newton with master clockmakers as genuine partners — and when the scientists gave up, "it was the craftsmen who persisted and completed the task."

## Evidence & specifics

- **German path:** Augsburg, Nuremberg, Ulm, 16th–18th c.; *Türkenverehrung* tribute from the mid-16th c. — the sultan, vizier, and pasha at Ofen (Buda) delighted in clocks they could not maintain and so replaced at short intervals. Tribute ended with the Peace of Zsitvatorok (1606); the Thirty Years War (1618–48) finished the market. The luxury trade passed to London assembler-entrepreneurs (Pinchbeck, Cox, Jaquet-Droz, Ilbury, Anthony) sourcing from Geneva and Neuchâtel for mandarins, maharajas, and pashas.
- **French path:** Blois and Paris; painting on enamel invented by Jean Toutin in the 1630s; repeated re-firings made spoilage ruinous. Later process innovations — standard-size rough movements (later 17th c.), Geneva paint shops producing enamel discs by the hundreds, and late-18th-c. specialization (one artist painting only faces, another clothes, another backgrounds) — an early fine-grained division of labor.
- **Astronomy:** Bernhard Walther of Nuremberg (c. 1430–1504) counted hour-wheel teeth from Mercury's rising to sunrise — fractional precision beyond the dial. Kassel star mapping (William IV of Hesse and Christoph Rothmann, late 16th c.) used right ascension, a time-measured coordinate. Tycho Brahe's progression: minutes mentioned twice in 1563–70; a minutes clock in 1577; seconds in 1581; by 1587, despairing at ±4 seconds of discord among four clocks, he fell back on a mercury clepsydra — weighed fluid as a proto-chronograph. Jost Bürgi, hired by William of Hesse in 1579, built a seconds-marking clock reportedly within a minute a day.
- **Longitude stakes:** Manila galleons, 9–10,000 miles, 7–8 months; over thirty lost in 250 years; Schurz estimated some sixty million pesos in property losses. Dead reckoning timed by an Ave Maria; some French seamen still shunned the log-and-line in the 1760s, two centuries after its introduction. Columbus-grade *sens marin* meant ~1% error (Bernáldez: 10 leagues in 1,000). Corrected longitudes cost Louis XIV "one-sixth of his domain." Prizes: Philip III (after 1598) offered a 6,000-ducat perpetual pension plus annuity and cash; Britain's 1713–14 Act offered up to £20,000.

## WM resonances

- The puritan watch as "monitor" — a tool "of planning and self-discipline" pulled into existence by Calvinist demand — is the watch joining the Modernity Machine's discipline apparatus from the consumer side; precision acts as exactly the kind of shared attractor that makes the MM a *pull* machine ([[legibility-as-machine-core]], [[clock-as-incomplete-discipline]]).
- Landes narrates horology's possibility-space collapsing onto one metric (accuracy), with the German complexity-toys and French ornament-line as the suppressed variety — a small, clean case of legibility's single measure outcompeting proliferation ([[divergence-machine]]).
- The attempt "to seize [time] in flight," immediately conceded as "logically impossible" and answered by a medieval poem on the ungraspable now, is the chapter admitting the fugitive remainder that measurement never captures ([[untimely]]).
- Longitude-by-time made global position readable — the earth itself conscripted as "the original clock," maps as money, the ocean rendered legible to states and trading companies ([[legibility-as-machine-core]]).
