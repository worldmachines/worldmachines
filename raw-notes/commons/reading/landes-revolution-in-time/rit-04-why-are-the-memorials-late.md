# RIT 04 — Why Are the Memorials Late? (Ch. 2) — China's second chance refused

Having shown (ch. 1) that Su Sung's great water clock died without issue, Landes asks why China also failed its *second* chance: the arrival of European mechanical clocks with the Jesuit mission. Matteo Ricci used striking clocks — "self-ringing bells, such as never before had been seen in China" — and superior eclipse-predicting astronomy as bait to penetrate the empire, reaching Peking in 1601 at the summons of an impatient emperor. The Chinese adored the machines; the market, the craftsmen, and even an imperial workshop under K'ang-hsi were all present. Yet no native industry emerged. Landes's answer is demand-side and cultural: precision time simply did not matter in a society where neither life nor work was organized by hours and minutes, where an official's time belonged wholly to the emperor, and where admitting the superiority of barbarian artifice was an assault on the Middle Kingdom's self-image. The clock was therefore filed as a toy — and the refusal it expressed ran forward to Macartney's rebuff (1793), the trade deficit, opium, and the Treaty of Nanking (1842).

## Key arguments

- **The turning point is ontological, not technical.** "Everything was changed drastically by the simple fact that once Ricci brought European clocks to China, progress lay not in the transformation of indigenous technology but in the adoption of an alien device." After Ricci, Chinese horology could only imitate; the indigenous clepsydra line was dead.
- **Demand, not capability, was missing.** China's population equaled all of Europe's, per-capita income (per the "bold comparativist econometricians") was nearly as high, and craftsmen were superbly skilled. What was absent was any social use for hours and minutes — calendar dates mattered (dynastic legitimacy), clock time did not. Cipolla: foreign machinery "was not the expression of a Chinese response to the problems set by a Chinese environment."
- **Trivialization as defense.** Calling clocks toys was partly genuine playfulness (kaleidoscopes fetched 500 ducats) but mostly "a protective reaction, an effort to erase the discrepancy between self-perception and reality." Denial (claiming Chinese priority — the striking clock "invented by a [Chinese] monk," even the steam engine) and disparagement (Wei Chün's 1630s pamphlet; Hsü Ta-shu's "pure waste") were the other two moves.
- **Time as property is the precondition of time discipline.** Court officials arrived at midnight for pre-dawn audiences and waited for drums and gates — like Russian peasants "catching" a train. The system assumed all of a mandarin's time belonged to the emperor; the waste could not even be perceived as waste. "The only way to correct this waste... would have been to recognize time as private property."
- **The refusal had a price.** Autarky shut the door and gave Europeans "the desire and incentive to smash it down": with timepieces among the few Western goods China would buy, the deficit was settled in silver, then opium — ending in the Opium War (1839–42) and Nanking.

## Evidence & specifics

- **Mission chronology:** Portuguese at Macao 1557; Chinese wall cutting off the compound 1573; Ricci joins the East mission 1577, masters mandarin Chinese within a year (per Father Alonso Sanchez, 1582–83); mission at Chao-ch'ing, then stepwise to Peking; emperor demands "where are the self-ringing bells?" — and disconnects a clock's bell so the dowager empress will lose interest rather than ask for it.
- **Calendar politics:** Ricci (1605) reports 200+ persons employed "at much expense" on the annual calendar — the symbol of sovereignty — yet it "fairly swarmed with errors"; his Ptolemaic (already obsolete) astronomy still out-predicted late-Ming methods.
- **K'ang-hsi's workshop:** Dutch ambassadors in 1665 found Chinese timekeepers "so poor that you cannot trust them"; K'ang-hsi then installed a palace clock shop (cf. the Louvre ateliers), boasted in verse and in letters of "hundreds and thousands of clocks... all of them keep accurate time," and gave each child ten or twenty chiming clocks "to play with." Yet supervisory work stayed with missionary horologists; the native trade did only repair and imitation — "at best poor copies of poor copies of European timepieces." Unlike Japan, China never evolved a clock style of its own.
- **Accumulation and loot:** Father Valentin Chalier (1730s) counts 4,000+ pieces "from the best masters of Paris and London" in the palace; the 1860 Anglo-French sack of the Summer Palace (British NCOs carrying jewellers' touchstones; a chaplain looting between sermons) and the post-Boxer looting dispersed them — Summer Palace provenance still raises auction prices.
- **The Fu-heng object lesson (Chao I, mid-18th c.):** the watch-festooned minister whose servants "mutually check the time against each other" still arrives after the emperor is seated; "those in the court who never miss meetings are the ones who do not own clocks."

## Quotables

- "Clockwork was God's work. (Conversely, God's work was clockwork.)" (on the Jesuit equation of technical with spiritual superiority, mid-chapter)
- "All the other machines are simply intricate oddities, designed for the pleasure of the senses. They fulfil no basic needs." (Ssu-K'u Ch'üan-Shu Ti-Yao catalogue, 1782, as quoted by Cipolla)
- "We have never set much store on strange or ingenious objects, nor do we need any more of your country's manufactures." (Ch'ien-lung's edict to George III, 1793)
- "Nothing is more symptomatic of a clockless, unpunctual society into which time intrudes as an unwelcome stranger." (on courtiers waiting from midnight for pre-dawn audiences, chapter's end)

## WM resonances

- [[clock-as-incomplete-discipline]] — a clean natural experiment: the device arrived in China stripped of the social machinery of discipline and stayed inert, a chiming toy. Landes shows discipline lives in social relations (whose time is it?), not in the escapement — the clock alone disciplines nothing.
- [[legibility-as-machine-core]] — China had state-temporal legibility at the calendar scale (200 functionaries; calendar as "symbol and expression of Chinese sovereignty") but no demand for hour-minute legibility of persons and work; the Modernity Machine's core artifact found no socket. Landes's "time as private property" marks the property regime legible time presupposes.
- [[thicket-pairing-ratio]] — Ch'ien-lung's 1793 refusal is a textbook *unpaired* refusal: rejection of the alien machine backed by self-image and autarky rather than by any constructed alternative habitat, and it ended with the door smashed down at Nanking.
