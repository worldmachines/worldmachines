# RIT 15 — The Good Old Days That Never Were (Ch. 13)

This chapter opens the production-side history ("Making Time") by attacking two myths at once: the romantic image of the "compleat" craftsman who made the whole watch from start to finish, and the revisionist economists' claim that division of labour and workplace hierarchy were devices imposed by exploitative employers to deskill workers and enforce subordination. Landes concedes the second view "some truth" in clock and watch history, but inverts its causality: masters who deployed further division to enhance control did so on the basis of prior experience of specialization's advantages — "the division of labour... was father, not daughter, to the thought." He then anatomizes specialization at three levels and shows that the guild order built atop it was neither golden age nor effective cartel: an anti-commercial, zero-sum institution that transmitted superb skill while extracting rent from journeymen, fighting technological change, and failing wherever its writ ran out — which, in politically fragmented Europe, was almost everywhere.

## Key arguments

- **Specialization is primordial, not capitalist.** Three levels: among crafts (smiths, leather, textiles — ancient), within crafts (textile spinners/weavers/fullers/dyers; fullers and dyers were "a proletariat, centuries before the Industrial Revolution"), and within the shop. All predate or accompany the mechanical clock from its beginning; every large clock was a team product.
- **The "compleat craftsman" ideal was potential, not actual.** It meant the master should *know* every operation, not perform them. Shop hierarchy (master / journeymen / apprentices) was pedagogy plus economics: simplified, repetitive tasks made unskilled trainees employable and apprenticeship affordable. "The aim is not hierarchy for its own sake, but efficiency and the maximization of return." This hierarchy existed before guilds; guilds merely fixed it in law.
- **Even the masters' versatility rested on subcontracting.** The signature on the back plate was a warrant of performance, not proof of making: Tompion's and Graham's clientele expected both clocks and watches, supplied via outside specialists. Springs were the limit case — spring steel and tempering were arcane, secret specialities no watchmaker sensibly attempted himself.
- **Guild ideology was the village's zero-sum ethic urbanized.** Quality talk masked turf defense: restricted entry, masterpiece requirements, bans on selling outsiders' work, sustenance guaranteed to those who "worked well, however inefficiently." Virtue: extraordinary skill transmission. Cost: technological conservatism and ceilings on growth.
- **Workers were more conservative than employers.** Journeymen feared division of labour more than masters did — it devalued skill, blocked all-round training, and invited mechanization ("the simpler and more repetitive the operation, the more it lent itself to mechanization").
- **Guild limits were rent extraction.** Caps on apprentices, journeymen, and masterships rationed a labour market where masters paid below marginal product, and bans on out-of-town employment kept the cheap labour captive.
- **Enforcement failed structurally.** Reiterated prohibitions, suburb belts just past the town line, royal exemptions sold for cash, grey markets through goldsmiths, and above all political fragmentation made guild monopoly a perpetual losing holding action. Where trade was free — London in practice, Holland — the industry thrived: "this freedom was both symptom and condition" of the trade's vigour.

## Evidence & specifics

- **Inter-craft collaboration:** Roger de Stoke's Norwich Cathedral astronomical clock (1321–25) needed its dial made in London, 114 miles away — three tries, one bankrupted engraver, an ox-cart haul of perhaps a fortnight. Watch ornamentation went international: David Ramsay's case engraved at Blois by Gerard de Heck (Dutch expatriate); Toutin's enamels adorning watches signed London/Amsterdam/Strasbourg; the Huaud family turning out polychrome cases "by the score" for batch-made standard-size movements across Europe (late 17th–early 18th c.).
- **Versatile origins:** clockmakers emerged from blacksmiths, braziers, locksmiths, gunmakers, and cannon founders — Jacques Cutriffin ("Jacques de Fribourg"), *magister horologiorum et bombardorum regalium* to Charles V of France, built the Grenoble tower clock and fought beside Joan of Arc; Kaspar Brunner, locksmith and cannon founder, spent three-plus years (1527–30) fixing Bern's tower clock.
- **Spring secrets:** each springmaker's quenching recipe was proprietary (Landes cites African smiths quenching blades in urine — lion's urine for brave warriors — and a French refugee in London tempering in talc); 18th-c. cookbooks couldn't replace touch; great springmakers signed their springs like watchmakers their watches.
- **Subcontracting chains:** Quare → Joseph Williamson (equation work); Brockbank → Thomas Earnshaw (pocket chronometers); Breguet ← Jacques-Frédéric Houriet (tourbillon ébauches); 19th-c. British makers ← Clerkenwell chronometer specialists. Sidelines of great artists: Quare's stick barometers, Urban Jürgensen's pocket thermometers in watch form, Breguet's rotating optical instruments (1830s–40s).
- **Guilds came late:** three hundred years after the mechanical clock's invention, two centuries after clockmaking was a defined trade, seceding from metalworker/locksmith bodies. In small-market Montbéliard the trades never separated (*serrurier-horlogeur*); Jean Reichert's 1737 admission was conditional on old locksmiths keeping the right to make clocks.
- **Exploitation arithmetic (Augsburg, mid-16th c.):** journeymen earned 20–24 kreuzer a week plus room and board (~1 gulden total) while a run-of-the-shop timepiece taking 4–8 man-weeks sold for 30 gulden — "there was no surer source of profit."
- **Leakage geography:** Blois ringed by manufacturing villages (Chouzy, Ouchamps, Vineuil, Saint-Gervais); Augsburg/Friedberg, Nuremberg/Fürth, Hamburg/Altona. The French crown's Louvre enclave and 300–400 *suivant la cour* exemptions (17th c., some selling for thousands of livres) breached the very monopolies the crown chartered — "a touch of fiscal genius."
- **Georg Roll (1546–92):** the chapter's set-piece interloper — bachelor shopkeeper at Friedberg, protected by Duke Albrecht, peddled 5,000 gulden of timepieces to Rome and Naples (1575–76), bought Augsburg citizenship in 1578 via a general store (*Kramerei*) and the mayor's daughter, ran a clock-prize lottery (*Glückshafen*) by imperial permission, sold clock-globes (mechanisms by Augsburg master Johann Reinhold), was jailed by Rudolf II for charging the emperor 1,200 thaler against his brother's 1,500, and died at 46 — probably of plague — at the peak of his wealth, his ~25-employee shop "something of a small manufactory."
- **Grey market:** the 1636 Blois raid on goldsmith-enameller Isaac Gribelin found 23 gold and 4 silver watches hidden in attic pine boxes, signed by established (some dead) makers; the court's verdict — sell them within a month outside Blois, 300-livre fine if repeated — was "a mere slap on the wrist." The 1640 Lemaindre/Chesnon ordinance authorizing searches of suspects' very pockets proved equally impotent.
- **Fragmentation:** Geneva's corporation controlled the rural Republic but not Nyon, Vevey, Lausanne, nor the guild-free mountains to the north-west; London's Clockmakers' Company was ceremonial almost from birth — anyone could practise for a small fee, and makers dodged the burden of office.

## WM resonances

- [[divergence-machine]] — political fragmentation is the chapter's deep engine: no sovereignty ran far enough to enforce monopoly, so watchmaking proliferated precisely where the guild's pull could not reach — Friedberg, Fürth, Altona, the lake's northern shore, the guild-free Jura. Independent local jurisdictions acted as push-forces multiplying centres of production against every attempt at corporate standardization; Landes's closing line makes freedom "both symptom and condition" of the trade's vitality.
- [[capture-resistance]] — the chapter is a catalogue of escape repertoires: apprentices jumping contract once they became assets, chamber-working journeymen selling to interlopers, Roll's serial evasions (court patronage, lottery license, a general store as front, litigation as delay), Gribelin's "custom of the trade" defence. The guild's legibility apparatus — registers, searches, masterpieces, seizure powers down to turning out pockets — generated mostly paper: "reiteration of law is proof of ineffectiveness."
- [[The Thicket]] — a precise negative case. The guild *was* a defended habitat for a way of being (anti-commercial ethic, sustenance for conscientious work, community over market), but it defended itself by legal border-enforcement rather than terroir and opacity — and since the skills, movements, and journeymen were all portable, the border leaked at every point. Where craft communities *did* persist (the spring-steel makers with their secret quenching recipes), it was opacity and non-portable tacit knowledge, not statute, doing the work.
