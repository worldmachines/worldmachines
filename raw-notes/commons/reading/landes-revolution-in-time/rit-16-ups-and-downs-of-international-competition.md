# RIT 16 — The Ups and Downs of International Competition (Ch. 14)

This chapter is Landes's anatomy of national horological rivalry in the long eighteenth century, and its thesis is double-layered. The surface story is the "accident of talent": five great London makers — Tompion, Quare, Graham, Ellicott, Mudge — gave Britain technical and commercial leadership after the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes (1685) drove France's best Protestant practitioners out. But Landes immediately undercuts the great-man reading: France soon had genius to spare (the Le Roys, the Berthouds, Lepine, Janvier, above all Breguet) and lost its industry anyway. "It takes more than a great name to make an industry. It takes efficient techniques, low costs, and access to large markets." Britain had all three; Paris had names, gold cases, and a hollowing-out beneath them as Geneva and the Jura swallowed actual production. The chapter's connective tissue is the *demand* story: British supremacy rested on the most precocious, socially diversified time-consciousness in Europe — down to factory workers buying cheap "turnips" to check their employers' clocks.

## Key arguments

- **Talent explains the lead only "in part."** The five-maker lineage (Tompion → Graham → Mudge, plus Quare and Ellicott) supplied real innovation — balance-spring exploitation, the repeater (Quare, 1680–86), cylinder and dead-beat escapements plus mercury pendulum (Graham), the lever escapement (Mudge, 1769–70). But the deeper engine was market structure.
- **Demand made the industry.** English wages were half again Continental levels; ~25% urbanization vs ≤10% on the Continent; cottage industry, turnpikes, and coach services (locked clocks for drivers, passengers checking watches) diffused time-awareness down the social ladder. The locomotive of the 1820s "found a society already prepared for it psychologically and horologically."
- **Factory discipline created defensive watch ownership.** Workers from cottage and field experienced the factory as "a kind of jail, with the clock as the lock"; employers used fines, clock-prizes, and window-tapping wakers. Suspecting clock-fiddling (Landes is skeptical it was common — multi-shift "fiddling will out"), workers bought their own watches at about a fortnight's wages to contest the employer's time.
- **France kept the style, lost the trade.** Berthoud's 1786 memorandum diagnoses the collapse: debased mastership, unscrupulous merchant-sellers, and good craftsmen forced to sign Genevan movements. "What triumphed in France was not so much the French watch as the French style of watch, which others could make about as well and cheaper." Breguet made Paris the watch capital while he lived but worked only for the rich and could not revive the industry.
- **The signature was a fiction; the trade ran on outside work, forgery, and smuggling.** Even the greatest names (Breguet demonstrably, Tompion probably) sold bought-in pieces; pseudonyms "Tarts" and "Wilter" covered thousands of Dutch-style watches; Genevan contraband moved in insurable volume, hidden in fish loads.
- **Britain's Achilles' heel was calibre fragmentation.** Every shop its own calibre, no shop large; product differentiation tied customers but "blunted the advantages that specialization might otherwise have yielded" — harmless against Geneva, fatal later against the Jura.

## Evidence & specifics

- Tompion (act. 1671–1713): Hooke's collaborator (and target — "a clownish churlish Dog"); pivot holes unworn after sixty years; parts "interchangeable in the rough" by the 1690s. Quare's name, like Julien Le Roy's, mass-forged on the Continent.
- Mudge as cautionary tale: gave up the civilian trade in 1771 for marine chronometers, made three superb dead-ends, out-competed by Earnshaw's spring detent — "a victim of his own prowess," like Ferdinand Berthoud and Arnold.
- French protectionism: absolute prohibition of British watches vs a 2-livre duty on Geneva pieces via Lyons; yet c. 1750 even "a coach driver would not wear a watch that wasn't English" (Alfred Franklin).
- Breguet: ~5,000 watches c. 1780s–1823; cheapest *souscription* pieces ≥600 francs — a Paris labourer's annual pay (~$8,000); bought Genevan/Jurassien movements and, Landes argues, sold some "Breguet à Paris" pieces himself.
- British scale: 150,000–200,000 watches/year by the late 18th century — roughly half of European (≈world) output; Prescot-to-Liverpool cottage specialization; Lancashire ébauches made to paper patterns and the Lancashire gauge; drawn pinion wire (up to fifty passes); Huntsman's crucible steel (c. 1740) still a British monopoly in 1795.
- Adam Smith noted a watch movement falling from ~£20 (mid-17th c.) to 20 shillings; John Grant told the Commons labour multiplied material value "a Thousand Times"; a pound of balance springs was worth more than its weight in gold.
- Watchmaking as seedbed of the Industrial Revolution: its machines modeled the new metalworking tools; Lancashire and Yorkshire mills advertised for clockmakers; spinning-machinery wheelwork was called "clockwork."
- Tariff endgame: 1784 excise on gold/silver hit British 22-carat makers vs French 20- and Swiss 18-carat; 1787 duty of 27.5% on imports — "Even that would not be enough."

## WM resonances

- [[clock-as-incomplete-discipline]] — the factory-discipline passage is a locus classicus: the clock-as-lock provokes Saint Monday ("Sunday was holy, Monday was holy, and Tuesday was often needed to recover"), backward-bending labour supply (higher wages bought *less* work from workers with "a strong preference for leisure"), and the defensive watch — workers arming themselves with the discipliner's own instrument to contest stolen hours. The disciplinary apparatus generates its own [[untimely]] remainder and the means of disputing it.
- [[legibility-as-machine-core]] — the chapter is equally a study in *illegibility* as commercial strategy: the maker's signature (the trade's central legibility device, "warrant of the watch's quality") systematically falsified by forgery, pseudonyms, and outside work; smuggling so routinized it was insurable yet left "few traces in the historical record." The asymmetry of time-knowledge between employer and worker replays medieval Flanders — whoever controls the legible clock controls the wage.
- [[divergence-machine]] — a precise negative case: British calibre fragmentation (every shop its own standard) is variety that *blunts* scale; Landes frames standardization-failure as the seed of Britain's later defeat by the Jura, a clean illustration of the legibility/variety tension running the other way.
