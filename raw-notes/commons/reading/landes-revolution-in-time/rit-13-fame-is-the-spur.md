# RIT 13 — Fame Is the Spur (ch. 11)

The chapter closes the longitude story by moving it from heroic invention to industrial competition: in the last quarter of the eighteenth century the British turned the marine chronometer into "an object of industrial manufacture and commercial use." Its protagonists are John Arnold (1736–99), courtier-craftsman who won George III with a minute repeater set in a ring, and Thomas Earnshaw (1749–1829), a journeyman too poor to afford a patent. Both arrived at the spring-detent escapement around 1781–82; Earnshaw's simpler, oil-free version won the trade, but Arnold's side held the social machinery of credit — patents, the Royal Society, the press. Landes's real subject is the divergence between the fame economy and the technical verdict, and he rides it all the way to a 1980 Christie's auction where the partisanship was still alive. The chapter ends with precision's runaway: clocks that outlived their market, then outran the rotating earth.

## Key arguments

- **Merit beat patronage on the bench, not in the record.** Earnshaw was "vastly inferior" in influence and wealth, yet his escapement displaced Arnold's within a generation on cost and performance — no oil on the scape wheel, simpler layout; the Board of Longitude called his machines "incomparably better." Once fixed (by ~1800), his model was the marine standard for the next century and a half.
- **The patent system priced out the inventor.** A patent cost about £100 in fees; Earnshaw publicized his escapement out of economic necessity (a journeyman advertising for work), which exposed him to copying. Arnold, tipped off via Thomas Wright's shop, filed a summary, barely defensible application a week after Earnshaw began watching the Registrar's office (No. 1328 of 1782); Wright later filed for Earnshaw (No. 1354 of 1783). Landes endorses Mercer's reading: parallel invention, with the sight of Earnshaw's watch triggering Arnold's hasty filing.
- **Patronage could bend even the Board of Longitude.** Sir Joseph Banks — Royal Society president from 1778 at age thirty-five, "enthusiastic patron of the Arnolds" — fought Earnshaw "tooth and nail," abetted by hydrographer Alexander Dalrymple. After the board's 3 March 1803 finding for Earnshaw, Banks's 1804 "Protest" reopened proceedings; the settlement was equal £3,000 prizes for Earnshaw and Arnold's son.
- **Chronometer adoption was faster than instrument counts imply** — a revisionist point. Historians infer slow uptake from boxed-chronometer numbers and textbooks (the 1809 Amsterdam naval handbook gave the chronometer 2 of 660 pages), but masters and mates bought pocket chronometers and deck watches at half the price, kept warm and steady in the pocket; lunars demanded a conjunction of four rare virtues and persisted mainly in the navy.
- **Precision became self-undermining.** A 1790s Arnold or Earnshaw pocket chronometer still keeps time today — so durable that replacement demand collapsed and the British industry shrank to a single firm. Then accuracy surpassed nature's clock: electric and vacuum controllers exposed irregularities in the earth's rotation, and in 1967 the second was detached from celestial motion entirely (9,192,631,770 caesium periods).

## Evidence & specifics

- Arnold: born Bodmin, learned watchmaking and German in Holland; 1764 ring repeater for George III; refused the Tsar's £1,000 for a copy. From the 1770s: pivoted detent, helical isochronous balance spring, his own compensation balance; switched to spring detent in the early 1780s because pivot oil deteriorated.
- The Wright shop scene: Arnold opened Earnshaw's watch over Wright's protest, sneering that no one there "knew what o'clock it was"; confirmed to Earnshaw by foreman Robert Best.
- Banks: £6,000/year inherited income (≈$1.5M); gave £10,000 toward Cook's first voyage; demanded a retinue of fifteen for the second, then quit for Iceland. Dalrymple published a laudatory pamphlet on Arnold's chronometers (1788) and attacked Earnshaw in The Times (1806).
- Banks's canvass of London makers ran against Earnshaw, who had alienated colleagues by claiming their signed work was his; Pennington called the escapements "certainly the same" while himself switching to Earnshaw's. Exception: jeweller William Frodsham testified he saw Earnshaw's improvements in late 1780/early 1781.
- Christie's 1980: an unsigned-date Arnold bench chronometer dated "c. 1775" as the earliest Earnshaw-type spring detent — Arnold first by seven years, Earnshaw a thief (The Times, 18 April 1981; Shakespeare–Bacon comparison). Demolished by internal evidence (Mercer: c. 1811 — up-down indicator, John Roger's compensation curb, Breguet-style bimetal, advanced separate passing spring) and by logic: Arnold silent for decades under accusation is unbelievable. Christie's retreated via a "typographic error" insert (c. 1775–1790); the clock failed, was bought in, later sold for £4,600.
- Trade scale by ~1815: British output 4,000–5,000 instruments, half signed Arnold, Earnshaw or Barraud; French output in the hundreds (Ferdinand Berthoud 80, Louis Berthoud 150, Breguet under 100). A good chronometer cost £25–£100 — half a year's to two years' skilled wages. British "good enough" utility vs. French finish; the French retort: with that many instruments, a few three or four standard deviations above the mean were inevitable.
- Precision ladder: electric/vacuum controllers → 1/100–1/1000 s per day; quartz → another order; caesium resonator (Teddington, 1955) → one part in 30 billion (a second per thousand years); gains averaging an order of magnitude every five years; NIST's 1999 standard — one second in twenty million years.

## WM resonances

- [[legibility-as-machine-core]] — the 1967 redefinition is legibility consuming its own referent: once "man's clocks were more accurate than nature's," the earth's rotation became the irregular thing measured, and the unit of time was re-grounded in the instrument's atomic interior. The standard no longer reads the world; the world is read against the standard. Earnshaw's design frozen for 150 years is the same move at the artifact level — the precision quest hardening into reproducible infrastructure.
- [[clock-as-incomplete-discipline]] / [[untimely]] — Landes's closing line concedes the discipline's incompleteness at its apex: measurement perfected to a second in twenty million years while time itself stays undefined and ungoverned. Total command of the metric, no purchase on the thing it metricizes.
