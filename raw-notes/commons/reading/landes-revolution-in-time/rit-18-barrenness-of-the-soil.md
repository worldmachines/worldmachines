# RIT 18 — Notwithstanding the Barrenness of the Soil (Ch. 16)

This chapter answers a paradox: how did the Swiss Jura — three thousand feet up, snowbound from October to May, soil so poor its main "crop" was cattle bought lean and sold fat — become the world's watchmaking heartland? Landes's answer is environmental-institutional, not heroic. The hostile setting itself manufactured the preconditions: stockmen's treks to distant fairs (selling to butchers from Basel, Strasbourg, even Paris) were "a school for commerce"; winter closure made every mountain peasant a forge-owning jack-of-all-trades — "smith, carpenter, cabinetmaker, basketmaker, cooper, leather worker, mason, tinker, tinkerer and inventor"; and export-oriented home industry (lacemaking, sold outside because the Jura could not afford lace) habituated whole families to wage work at the bench. When watchmaking arrived in the early eighteenth century "it swept all else aside" — many farmers rented their land to Bernese outsiders and went full-time horological. The chapter then tracks how this skill pool, organized through *établissage* and mechanized rough-movement supply, undercut Geneva and Britain with a new kind of watch — thin, cylinder-escapement, going-barrel, fusee-less — and a marketing intelligence the British never matched.

## Key arguments

- **Environment bred the inputs.** Isolation + winter closure → polyvalent mechanical skill + family labour discipline + commercial contacts. Landes treats the JeanRichard legend (Daniel JeanRichard of La Sagne, 1665–1741, who repaired a horse trader's English watch as a teenager, fetched tools from Geneva, and seeded apprentices across the villages) as real but partial: Protestant refugees from France brought skills *and* the commercial networks an export trade needed; a pre-existing domestic clock trade converted; Geneva served as school, supplier, and model — while sneering at its imitators.
- **Établissage had it both ways.** Subdivided tasks let women and children do work reserved in Britain and France for trained journeymen, while the sheer size of the pool let the system spot and promote the gifted into high-skill tasks (layout, finishing, springing, adjusting): "an abundance of skilled labour at low cost (compare Japan in the 1980s)" plus a technically creative cadre (Perrelet invents the self-winding watch and teaches Breguet).
- **Standardized ébauches collapsed costs.** Frédéric Japy of Beaucourt (Montbéliard — then a Württemberg exclave pushed eastward by French embargo) bought mass-production machines from Jeanneret-Gris of Le Locle in 1776 for 600 louis d'or; by 1780 ~50 "apprentices" turning out 43,200 pieces (even halved, enormous); by 1795 a power-driven factory making 40,000 ébauches/year — ten times Voltaire's Ferney output with half the workers; ten years later 100,000/year at 2.5 francs each, a tenth or less of finished-watch value. Neuchâtel hedged its dependence with Fontainemelon (1793); fifty years on, the two supplied half a million movements.
- **A different watch, not just a cheaper one.** Dropping fusee and chain (the cylinder escapement with going barrel needed no compensation) made the watch cheaper *and* thin — the Lepine calibre (~1770: separate bridges, no full plate) as improved by Breguet defined "a modern watch" for half a century, while the British clung to full-plate, fusee'd half-pound chronometers and duplexes: precision over fashion.
- **Variety as strategy.** The Swiss industry was "a congeries of sub-branches": Vallée de Joux for complications, Le Locle for chronometers, La Brévine for chains, Couvet for tools, Fleurier for the Chinese market (centre jump seconds, identical enamel pairs). "No run was too small, no order too special" — the exact inverse of Britain's sterile calibre fragmentation in ch. 14.

## Evidence & specifics

- Growth: Neuchâtel occupational censuses — 464 watchmakers (1752) → 686 (1762) → 3,458 (1792), with a quarter of the principality's population incomers drawn by the industry. Courtelary report (1809): "at least six thousand workers... all of them from the age of eight."
- Daveau's "veritable hierarchy among the watchmaking districts": La Chaux-de-Fonds établisseurs seeded the Suze valley (Saint-Imier, Courtelary, Cortébert) — "downstream in terms of topography and upstream in terms of stage of manufacture"; the Bernois then became établisseurs in the Franches Montagnes; Le Locle colonized the Val de Travers, displacing lace.
- Division of labour: ~150 workers contributed to one watch; only perspiring hands disqualified; loupes and water lamps accommodated weak eyes.
- Prices: Jura silver watch ~20 francs average by the early 1800s vs Geneva's ~150-franc mean (c. 1815, mostly gold; Geneva ceded the low end, retreating into form watches, enamels, musical/automaton pieces, and erotic watches — animated, concealed behind panels, "more amusing than arousing").
- Marketing: US-market dials engraved with screaming eagles, West Point, Hudson steamboats vs British scenes "devoid of associations"; jewel counts advertised on cuvettes (the sixteen-jewel watch: one centre jewel for show); first movers in women's watches. Washington's 1788 commission via Gouverneur Morris confirms the fashion verdict: Romilly "of the old School... out of Fashion," L'Epine "at the Head of his Profession."
- The Caron/Beaumarchais subplot: the first flat-watch claim is Pierre-Augustin Caron's letter of 16 June 1755; Lépine married Caron's sister in 1756 — Landes doubts the "independent invention."

## Quotables

- "It was a marriage of convenience, but also of love." (on watchmaking and the Jura family economy, ~p. 277)
- "These were skills learned when young, at the family bench, and they became rooted over time in the terroir." (~p. 279)
- "The result is that by making identical pieces, they make them faster, better, and without pause; and this conduces to habits of regularity, which in turn enhance the value of time." (1818 Geneva prize essay, ~p. 281–82)
- "In short, the Swiss made watches to please the customers; the British made watches to please themselves." (~p. 290)

## WM resonances

- [[The Thicket]] — Landes uses the literal word *terroir* for the Vallée de Joux's complication-making skill: learned young at the family bench, "rooted over time in the terroir," non-portable for two centuries (Patek Philippe still goes to Piguet in Le Sentier). The Jura reads as a thicket that turned outward: seasonal snow closure as defensive border, interior space where skills compounded beyond what any low-cost rival could copy.
- [[legibility-as-machine-core]] — Japy's standard ébauche and the 1818 prize essay are a miniature of the Modernity Machine's logic: identical parts, pre-matched supplies, "another pattern of division," and — explicitly — "habits of regularity, which in turn enhance the value of time." The instrument of time discipline becomes cheap precisely by being made legible to itself.
- [[divergence-machine]] — the "congeries of sub-branches" is variety made profitable *on top of* a standardized base: independent local fabriques, each its own speciality, catering to every ontology of the watch (jewel-counting Americans, pair-buying mandarins, fashion-bound Parisians). Where British calibre fragmentation (ch. 14) was variety that blunted scale, the Jura stacked proliferation on legibility and won both games at once.
