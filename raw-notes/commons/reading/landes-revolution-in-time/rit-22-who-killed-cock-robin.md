# RIT 22 — "Who Killed Cock Robin?" (ch. 20, pp. 347–364)

The Cock Robin of the title is the American factory watch industry, and the implicit verdict matches the rhyme: the killer confesses from inside the house. After the 1876 Philadelphia shock, the Swiss neither panicked nor converted wholesale — they audited the American claim, found "absolute interchangeability" largely bluff (Waltham ducked a five-watch swap test at the 1878 Paris Exposition), and mechanized *selectively*, keeping hand finishing, outwork, and variety. Two generations later they led even in mechanical innovation, held over half of world output 1890–1913 (briefly >80% after WWII), and survived the interwar crash via one of history's tightest cartels. The American pioneers were gutted from within: Waltham fell to financial extraction under Frederic Dumaine; marketing replaced technique; tariffs invited smuggling. Cock Robin bled out at the top of the S-curve.

## Key arguments

- **Interchangeability was a retrospective myth.** Random-assembly interchangeability arrived decades late — first in turn-of-the-century dollar watches (too cheap to hand-finish; quality control shifted to the customer), in jewelled watches only in the 1930s (ex-Elgin head watchmaker D. W. Leverenz), once machines hit 1/100,000-inch tolerances. The American advance was real (parts orderable by number, fitted "with a few retouches"); the absolute claim was advertising.
- **The Swiss strategy: hybrid production, dispersed structure.** Smaller factories, new and old methods side by side, outworkers, subcontracting — versatile adjustable machines run by skilled labour vs. single-purpose American machinery. In 1901 the average Swiss firm had 38 employees vs. ~529 per US enterprise (thirteen US manufacturers in all); firm multiplicity was judged the best insurance against missing new techniques.
- **Mechanization as labour discipline.** For the *établisseur*, the machine's chief virtue was curbing the "workshop aristocrats" who dictated the rhythm of work — Le Coultre: output had depended on "the acquiescence of a few workers."
- **Watch consumption is modernization's best gauge.** Better than energy-per-capita, Landes argues: it "quantif[ies] the imponderable" — the inculcated values that make the industrial system go. Demand grew fastest where development was steepest (Austria-Hungary +80% 1900–11; Russia, Japan, Argentina fastest of all).
- **Cartelization as crisis response.** Unlike the competitive 1870s response: FH (1924), Ebauches SA (1926), UBAH (1927), collective agreements from 1928 against *chablonnage*, the state super-holding ASUAG (1931), and a 1934 federal statute giving cartel rules force of law — export permits, licensed machinery sales, federal approval to expand, move, or sell.
- **Financial capitalism killed Waltham.** Dumaine (1923–43) made black ink by starving the firm: ~$65,000/year on equipment (1.5% of book value), fixed assets written down $4.3M→$1.2M, over half of $17.2M cumulative cash spent buying up shares — sold out in 1944 as wartime orders ended. The Amoskeag template — hoard reserves, refuse reinvestment, drain cash into a holding company — drew a 1937 Senate verdict of "nothing short of financial sabotage."
- **Marketing beat manufacturing at the S-curve's top.** With process gains levelling off, product innovation ruled — the Swiss (small runs, designers, horology schools, Rolex/Omega wristwatch chronometry, thinness-as-chic) outran American makers, while American *assemblers* (Bulova, Benrus, Gruen) cased Swiss movements and outspent all American manufacturers combined on radio advertising.

## Evidence & specifics

- Swiss labour transition: 1870 — ~5,000 of 40,000 in shops of ten-plus, 35,000 at home; by 1905 — 38,000 in larger units, 12,000 at home. Exports 4.8M units (1890) → 16.8M (1913), >95% sold abroad; then 18M (1916) → 8.4M (1921) → 14.4M (1923).
- Deceptive Swiss copies for the US market — Wm Ellery → "Wm Elley," Waltham → "Walham" — banned by the Dingley tariff (1898). Yet in 1900 the US was only the tenth-largest Swiss market; Germany bought ten times as much.
- Waltham's decay: workers on cut piece-rates did just enough to take home the same pay (like eighteenth-century cottage weavers, Landes notes); pilferage, fief-like shops, 4,000 employed where consultants said 2,000 would do. Elgin, by contrast, sold 18M watches 1918–40 (Waltham: 8.3M) and ran the Elgin Watchmakers College (1920–60, 6,000+ graduates).
- Smuggling: legal Swiss imports nearly quadrupled 1934–40 despite duties; a ship's baker carried 780 movements in a belly-belt; Dumaine estimated 1–2M smuggled movements/year; informer-reward auctions of seized goods undercut US makers — a Catch-22. Swiss advice: lower the tariff, as Geneva told the French 150 years before.
