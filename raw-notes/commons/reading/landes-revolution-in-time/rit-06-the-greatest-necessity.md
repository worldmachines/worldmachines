# RIT 06 — "The Greatest Necessity for Every Rank of Men" (Ch. 4)

The chapter title comes from Galvano Fiamma, chronicler of Milan, praising a restored striking clock in 1333 as *summe necessarium pro omni statu hominum*. Landes's argument: the mechanical clock was born inside monastic bell-discipline ("seen ontologically and functionally, these timekeeping machines began as automated bells") but its destiny lay with the medieval city. Urban density multiplied time signals; multiple discordant bells made the hour disputable; textile capitalism made the working day a contested wage boundary. The mechanical clock won because it supplied *verifiable, equal-interval* public time — and in doing so it dragged Europe from the Church's variable temporal hours to equal hours, taught urban populations to count, and transferred the rhythm of life from clergy to bourgeoisie. The surprise Landes insists on: workers did not hate the clock. They hated arbitrary bells controlled by employers; the chiming tower clock and dial, by limiting abuse, were *welcomed* by the people they would eventually discipline.

## Key arguments

- **Monastic origins.** Benedict ("Idleness is an enemy of the soul"), *laborare est orare*. Early monastic horologia were night timers (*horologia nocturna/excitatoria*) that tripped an alarm to wake the sacristan, not continuous clocks; their weight-driven hammer mechanism was probably the forerunner of the verge-and-foliot escapement. Monastic bell-time was deliberately de-individuating: "only one time, that of the group… excluding individual and autonomous time."
- **Cistercian economics as stimulus.** The 11th–12th-century orders ran Europe's most advanced agriculture, mills, and mines, with hired labor and water power; their need for organizational timekeeping may have precipitated the invention of the mechanical clock itself. Robertus Anglicus's *artifices horologiorum* implies an established craft and market already existed.
- **Signal multiplication breaks single-source time.** Landes's elegant principle: one time source needn't be accurate — the hour is what it says. Multiply bells (work start, meals, gates, markets, curfew…) and the hour becomes a dispute. The erratic clepsydra could only aggravate conflict; hence the urban search for the mechanical clock.
- **The two-edged sword.** Work bells in textile towns provoked resentment and revolt — but eliminating them was impossible once the workday was defined temporally rather than naturally, because workers also needed the boundary marked. The tower clock's regular strike and public dial *limited employer abuse* by making time verifiable by all parties.
- **Equal hours as unintended revolution.** Public turret clocks, being automated bells with no practicable variable detent, perforce struck equal hours (unlike Japan's later movable-numeral chamber clocks). Equal units made time arithmetic possible (how long until? how long since?) and schooled an innumerate population. The Church kept temporal hours and lost the rhythm of life to lay authority: "the monks had wrought too well."
- **Clock as totem.** Show clocks with automata were the secular successors to cathedrals — "a miniaturization of heaven and earth," prestige objects for Europe's uniquely autonomous chartered communes (nothing comparable in Islam or East Asia, Landes notes).

## Evidence & specifics

- Etymology: *horologium* → horloge/orologio/reloj; but English *clock*, Dutch *klokke* = bell (cf. Glocke); French shifted *sein/sain* → *cloche* around the same time.
- Brussels work bells: *joufvrouwenclocke* (dawn), *werckclocke*, *drabclocke* (evening, weavers/twisters), *lesteclocke* (tapestry workers, cobblers, whitesmiths). Amiens 1335: royal grant to ring a distinct work-ordinance bell from the Belfry. Ghent 1324: abbot of Saint-Pierre authorizes fullers' bell near the Hoipoorte.
- Labor conflict: Florence's *ciompi*, Flanders' "blue nails"; putting-out workers with backward-bending labor supply (higher pay → less work). Thérouanne 1367: workers' bell silenced "forever." Post-Black Death (1347–50), bells became "tocsins of revolt"; at Commines, a 60-pound fine for ringing assembly, death for ringing revolt.
- Synchronization politics: Charles V of France decreed (1370) all Paris clocks be regulated on his palace clock — royal primacy, technically futile ("C'est l'horloge du palais; elle va comme il lui plaît"); Emperor Charles V died still trying to make his clocks chime together.
- Earliest secular time standard: Sarum, England, 1306 — no victuals sold "before the clock of the Cathedral had struck one."
- Domestic clocks were contemporaneous, not later miniaturizations (Simoni's argument); Jean de Meung's Pygmalion has chiming chamber clocks in the late 1200s; Charles V's 1380 inventory lists a silver clock made for Philip the Fair (d. 1314). Landes infers far more clocks than records show: "a handful of clocks in London and Paris? There must have been ten times that many and more by 1400."
- Reception: poets — normally hostile to technique — celebrated it: Dante's "glorious wheel" (*tin tin sonando con sì dolce nota*), Froissart's *L'horloge amoureuse* (1369); Caen bridge clock inscription, 1314.

- [[clock-as-incomplete-discipline]] — this chapter is primary-source territory: bell time was contested from the start (Thérouanne silencing, bells repurposed as tocsins of revolt), and discipline only stabilized when it became *mutually* verifiable. Landes complicates the domination reading — the clock bound the employer too.
- [[legibility-as-machine-core]] — equal hours are an early legibility apparatus at the Modernity Machine's ~1200 opening: standardized, addable units replacing variable local hours, teaching numeracy to an innumerate population, and (via Charles V's 1370 decree) tying time-standardization to central authority before the technology could deliver it.
- [[untimely]] — the putting-out workers who earned what they needed and stopped, immune to wage incentives, are exactly the surplus disciplinary time could not govern; the bells were sent into their cottages precisely because piece-rates failed to capture them.
- [[capture-resistance]] — the tocsin inversion: the very instrument of temporal capture became the signal of revolt, which is why ringing it carried the death penalty.
