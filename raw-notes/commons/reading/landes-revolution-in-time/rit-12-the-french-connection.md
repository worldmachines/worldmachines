# RIT 12 — The French Connection (Ch. 10)

Harrison proved a marine chronometer could be built; Landes opens by noting the proof "bore fruit at this point not so much in England as in France." The chapter sets the French effort inside the long Anglo-French contest for hegemony — colonial-wares trade (sugar, coffee, tea, tobacco: "Europe's 'big fix'") riding on merchant-marine productivity — and then springs a paradox: despite obvious military-commercial stakes, longitude was run as *open* science, shared across enemy lines through academies, expeditions, and publication. The French route then forks between two antagonists: Pierre Le Roy, the theorist who built the conceptual prototype of all later chronometers, and Ferdinand Berthoud, the Neuchâtel-born craftsman whose tenacity and state patronage — not his principles — made him the trunk of French chronometry. The chapter is simultaneously a priority dispute, a study of invention-by-principle versus invention-by-trial, and an institutional autopsy of why France won conceptually and lost industrially.

## Key arguments

- **Openness beat secrecy by design.** Today longitude would be a state secret; then, it was framed as "intellectual and humanitarian concern transcending national interests." Priority and national honour were fiercely contested, "but such prizes are not the rewards of secrecy" — the British actively showed Harrison's machines to French commissioners because the competition's whole point was diffusion: "The whole world was supposed to know how to find the longitude."
- **The expedition was science's new entrepreneurial vehicle** — a military logistics technique repurposed by the Paris and London academies for multi-disciplinary harvesting (geodesy, hydrography, cartography, flora/fauna, ethnography), with political empire-scouting (Bering, Cook) as incidental argument for funding.
- **Technology transfer fails where precision lives in execution, not design.** Berthoud learned H.4's layout from Mudge, yet reported that the watch's precision rested "less on the principles of its design than on extremely exact execution" — pierced-ruby bearings couldn't even be made in France. He copied only Harrison's bimetallic grill and remontoir; the diamond-pallet verge was uncopyable "not even in England."
- **Le Roy invented the concept, not the artifact.** His 1748 spring-loaded detent was the first true detached escapement for a balance timekeeper; his 1766 sea clock combined a detached escapement, temperature compensation by adjusting the balance's moment of inertia, and an isochronized balance spring — Gould's three devices "found in every chronometer made today." But Landes corrects the canon: the 1766 escapement was actually a fast *lever*, not a spring detent (the Conservatoire catalogue calls it "a sort of anchor"), so the clock was the chronometer's inspiration, not its prototype — Gould and the Musée de la Technique label notwithstanding.
- **Reproducibility was the real solution to longitude.** Le Roy saw that one accurate watch wasn't enough; supply had to be assured or the invention "would serve only a small number of Navigators lucky enough to have one." His lucid published memoirs let a London *mechanic* (not a watchmaker, per John Bevis) build a working copy — a since-lost "missing link" to the English chronometer.
- **Institutions, not principles, set the lineage.** Berthoud worked by trial and error (his No. 3 went dead-beat → double virgule → pivoted detent), but his appointment as Horloger-Mécanicien de Sa Majesté et de la Marine and his erasure of Le Roy from the 1773 *Traité des horloges marines* meant French chronometry "went through his clocks and not those of Le Roy." The chosen-purveyor model then froze innovation: with Le Roy gone, no emulation, no multiplication of makers — uncle and nephew produced ~230 exquisite pieces total, and "French chronometers remained rare and unconscionably expensive."

## Evidence & specifics

- 1763, after the Treaty of Paris: France sends Camus and Berthoud to join Lalande in London; James Short (Board of Longitude) gets them in to see Harrison's first three machines; Harrison, still unpaid the full prize, withholds H.4. Berthoud later learns its workings from Thomas Mudge — Harrison "exploded," though no one had told Mudge it was secret.
- Berthoud's report of 14 March 1766 to the Navy minister: design simple, execution "very difficult"; proposes buying Harrison's full disclosure (plus his "completely unknown" astronomical pendulum clock) for a sum he calls modest — the minister declines.
- Backstory of expedition science: Jean Richer at Cayenne, 1672–3 — seconds pendulum 990 mm vs 994 mm at Paris; Huygens (with Newton's gravitation) infers the oblate earth; Cassini resists; two further royal geodetic expeditions confirm "the outrageous notion."
- Le Roy trials: 1767 Académie prize lapses untested; the marquis de Courtanvaux builds the *Aurore* as a floating laboratory — Pingré supervises three months in the North Sea, one clock "effectively isochronous," the better accurate within two minutes of longitude. 1769: doubled prize; frigate *Enjouée* runs France–Newfoundland–St Lawrence–west Africa; young Cassini awards the double prize as "encouragement to further research." French juries more generous than the British, but stakes in thousands of livres, not pounds.
- Later supersessions: Earnshaw's laminated brass-steel circle replaces Le Roy's alcohol-mercury compensation balance; Arnold's helical spring (mid-1770s) replaces his spiral pair; the lever's "civilian" future derives from Mudge, not Le Roy.
- The feud: Le Roy — "I do not like to follow in other people's footsteps"; mocks Berthoud's reversals (the free escapement's "promptness of its effects frightens the imagination"). Berthoud's stipend: 3,000 livres/year (a thousand days' skilled Paris pay, ~$100,000–150,000 today) plus 1,500 livres pension for the *Traité*. Posterity's verdict — Gould's "a talented plodder," the Institut doggerel emptying the hall — is, Landes insists, unfair: Berthoud's No. 9 of 1771 (ending as pivoted detent) was the turning point, after which he stayed with detents, "purifying as he went."

## Quotables

- "The search for the longitude was perceived from the beginning as a project of intellectual and humanitarian concern transcending national interests and boundaries." (~p. 168)
- "On the joint evidence of machine and memoir, I regard Le Roy as the greatest horological genius who ever lived." (Gould, quoted ~p. 174)
- "Nothing can be too simple in a machine for sea purposes, and to be transported into all places and climates." (Le Roy, *Exposé succint*, ~p. 175)
- "What was needed at that point was some emulation, a multiplication of sources of innovation and improvement, especially in the direction of simplicity and reproducibility." (~p. 179)

## WM resonances

- [[legibility-as-machine-core]] — the longitude project is the Modernity Machine gridding the ocean: expeditions bundle geodetic, hydrographic, cartographic, and ethnographic surveys into one legibility-harvesting vehicle, and the enterprise is deliberately *anti*-secret because a coordinate grid only works if "the whole world" can read it. Le Roy's reproducibility doctrine is the same logic at the artifact level: the masterpiece (Harrison's "almost inimitable" H.4) cannot scale; only a design executable "by the generality of such" workmen can.
- [[divergence-machine]] — Landes's counterfactual is explicitly about proliferation: France needed "a multiplication of sources of innovation," but the state's chosen-purveyor monopoly (an *horloger de la Marine* until the mid-1800s) collapsed the field to one lineage and ~230 hand-finished pieces. A pull-style single attractor strangled the push dynamics of many independent makers — the structural reason France lost the chronometer to England's competitive trade.
