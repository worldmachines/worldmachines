# RIT 05 — Are You Sleeping, Brother John? (ch. 3)

This chapter answers the demand-side question the previous chapters set up: who in medieval Europe *wanted* time measured badly enough to invent a machine for it? Landes's answer is the Western Church, specifically monasticism. He first clears away two rival origin stories — the legend that Gerbert (Pope Sylvester II, 999–1003) built the first mechanical clock, and Derek de Solla Price's thesis that the clock was an unintended spin-off of astronomical automata — then builds his positive case: Christianity, alone among the monotheisms, tied salvation to prayer at *set clock points* rather than natural bands, performed collectively and simultaneously, including in the dead of night. That regime created both the sensibility (time as precious, punctuality as duty) and the concrete engineering problem (waking reliably for matins) from which the mechanical escapement emerged.

## Key arguments

- **Against Price's "fallen angel" thesis.** Price held that the Wallingford and Dondi astronomical machines were the first mechanical clocks and that simple time-tellers were a later "degeneration." Landes counters on logic (technology runs simple → complex, a rule Price himself states) and evidence: by Wallingford and Dondi the mechanical clock was "in its third or fourth generation — at least." Dondi explicitly dismisses the common clock as too well known to describe. Causality runs the other way: the weight-driven clock *enabled* the automated astrarium, and eventually precision astronomy itself.
- **The central thesis, stated flat:** the clock did not create an interest in time measurement; the interest in time measurement led to the invention of the clock. Demand pulled the invention.
- **Comparative religious time-discipline.** Judaism (three daily prayers) and Islam (five) set prayer times as *bands* cued by nature — rooster, three stars, blue vs. black sky — requiring no timepiece; prayer is personal, not necessarily simultaneous. Western monastic Christianity set *points* (Tertullian's third, sixth, ninth hours, early 3rd c.), a "first step towards a liturgy independent of the natural cycle."
- **Simultaneity as theology.** Offices were collective because simultaneity was thought to enhance the force of prayer — chanting aloud "compelled simultaneity"; the whole was greater than the sum of the parts. Punctuality was therefore a salvific obligation, not a convenience.
- **The night office as forcing function.** Vigils/matins after sleep imposed "a new and special kind of temporal servitude": someone had to wake the house reliably. With Rome's urban night watch gone, "the medieval Church would learn to make alarm mechanisms" — the alarm, not the dial, is the technological hinge.
- **Temporal sensibility preceded the instrument.** John of Salisbury (early 12th c.) treats time's preciousness as axiomatic, needing no justification — evidence against the instrument-historians' claim that the clock created time-consciousness.

## Evidence & specifics

- One word, *horologium*, covered every timekeeper, making 13th-c. references ambiguous. First unmistakable mechanical clocks: Roger Stoke's Norwich Cathedral tower clock (1321–25), Richard of Wallingford's St Albans astronomical mechanism (begun c. 1330, thirty years to build), Dondi's astrarium (completed 1364) — the latter two documented well enough for modern working replicas.
- Indirect evidence for earlier mechanical clocks: a sudden spate of late-13th-c. account-book entries (repairs, paid keepers, the new profession of *horologeur* — C. F. C. Beeson's argument); tower siting incompatible with clepsydras (freezing, hauling water) and zero mention of water, leaks, or rust in the records; Dante's clock-train simile in *Paradiso* 24 (1316–21). No early clock survives because brass and iron were recycled as trade "perks."
- Le Goff on why demand didn't come from the 90% on the land: labor time was "free of haste, careless of exactitude, unconcerned by productivity."
- Institutional lineage: Pachomius (Upper Egypt, early 4th c.) institutes the first minutely regulated communal office; Rule of St Benedict (c. 530) fixes seven day offices plus night vigils — hence "canonical hours"; Cluny (founded 910); after the 11th-c. Benedictine malaise (Southern), the Cistercians impose uniformity via "a single line of command... no escape from the ubiquitous eye of superiors."
- Time was the *only* medieval science that advanced: Isidore's De Temporibus (615) vs. Bede's De Temporum Ratione (725); computus centers at Sankt Gallen and Auxerre; Easter calculation so hard even Gauss couldn't fully algorithmize it. A 10th-c. table divides hours into *puncta* (5/hour) and *ostenta* (12/punct ≈ 1 minute) — finer than any clock of the day could measure.
- Internalization shown by anxiety dreams: Raoul Glaber's demon urging sleep-in; Peter the Venerable's Brother Alger, woken by the devil at the *wrong* time so he'd sleep through the real bell; the Villers Abbey alarm-setting instruction (1267–68); Frère Jacques as the fear of missing matins, fossilized in a children's song.
