# RIT 10 — Approaching the Asymptote (ch. 8)

The chapter's governing image is the asymptote: once watches were within a few minutes a day, the longitude prize demanded a variance a twentieth of that or less, and "the last gains are the hardest." Landes organizes the eighteenth-century pursuit of precision around the two error sources that now dominated — temperature and friction — and narrates a three-front attack on friction (gear mesh, pivot holes, escapement) in which marginal, almost invisible improvements were wrung out by the best minds of the scientific revolution working beside (and sometimes behind) craftsmen. The payoff: dead-beat and detached escapements that improved accuracy by one to two orders of magnitude and "shaped the character of precision timekeeping for the next two centuries."

## Key arguments

- **Technological improvement is asymptotic.** Remaining errors were subtle, slow to show, weak, and expensive to fix relative to the gain — yet the marine chronometer was "an irresistible lure" that pulled the profession's best talent onto one problem.
- **Theory lagged craft on temperature.** Scientists resisted the very fact of thermal effects on solids: Huygens cited Wendelin's winter/summer pendulum observation in *Horologium* only to refute it, and as late as 1690 contested Papin on rod expansion — backed by observers whose brass rules, carried to the tropics, naturally "measured" unchanged brass pendulums. Craftsmen "unhampered by theory knew better" (cannon founders had shrunk white-hot hoops onto barrels for centuries); compensation still took fifty-plus years to engineer.
- **Friction was attacked at three points** — mesh (epicycloid tooth profiles, wheel-cutting engines), pivots (jewelled bearings), escapement (eliminating recoil) — and the residue, lubrication, remained "the bane of horology": Breguet quipped that the perfect oil would yield the perfect watch.
- **Precision tooling seeded industrialization.** Wheel-cutting and fusee engines enabled less-skilled labor, lower costs, wider markets, finer division of labor — they "helped turn an art into an industry and provided the models for eventual mechanization and interchangeable parts."
- **The English jewelling monopoly was demand-side, not secrecy.** Landes rejects the secret-keeping explanation: continental makers skipped rubies for a century because they had bigger errors to chase and "were less concerned for precision than the British."

## Evidence & specifics

- **Temperature compensation:** Harrison's gridiron pendulum (1720s), Graham's mercury pendulum (1726), Ellicott's self-adjusting bob (1730s); compensation curbs on the hairspring (Harrison 1730s; Berthoud and Arnold 1760s); self-compensating balances (Pierre Le Roy 1765; Arnold 1773; Earnshaw 1784). Another hundred years before such refinements reached good civilian pocket watches.
- **Gear theory:** epicycloid profile established by Desargues (1640s manuscript), Roemer (1674 paper to the French Academy — credited by Leibniz and Huygens, unmentioned by La Hire), La Hire's *Traité des épicycloïdes* (1694); Hooke's wheel-cutting engine c. 1672; Thiout's 1741 treatise shows the engines standard in Paris.
- **Jewelled bearings:** Tompion's 1675 Greenwich clock choked on oil-swarf and stopped by November (Flamsteed's diagnosis). Nicolas Facio — Mont Blanc measurer, Royal Society at 24, Protestant refugee of the Revocation — pierced rubies with the Debaufre brothers; their 1704 patent was granted, then broken by the Clockmakers' Company, which brandished an Ignatius Huggeford watch whose "ruby" bearing proved, 250 years later, mere decoration. Britain then held the jewelling monopoly ~100 years; Breguet broke it in the 1790s, buying pre-drilled stones from Arnold and luring an English jewel-maker to Paris "at great danger and expense" (artisan emigration was illegal).
- **The four escapements:** Graham's dead-beat (1715) plus compensated pendulum: a fraction of a second a day, the observatory standard for 200 years. Graham's cylinder (1727–8): British finest for fifty years, then abandoned; the French, after opposing it as "a matter almost of national honour," made tens of millions in the nineteenth century as the cheap staple. Mudge ("Beethoven among watchmakers") built the lever — Queen Charlotte's watch, c. 1769, under a second a day — then made only one more; via Litherland's rack lever (1791) and Massey's detachment (c. 1814) it conquered British manufacture by the 1820s and ultimately everything down to the Roskopf "montre du prolétaire" (1866), the Ingersoll dollar watch, and the Timex. Le Roy's detent (1748), perfected in simpler forms, gave a second a day or better and equipped marine chronometers from the 1780s until quartz.

## Quotables

- "The curve of technological improvement is typically asymptotic, approaching ever closer to some limit, but by ever smaller increments." (ch. 8 opening, ~p. 143)
- "No instrument is so unforgiving of even small error as a timekeeper: seconds add up to minutes, and minutes are too much." (~p. 144)
- "Give me the perfect oil, the great Breguet quipped, and I'll give you the perfect watch." (~p. 149)
- "It is hard to think of any mechanical device in the annals of modern technology that has reigned supreme for so long." (on the dead-beat-plus-pendulum regulator, ~p. 151)

## WM resonances

- The asymptotic chase is the Modernity Machine's *pull* dynamic in miniature: one shared attractor (accuracy for longitude) bends the whole profession's "best minds and hands" onto a single metric, and the tooling it spawns — engines, de-skilling, interchangeable parts — propagates legibility's logic from the instrument into its own production ([[legibility-as-machine-core]]). Notably the pull was uneven: continental makers, "less concerned for precision," resisted for decades — the attractor had to win adherents, not just exist.
- Lubrication is the chapter's fugitive remainder: the instrument that disciplines the world cannot govern its own oil — organic, seasonal, dust-gathering, a matter of private recipes that manuals ignored, the timepiece "choking in its own waste." At the material register this mirrors the clock's social incompleteness: discipline secretes what it cannot capture ([[clock-as-incomplete-discipline]], [[untimely]]).
