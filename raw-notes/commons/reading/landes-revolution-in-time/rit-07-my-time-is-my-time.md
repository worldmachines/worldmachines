# RIT 07 — My Time Is My Time (Ch. 5)

Chapter 5 is the pivot of Landes's social argument: the moment timekeeping moves from the tower into the pocket. The technical story (brass wheels, the coiled mainspring, the fusee equalizer) enables a cultural one — the *privatization* of time. Where the public bell signaled moments to a regulated community, the domestic clock and especially the watch became an "ever-visible, ever-audible companion and monitor," converting time-discipline from something imposed into something self-imposed. Landes locates the demand side in late-medieval anxiety (Black Death, urban disorder, memento mori piety) and traces the supply side's migration from Catholic Italy to Protestant northern Europe — a chronometric variable he notes Max Weber never spotted.

## Key arguments

- **Miniaturization was the revolutionary step, not complication.** Spring drive plus fusee (force-equalizing conical pulley) made the movable clock and then the watch possible; the watch was less an invention than a "silent transition" occurring in several centres in the last quarter of the 15th century — Landes sides with Italian historians against the Peter Henlein/Nuremberg myth.
- **Proliferation produced a market feedback loop for accuracy.** Inaccurate timepieces multiplied → owners compared → comparison entailed emulation → consumer preference rewarded the best makers. Accuracy improved through market selection, not decree.
- **Privatization of time stimulated individualism.** The minute hand is "a measure of time used, time spent, time wasted, time lost" — prod and key to personal achievement. The watch enabled "self-imposed programmes" beyond what public bells could police, creating "partisans and militants of a new punctuality."
- **Time-thrift was first a salvation technology, then a profit one.** Clergy translated monastic regimen into lay spiritual discipline (à Kempis, Teresa of Avila); merchants ran the same calculus for gain (Datini, Alberti). What's saved for the sacred is as easily saved for the profane.
- **Weber extension:** "what the clock was to the cloistered ascetics of the Middle Ages, the watch was to the in-the-world ascetics of post-Reformation Europe." Watch production and watch-wearing were both disproportionately Protestant.
- **Institutional synchronization lagged personal ownership.** Armies and transport could only use shared time once cheap watches diffused privately; bureaucracies barely issued them even in WWII.

## Evidence & specifics

- Turret clocks: bloomery iron, driving weights up to 1,000 lb; chamber clocks needed brass. Spring-driven clocks appear early 15th c.; earliest recorded fusee clock is the "Burgundy clock" of Philip the Good, 1430.
- Miniature prestige watches: dagger-hilt watches bought by Francis I (1518); Elizabeth I's finger-ring watch with a scratching alarm prong.
- Watches with built-in sundial and compass for setting by the sun; John Suckling's *Aglaura* epilogue (1637) uses watch-setting as a figure for private judgment.
- Anxiety context: Bouwsma's "Anxiety and the Formation of Early Modern Culture"; Black Death (1347–50) killed a quarter to a third of Europe; skull-form memento mori watches.
- Time-thrift voices: Datini of Prato's wife ("when you waste an hour, it seems to me a thousand"); Thomas à Kempis, *Imitation of Christ* (1417–21); Teresa of Avila; Petrarch to Charles IV — time is the one possession that "can justify avarice"; Alberti (1433) budgeting each day's tasks by hour.
- Schedules: All Souls, Oxford, first college to stipulate hours in its rules (1443); the word "watch" = something to pay attention to.
- Protestant horology: Augsburg 1600–1700, 165 of 189 master clockmakers of known affiliation (87.3%) Protestant; Calvin's acceptance of the watch let Geneva's jewellery trade reconvert; Revocation of the Edict of Nantes (1685) expelled 200,000 réformés, devastating French watchmaking and seeding the Swiss mountain industry.
- Coordination before standard time: Augsburg courier rules timed Venice mail to the hour; Basel started its day at noon but called it one o'clock; "Italian" 24-hour vs "German"/"French" 12-hour reckonings forced travellers to carry conversion tables. Railways forced time zones (19th c.); international agreement made them global by the early 20th.
- War: battalion size (500–600 men) set by the range of voice and sight; the marchese of Mantua begging a replacement clock on campaign (1479); watchmaker Henry Sully taken on the 1708 Flanders campaign; Waltham's cheap-model sales to Civil War soldiers turned "red ink into black"; Roskopf's 20-franc watch (late 1860s) ordered 240-at-a-time for the Russian 23rd Infantry; WWII US issued watches only to pilots, navigators, and selected officers — everyone else bought their own.

## Quotables

- "Privatization (personalization) of time was a major stimulus to the individualism that was an ever more salient aspect of Western civilization." (ch. 5, ~p. 92)
- "A turning hand, specifically a minute hand... is a measure of time used, time spent, time wasted, time lost. As such it was prod and key to personal achievement and productivity." (~pp. 92–93)
- "What the clock was to the cloistered ascetics of the Middle Ages, the watch was to the in-the-world ascetics of post-Reformation Europe." (~p. 96)
- On the GI's radium-dial watch, most valuable at night: "it set off the time that was his from the time that was his employer's." (~p. 101)

## WM resonances

- [[clock-as-incomplete-discipline]] — the chapter's closing image is exact: the soldier's watch glows in the dark not to enforce reveille but to certify the hour or two still *his*; Landes generalizes it to the medieval worker, whose clock "set off the time that was his from the time that was his employer's." The disciplinary instrument itself produces and marks the private remainder — a textbook case of the [[untimely]] surplus secreted by disciplinary time.
- [[legibility-as-machine-core]] — the babel of local hours (Basel's noon-as-one, Italian vs German hours, conversion tables) shows time *before* legibility; couriers' timetables, railway time zones, and the global standard are the Modernity Machine's pull toward a single readable grid. Notably, Landes shows the grid arriving last: personal time-discipline preceded standardized time by centuries.
