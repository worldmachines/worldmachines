# Sheep Devouring Men: Hythloday's Case Against Hanging Thieves

At a dinner hosted by Cardinal John Morton, Archbishop of Canterbury, the narrator answers an English lawyer's boast that thieves are "hanged so fast that there were sometimes twenty on one gibbet" by arguing that capital punishment for theft is both unjust and futile, then builds outward into a causal indictment of the conditions that manufacture thieves in the first place: noblemen's idle armed retainers, turned out to starve when a lord dies or falls sick; France's standing armies, kept "in pay" for the same reason noblemen keep retainers; and, at greatest length, the conversion of arable land to sheep pasture, which evicts entire villages and leaves the dispossessed no option but theft, begging, or death. The exchange closes on an aphorism that reframes the whole penal question: the state manufactures its own criminals and then hangs them for the crime it created.

## Themes

**Severity without remedy is neither just nor effective.** Hythloday's opening move against the lawyer is structural, not sentimental: he does not deny that theft should be discouraged, but insists that "no punishment, how severe soever," can restrain someone "who can find out no other way of livelihood." Punishing an effect while leaving its cause untouched is bad craft as much as bad ethics: Hythloday compares English justice to "ill masters, that are readier to chastise their scholars than to teach them."

**Idle retainers: the nobility's disposable soldiers.** Noblemen keep "a great number of idle fellows" for status and as a private reserve of martial force, then discard them "as soon as either their lord dies, or they themselves fall sick." Bred to walk about "with his sword and buckler, despising all the neighbourhood with an insolent scorn" and unfit for "the spade and mattock," these men have no path back into productive life — the same households that produce them make it impossible for them to become anything but thieves.

**France as a cautionary parallel: standing armies as idle retainers at national scale.** Hythloday widens the frame from private households to the state itself: France keeps soldiers "in pay" in peacetime on the same reasoning nobles use for retainers, quoting Sallust on keeping hands "in use, that they may not grow dull by too long an intermission." He points to Rome, Carthage, and Syria as civilizations "overturned and quite ruined by those standing armies" — a permanently mobilized capacity for violence is corrosive even when, especially when, it is not at war.

**Enclosure: the machine that turns sheep into man-eaters.** The unit's longest and most famous passage indicts the wool trade directly: sheep "which are naturally mild"... "may be said now to devour men and unpeople, not only villages, but towns." Landlords enclose common and tenanted land for pasture, evicting "the owners, as well as tenants"... "by trick or by main force," leaving families with nothing to sell and nowhere to go, criminalized first as vagabonds and then as thieves. This is a textbook instance of what WMP calls [[legibility-as-machine-core]]: enclosure converts customary, illegible common tenure into privatized, measurable, monopolizable property — legible to rent and to market, illegible to the people who lived on it. The existing corpus already reads *Utopia* as staged at exactly this hinge, within the [[modernity-machine]]'s construction era — this unit is the diagnosis of the sick, newly-enclosed world that Book 2's imagined alternative answers. The evicted, who "would willingly work but can find none that will hire them," are people whose only remaining assertion of [[Conatus]] — their bare drive to persist — is the crime the law then hangs them for.

**The closing indictment: manufactured crime, then punished.** Hythloday's peroration collapses the whole argument into a single charge: give people no lawful way to live, corrupt their upbringing through want, "and then punish them for those crimes to which their first education disposed them" — "what else is to be concluded from this but that you first make thieves and then punish them?" It is less a policy proposal than an accusation of civilizational bad faith.

## Notable passages

**The lawyer's boast and the rebuttal (c1).** The exchange opens the whole unit: the lawyer marvels that so many are hanged and so many still steal; Hythloday answers that this is no mystery once you see that "the severity was too great" while "the remedy was not effectual" — a proportionality argument aimed as much at the law's competence as its cruelty.

**Idle retainers turned to thieves (c2).** The portrait of the discarded retainer — proud, unemployable, and starving — is Hythloday's first concrete mechanism for how English institutions manufacture the crime they punish.

**France's standing armies (c4).** A comparative aside that generalizes the retainer argument to statecraft: keeping violence permanently on payroll, whether in a nobleman's hall or a kingdom's muster rolls, produces the very instability it claims to guard against.

**Sheep devour men (c3).** The unit's centerpiece. Enclosure for wool empties villages, "reserving only the churches," and turns the best inhabited places into solitudes — economic rationalization at the landlord's scale converted directly into vagrancy and hunger at the tenant's.

**You first make thieves and then punish them (c5).** The closing line is the unit's thesis compressed to a single sentence, and the one most likely to be cited on its own: the entire preceding catalogue of causes — idle retainers, standing armies, enclosure, luxury and gaming — exists to earn this indictment.
