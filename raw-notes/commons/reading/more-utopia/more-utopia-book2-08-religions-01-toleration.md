# The Religions of Utopia: Natural Theology, Christian Reception, and the Law of Toleration

Hythloday surveys the archipelago of local Utopian religions — sun-worship, moon-worship, planet-worship, and the veneration of dead heroes as gods — set against the "greater and wiser sort" who worship a single eternal, infinite, invisible Deity they call Mithras and credit with governing the beginning, increase, and end of all things; from there the account moves to how the travelers' report of Christ's life, martyrdoms, and doctrine won eager converts (checked only by the missing priesthood, which permits baptism but not the other sacraments), to the one law under which Utopia manages this variety — a strict separation of civil peace from religious opinion, established by the founder Utopus himself — and closes on the one belief Utopian law does police (denial of an afterlife and providence) and on funeral customs premised on the abiding presence of the dead.

## Themes

**Natural theology beneath ritual variety.** Utopia's many local cults are explicitly graded: crude sun/moon/planet worship and hero-cults sit below "the greater and wiser sort," who converge on one eternal Being "spread over the whole universe... by His power and virtue," named Mithras in the local tongue. More frames this as convergence rather than mere plurality — inferior religions are expected to "fall off" over time toward "that one religion that is the best and most in request," a process arrested only by superstitious dread that a locally-slighted god has "interposed and revenged" some unhappy accident.

**Christianity's warm but structurally incomplete reception.** The Utopians' enthusiasm for the Christian account is explained partly by its resonance with their own "community of goods" — they recognize in Christ and his followers a life already familiar to them — and partly, Hythloday allows, by "any secret inspiration of God." But because none of the four surviving travelers held priests' orders, converts receive baptism and no more; the text notes their unresolved debate over whether a priest chosen by the Utopians themselves, without papal authority, would be validly qualified — a live ecclesiological question dropped mid-thought as Hythloday's voyage ends.

**The civil/religious distinction as the load-bearing legal principle.** Only one Utopian is ever punished on a religious matter, and the text is precise about why: not for zealously preferring Christianity or condemning other rites, but for "inflaming the people to sedition." The generalizing sentence follows immediately — "this is one of their most ancient laws, that no man ought to be punished for his religion" — making explicit that Utopian law targets public disorder, never belief.

**Utopus's founding rationale: pragmatic peace fused with epistemic humility.** The law's origin is coldly strategic — Utopus conquered the island precisely because its prior inhabitants' sectarian factions "fought by themselves" instead of uniting against him — but More has him convert that lesson into a philosophical commitment: different religions "might not all come from God," coercion degrades true religion, being "choked with superstition, as corn is with briars and thorns," and truth needs nothing but "the strength of argument, and attended to with a gentle and unprejudiced mind" to prevail. This is a founder-legislator engineering a single, uniform, legible civil floor — one law, evenly enforced — expressly to host a tolerated proliferation of belief beneath it; the structural shape (a legible political container permitting non-convergent variety) rhymes with the WMP tension between [[legibility-as-machine-core]] and the [[divergence-machine]], staged here in miniature four centuries before either machine's construction.

**The one hard exception: denial of providence and the afterlife.** Utopus's tolerance stops at materialism: those who hold "that our souls died with our bodies, or that the world was governed by chance, without a wise overruling Providence" are barred from honors, office, and public trust, and forbidden to dispute their view before common people (though free to argue it privately with priests). Crucially, they are not punished, "because they lay this down as a maxim, that a man cannot make himself believe anything he pleases" — coerced profession would itself be "a sort of fraud," and fraud is "abhorred by the Utopians." The stated civic justification is functionalist rather than theological: a man who fears nothing after death "will not scruple to break through all the laws of his country... when by this means he may satisfy his appetites."

**Death, memory, and ancestor-presence as moral technology.** Utopians read a reluctant, fearful death as a bad omen of guilt and mourn it in silence; a cheerful, hopeful death is met with hymns, a pillar, and public retelling of "his good life, and worthy actions." The belief that the dead remain present and "observe all they say or do" is treated as functionally real — both an incentive (public memory of the virtuous) and "a restraint that prevents their engaging in ill designs," binding the living to conduct legible to an audience that never fully leaves.

## Notable passages

The Mithras passage stakes out the chapter's central move — plural local religions resolving into one philosophically respectable natural theology — and gives the "greater and wiser sort" formula that recurs whenever More wants to rank Utopian opinion without imposing uniformity by law.

The Christianity-reception passage is the chapter's most autobiographically loaded moment: Hythloday, one of the travelers, reports the limits of his own missionary authority (no ordained priest among them) as a plain narrative fact, not an argument, letting the sacramental gap speak for itself.

The zealot-and-sedition passage carries the chapter's legal thesis in miniature: a single case, narrated in enough procedural detail (accusation, trial, banishment) to demonstrate rather than assert that Utopian law punishes disruption, not doctrine.

Utopus's rationale passage is the philosophical keystone — the closest the Utopia text comes to a free-standing argument for toleration, run through both a pragmatic register (peace) and a truth-seeking register (argument over force).

The materialist-exception passage is the chapter's most legally intricate turn: exclusion from office is real and severe, but the text goes out of its way to distinguish exclusion from punishment, and to root even that distinction in a considered claim about the limits of coerced belief.

The funeral-customs passage closes the chapter on a different register — not law but practice — showing how a metaphysical belief (the abiding, attentive dead) does real behavioral work without any legal enforcement at all.
