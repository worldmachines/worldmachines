# Utopia's Peroration: The Conspiracy of the Rich and the Case Against Private Property

Raphael Hythloday closes his account of the Utopian commonwealth with its most polemical argument yet: Utopia is “the only commonwealth that truly deserves that name” because it has abolished private property, and every other nation's government is, in his blunt formulation, “a conspiracy of the rich.” He illustrates the claim first with the inversion of who prospers under money-based economies — idle noblemen and bankers live in splendor while ploughmen and smiths, whose labor is indispensable, die in want — then with the parable of a famine year in which the granaries of hoarding rich men held enough surplus to have saved everyone who starved. He diagnoses pride, not scarcity, as the true obstacle to reform, since pride “does not measure happiness so much by its own conveniences, as by the miseries of others.” The unit ends with the dialogue's frame reasserting itself: the narrator “More,” privately unpersuaded on several points (Utopian war-making, religion, and above all the abolition of money and the “nobility, magnificence, splendour, and majesty” it underwrites), declines to argue with a visibly weary Raphael and instead closes the book on a note of qualified admiration — wishing “rather... than hope” to see Utopian laws followed at home.

## Themes

**No property, no faction, total legibility.** Hythloday's core claim is causal: because “no man has any property,” Utopians “zealously pursue the good of the public,” whereas elsewhere “every man only seeks his own wealth” out of the sheer necessity of self-preservation. The mechanism he describes — a single communal store whose fullness every citizen can verify, distributed by need rather than earned by labor or inherited by birth — is a fantasy of total [[legibility-as-machine-core]]: nothing hoarded, nothing hidden, no private ledger competing with the public one. Written in 1516, near the start of what the World Machines framework calls the [[modernity-machine]]'s Dawn, Utopia imagines legibility pushed toward communal rather than capitalist-accounting ends — a road-not-taken variant of the same impulse that produced double-entry bookkeeping and the cadastre.

**The conspiracy of the rich.** The unit's sharpest line describes ordinary governments as “a conspiracy of the rich, who, on pretence of managing the public, only pursue their private ends” — securing their own ill-gotten gains and then using “the show of public authority” to convert private advantage into law. It is a vivid ur-description of elite capture succeeding outright — the rich seize the lawmaking apparatus itself — which sharpens by contrast what the World Machines vocabulary calls [[capture-resistance]]: Utopia's abolition of property is presented as the structural fix, since there is no private wealth left to convert into public power.

**The granary parable.** Hythloday's concrete proof against the “necessity” of scarcity is the famine year in which “many thousands have died of hunger” while the hoarded grain of rich men, properly distributed, would have prevented every death — the culprit isn't nature but “that blessed thing called money,” which he calls the thing that “obstructed” necessities from reaching people who needed them.

**Pride as the load-bearing vice.** Hythloday doesn't blame ignorance or even greed alone for the persistence of unjust systems; he blames pride, “that infernal serpent that creeps into the breasts of mortals,” which measures its own happiness only by contrast with others' misery and so has a structural interest in preserving inequality rather than abolishing it.

**The narrator pulls back.** The book's famous closing move undercuts the speech it just gave the reader in full: “More” the character finds much “absurd,” judges Raphael too weary and too invested in his own wisdom to be safely contradicted, and settles for outward praise while privately reserving judgment. The irony is structural to the whole work: the reader gets Hythloday's argument at full force and then watches the author-narrator decline to endorse it, leaving the famous closing clause — wishing rather than hoping — as More's real position.

## Notable passages

- The opening declaration that Utopia is “the only commonwealth that truly deserves that name,” and the causal argument that security from want, not virtue, is what lets its citizens set aside self-interest for the public good.
- The systematic contrast between the idle rich (“a nobleman, a goldsmith, a banker”) who live “in great luxury and splendour” and the essential laborers (“a carter, a smith, or a ploughman”) whose condition Hythloday judges worse than that of beasts.
- The “conspiracy of the rich” indictment of government as a legal machine for converting private greed into public authority.
- The granary parable, Hythloday's empirical rebuttal to the claim that scarcity — rather than money and hoarding — is what kills the poor in famine years.
- The pride passage, diagnosing why the rich would resist Utopian equality even where it would serve their own deeper interest.
- The closing frame-break, where the narrator's polite retreat from argument supplies the book's most quoted ambivalence.
