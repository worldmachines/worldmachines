# Against Private Property, For a Community of Goods

Continuing the dinner-table dialogue that frames the whole work, Raphael Hythloday delivers his fullest statement of why no nation with private property and money can be governed justly or happily; Peter Giles objects on practical grounds of incentive and civil order; Raphael answers not with counter-argument but with the authority of his own five years living among the Utopians; and after a digression on Utopia's antiquity and a chance shipwreck through which the Utopians absorbed Roman and Egyptian learning, he agrees to More's request to describe the island in full, which sets up Book 2.

## Themes

**Property and money as the structural cause of unjust, unhappy government.** Raphael states the thesis without hedging: “as long as there is any property, and while money is the standard of all other things, I cannot think that a nation can be governed either justly or happily.” Injustice follows because “the best things will fall to the share of the worst men”; unhappiness because wealth is “divided among a few... the rest being left to be absolutely miserable.” Money and property here function as the standard by which everything else in the polity gets measured and sorted — the same ordering logic this project elsewhere reads through [[legibility-as-machine-core]], the technology of making a world countable and controllable. Raphael's charge is that this particular legibility regime sorts wrongly: it makes wealth legible to law, but not desert legible to justice.

**Endless law cannot secure what property promises.** Raphael's sharper point is that even where “every one has his property,” the laws meant to protect it “have not the power either to obtain or preserve it, or even to enable men certainly to distinguish what is their own from what is another’s” — evidenced by “the many lawsuits that every day break out, and are eternally depending.” Proposed reforms (capping land and money, restraining prince and people, unpaid public office) are conceded to work only as palliative — like “good diet and care” for “a sick man whose recovery is desperate”, curing one symptom while provoking another elsewhere in the body politic. It is a striking early statement of a gap the [[modernity-machine]] would spend centuries chasing: between a legal order's claimed power to make ownership legible and its actual capacity to do so at scale.

**Property produces two classes, and is how the few capture the many's surplus.** Raphael names the split directly: “two sorts of people... the former useless, but wicked and ravenous; and the latter, who by their constant industry serve the public more than themselves, sincere and modest men.” Regardless of how plentiful a nation is, “a few dividing the wealth of it among themselves, the rest must fall into indigence.” The diagnosis sits close to what this project frames through Spinoza's [[Conatus]] and [[capture-resistance]]: a system that redirects the productive striving of the many into bare “cares and anxieties” while an unproductive few draws off the surplus.

**The counter-case: without private stakes, plenty and order both collapse.** Peter Giles voices the standard objection Raphael has been arguing past: “How can there be any plenty where every man will excuse himself from labour?... If people come to be pinched with want, and yet cannot dispose of anything as their own, what can follow upon this but perpetual sedition and bloodshed” — and how can magistrates command respect “among those that are in all things equal to one another”? More's dialogue form keeps both positions live on the page rather than resolving between them; the resolution, such as it is, is deferred to the ethnographic evidence of Book 2.

**Argument answered with witness, not counter-argument.** Rather than rebut Peter's logic point by point, Raphael shifts registers entirely — from philosophical dispute to eyewitness report: “if you had been in Utopia with me, and had seen their laws and rules, as I did, for the space of five years... you would then confess that you had never seen a people so well constituted as they.” This is the hinge the whole book turns on: abstract debate about whether common ownership *could* work is superseded by the claim that it demonstrably *does* work, somewhere Raphael has actually lived — converting the rest of *Utopia* into travel report rather than pure argument.

**Asymmetric learning: a chance shipwreck versus a wasted encounter.** The digression on Utopia's antiquity turns on a single accident: Roman and Egyptian castaways washed ashore twelve centuries ago, and the Utopians “drew the advantage of learning from those unlooked-for guests,” absorbing “all the useful arts” the castaways knew and even extending arts the castaways “could not fully explain.” Raphael contrasts this with Europe's encounter with him, which he predicts will be “forgot by our people that I was ever there,” concluding “it would be long before we should learn or put in practice any of the good institutions that are among them.” The point isn't relative cleverness — Raphael concedes “we are more ingenious than they are” — but differential capacity to metabolize contact with a better-ordered society into actual institutional change.

## Notable passages

**The thesis (c1).** The flat opening claim — property and money foreclose just or happy government — is the sentence the rest of the book exists to test, and the clearest single statement of Raphael's position in the whole dialogue.

**The two sorts of people (c2).** Raphael's account of how property splits a nation into an idle, “wicked and ravenous” few and an industrious, “sincere and modest” many is the passage that gives his abstract claim social teeth: this is capture, named plainly, four centuries before the vocabulary of capture and extraction became common.

**Peter's objection (c3).** The counter-case — that common ownership guarantees sloth, want, sedition, and the collapse of magistracy — is the strongest challenge the dialogue stages against Raphael, and it is never directly answered on the page.

**Five years among the Utopians (c4).** Raphael's pivot from argument to autobiography is the rhetorical hinge of the entire book: it is what licenses everything that follows as reportage rather than speculation.

**The shipwreck (c5).** The story of the Roman and Egyptian castaways, set against Europe's failure to learn anything from Raphael's own visit, is Raphael's own theory of why Utopia is better governed — not superior intellect, but superior capacity to absorb and institutionalize what contact teaches.

The unit closes on the frame narrative itself: dinner, a request that Raphael “be not too short” but describe Utopia's “soil, their rivers, their towns, their people, their manners, constitution, laws,” and his agreement — the transition into the systematic description that makes up Book 2.
