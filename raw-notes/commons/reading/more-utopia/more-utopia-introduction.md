# Thomas More's Life and the Making of *Utopia*

Henry Morley's editorial introduction to the Burnet translation traces Sir Thomas More's life from his 1478 birth in Milk Street through his formation in Cardinal Morton's household, his Oxford Greek studies under Grocyn and Linacre, his legal and parliamentary career, and the 1515 diplomatic embassy to Flanders that supplied Utopia's fictional frame, before turning to the book's composition, its Louvain first printing under Erasmus's editorship, its Basle revision, and the chain of English translations culminating in Gilbert Burnet's 1684 version reproduced in this volume.

## Themes

**Patronage is the ladder of Tudor advancement.** More is placed as a boy in Cardinal Morton's household, where "the youth wore his patron's livery, and added to his state"; Morton predicts his greatness at table before More moves on to Oxford's new Greek learning under Grocyn and Linacre and then to law at Lincoln's Inn.

**Early political courage rehearses the book's dissenting spirit.** More's 1503 opposition in Parliament to Henry VII's marriage-subsidy for his daughter was forceful enough that "the House refused to grant it," costing him royal favor and nearly driving him from the country — the introduction frames this as the same willingness to needle power that animates Utopia's critique.

**A real embassy supplies the fictional frame.** The 1515–16 diplomatic missions to the Low Countries with Cuthbert Tunstal, the Bruges negotiations, and the Antwerp friendship with Peter Giles are narrated as documented fact, right up to the moment the introduction says fact "slides into fiction with the finding of Raphael Hythloday."

**The book was built back-to-front.** Part II, describing the place itself ("Nusquama," or "Nowhere"), was drafted first in late 1515; the introductory Part I was added early in 1516 — the frame narrative is a later addition wrapped around an already-finished utopia.

**Printed everywhere but England.** Louvain (1516, edited by Erasmus and Giles), Basle (1518, revised by More), Paris, and Vienna all printed the Latin text, yet it "was not printed in England during More's lifetime"; English readers had to wait for Ralph Robinson's 1551 translation and then Gilbert Burnet's more skillful 1684 version, produced, the editor notes, from Burnet's own "sense of unreason in high places" after defending the executed Lord William Russell.

**Satire as protective veil.** The introduction reads Utopia's "ideal communism" as the disguise for "a noble English argument," with More displacing England onto France and inserting "ironical praise of the good faith of Christian kings" precisely so the book could not be read as an attack on Henry VIII's policy. This is the same text the World Machines project elsewhere reads as [[modernity-machine]]'s "last sincere architectural drawing" — a vision of total legibility and benevolently coordinated order sketched in the Dawn of the machine it anticipates, before that machine had actually been built.

## Notable passages

The Morton household anecdote is the introduction's clearest link between More's early service and the book itself — Morton's table talk is said to leave "recollections in 'Utopia,'" and his prophecy that the waiting-boy would "prove a notable and rare man" frames the whole biography as fulfillment.

The publication-history paragraph is the unit's densest factual passage, functioning as the source's own provenance record: composition order (Part II before Part I), the Louvain 1516 and Basle 1518 printings, the absence of any English edition in More's lifetime, and the Robinson-to-Burnet translation lineage that ends with "the translation given in this volume."

The Plato-and-Plutarch passage states outright what Utopia is doing formally: borrowing from the *Republic* and Plutarch's Sparta to carry "a noble English argument" safely disguised as fiction about a distant place, with irony toward "Christian kings" doing double duty as censorship insurance.

The etymology-and-Hythloday passage is the essay's hinge from documented fact to invented fiction — it is also, incidentally, the origin of the word "Utopian" itself as "an impracticable scheme."

Erasmus's reception closes the introduction on contemporary testimony: his wish that readers "see the true source of all political evils," and his report of an Antwerp burgomaster who "knows it all by heart," attest that More's contemporaries read the fiction as substantive argument, not mere jeu d'esprit.
