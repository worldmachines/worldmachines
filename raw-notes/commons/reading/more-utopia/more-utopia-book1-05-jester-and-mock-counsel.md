# The Cardinal's Fool and the Achorians: Court Flattery and the Case Against Conquest

Raphael Hythloday finishes his account of dinner at Cardinal Morton's table with a comic interlude — a licensed Fool baits a pompous Friar into a rage over how to provide for the sick and aged poor — then turns the anecdote into an argument: courtiers value nothing except what power has already blessed. Thomas More presses him, on the strength of Plato's philosopher-kings, to offer his counsel to real princes anyway; Raphael answers with an imagined scene at the French king's war council, culminating in the fable of the Achorians, a people who conquered a second kingdom and learned that keeping it cost more than it was worth.

## Themes

**Flattery snaps to whatever power signals, not to the merit of the idea.** The jester's proposal — herd all beggars into monasteries, men as lay-brothers, women as nuns — is greeted as a throwaway joke until the Cardinal smiles at it, at which point "the rest liked it in earnest." Raphael stages the whole scene explicitly to make this point to More: the same courtiers who scorned his own proposals about thieves and vagabonds fell over themselves praising the Fool's idea the instant it looked like the Cardinal's favorite.

**The Friar's overreaction satirizes clerical self-regard.** Baited by the Fool's jab that the "vagabonds" the Cardinal proposed restraining include Friars, the Friar escalates from wounded pride into a citation war — Scripture verses, the mockers of Elisha, a threatened papal bull of excommunication — that reveals more about clerical vanity and hair-trigger zeal than it does about the Fool's actual offense. The Cardinal has to intervene twice before shutting the exchange down and moving on to hear causes.

**Disinterested counsel cannot survive a court built on flattery.** More's plea that Raphael serve a king "for [Plato] thinks that nations will be happy when either philosophers become kings or kings become philosophers" is met with the historical caution of Dionysius: philosophers have already written the books; kings raised from childhood on false notions will not adopt their counsel unless they are philosophers themselves. The jester scene is Raphael's proof by demonstration.

**The Achorian parable is the unit's real argument: conquest is a losing trade.** Raphael imagines himself dropped into the French king's council amid a catalogue of period realpolitik — holding Milan, retaking Naples, subduing Venice and Italy, absorbing Flanders and Burgundy, buying the Swiss and the Emperor, managing England through feigned friendship and a backed pretender — and imagines proposing instead the Achorians' experience: a conquered kingdom that could never be pacified, that drained taxes and blood without benefiting the people even in peacetime, that corrupted manners into lawlessness, and that left the king too divided to govern either realm well.

**The moral: govern what you have, and leave the rest alone.** The Achorians eventually force their king to choose one kingdom over the other — "no man would willingly have a groom that should be in common between him and another" — and Raphael draws the lesson explicit: a king does better to make his own ancient kingdom flourish, live among his people and be loved by them, than to chase conquests he may be forced to abandon anyway. More's one-line concession that such a speech "would not be very well" heard closes the unit on a note of irony: the argument is unanswerable and also, by the frame's own logic, unusable at court.

## Notable passages

The Fool's mock-legislative solution to poverty — dumping beggars into monasteries as forced lay-brothers and nuns — sets up the comic register and the class/clerical satire that follows: "‘Leave that to me,’ said the Fool, ‘and I shall take care of them, for there is no sort of people whose sight I abhor more..." through "...to be made lay-brothers, and the women to be nuns.’"

The Friar's tit-for-tat escalation, trading half-apt biblical citations with the Fool until he threatens excommunication under a standing bull, is the set-piece's comic peak and its clearest jab at institutional religion's thin skin: "‘Good Friar,’ said he, ‘be not angry, for it is written..." through "...We have, likewise, a bull, by which all that jeer us are excommunicated.’"

Raphael's direct statement of why he told the story at such length — to show More exactly how courtiers behave — is the hinge that turns comedy into argument: "“Thus, Mr. More, I have run out into a tedious story, of the length of..." through "...how little courtiers would value either me or my counsels.”"

The Achorian parable itself is the unit's load-bearing political claim, cataloguing conquest's costs in taxes, bloodshed, lawlessness, and a divided king's attention, before the people force a choice between the two kingdoms: "the resolutions of the Achorians, a people that lie on the south-east of Utopia..." through "...was forced to quit his new kingdom to one of his friends (who was not long after dethroned), and to be contented with his old one."

Raphael's closing moral generalizes the fable into direct counsel — cultivate one's own kingdom, be loved by one's people, leave others alone — and More's terse concession closes the frame with irony: "To this I would add that after all those warlike attempts, the vast confusions..." through "“I confess,” said I, “I think not very well.”"
