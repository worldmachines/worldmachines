# Of Their Traffic

This unit of Book II describes the machinery underlying Utopian 'commerce' in the broadest sense — not foreign trade but the internal circulation of people, land, and goods. Hythloday walks through the patriarchal family as the base unit of production; the strict numerical caps on family and city size, rebalanced by shuffling children and citizens between households, towns, and overseas colonies; the natural-law justification for seizing land from neighbors who refuse to conform to Utopian law; and the moneyless system of central storehouses, communal dining halls (organized under the Syphogrants), and public hospitals through which goods and care are distributed without payment, exchange, or want.

## Themes

**Demography is treated as an engineering problem, not a fact of nature.** Family size is bounded between ten and sixteen persons; city size is capped at six thousand families; and both limits are actively maintained by moving children from over-full households to under-full ones, and by moving citizens between towns or out to colonies when the island's population grows past capacity. Population loss from plague is corrected symmetrically, by recalling colonists. Bodies here are a legible, adjustable quantity, continuously rebalanced to keep every administrative unit within its designed tolerance — a textbook instance of [[legibility-as-machine-core]]: the world is redrawn into countable, correctable units so that a central rule can govern it. Utopia was written and published in 1516, squarely inside the ~1200–1600 construction window the World Machines framework assigns to the [[modernity-machine]]'s Dawn — More's imagined commonwealth reads as a fully worked architectural drawing of the legibility project other Modernity Machine institutions (cadastres, parish registers, guild rolls) were only beginning to build in his own lifetime.

**Land itself is sorted into legible and illegible categories, and the sorting licenses force.** When neighboring peoples welcome Utopian colonists, 'they quickly enter into their method of life and conform to their rules,' and the arrangement is framed as mutual benefit. When natives refuse, the Utopians expel them and 'use force if they resist,' on the argument that leaving soil 'idle and uncultivated' when others could work it is itself a 'just cause of war.' The classification of land as cultivated versus wasted — not the natives' prior claim or consent — is what does the moral work; this is the same legibility operation Scott describes in state land regimes, where declaring land 'unused' is the administrative move that precedes its seizure.

**The distributive system is engineered against a specific theory of human vice.** Goods flow from family workshops to central storehouses to whichever father asks, 'without either paying for it or leaving anything in exchange,' and no one takes more than needed because no one fears future want. Hythloday is explicit about the psychology this is meant to defeat: 'it is the fear of want that makes any of the whole race of animals either greedy or ravenous,' but beyond fear, humans also carry a 'pride that makes him fancy it a particular glory to excel others in pomp and excess' — and it is this second, specifically human vice that guaranteed sufficiency is designed to starve out.

**Care is centralized, generous, and physically walled off.** The four hospitals per town are deliberately built outside the walls, large enough to function as small towns in themselves, spacious enough to isolate contagious cases, and so well provisioned that 'there is scarce one in a whole town that, if he should fall ill, would not choose rather to go thither than lie sick at home.' The same boundary-drawing logic pushes slaughtering and 'filth' outside the town onto slaves, protecting both the citizens' capacity for pity and the town's air from infection — cleanliness and moral order are maintained by exporting the abject and the violent past the town's edge.

**Collectivism is not flat — it is continuously re-performed as hierarchy.** The communal halls seat everyone by age and office: the Syphogrant and his wife at the head, the oldest next, the young interspersed among elders so that 'the gravity of the old people... might restrain the younger from all indecent words and gestures.' Every dinner and supper opens with a short moral lecture, and elders are expected to draw the young into conversation deliberately, 'that so they may, in that free way of conversation, find out the force of every one's spirit and observe his temper' — the shared meal doubles as a standing surveillance and formation ritual.

## Notable passages

The family/city quota passage is the clearest statement of Utopian demographic engineering — the rule that lets population imbalance be 'easily observed' and corrected by moving children between households is the same logic that later scales up to moving whole citizens between towns and colonies.

The colonization passage shows the same legibility logic turned outward and made violent: land classified as 'idle and uncultivated' becomes, by that classification alone, land any nation has a natural-law right to seize.

The common-storehouse passage lays out the moneyless distribution mechanism and, crucially, its stated psychological rationale — fear of want breeds greed, but pride breeds excess, and only the second is treated as the truly human vice worth designing against.

The hospital passage is the unit's most sympathetic institutional description, notable for its almost inverted logic: a well-run public institution people would rather use than avoid, sited outside the walls both for practical contagion control and, implicitly, for civic cleanliness.

The meal-ritual passage closes the unit by showing that Utopian commensality is not egalitarian in the flat sense — it is graded by age and office at every table, every day, with the young's characters actively probed by their elders during the meal itself.
