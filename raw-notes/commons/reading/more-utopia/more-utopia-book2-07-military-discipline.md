# The Calculated Restraint of Utopian War

Raphael describes the Utopians' relationship to warfare: they hold war in genuine contempt as a brutal, inglorious activity, train for it constantly out of prudence rather than appetite, restrict themselves to narrowly defined just causes, and then — when war becomes unavoidable — wage it through an almost administrative logic of bounties, hired mercenary labor, and calculated restraint, prizing a bloodless victory won by cunning above any triumph won by force.

## Themes

**War is inglorious, not heroic.** The chapter opens by inverting the martial values of "almost all other nations": the Utopians detest war "as a very brutal thing" practiced more by men than beasts, and hold that "there is nothing more inglorious than that glory that is gained by war." Training is universal — men and women both — but purely defensive in intent, a hedge against necessity rather than an appetite for conquest.

**Just cause is narrowly and legally defined.** War is permitted only in self-defense, in aid of allies who have exhausted peaceful remedies (the Nephelogetes/Aleopolitanes precedent), or "out of good nature or in compassion" to help an oppressed people overthrow tyranny. Even injuries to Utopian trade abroad are absorbed as a state cost rather than avenged, since "the public, in such a case, only suffers" — war is reserved for violence against persons, not property.

**War is administered like an economy.** Once war is declared, the Utopians treat it as a problem to be solved by capital rather than casualties: sealed bounty schedules price the deaths or captures of enemy leaders in proportion to danger, corrupting the enemy's own people into betrayal; the Zapolets — a "rude, wild, and fierce nation" who are "made, as it were, only for war" — are hired as expendable troops, explicitly so the Utopians can "seek out the best sort of men for their own use at home" while consuming "this worst sort of men" abroad; and a conquered enemy is converted into a standing revenue stream, risen to "above 700,000 ducats a year." This reduction of violence to calculable, priced, administered transactions — bounties scaled to risk, populations sorted into best and expendable, tribute converted into an annuity — is [[legibility-as-machine-core]] applied to war itself: making the chaos of conflict readable, measurable, and manageable from a distance.

**Kinship, not glory, produces courage.** No citizen is compelled into a foreign war, but wives fight beside husbands and families are deliberately clustered in the battle line, so that "those whom nature has inspired with the greatest zeal for assisting one another may be the nearest and readiest to do it." Surviving a spouse or child in battle is a matter of shame, not honor — the discipline is domestic and relational rather than martial in the conventional sense.

**Restraint toward the defeated.** Utopian conduct after victory is markedly non-punitive: no plundering of stormed towns, no burning of crops even on the march, no harm to the disarmed or the surrendered garrison beyond enslavement of active resisters, and no share of spoil kept for the Utopians themselves — proceeds go to auxiliaries and to those who counseled surrender.

## Notable passages

The opening paragraph states the chapter's inverted martial ethic directly: the Utopians "detest war as a very brutal thing" and count as just cause only self-defense, aid to allies, or the liberation of an oppressed people from tyranny — a compact statement of the whole chapter's moral architecture.

The bounty-schedule passage is the darkest and most striking illustration of the war-as-administration theme: sealed proclamations price the death or capture of enemy leaders, deliberately manufacturing distrust among the enemy's own citizens so that betrayal substitutes for battle.

The Zapolets passage makes explicit the logic of sorting populations into usable and expendable: the Utopians keep "the best sort of men for their own use at home" while treating this "worst sort of men" as consumable war material, reckoning their deaths "a service done to mankind."

The family-in-arms passage shows the other face of Utopian war-making — courage generated by kinship proximity rather than martial glory, with wives standing "next their husbands in the front of the army."

The closing passage on treatment of the defeated traces the arc from ethical restraint (no plunder, protected surrender) to administrative extraction — a conquered enemy converted into an annual tribute of "above 700,000 ducats."
