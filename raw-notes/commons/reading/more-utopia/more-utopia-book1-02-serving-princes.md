# Raphael’s Refusal: Counsel, Flattery, and the Uselessness of Wisdom at Court

Continuing straight out of Raphael Hythloday’s travelogue of the manners and laws he has observed abroad, this unit turns to a frame dialogue: Peter Giles presses Raphael to enter a king’s service as a counsellor, the narrator (addressed by Raphael as “Mr. More”) seconds the appeal on more idealistic grounds, and Raphael refuses at length — not from modesty but from a considered diagnosis of what courts actually are. The unit ends on the hinge into the next episode, as Raphael pivots from general argument to a specific memory: a visit to England “not long after the rebellion in the West was suppressed, with a great slaughter of the poor people that were engaged in it.”

## Themes

**Service to a prince is framed as a form of capture, not a career choice.** Peter tries to soften “slave” into “assist,” and Raphael’s whole rebuttal is that the substitution is cosmetic: “The change of the word does not alter the matter.” Raphael has already discharged what he owes his friends and kin while young and healthy, and sees no further debt that requires surrendering his independence — “Now I live as I will, to which I believe, few courtiers can pretend.” This is close to Spinoza’s [[Conatus]] read backward into a Renaissance text: the striving to persist in one’s own nature and pace of life against an institution that would harness it to extrinsic ends. It also anticipates [[capture-resistance]] in the WMP sense — the refusal to be instrumentalized, asserted here as a personal ethic centuries before the framework that names it.

**More’s counter-case is the strongest version of the opposite position: that proximity to power is leverage for good.** His argument culminates in the image that “the springs both of good and evil flow from the prince over a whole nation, as from a lasting fountain” — if the fountain is upstream of everything, influence at the source outweighs the discomfort of compromise. The unit does not resolve this tension; it stages it and lets Raphael answer it with a structural rather than a moral objection.

**Raphael’s objection is structural: courts are closed flattery-loops that cannot metabolize outside counsel.** Ministers who are not genuinely wise nonetheless believe themselves so, admit no rivals, and cultivate only those already in the prince’s favor — “the old crow loves his young, and the ape her cubs.” Anyone who proposes something read in history or observed abroad threatens the reputation for wisdom the incumbents have built, so it must be run down.

**Where argument fails, appeal to ancestors succeeds — tradition as an unfalsifiable veto.** When a courtier cannot rebut a better proposal, the fallback is that “such or such things pleased our ancestors,” treated as “a sufficient confutation of all that could be said, as if it were a great misfortune that any should be found wiser than his ancestors.” This closes the loop: it is not merely that good counsel goes unheard, but that the court has a ready-made, content-free device for dismissing it regardless of merit.

**The unit is a hinge, not a self-contained argument.** It ends mid-transition, as Raphael moves from the general case against court service to a specific recollection of England, setting up what becomes (in the material that follows) his account of dining with a Cardinal and witnessing first-hand the abuses — enclosure, vagrancy, capital punishment for theft — that his abstract argument here has only gestured at.

## Notable passages

The opening exchange (**c1**) sets the terms: Peter’s case that Raphael’s learning is wasted outside a king’s service, and Raphael’s reply that he has already settled his debts to friends and will not “enslave” himself further. The short repartee that follows (**c2**) is the unit’s cleanest single move — Peter’s euphemism (“assist,” not “slave”) rejected outright as no change at all, then pressed again on the grounds of Raphael’s own happiness. More’s reply (**c3**) is the dialogue’s fullest statement of the case for engagement, turning on the fountain metaphor. Raphael’s anatomy of flattery (**c4**) and his account of ancestor-reverence as an argument-stopper (**c5**) together form his real answer: not that he is unfit to counsel, but that no court is structured to receive counsel at all.
