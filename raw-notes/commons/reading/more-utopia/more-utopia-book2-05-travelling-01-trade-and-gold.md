# Travel, Legibility, and the Utopian Contempt for Gold

This unit of More's *Utopia* moves through three linked institutions: the licensing and policing of travel between towns, the annual council at Amaurot that pools and redistributes provisions across the island, and the elaborate cultural machinery by which Utopia strips gold and silver of prestige — climaxing in the set-piece of Anemolian ambassadors humiliated by their own gold finery.

## Themes

**Mobility is a licensed, tracked privilege, not a right.** A Utopian who wants to visit another town needs leave from the Syphogrant and Tranibors and travels under a Prince's passport that both grants and time-limits the journey. Travelling without one is punished as vagrancy on the first offense and slavery on the second — even movement within one's own city's precinct requires a father's permission and a wife's consent, and requires the traveller to labor wherever he stops. The state's grip on where bodies are allowed to be, and for how long, is total.

**Everyday life is organized around total visibility rather than private space.** The absence of taverns, ale-houses, and stews is explicitly framed as the absence of "getting into corners, or forming themselves into parties" — Utopia eliminates the illegible social spaces where factions or idleness could form, so that "all men live in full view." This is the same logic as the travel restrictions applied inward: a society made maximally readable to itself, which the text presents as the precondition for universal labor and hence universal abundance. This civic optics — turning private corners into public view so behavior can be centrally accounted for — is close kin to the [[legibility-as-machine-core]] pattern: making people, movement, and resources readable in order to control and distribute them at scale.

**Redistribution across towns is accounting, not exchange.** Once a year, three delegates from every town meet at the great council in Amaurot to compare surpluses against shortages and reallocate "freely, without any sort of exchange," so that "the whole island is, as it were, one family." Surplus beyond a two-year reserve is exported abroad, a seventh given free to the foreign poor, the rest sold at moderate rates — with the resulting gold and silver held not by individuals but in the name of the town itself, functioning purely as a stockpile for the one purpose money is good for in Utopia: hiring foreign mercenaries in war, precisely because gold otherwise has almost no domestic use (Utopia imports little besides iron).

**Gold and silver are deliberately debased as social technology.** More's narrator (Hythloday) grounds this in a philosophical claim — value should track utility, not scarcity, so iron rightly outranks gold since "men can no more live without iron than without fire or water." Utopia enacts this belief institutionally: chamber-pots and close-stools are made of gold and silver, slaves' chains and fetters are gold, and a gold earring marks a badge of infamy. Children wear the island's pearls and diamonds as toys and discard them, ashamed, on reaching maturity. The design intent is explicit: to "render gold and silver of no esteem," so that surrendering all of it (when war requires it) costs a Utopian no more than a subject of any other nation would feel over "the loss of a penny."

**The Anemolian episode makes the argument as satire.** Ambassadors from Anemolia, unfamiliar with Utopian custom, arrive dressed head to foot in gold, chains, and pearls, expecting to "strike the eyes of the poor Utopians with their splendour." Instead the plainly dressed Utopians read the display backwards: children mock the gold-laden ambassadors as fools playing with baubles, mothers assume they must be "the ambassadors' fools," and the whole party is read as a slave train given how much more gold Utopia's actual slaves wear in their fetters. The reversal collapses only once the ambassadors see how commonplace gold is inside Utopia and how despised. The unit closes by generalizing the moral beyond costume: a society that lets a man command "wise and good men to serve him, only because he has a great heap of that metal" has let dead matter outrank human worth — and would, by the same rule, degrade that same man to servant status the instant the metal changed hands by "some accident or trick of law."

## Notable passages

The travel-and-passport passage establishes how tightly regulated Utopian mobility is, with slavery as the escalation for a second offense of unlicensed travel. The "full view" passage generalizes that same control logic to everyday civic space, explicitly naming the elimination of private "corners" and "parties." The Amaurot council passage is the clearest statement of Utopia's redistributive accounting — provisions tracked and reallocated across towns "as if" the whole island were kin. The chamber-pot/chains passage and its "tore out their bowels" contrast passage are where the philosophical claim about gold's true worth becomes concrete institutional design. The Anemolian children's mockery is the narrative payoff of that design, and the closing reflection on the "man of lead" generalizes it into a critique of wealth-based deference untethered from human merit.
