# True and False Pleasures: The Utopians' Rational Calculus of Delight

Continuing his account of Utopian ethics, Hythloday lays out the Utopians' taxonomy of pleasure. Against the sophisticated but empty pleasures pursued by "the rabble of mankind" — fine clothes, bare-headed deference, inherited nobility, gems, hoarded gold, and hunting — the Utopians set a taxonomy of true pleasures, divided between the mind (knowledge, contemplation of truth, a good conscience, hope of future happiness) and the body (health, the relief of need through eating, drinking, and sex, music, and sensory delight). Health emerges as the argument's keystone: not one pleasure among others but the "foundation and basis of all the other joys of life," without which no other pleasure is even possible. The whole passage is a demonstration that pleasure can be sorted by reason into true and counterfeit kinds, with nature and utility as the tests.

## Themes

**False pleasure is a corruption of judgment, not a property of the object.** The Utopians' method throughout is to ask what the thing itself supplies, independent of custom. Fine cloth offers no more warmth or use than coarse cloth; a gem is indistinguishable from paste to the unaided eye; a bent knee gives no ease to the man it bends before. The text names the mechanism directly: pleasure is corrupted by "a depraved custom, which may so vitiate a man's taste that bitter things may pass for sweet, as women with child think pitch or tallow taste sweeter than honey." Custom, not nature, manufactures the false pleasure — which is why the Utopians think it correctable by reason rather than fixed in the senses.

**Status is a self-inflicted fiction.** The critique of fine clothes and hereditary nobility is really a critique of relational value: a man feels himself "doubly mistaken, both in the opinion they have of their clothes, and in that they have of themselves." The same logic extends to men who hoard wealth merely to contemplate it, or bury it out of fear of losing it — value that exists only in the owner's head, evaporating the instant it is tested (a hoarder robbed without ever discovering the theft loses nothing real, because he possessed nothing real to begin with).

**Hunting is dissected until its actual pleasure is exposed.** The Utopians apply the same forensic method to a beloved aristocratic pastime: if the pleasure is in watching dogs run, watching one dog chase another should do as well; if it is in the kill, then the object of delight is a "weak, harmless, and fearful hare" being "devoured by strong, fierce, and cruel dogs" — which should provoke pity, not joy. Utopia accordingly delegates hunting to slave-butchers and treats the desire for bloodshed, even of animals, as either evidence of a mind "already corrupted with cruelty" or a habit that will produce that corruption through repetition.

**Health as the foundation of pleasure, and pleasure as nature's reward for self-preservation.** The Utopians argue that eating, drinking, and the other appetites are pleasant only because they restore what sickness or want has depleted — "the pleasure of eating" is health itself "recruiting itself" and "recovering its former vigour." This is explicitly framed as a providential design: appetites exist "to lead us to the propagation of the species," and Nature has made necessary self-preservation pleasant so that "those daily diseases of hunger and thirst" do not have to be endured as bare affliction. The whole argument is a striving-to-persist read as ethics — the body's drive to maintain and continue itself is treated as the root of legitimate pleasure, close to a Spinozist [[Conatus]] avant la lettre, here given providential rather than metaphysical grounding.

**A rational, legible ordering of desire.** What distinguishes this passage from ordinary moralizing against luxury is its method: pleasure is not simply condemned or praised but sorted, ranked, and tested against criteria — utility, health, virtue — that hold universally and apply to any case put to them (dice, dogs, gems, bare heads). It is an attempt to render the interior, subjective domain of desire fully orderable by reason, the same totalizing move that makes More's Utopia elsewhere read as a blueprint for [[modernity-machine|a rationally legible social order]]: not just institutions but appetite itself is to be made calculable and correct.

**Moderation, not asceticism.** The unit closes by rejecting self-denial as its own kind of error: wearing out one's health or strength through fasting or excessive labor is "madness" unless done to serve the public or others' happiness, since it wastes a good "Nature" gave rather than honoring it. The Utopians' position is neither indulgence nor mortification but disciplined enjoyment of exactly what is needed, no more.

## Notable passages

The clothes-and-nobility passage is the unit's opening salvo, establishing that the offense in false pleasure is intellectual (a mistaken opinion) before it is moral. The wealth-hoarding passage pushes the same test to its starkest form: a decade-long undiscovered theft that changes nothing, proving the pleasure was never real. The hunting passage is the unit's most sustained piece of moral reasoning, walking through the possible sources of the sport's appeal one by one until only cruelty is left standing. The health passage is the argument's pivot — the one bodily pleasure the Utopians treat as unconditionally good, and the basis for ranking every other bodily delight beneath it. The final passage on ranking pleasures closes the taxonomy by subordinating even legitimate bodily pleasure to virtue and to need, making explicit that eating and drinking are valued only as remedies, not as goods in themselves.
