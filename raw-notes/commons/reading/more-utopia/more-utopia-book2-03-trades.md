# Of Their Trades, and Manner of Life

Hythloday describes how Utopia organizes work: every child learns agriculture at school and in the fields, and every adult additionally masters one craft — usually the father's, but reassignable by "genius" through adoption into another trade-family with both father's and magistrate's consent. Dress is uniform across the island, distinguished only by sex and marital status, and never changes with fashion. The Syphogrants' chief duty is ensuring no one is idle, and the day is split into an audited six working hours, eight sleeping hours, and a disciplined remainder given over to public lectures and reading rather than "luxury and idleness." The bulk of the chapter is Hythloday's argument for why six hours suffice: no idle nobility, clergy, or money-driven "vain and superfluous" trades exist to waste labor, so foresight in building and durability in dress let the same output be produced with a fraction of the effort other nations spend. The point of the whole design, stated at the close, is that the constitution regulates labour only by public necessity so that time is freed for "the improvement of their minds."

## Themes

**A legible island.** Utopian material culture is built for readability at a glance: clothing marks only sex and marital status and never varies with fashion or rank, trades are registered and reassignable only through the magistrate, and the day itself is subdivided into audited hours — three before dinner, three after, eight for sleep, the rest "left to every man's discretion" but not to be abused. This is [[legibility-as-machine-core]] enacted as utopian virtue rather than state imposition: More is writing in 1516, right at the edge of the [[modernity-machine]]'s construction window (Dawn, 1200–1600 per WMP periodization), and the chapter reads as an early literary rehearsal of legibility's logic — a population made countable, its labor made auditable, its dress made a census category — centuries before the apparatus (cadastres, timetables, double-entry books) actually arrived.

**An anti-luxury theory of abundance.** The chapter's economic argument is explicit and almost accountant-like: tally every idle class elsewhere (idle wives, "idle priests," "noblemen and gentlemen" with their retinues, "strong and lusty beggars") and every trade that exists only because "we, who measure all things by money, give rise to many trades that are both vain and superfluous, and serve only to support riot and luxury" — and the labor actually needed for "the conveniences of life" turns out to be small. Six hours is not asceticism; it is what remains once waste is subtracted.

**Foresight over vanity.** The same logic runs through building and dress. English houses decay through "a thriftless heir" or an owner's vanity about "the beauties of architecture," forcing repeated costly rebuilding; Utopians instead show "foresight in preventing their decay," so buildings last with little labor. Clothing follows the same pattern — one durable, undyed garment worn for years, against the English fashion of many colored garments that make a man no warmer and no better dressed.

**Conatus permitted, then overruled.** Trade choice is not pure hereditary compulsion — a man whose "genius lies another way" can be adopted into a different trade-family, and after learning two trades he "follows that which he likes best." But the clause that closes the sentence is the tell: "unless the public has more occasion for the other." Read against [[Conatus]] and [[capture-resistance]], this is capture's most benign face — individual striving is given real room to express itself, but the moment it conflicts with collective utility it yields, framed as civic virtue rather than domination. It is the inverse of a Liveness-style refusal: consensual, meritocratic, and thoroughly legible, but capture all the same.

**Labour as means, contemplation as end.** The chapter's closing thesis makes the whole design's purpose explicit: the constitution regulates labor only by "the necessities of the public," precisely so that time can be reallocated to "the improvement of their minds, in which they think the happiness of life consists." Even the recreational life is didactic rather than idle — one of the two Utopian chess-like games stages "a battle between the virtues and the vices," modeling ethical contest as a leisure activity rather than an escape from it.

## Notable passages

**Trade assigned by descent, redirected by inclination.** The mechanism by which a man can leave his father's trade — adoption into another craft-family, sanctioned by "not only... his father, but... the magistrate" — shows individual preference operating inside, and ultimately beneath, a public-utility ceiling.

**The six-hour day.** The precise subdivision of the twenty-four-hour day into audited work, sleep, and supervised leisure blocks is the mechanism underwriting the whole abundance argument — legibility of time as the condition for the chapter's claimed prosperity.

**Money manufactures vanity, not value.** The chapter's sharpest line names money itself as the generator of waste: trades exist not because they're needed but because a money economy manufactures artificial demand for them.

**One garment, two years.** The clothing passage is the concrete case study for the anti-luxury argument — a single undyed garment against the English wardrobe of "four or five upper garments... of different colours," with the deadpan observation that more clothes make a man neither warmer nor better dressed.

**Labour as means, mind as end.** The closing sentence states the chapter's real subject: the entire apparatus of trades, hours, and uniformity exists to clear space for study, because that is where the Utopians locate happiness.
