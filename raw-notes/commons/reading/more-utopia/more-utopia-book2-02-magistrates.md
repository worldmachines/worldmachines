# Of Their Magistrates

This unit lays out Utopia's system of civil government: a bottom-up electoral hierarchy (thirty families choose a Syphogrant/Philarch; ten Syphogrants and their families choose a Tranibor/Archphilarch; two hundred Syphogrants choose a Prince by secret ballot from a slate of four) paired with a deliberately engineered procedural architecture — staged consultation, a mandatory cooling-off period before debate, and criminal penalties for off-channel political meetings — whose explicit purpose is to keep the Prince and Tranibors from conspiring together to seize the state and enslave the people.

## Themes

**Legibility as the substrate of legitimate rule.** The passage opens not with values but with an org chart: thirty families per Syphogrant, ten Syphogrants per Tranibor, two hundred Syphogrants electing a Prince from a shortlist of four drawn from the city's four divisions. Every layer of Utopian society is countable, nested, and named — the polity is rendered legible before it is rendered just. This is a textbook instance of political legibility in More's proto-modern sense: authority flows through an administrative lattice built for auditability, not through personal charisma or inherited status. It anticipates the census-and-cadastre logic that the Modernity Machine would later scale into statecraft — [[legibility-as-machine-core]].

**Secrecy of the vote, permanence of tenure, revocability of power.** The Prince is elected for life — an unusual concentration of tenure for an otherwise annually-rotating magistracy — but his security is conditional: he can be removed "upon suspicion of some design to enslave the people." The text pairs a durable executive with an explicit tripwire against its abuse, and it protects the electors themselves with a secret ballot ("they give him their voices secretly, so that it is not known for whom every one gives his suffrage") so that no one, including the Prince, can trace or punish a vote against him.

**The state is engineered against its own capture.** The unit's clearest thesis is structural, not moral: "These things have been so provided among them that the Prince and the Tranibors may not conspire together to change the government and enslave the people." Every mechanism described — the staged referral from Syphogrants to families to senate to the whole island's council, the rotating pair of Syphogrants who sit in on Tranibor sessions, the capital penalty for any unauthorized political meeting — exists to make elite collusion structurally difficult rather than merely forbidden by oath. This is a governance-level analogue of capture resistance: the design does not trust virtue to hold the center, it distributes and interlocks the mechanism so that no small clique can quietly redirect it — a concern that rhymes with [[capture-resistance]] even though Utopia's target is an institution rather than a single autonomous agent.

**Deliberate friction against haste and ego.** The most psychologically acute passage is the rule against same-day debate: matters must wait for a second meeting so that men do not "rashly and in the heat of discourse engage themselves too soon," then dig in out of a "perverse and preposterous sort of shame" rather than admit they misjudged. More diagnoses sunk-cost reasoning and reputational face-saving as structural risks to good government three centuries before either had a name, and prescribes an institutional cooling-off period as the remedy — deliberateness engineered into the calendar itself.

## Notable passages

The opening paragraph (c1) is pure administrative architecture: the nested electoral pyramid and the secrecy of the Prince's election. The second (c2) pairs the Prince's life tenure with its revocability, the Tranibors' annual reappointment, the rotating Syphogrant observers, the three-day debate minimum, and the capital penalty for unauthorized political meetings — the full toolkit of proceduralized restraint. The third (c3) states the design intent outright: preventing conspiracy between Prince and Tranibors by routing all major decisions back through the family-Syphogrant-senate-island chain. The fourth (c4) supplies the psychological rationale for the one-day rule, reading like an early anatomy of confirmation bias and status-preserving stubbornness in deliberative bodies.
