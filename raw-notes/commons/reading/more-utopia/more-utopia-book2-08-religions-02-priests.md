# The Brutheskas and the Priesthood of Utopia

Raphael Hythloday describes Utopia's ascetic religious order — called Brutheskas — alongside the structure, election, moral-education role, civil immunity, and wartime function of the island's small priesthood, presenting a religious authority that governs almost entirely through voluntary reverence rather than coercive force.

## Themes

**Two paths of devotion, both honored.** Utopia's religious enthusiasts split into two “sects”: celibate, meat-abstaining ascetics who court “the hardest and painfullest methods possible” in pursuit of the afterlife, and married, flesh-eating laborers who treat childbearing as “a debt which they owe to human nature, and to their country.” The Utopians rank the married sect as “the wiser” but reserve their highest esteem — “the most holy” — for the ascetics, while explicitly refusing to endorse either choice on grounds of reason alone: only when the choice springs from religious motive do the Utopians “reverence and admire” it.

**A deliberately undersized, elected priesthood.** Only thirteen priests per town — one per temple — chosen by secret popular suffrage “for preventing of factions,” then consecrated by the college of priests. In wartime, half accompany the army and half remain, rotating on return. Scarcity is treated as a feature: fewer priests means each can be screened more carefully, and the office's prestige stays concentrated rather than diluted.

**Authority that stops at exhortation.** The priesthood inspects “the manners of the people” but its coercive toolkit is a single instrument — exclusion from worship — deliberately kept separate from the Prince's and magistrates' power to correct and punish. Excommunication works through internalized shame (“it fills them with secret horrors, such is their reverence to their religion”); genuine legal punishment only follows if the excluded person fails to satisfy the priests of true repentance, at which point the Senate — not the priests — takes over.

**Moral formation of the young as the priesthood's real civic function.** Priests oversee education, but the text is emphatic that this means shaping “minds and manners” rather than literacy: early impressions “follow men through the whole course of their lives” and are credited with preserving “the peace of the government,” since civic disorder is traced to “vices that rise out of ill opinions.” Religious authority here operates as the mechanism that keeps the whole political order stable — governance by formed disposition rather than by force.

**Sacred office as legally untouchable, and why that doesn't corrupt.** Priests who commit crimes are not questioned by the state at all — “their punishment is left to God, and to their own consciences” — because no one may “lay hands on any man... that has been in a peculiar manner dedicated to God.” The text is careful to explain why this immunity is safe: priests are few, rigorously vetted for virtue before elevation, and — crucially — “have no authority but what rises out of the respect that is paid them.” Immunity paired with an absence of coercive apparatus is what keeps the arrangement from producing tyranny; this is a strikingly explicit design argument for [[capture-resistance]] — an office insulated from the state's punitive reach precisely because it commands no force of its own to abuse.

**Priests as battlefield sanctuary.** In war, priests kneel in sacred vestments praying first for peace and only then for victory “without the effusion of much blood on either side.” When their own side wins, priests physically run in among the soldiers to check the slaughter; any enemy who reaches them or merely touches their garments has “not only their lives, but their fortunes secured.” The passage credits this practice with real diplomatic effect: neighboring “barbarous” nations revere Utopian priests enough that the priests have repeatedly turned routs away from massacre and brokered peace “on very reasonable terms.”

## Notable passages

The two-sects passage (c1) sets up the chapter's whole logic: Utopia refuses to legislate a single correct religious life, ranking devotion by sincerity of motive rather than by the specific practice chosen. The election-and-structure passage (c2) establishes the priesthood's small, carefully vetted, non-hereditary character before any claim about its power is made. The exhortation-only passage (c3) draws the line between moral suasion (the priests' domain) and coercive punishment (the Senate's), showing excommunication working through internalized dread rather than physical sanction. The immunity passage (c4) is the unit's sharpest political argument: legal untouchability is rendered safe only because the office holds no coercive power to begin with — a pairing that keeps consecrated status from becoming a shield for abuse. The battlefield-mediation passage (c5) shows that same reverence converted into practical effect: priestly sanctity functioning as a portable truce line that has repeatedly stopped slaughter and produced negotiated peace among otherwise hostile nations.
