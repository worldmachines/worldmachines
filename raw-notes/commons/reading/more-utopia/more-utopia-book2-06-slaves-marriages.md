# Slaves, Marriages, Voluntary Death, and the Rule of Plain Law in Utopia

Hythloday continues his account of Utopian institutions: the three classes of slaves and how they are used and punished; the treatment of the incurably sick, including a priest-sanctioned form of voluntary death; the rules governing courtship, chastity, and marriage, down to a mutual nude inspection before the match is sealed; divorce and adultery law; the treatment of “fools” and the public honoring of virtue; the modesty of magistrates; Utopia’s deliberately minimal, plainly-worded laws and its abolition of lawyers; the practice of lending incorruptible Utopian magistrates to neighboring nations; and, closing the unit, Utopia’s principled refusal to enter any treaty or league with a foreign power.

## Themes

**Slavery is a calibrated penal category, not a spoil of conquest.** Utopia does not enslave ordinary war captives or foreigners by birth; its slaves are domestic criminals, foreigners already condemned to death abroad and bought cheap (or free) from other nations, and — a separate, better-treated class — poor foreigners who volunteer for the labor. Perversely, Utopia’s own citizens are punished harder than foreign-born slaves, on the theory that a superior education makes their crimes less excusable: the system reads guilt as a function of prior advantage, not just the act itself.

**Death itself is administered, and its legitimacy is a matter of public ritual.** For the incurably suffering, priests and magistrates counsel a voluntary death, framed as pious and honorable, and the compliant either starve themselves or take opium; but a self-killing taken without “the approbation of the priests and the senate” is denied funeral rites and the body is thrown into a ditch. Sanctioned and unsanctioned death are treated as opposite moral categories although the physical outcome is identical — only the community’s authorization changes what the act means.

**Marriage is preceded by a mandated, mutual physical inspection.** A matron and a “grave man” present the naked bride and bridegroom to each other before the match is sealed, justified by direct analogy to buying a horse: no one would purchase an animal sight unseen, so no one should marry on the strength of “about a handsbreadth of the face.” This is [[legibility-as-machine-core]] applied to the body — concealment by clothing is treated as the vector of fraud, and the remedy is enforced visibility before contract, though here the inspecting authority is the couple itself rather than a distant sovereign.

**Divorce is a Senate-adjudicated exception, never a private choice.** Utopia permits neither polygamy nor casual divorce; separation is granted only for adultery or “insufferable perverseness,” decided slowly and jointly by senators and their wives so that easy exits don’t “shake the kindness of married people.” Adultery is itself punished by relegation to slavery, with death reserved for relapse after pardon — marital stability is treated as a public good the state actively engineers, not a private matter left to drift.

**The law is deliberately made plain, and lawyers are abolished as a class.** Utopia has “but few laws” and no lawyers, on the theory that professional advocates exist “to disguise matters and to wrest the laws”; litigants plead their own cause before a judge, and “the plainest meaning of which words are capable is always the sense of their laws.” This is a direct, unforced statement of [[legibility-as-machine-core]] turned toward the ruled rather than wielded by a ruler: law is written to be legible to its own subjects, collapsing the gap a professional intermediary class would otherwise occupy and monetize.

**Incorruptibility is engineered through outsider status.** Neighboring nations, freed from tyranny with Utopian help, ask Utopia to send them rotating magistrates precisely because Utopians hold no local wealth, faction, or grievance that could sway them — foreignness functions as a structural safeguard against capture by local interest, a governance mechanism built on the fact that the governors have nothing to gain by staying.

**Utopia refuses leagues altogether, and the text turns the critique on European princes.** Where other nations “are perpetually either making leagues or breaking them,” Utopia enters none, arguing that if “the common ties of humanity do not knit men together, the faith of promises will have no great effect.” The aside on Europe is corrosive: princes have their treaties “purposely couched in such ambiguous terms” that they can always find a “loophole to escape at,” producing what the text names two sorts of justice — one “mean” and strictly bound, fit only for “the lower part of mankind,” the other “the peculiar virtue of princes,” measured only “by pleasure and interest.” This is [[legibility-as-machine-core]] again, inverted along class lines: plain, binding legibility for the ruled, cultivated illegibility as an escape hatch reserved for the rulers.

## Notable passages

The slavery passage (c1) sets the premise the rest of the unit works from: slave status is a legal outcome tied to crime or destitution, not ethnicity or war, which is what makes the later punishments for adultery, rebellion, and native profligacy legible as extensions of the same penal logic rather than exceptions to it.

The euthanasia passage (c2) is the unit’s most startling institution: dying is not a private event but a sanctioned, witnessed act, and the text’s harshest line — “they give him none of the honours of a decent funeral, but throw his body into a ditch” — shows how completely the community’s verdict, not the death itself, determines a life’s final meaning.

The nude pre-marital inspection (c3) is offered by the text as “wisdom,” not indecency, and its horse-buying analogy exposes the logic plainly: the passage treats the body as a document that must be rendered legible before a binding contract can be entered into, with fraud defined as anything the eye cannot verify in advance.

The no-lawyers passage (c4) is the unit’s cleanest articulation of legal plainness as a design principle — law that requires “a quick apprehension and much study” to parse is, in Utopia’s own reasoning, functionally the same as no law at all for the people it is supposed to govern.

The “two sorts of justice” passage (c5) and the closing appeal to natural “partnership” over leagues (c6) together form the unit’s argument about power and legibility: ordinary subjects get law stripped of ambiguity, while princes write their own obligations in language built to be escaped, and Utopia’s answer is to refuse the entire treaty apparatus rather than participate in its bad faith.
