# Corrupt Counsel, Captured Courts, and the Macarian Vow Against Treasure

Raphael Hythloday imagines himself among a different council of ministers — not the flatterers of the previous unit, but specialists in swelling a prince's treasury. He walks through five stratagems in turn — manipulating the currency, staging a pretext for war, reviving forgotten penal laws, selling dispensations from harsh prohibitions, and finally suborning the judiciary — then reverses the entire logic: a king's true safety lies in his people's prosperity, not his own hoard. The unit closes with the law of the Macarians, a neighboring people whose kings swear by solemn oath never to hold more than a thousand pounds of gold in treasure, an institutional cap designed to make oppression and foreign aggression alike structurally difficult.

## Themes

**The five arts of raising a prince's treasure.** Raphael's catalogue is a taxonomy of soft extraction rather than open plunder: debasing or raising the coin's value depending on whether the king owes or is owed; manufacturing a pretext for war (dressed in "appearances of religion") so money can be raised and then a convenient peace concluded; digging up "old musty laws" long fallen into disuse so their penalties can be levied under cover of "executing a law and the doing of justice"; and prohibiting ordinary activities under severe penalty precisely so that dispensations can be sold at a price. Each scheme shares a structure — legal or fiscal machinery repurposed from its ostensible function (justice, piety, prohibition) into an extraction technology, with the appearance of legitimacy doing the real work.

**Judicial capture as the linchpin.** The fifth and longest scheme is the corruption of the bench itself: judges are "often sent for to court" until some among them, out of "contradiction to others, or the pride of singularity, or to make their court," supply a pretence for the king's side; once judicial opinion is shown to differ at all, "truth being once brought in question, the king may then take advantage to expound the law for his own profit." The passage traces a précise mechanism — patronage, competitive flattery, and manufactured disagreement — by which an independent judiciary is converted into a rubber stamp, with the king's "undoubted prerogative" as the last-resort trump card "above all law." This mechanism, together with the currency and revived-statute schemes, reads as a portrait of what [[legibility-as-machine-core]] looks like when captured by extraction rather than accountability: the same instruments that make debts, laws, and legal reasoning legible and trackable become the instruments by which a sovereign extracts from subjects while preserving the outward form of justice.

**Crassus's maxim and the logic of raison d'état.** The counsellors' schemes converge on a single totalizing premise, attributed to Crassus: "a prince cannot have treasure enough," that a king can do nothing unjust, that "all property is in him, not excepting the very persons of his subjects," and that whatever a subject keeps is only what the king's goodness permits. Poverty, on this logic, is a control technology — it "blunts" the people, makes them "patient," and "breaks that height of spirit" that might otherwise incline them to rebel.

**Raphael's inversion: the shepherd, not the flock's owner.** Raphael answers this directly: such counsel is "both unbecoming a king and mischievous to him," because subjects choose a king "for their own sake, and not for his." Poverty does not pacify — it radicalizes: "who quarrel more than beggars?" A king who can only hold his subjects' duty through oppression would do better to abdicate than keep "the name of authority" while losing "the majesty due to it." Raphael invokes Fabricius — who said he "would rather govern rich men than be rich himself," since one man's abundance amid others' misery makes him "a gaoler and not a king" — and the figure of the unskilful physician who cures one disease by inflicting another, urging the king to live within his own revenue, punish and prevent crime rather than let it accumulate before cracking down, and never revive abrogated laws or claim penalties a private judge would disallow.

**The Macarian law: an institutional cap on capture.** The unit's final move supplies a positive counter-model rather than just a critique: among the Macarians, a people near Utopia, every king swears on his coronation day, "confirmed by solemn sacrifices," never to hold at once more than a thousand pounds of gold (or its silver equivalent) in his treasury. The law's designer reasoned the sum was enough for genuine emergencies — suppressing rebellion, repelling invasion — but not enough to tempt a king into invading his neighbors, "the chief cause of his making that law," while also keeping money in free circulation for commerce. Any surplus above the cap must be distributed, structurally reducing the king's incentive to squeeze his subjects. Read against the WMP frame, the oath functions as a piece of constitutional [[capture-resistance]]: rather than trusting a ruler's virtue, the Macarians engineer a hard resource ceiling that resists the crown's capture of the commonwealth's wealth — and, as a side effect, resists the crown itself being captured by the temptations that accumulated treasure creates (standing armies, foreign war, domestic extraction).

## Notable passages

The opening scenario (c1) sets the frame with the currency-manipulation scheme, establishing counsel-giving itself as a site of moral hazard. The judges passage (c2) is the unit's technical centerpiece — a step-by-step account of how an independent judiciary is converted into an instrument of the prerogative. The Crassus-maxim passage (c3) compresses the entire extractive worldview into a single, quotable syllogism about total royal property. Raphael's rebuttal and the Fabricius quotation (c4) supply the unit's ethical counter-thesis: poverty is combustible, not pacifying, and a true king's greatness is measured by his subjects' wealth, not his own. The Macarian law passage (c5) is the unit's payoff — the only concrete institutional design offered in the whole passage, converting the preceding critique into a specific, oath-bound mechanism.
