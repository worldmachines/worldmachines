# Correspondence versus Interaction, Understanding versus Undercommoning

Continuing his account of attentive walking, Ingold defines correspondence — a longitudinal, middle-voiced joining-with things — against interaction's transverse exchange of interests, then argues that living correspondence well means choosing undercommoning's shared uncertainty over the false security of settled understanding, sustaining the same prolonged, uncategorizing astonishment that Erin Manning locates in autistic attention and in artists.

## Themes

**Correspondence is joining-with, not joining-up.** Ingold distances his sense of “correspondence” from the logico-mathematical one, where matching elements of one set to another follows a principle of homology. His correspondence is instead the process “by which beings or things literally answer to one another over time” — in an exchange of letters or words in conversation, or of gifts, or even in holding hands (citing Ingold 2017). Observing the moon is not connecting an eye to the moon along an optical line — that would be joining up; to observe the moon is to correspond with it, because the moon “questions the watcher, arouses his curiosity,” moving him to a response that is not mere reaction but a prolongation of his own prior desire to become better acquainted with the moon, not simply to know more about it.

**Interaction is transverse and interest-bound; correspondence is longitudinal and open-ended.** The unit's sharpest formal move is this contrast. Interaction — as in an interview — goes back and forth face-to-face, bounded by ends “set in advance by each party” and known as ‘interests’ (from Latin inter + esse); it oscillates between two fixed points, with participants alternating as agent and patient through successive reversals. Correspondence, by contrast, is side-by-side and multilinear — lines that “join not at the ends but in the middle,” where beginnings and endings produce each other reciprocally rather than being fixed in advance: “Every end is not a terminal but a moment along the way.” [[Configurancy]] is the closest WMP analogue — like Rao's non-teleological co-emergence, correspondence has no destination given in advance, only an ongoing relational unfolding.

**The middle voice: every doing is an undergoing.** Where interaction alternates active and passive roles, correspondence unfolds in the “middle voice” (Benveniste 1971), in which “whatever you do is also achieved in you.” Ingold's walking example makes this concrete: “I am my walking, and my walking walks me.” The same holds for the moon-watcher, whose seeing is illuminated and borne by the moon's own light — walking and looking alike are instances of “doing in undergoing,” neither strictly active nor passive.

**Not an inversion of interaction, but a different axis.** Ingold explicitly rejects the idea that correspondence is reached merely by inverting interaction's back-and-forth — the move he attributes to Karen Barad's ‘intra-action’. Correspondence is instead reached by turning from the transverse to the longitudinal: “a feeling forward, an improvisation” that is “continually responsive to the movements of others.” Following philosophical anthropologist Thomas Schwarz-Wentzer, this responsiveness ‘precedes responsibility; it is the existential condition to the answer that I am’ — responsibility follows once something falls to us as a task; in correspondence, everything we do is a task.

**Understanding buys safety by foreclosing possibility; undercommoning risks staying open.** Every task risks “falling for the things that fall to you.” Understanding offers a firm base, a ground of certain knowledge, against which correspondence's improvisational falling-feeling looks merely uncertain — yet the same openness is what admits possibility, anticipation, hope. Ingold poses the choice explicitly: understanding, or undercommoning (Harney and Moten 2013). Undercommoning is not a return to a shared baseline of what we already hold in common, but “a way of living together in possibility,” joining others in “the adventure of undergoing” — the same vertiginous, hyper-attentive state as realizing you are lost while walking in the hills.

**Manning's autism/artist analogy: dwelling in incipience instead of rushing to categorize.** Most people pass through uncertainty about what something is so quickly they never notice it: “From the astonishment of ‘I can see’ we slide into the unsurprising normality of ‘I see this, or that’.” Erin Manning (2016) suggests autistic people may prolong that moment; Ingold glosses it as lingering “in the fullness of attention, in which things are as yet unformed” — open to what things might be rather than settled on what they are. He extends the analogy to artists, “never surprised but perpetually astonished.” This lingering-before-categorization sits close to what the WMP calls the [[untimely]]: a surplus that ordinary perceptual/temporal discipline (here, instant categorization) throws off but cannot fully govern.

**Thinking itself is a risk-laden reaching-out that unsettles thought.** The closing image returns to the telescope: to gaze through the eyepiece “is not to take a snapshot but to linger in the realm of astonishment,” a realm of hopes and dreams prior to the partitioning of experience into named categories. The moment of naming — ‘Oh, so that was the moon!’ — ends the dream exactly as it resolves it: “the dream has already faded into oblivion.”

## Notable passages

- The moon-watching passage (c1) states Ingold's technical definition of correspondence against the “joining-up” sense and gives the governing example that recurs through the unit.
- The interaction/correspondence contrast (c2) is the argument's formal spine: transverse vs. longitudinal, interests vs. tasks, terminal ends vs. ends as moments along the way.
- The middle-voice passage (c3) supplies the grammatical mechanism — doing as undergoing — illustrated through walking and moon-watching.
- The understanding/undercommoning passage (c4) pivots from the correspondence/interaction distinction to the existential stakes of living it, introducing Harney and Moten's undercommons and the lost-in-the-hills image.
- The Erin Manning passage (c5) is the unit's most concrete illustration of sustained, uncategorized attention, borrowed from autism and extended to artistic perception.
- The closing telescope passage (c6) returns to astonishment against categorization, closing the unit on the same image (the moon, looking) that opened it.
