# Real Problems; Real Freedom

The essay's conclusion draws a final distinction — between fake problems and real ones, and, by extension, between fake freedom and real freedom — then uses it to indict the institutions that promise us safety through predictive knowledge, before closing on not-knowing reframed as wisdom rather than deficiency, walking in the moon's company rather than planting a flag on it.

## Themes

**Fake problems and fake freedoms both come pre-loaded with their own resolution; real ones do not.** The jigsaw puzzle, the Rubik's cube, the crossword, and the maze are "fake problems" because the solution is already contained inside them, waiting to be found. Ingold extends the same logic to freedom: "fake freedom," which offers "the choice of multiple possibilities," is really just the mirror image of "mechanical predetermination" — a menu of pre-fabricated objectives dressed up as choice. Real problems, by contrast, "have no solutions; rather, one problem leads to another" — the path is the paradigm case, and so is life itself. Real freedom, correspondingly, is not a menu of destinations but "the singular possibility of the path": a freedom that "un-destines things," refusing to impose a target or a prefigured outcome in advance. This lines up closely with how the World Machines context frames [[capture-resistance]] and [[Conatus]] — capture works by subordinating a thing's own striving to an extrinsic goal, while the Spinozist counter-move (freedom as attunement to the relational structure of causes, not willed mastery of them) is exactly the "attention to things and the ways they want to go" that Ingold names as freedom's real necessity: "It is the necessity, in short, of constraint – literally of binding together – such that each, along its own way, participates in the fortunes of the others."

**Education, science, and the state sell a legibility-shaped freedom that is itself illusory, and in doing so manufacture the ignorance they claim to cure.** Ingold names the institutional version of fake freedom directly: liberal societies' schools, sciences, and states "arm us with certain knowledge" and "offer immunity," promising safety and security against "the existential risks of exposure." But "the predictive certainty these institutions offer... is as illusory as is fake freedom" — and worse, the demand that everything be explicated perpetuates the equation of not-knowing with ignorance. Citing Rancière, Ingold calls education, science, and the state "all-powerful machines for the production of ignorance": children, laypeople, and indigenous people are all made to feel ignorant "as a prelude and an excuse for their education," with educational success measured by how faithfully that ignorance reproduces itself in the next generation. This is the promise of [[legibility-as-machine-core|legibility]] — measurable, orderable, predictively certain knowledge that offers control — turned inside out as a critique: the very apparatus that makes the world knowable and governable is shown to run on manufacturing the ignorance it claims to dispel.

**Not-knowing is reframed as wisdom, and the essay ends by choosing companionship with the unknown over conquest of it.** Against the ignorance-producing machines, Ingold proposes a world "rich with the possibility of growth and transformation," a "world of life, of souls rather than selves," in which not-knowing is "not a form of ignorance but a form of wisdom" — a wisdom of turning toward the world rather than away from it, of "paying attention to things." The essay's final image makes this literal and figurative at once: better to "welcome [the moon] into our lives, and to walk in its company" than to "plant our feet upon the moon itself, only to leave our prints in the dust" — attention and correspondence over predictive mastery and footprints. It closes on Rilke's astonishment before the unknown, and a final open question about whether the poet's double was really who Ingold met on the way home from school.

## Notable passages

The opening move sets up the whole conclusion: fake problems (and, by parallel, fake freedoms) are pre-solved from the start, while "the path is a real problem in this sense; so also is life" — no solution built in, one problem leading only to another.

The essay's central claim follows immediately: fake freedom is a disguised form of predetermination, offering pre-fabricated objectives, while real freedom is "the singular possibility of the path" — an un-destining, attentive correspondence with others where "freedom and necessity are one and the same," ending "it is about joining with."

The institutional critique names education, science, and the state as machines that promise a legibility-style security through predictive certainty, and in the same gesture "are all-powerful machines for the production of ignorance."

The turn toward not-knowing as wisdom reframes the whole distinction in existential terms — not deficiency but a form of attention, "paying attention to things."

The closing passage delivers the essay's governing image (walking with the moon rather than planting a flag on it) and ends on Rilke's astonishment, leaving the reader with an open, slightly uncanny question rather than a resolved argument.
