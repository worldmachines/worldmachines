# Turning Toward the World: Wisdom, Post-Truth, and Uncertainty as Possibility

Opening with Andrew Greig's warning that "the more you know the less you see," Ingold argues that wisdom is not assembled from facts or "data" but arrives when we stop holding things at arm's length and instead attend to them, join with them, and let them guide our attention in turn; he then carries this correspondence-based epistemology into a defense of uncertainty, arguing — via Bergson — that an unpredictable future is not a deficit of knowledge to be regretted but the very excess that makes genuine, non-probabilistic possibility real, with the moon's endless non-repetition as the closing emblem.

## Themes

**Wisdom lies beyond the facts.** Ingold distinguishes "the facts of a case" from "the truth of wisdom": facts can be manufactured, propagated, and manipulated, feeding anxiety about a "post-truth" era, but truth was never reducible to what can be objectively ascertained in the first place. Even where facts settle a matter — a criminal's guilt — the truth of that guilt is "ever tested in the trials of conscience," unfathomable no matter how much more information is gathered.

**Observation as correspondence, not extraction.** To truly attend to something is "to bring things to presence," which requires joining with things and following them rather than merely looking at them. Ingold turns this into an ethical claim about science itself: the normal-science protocol of data collection treats data as something extracted from the world "by force or subterfuge," after which we "cut and run, turning our backs on the world." He calls this "deeply unethical" and argues science needs to be re-embedded in wisdom — in a practice of correspondence and care that is, in his words, "a labour of love."

**Uncertainty is excess, not deficit — therefore possibility.** Against the wish for certainty (which would only "augur the dead-end"), Ingold reframes uncertainty as the sign that life "continually overreaches itself" — every ending issuing into new beginning, life as "pure excess." This resonates with [[Conatus]]'s picture of a being that strives to persist and expand beyond any settled state. What the "deficit model" reads as missing knowledge is, in the light of excess, revealed instead as possibility — close to the fugitive surplus that [[untimely]] names as what discipline generates but cannot fully govern, here applied to the discipline of predictive knowledge rather than clock-time. Ingold also notes a generational shift: younger people increasingly experience the future not as an open landscape but as "a plateau bearing down upon them" — an excess of certainty (the end of history, even of life) rather than too little.

**Bergson: radical possibility outruns science's probability.** Science, restricted to "what repeats, or on what is precisely replicable," can only render possibility as probability, leaving whatever can't be determined to "chance." Ingold invokes Bergson's "incommensurability between what goes before and what follows" and the "absolute originality and unforeseeability of forms" to argue that the chance/determination binary itself "drains life of its creative impulse, reducing freedom to random variation within a phase space" — a diminished, statistical freedom.

**The moon as figure of inexhaustible newness.** Ingold closes by returning to the moon: mechanically it just "continues on its repetitive course" under gravity, yet "every time we open our eyes to the moon, it is as if it were for the very first time." Moonshine "never repeats" — it "brims with miraculous possibility," the concrete image for the essay's abstract claim about uncertainty and excess.

## Notable passages

The unit's hinge is a three-step move: an epistemic claim (wisdom exceeds fact), an ethical one (data extraction is unethical; science must re-embed itself in wisdom), and a metaphysical one (uncertainty is excess, and excess is possibility, not deficit). The Bergson passage is the clearest theoretical anchor: it names why science's probabilistic idiom cannot capture "radical possibility," setting up the final return to the moon as a lived counter-example — an object fully law-bound and yet, in Ingold's phenomenology of attention, never twice the same. The "trials of conscience" line is a compact instance of a move Ingold repeats throughout: taking a settled-sounding case (facts prove guilt) and showing that its truth still outruns any facts assembled about it.
