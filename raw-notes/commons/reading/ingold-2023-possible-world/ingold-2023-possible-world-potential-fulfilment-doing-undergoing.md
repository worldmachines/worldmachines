# Replenishing Potential, Not Fulfilling It: Doing and Undergoing

Ingold opens by rejecting the familiar image of a life as the progressive closure of possibilities — many paths narrowing to the one actually taken — and offers instead the Pintupi image of life as a single possibility continually replenished as one walks; he then carries the same distinction into John Dewey’s pairing of doing and undergoing, arguing that undergoing is not a bounded episode inside an act but an excess that overflows every doing and carries forward into the next, so that a life is a longitudinal becoming rather than a string of discrete, targeted acts.

## Themes

**Fulfilling potential vs. replenishing it.** The Western/developmental picture, exemplified by Geertz’s line that we all begin equipped to live a thousand kinds of life but end having lived only one, treats a life as a funnel: potential exists to be spent down to a single realized outcome, after which “there is nowhere further to go.” Ingold sets this against the Pintupi claim, reported by Fred Myers, that life is a ‘one-possibility thing’: because Pintupi people dwell inside the country the ancestors are still in the act of creating (the Dreaming), their lives don’t run from a starting-point to a destination but carry on, so that possibility is something walked into and renewed rather than a menu progressively exhausted — Pintupi people may hold more power at the end of life than at its beginning.

**The astronomer’s arc vs. the rocket’s line.** Ingold reaches for an analogy to name the difference between fulfilment and replenishment: space science fires rockets along a determinate line to a predetermined target (the moon), but the amateur astronomer’s telescope “proved, after all, not to be a gun” — to observe is to join an arc of attention to the moon’s radiance in real time, an arc running orthogonal to the line connecting eye and target. This arc of attention, continually replenished rather than aimed and discharged, becomes his figure for possibility itself.

**Doing and undergoing (Dewey).** Every deed is an intentional act — we know what we are doing — but every doing is also something we undergo: we are modified, perhaps transformed, by having done it. Ingold takes up Dewey’s 1934 puzzle (*Art as Experience*) over whether undergoing is sandwiched inside doing, contained between an act’s original intention and its consummation. If it were, Dewey worried, life would fragment into disconnected episodes with no carry-over from one deed to the next. Ingold’s answer, following Dewey, is that undergoing always overflows doing: whatever you do incorporates something of what you did before and is carried into what you do next, so that with every doing you become ‘a somewhat different person’.

**Transverse acts vs. longitudinal life; becoming rather than being.** Ingold formalizes this with a figure: any single act of doing is a transverse connection between an intention (I) and an objective (O), but the undergoing that accompanies it runs longitudinally, orthogonal to every such transverse link, tracked as a continuous wavy line he labels P for possibility. A life followed along P continually overtakes itself — it is a life of becoming rather than being. What it yields is not the objective consequences of its acts (these are “discards left along the way”) but further possibility, extended not only to itself but to every other life it tangles with, including, Ingold notes, its generational offspring. This non-teleological, relational unfolding — possibility generated through entanglement rather than aimed at a predetermined end — resonates with [[Configurancy]]’s account of co-emergence stripped of teleology, and the idea of a life that persists precisely by continuing to generate further possibility for itself and others echoes [[Conatus]]’s striving to persist and expand.

## Notable passages

The Geertz/Pintupi contrast supplies the unit’s governing opposition: a life as funnel narrowing to one fulfilled outcome versus a life as a single possibility perpetually renewed, with the Pintupi explicitly denying that life ever presented itself as “a menu of options from which to choose.” The telescope-not-a-gun passage translates that opposition into a perceptual register — aiming versus joining an arc of attention — that lets Ingold name possibility itself as something replenished by real-time engagement rather than consumed by achieving a target. The Dewey passage supplies the philosophical mechanism: undergoing must overflow doing, on pain of life fragmenting into disconnected episodes, and this overflow is what makes a person ‘somewhat different’ after every act. The closing passage draws the geometrical/ontological conclusion — doing is transverse (I to O), undergoing/possibility is longitudinal (P) — and states the unit’s strongest claim: life is becoming rather than being, yielding not to spent objectives but to further possibility shared with other entangled lives, including one’s generational offspring.
