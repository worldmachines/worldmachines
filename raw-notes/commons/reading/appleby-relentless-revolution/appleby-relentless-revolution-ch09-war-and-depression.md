# Two World Wars and a Global Depression, 1914–1945

Capitalism's own success — the industrial wealth and steel-plated navies it financed — armed the alliance system that dragged Europe into World War I in 1914; the war's punitive peace terms then poisoned the interwar decades, feeding an agricultural glut, a gold-standard collapse, and a leaderless world economy into the Great Depression, which discredited laissez-faire, brought Keynes and the New Deal onto the stage, and cleared ground for fascism and a second, far bloodier world war whose end left the European colonial empires mortally wounded but not yet dead.

## Themes

**Capitalism armed its own catastrophe.** The wealth generated by a half-century of industrial growth let Germany, Britain, and their rivals trade wooden ships for steel battleships and let firms like Vickers and Krupp profit from the arms race that jingoism and colonial competition stoked. Appleby calls the war's outbreak "overdetermined," not because there were real causes, but because "widespread imprudence and massive miscalculations" were so abundant — alliances meant to guarantee safety instead multiplied the triggers for war.

**The peace punished the losers and betrayed the colonized alike.** Versailles stripped Germany of territory and population and imposed reparations that, layered onto war debts and paper-money inflation, produced German hyperinflation and "bitter memories sown that were not likely to be forgotten." At the same time, Wilson's rhetoric of self-determination raised hopes across Egypt, China, India, and French Indochina that the victorious powers had no intention of honoring — secret partition deals and renewed colonial suppression instead laid "the foundation for enduring anti-American feelings." This is close to a textbook [[gramsci-gap]]: the nineteenth-century order of gold-standard free trade and formal empire was visibly dying, but nothing legitimate had yet been built to replace it, leaving fascism, Soviet communism, and fragile new nation-states to fight over the vacancy.

**Global integration turned an ordinary downturn into the Great Depression.** Appleby frames the Depression's real mystery as not why a slump occurred — slumps were routine — but why "the normal rebound didn't take place." Her answer braids together rural-to-urban migration, tightening world-market interdependence, an agricultural glut left over from wartime overproduction, and a United States that had the power to stabilize the system but chose to act as "the Lone Ranger," raising tariffs and calling in war debts instead. Britain's abandonment of the gold standard in 1931 left the world economy, in her words, "a ship adrift without a captain."

**Keynes and the New Deal broke with orthodoxy, but only partway.** Keynes's rejection of balanced-budget orthodoxy and his diagnosis of a "liquidity preference" that kept manufacturers from investing gave Roosevelt's relief and jobs programs their theoretical cover; the CCC, WPA, and eventually the Wagner Act reshaped the state's relationship to labor and the economy. But budget-balancing instinct kept reasserting itself — Roosevelt's 1936 tax hikes and spending cuts drove unemployment back up, and it took the mobilization for World War II, not the New Deal alone, to finish the job.

**Total war blurred capitalist and command economies.** Wartime mobilization drew every belligerent — Britain, Germany, the United States, and the Soviet Union alike — into centrally directed production, and Appleby is blunt that "in wartime, all economies become command economies." Soviet planning outperformed Nazi Germany's despite the USSR's inexperience, and American planning, despite even less institutional history with state direction, proved more effective still — a convergence that briefly erased the ideological line the rest of the chapter insists on.

**The chapter closes by naming capitalism itself a juggernaut.** Having traced wealth into war, war into peace-table betrayal, and depression into a second war, Appleby ends on the Hindu image of a juggernaut: a power whose "direction was uncertain," whose force was "conspicuous," and whose devotees were "capable of great self-destruction" — setting up the postwar chapter's question of whether capitalism could finally prove its "beneficent qualities."

## Notable passages

The unit opens by insisting World War I had no real causes worth the name, only alliance-driven miscalculation piling up until war became inevitable — a framing that refuses any single villain or rational strategic logic. It then follows the war's peace settlement into the double betrayal of Germany (crushing reparations, forced territorial loss) and of the colonized world (Wilsonian promises abandoned the moment the ink dried), tracing a direct line from 1919's grievances to the anti-American and anti-colonial sentiment of the following decades. The Depression section is organized around a genuine puzzle — not the downturn itself but the absence of the usual recovery — and Appleby's answer leans on structural overexposure (global integration, an agricultural glut, gold-standard brittleness) rather than any single culprit. Keynes enters as a stage-clearing figure whose "prime the pump" prescription reorganized what governments believed they could legitimately do. The chapter's account of wartime production quietly undercuts its own capitalism-versus-communism framing by showing Soviet and American state planning both outperforming Germany's. And the closing juggernaut image — power without direction, capable of destroying its own devotees — reads as Appleby's verdict on the entire 1914–1945 arc, not just on World War II.
