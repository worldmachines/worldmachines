# The 2008 Financial Crisis: Deregulation, Leverage, and the House of Cards

Appleby traces the 2008 financial crisis to two long-building causes and a psychological wild card: three decades of financial deregulation (culminating in the 1999 repeal of Glass-Steagall) that let commercial banks, investment banks, and insurers merge and trade unsupervised; and a flood of cheap Asian savings that fed a securitized-mortgage boom built on leverage, credit default swaps, and collusive optimism. When U.S. housing prices fell in 2007, the resulting "house of cards" of derivatives and hedge funds unraveled globally, forcing bank bailouts, an auto-industry rescue, and a worldwide reckoning over whether markets can be trusted to self-correct.

## Themes

**Deregulation dismantled the guardrails one law and one lawsuit at a time.** Appleby opens not with Wall Street but with Cleveland's 2002 ordinance against predatory lending -- overturned by Ohio banks in court, then preempted by the state legislature, then by the U.S. Office of the Comptroller of the Currency, which ruled that not even states could regulate national banks. That local defeat previews the larger pattern: the 1999 Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act repealed Glass-Steagall's wall between commercial and investment banking, and a 2004 SEC vote exempted the largest investment banks from debt-to-asset limits. Each step framed regulation as Lilliputian string tying down a capitalist Gulliver.

**Securitization and leverage built a house of cards no one could see the risk in.** Bank mortgages were sliced into derivative securities, sold on to unregulated investment banks, resold again, while credit default swaps -- insurance against default that anyone, not just the exposed party, could buy -- ballooned from $900 billion in 2001 to $62 trillion in 2007. Leverage ratios as high as 1:30 let "sober men and women" spread risk without tracking it, and credit-rating agencies, disinclined to offend the clients they hoped to work for, kept assigning artificially high ratings.

**Unintended consequences of individually rational choices converged into systemic collapse.** Asian households saving after 1997 didn't intend to cheapen American credit; homeownership policy didn't intend to trigger a subprime issuance race; bonus structures didn't intend to suppress dissent. Appleby's point is structural, not moral: "the freer the market system, the more widespread are individual initiatives that pull along in their train the unintended consequences of their actions." This is a case where locally rational actions never add up to a plan, only to an outcome -- a dynamic that resonates with the proliferation and non-convergence the [[divergence-machine]] names, here running unchecked past the reach of any regulator's legibility.

**The "wild card" was psychological: capitalism's contagious, self-renewing optimism.** "The history of capitalism doesn't repeat itself, but capitalists do," Appleby writes -- an optimism that denies reality until a Lehman Brothers or a Bernard Madoff punctures it. The same confidence that inflated 1990s dot-com and 2000s housing bubbles reappears every cycle because no one is in charge and everyone is searching for easy money outside regulation's patrolled precincts.

**Money in politics made deregulation self-reinforcing.** Television-era campaign costs threw officeholders into dependence on business contributions; SEC staff feared offending future employers; credit-rating agencies depended on the firms they rated. Appleby frames the crisis as jointly authored by financiers seeking a free hand and officeholders who needed money for reelection -- "a toxic combination of greed and need."

**Globalization made the meltdown universal and closed with a call for capitalism's legal scaffolding.** The crisis reached Iceland, Latin America, and Asia (only India, with its conservative banking, was largely spared), while Bernard Madoff's fifty-billion-dollar Ponzi scheme unfolded as a darkly comic coda to the same era of unexamined trust. Appleby's closing argument is that capitalism's "relentless revolution" cannot stabilize itself: it needs an external legal and civic substrate -- courts, clean politics, transparent finance -- that the deregulatory decades had eroded. The old laissez-faire legitimacy stood discredited while a new regulatory settlement had not yet been built, an interregnum with the shape of a [[gramsci-gap]].

## Notable passages

The opening frame (c1) states the thesis in miniature: a "rickety structure of derivatives and hedge funds" built on American mortgages, whose 2007 collapse turned a liquidity crisis into a solvency crisis and revived calls for regulation. The credit-default-swap passage (c2) explains the era's signature instrument through Appleby's own analogy -- insuring a neighbor's house against his negligent smoking -- and traces how it drew even the conservative insurer AIG toward collapse. The deregulation passage (c3) names the two-decade legislative arc, from 1970s anti-regulation sentiment through Gramm-Leach-Bliley, as the crisis's first predisposing cause. The unintended-consequences passage (c4) is the unit's structural argument in full: that no single actor intended the crisis, only a convergence of locally rational decisions. The closing passage (c5) delivers Appleby's ultimate claim -- that capitalism's health depends on legal and civic institutions external to the market itself, and that whether the political will exists to rebuild them "is now the question."
