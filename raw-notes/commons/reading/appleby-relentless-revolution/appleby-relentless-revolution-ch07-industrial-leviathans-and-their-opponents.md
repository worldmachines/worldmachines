# The Leviathans of Industry, Their Workers, and the Rise of the Consumer

This chapter tracks nineteenth-century industrial capitalism through three linked stories: the self-made entrepreneurs (Vanderbilt, Carnegie, Rockefeller in the United States; Thyssen, Siemens, Zeiss in Germany) who built monopolistic “leviathans of industry,” the fifty-million-strong global migration that fed cheap labor into their factories and the labor movements — syndicalist, anarchist, Marxist, craft-unionist — that rose to oppose them with wildly different success across nations, and the eventual discovery, by the early twentieth century, that industrial workers were also consumers, giving birth to a department-store, advertising-driven mass market.

## Themes

**Self-made industrialists built monopolistic empires through ruthless competition, vertical integration, and high fixed costs that foreclosed the easy entry Adam Smith's invisible hand assumed.** Vanderbilt, Carnegie, and Rockefeller rode fixed capital costs into railroads, steel, and oil respectively, forming giant corporations “too large to be run by a single man or even a family.” Vanderbilt's imposition of order on New York's chaotic competing railroads — consolidating them into Grand Central Station — is a small but vivid instance of the same legibility-making impulse [[modernity-machine]] runs on at civilizational scale: rendering a tangled, illegible system readable and controllable to enable command at scale.

**German industrial capitalism followed a different model from the American one: institutional and technical support over individual ferocity.** Zeiss and Siemens relied on craft traditions, university contracts, and technical education (Siemens's path ran through the Prussian artillery corps' engineering training); Thyssen, by contrast, was dubbed “the American” precisely because his aggressive individualism stood out against German norms.

**Ideological opposition to industrial capitalism split into competing camps — syndicalism, anarchism, Marxism — that agreed on the diagnosis but differed on the cure and on the use of force.** Proudhon's anarchist claim that “Property is theft” reframed private property as a device for confiscating workers' own creation; Marx saw industrialization as a necessary stage toward the socialization of wealth; syndicalists wanted direct workplace control via general strikes. The First International (1864) briefly united these currents before the anarchist–Marxist split.

**Labor's political success diverged sharply by nation, shaped by each country's inherited class structure and legal tradition.** Bismarck's Germany simultaneously outlawed socialist organizations and built the first modern welfare state (health, accident, old-age insurance), an early instance of repression paired with concession. Britain's craft-then-unskilled unions built a Labour Party that displaced the Liberals within two decades. The United States, lacking an aristocracy and steeped in an ideology of individual self-reliance, produced a comparatively weak labor movement (the AFL never exceeded 5 percent of the workforce) further fractured by racial exclusion, nativism, and the collapse of the Knights of Labor after Haymarket.

**American exceptionalism in labor organizing was reinforced by legal doctrine, racial segregation, and a wave of xenophobia that culminated in the 1921 immigration quota law**, even as robber-baron abuses (Standard Oil, meatpacking) provoked Progressive-era muckraking and reform — Tarbell's Standard Oil exposé, Sinclair's *The Jungle*, and Theodore Roosevelt's Sherman Antitrust prosecution.

**By the early twentieth century capitalism discovered its own workers were also its customers, and this insight — not ideology — reshaped the system.** Gompers's “more” campaign reframed labor demands around rising wages that would, in turn, expand the market for mass-produced goods, displacing the old “iron law of wages.” The resulting consumer economy of department stores, brand advertising, and installment credit multiplied variety and identity-signaling through goods — “a rich palette for painting one's identity” — a proliferation of consumer difference that echoes how [[divergence-machine]] logics push outward past any single legible order.

## Notable passages

Vanderbilt's consolidation of New York's warring railroads into Grand Central Station is the chapter's clearest miniature of legibility-as-control: turning an “untidy mess” into a single readable, governable system. The German-model passage crystallizes the chapter's comparative argument — that Zeiss's and Siemens's reliance on institutional and technical support, versus Thyssen's individualist “American” style, tracks a real divergence in how German versus American capitalism organized itself. The migration passage quantifies the human material of industrialization: fifty-six million Atlantic crossings, “the largest migration in history,” alongside America's explosive population growth from 5.5 to 92 million in a century. Proudhon's “Property is theft” anchors the ideological core of anarchist opposition to industrial capitalism. The department-store passage traces how mass retailing became capitalism's next “battlefield,” displacing mom-and-pop shops through what Appleby calls “creative destruction.” Gompers's 1887 “more” speech closes the arc: labor's demand for higher wages, once feared as economically ruinous, turns out to be the very engine that consumer capitalism needs.
