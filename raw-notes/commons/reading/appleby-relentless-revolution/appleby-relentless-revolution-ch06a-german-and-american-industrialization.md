# Two Roads to Industrial Ascent: Germany and the United States Overtake Britain

Appleby turns from Britain's pioneering industrialization to argue that its true historical significance lies in what came after: within twenty years of the 1851 Crystal Palace exhibition, Germany and the United States — despite starting from opposite conditions of political fragmentation and colonial "backwardness" — matched and then surpassed Britain in steam power, steel output, and eventually innovation itself, proving that capitalism was a transferable stage of world history rather than an English peculiarity.

## Themes

**Catching up meant rejecting Britain's own free-trade gospel.** Germany and the United States pursued Max Weber's “closed national state” model, using tariffs to shelter infant industries against the very free-trade order Britain had built to draw the world into its commercial orbit.

**Technology transfer preceded indigenous invention.** Before either country could out-produce Britain, it had to acquire British machines and know-how — through industrial espionage, smuggled equipment, and emigrant artisans — since patents and emigration bans made legal transfer impossible.

**Nation-building and economic development were the same project, pursued in opposite directions.** In Germany, modernizing 350 fragmented principalities' weights, currencies, tariffs, and rail gauges — the Zollverein's slow construction of a legible national market — *was* the path to political unification, a case where standardizing measurement and exchange functioned as literal nation-building infrastructure (echoing [[legibility-as-machine-core]]). In the United States, a nation already existed in 1789; economic development instead became the outlet for an already-declared nationalism.

**The two paths diverged sharply in mechanism.** Germany's ascent was state-directed and, at the decisive moment, militarized: Bismarck's “iron and blood” wars against Denmark, Austria, and France secured unification on Prussian terms. America's was decentralized and improvisational: Hamilton's fiscal machinery and Jefferson's dismantling of it together left development to state charters, competing banks, cheap patents, and a “risk-taking genie” of ordinary entrepreneurs.

**Industrial ascent congealed into a new ideology of time.** The Crystal Palace's celebration of "inventors" and "authors" as new heroes marked the moment "progress" stopped meaning mere movement and became a value-laden historical destination — the teleological self-understanding of the [[modernity-machine]] taking hold as common sense.

**Slavery is treated as economically potent and developmentally corrosive at once.** The cotton gin and the ballooning value of enslaved people made the domestic slave trade the South's dominant entrepreneurial activity, but Appleby weighs this measurable wealth against the unmeasured, long-run loss of cultural capital and moral legitimacy it cost the region.

## Notable passages

The chapter's thesis is stated directly in the Weber-inflected framing of tariffs as building a “closed national state” for capitalism's development, inverting Britain's free-trade example. The espionage passage shows how badly both rivals needed Britain's machines before they could improve on them. Bismarck's unification is rendered as a deliberate military-political achievement rather than a byproduct of economic growth. Appleby's excerpt from Tocqueville lets a contemporary observer credit American shipping dominance to temperament, not cost. And her closing judgment on slavery — wealth measured against an immeasurable loss — signals that this account of American development refuses to treat the South's prosperity as a settled economic success.
