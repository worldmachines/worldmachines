# Sugar and Steam: Slavery, Plantation Capitalism, and the Roots of Industrial Invention

This unit argues that the Atlantic slave-plantation system and Britain's later steam-powered mechanization were not unrelated historical currents but "twin responses to the capitalist genie" — both radical departures from peasant and craft tradition, both dependent on concentrated capital investment, and both imposing disciplined, routinized labor at a scale the pre-capitalist world had never seen. The unit traces the slave trade from its Portuguese origins in Madeira and São Tomé through its transplantation to Brazil, the Spanish Caribbean, and England's continental colonies (Virginia, Barbados, South Carolina); examines the racial ideologies invented to rationalize it; and follows its uneven abolition across empires. It then pivots to the specifically English preconditions — an agricultural revolution that freed half the workforce from the land, unusually high wages, and abundant cheap coal — that made mechanized industry a rational substitute for costly human and animal muscle, closing with Appleby's argument that "Industrial Revolution" is a misleading label for what was really a century-long, evolutionary accretion of small technical adjustments.

## Themes

**Plantation and factory as twin capitalist inventions.** Appleby's organizing move is to refuse the conventional separation of slavery from the "contributions of a free enterprise system." Sugar plantations and steam-powered manufacture both broke from inherited practice — peasant farming's varied, village-embedded tasks versus the plantation's de-novo, single-crop, military-style routines, or the artisan's workshop versus the coal-fired factory. Both required unprecedented concentration of capital (a sugar estate cost more to start than a cotton factory) and both converted employers' mere preference for hard work into an economic imperative once that capital was sunk into slaves or machinery. The routinized, monocropped, clock-like discipline Appleby describes on the plantation — labor organized into legible, interchangeable units stripped of local custom — previews the same logic of standardization and measured control that would later organize the factory floor, a resonance with the [[legibility-as-machine-core]] concept: both are early instances of the deep infrastructure the [[modernity-machine]] was assembling across these centuries.

**The invention of race as economic alibi, not its cause.** The unit is emphatic that racial ideology followed enslavement rather than justified it in advance: "circular reasoning kicked in" whereby the very traits slavery instilled — "indolence, insolence, sluggishness, lethargy" — were then cited as reasons Africans deserved bondage. Appleby distinguishes ancient and medieval slavery (ethnic but not consistently racial, tied to war captivity) from the modern Atlantic system, where race became the durable rationalization precisely because so few Europeans had direct contact with the institution's brutality — "out of sight is out of mind."

**Mercantile war reshaped trade toward the colonies.** Recurring dynastic wars between 1689 and 1815 (sixty-three years of conflict) diverted intra-European trade toward colonial extraction, hardened tariff walls, and pushed England and France into proxy confrontations across five theaters — India, West Africa, the Caribbean, the Ohio Valley, and Hudson Bay. This is presented as backdrop for why raw-material processing (sugar refining, tobacco rolling) increasingly happened at home under mercantilist law (the Navigation Acts) rather than in the colonies themselves.

**Slavery's regional variation had accidental, not moral, causes.** The unit catalogs how the mix of slaves-to-settlers differed by colony (Virginia's tobacco, Barbados's and South Carolina's sugar and rice) and by empire — Anglo-American slaves reproduced naturally and thus needed fewer fresh imports, while Caribbean plantations worked slaves to death and required replacing the labor force roughly every decade. Appleby is careful to attribute these differences to demographic and economic accident (sex ratios, climate, farm size) rather than differing moral character among colonizers, and she flags the historiographical debate over whether British abolition reflected genuine "moral outrage" or the sugar industry's declining profitability — concluding the economic-decline thesis has been undermined by later data showing West Indian trade was still rising when Parliament acted.

**"Industrial Evolution," not revolution.** Appleby explicitly rejects "revolution" as a name for what was a century-plus, incremental, trial-and-error process — she prefers the Darwinian vocabulary of "replications," "random variations," "waste," and "survival of the fittest" to describe how the steam engine and its kin were slowly perfected, even while conceding the term "Industrial Revolution" is here to stay.

**The specific English recipe: freed labor, high wages, cheap coal.** The unit is precise about what made mechanization rational specifically in England: an agricultural revolution that cut the farm workforce from 80 to 40 percent, driving up wages for the labor that remained; a domestic putting-out system that was already dispersing capital and manufacturing skill through the countryside; and abundant, cheap coal (a substitute for depleting timber) that made fuel-intensive industries like glassblowing, brickmaking, and iron-casting viable. High wages plus cheap fuel is presented as the deliberate economic arithmetic behind inventing "machines using fuel that could vastly increase the output of human laborers."

## Notable passages

The "twin phenomena" passage is the unit's thesis statement, explicitly naming slave plantations and mechanical invention as "twin responses to the capitalist genie" and describing plantation labor as work performed "like robots in military-style routines growing a single exotic crop" — the clearest statement of Appleby's throughline linking coerced agricultural labor to industrial labor discipline.

The slave-trade tally ("Between 1501 and 1820 slavers took 8.7 million Africans in chains") anchors the unit's moral argument in hard demography, setting the scale of the trade (11 million total) against the 2.6 million Europeans who crossed the Atlantic in the same period, and noting British and Portuguese traders carried 70 percent of the human cargo.

The passage on "circular reasoning" — that slavery-instilled traits were then used to justify enslavement — is the unit's sharpest analytical claim about how racial ideology functioned as retrospective alibi rather than founding cause.

The "Industrial Evolution" digression is Appleby stepping outside the narrative to make an explicit historiographical argument about naming and pace, distinguishing dramatic *change* (real) from *suddenness* (illusory).

The closing passage on "high wages and cheap fuel" states the unit's causal argument for why mechanization happened in England specifically, and ends on the line that no element of the automobile was missing at Leonardo da Vinci's death except "an engine that could turn heat into work by burning fossil fuels."
