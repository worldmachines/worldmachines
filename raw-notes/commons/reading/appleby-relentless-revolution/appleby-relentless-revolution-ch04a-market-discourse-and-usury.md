# Debating the Market: Discourse, Mercantilism, and the Decline of the Usury Taboo

England's shift to a market economy required not just new institutions but a new vocabulary and a new theory of human nature, argued into being over two centuries by merchants, moralists, and "hired pens" writing in an unusually open public sphere. Appleby follows this discourse from the calico craze's demonstration of ordinary people's appetite for novelty, through the 1620s clothiers' crisis that forced open the question of employers' obligations to workers, Thomas Mun's mercantilist case for a market beyond royal control, the centuries-long unraveling of the biblical ban on usury, and English fascination with the seemingly miraculous Dutch economy — all converging on a new, supposedly invariant picture of human beings as self-interested, calculating, and rational.

## Themes

**A public sphere argued capitalism into legitimacy.** England's exceptionally vibrant press — nurtured by seventeenth-century religious and constitutional disputes, then freed further when the licensing law lapsed and the Bank of England was founded in the same pivotal moment — gave pamphleteers, moralists, and "hired pens" for trading companies a national stage that Spain, the Netherlands, and China's much more censored publics lacked. Appleby is explicit that this was necessary work, not incidental noise: "people need to take stock of innovations, assess their impact, search out the meaning for their lives" before institutions can change, and capitalism could not simply be imposed because its genius was individual initiative. This is a [[history-machine]]-shaped process — a paradigm assembling itself silently out of scattered, mostly anonymous pamphlets, advice books, and tracts until it "had entered the public discourse in a way that decisively influenced all subsequent social thought," only later finding its systematizer in Adam Smith.

**The 1620s clothiers' crisis broke the paternalist consensus.** When a glut of cloth led English clothiers to lay off workers rather than absorb losses, contemporaries and officials alike were appalled — market-driven unemployment felt intolerable in a way that hardship from bad weather did not, because it violated the crown's sense of a commonwealth of mutual obligation. Out of the ensuing debate came Thomas Mun's argument that trade followed its own impersonal logic, not political will: a decisive move toward thinking of the economy as autonomous rather than governable by "princes" or moral censure.

**Mercantilism persisted even as its logic was being dismantled.** Mun's own reasoning — that money simply follows the balance of trade — was repeatedly misread and flattened into the durable mercantilist doctrine that national wealth is a zero-sum pie won by selling more than buying. Francis Bacon's contemporaneous turn toward empirical fact over "opinion" (what Appleby glosses as ideology) reinforced the broader climate in which such economic claims could be tested against evidence rather than simply asserted.

**The usury taboo eroded gradually rather than being repealed.** The Deuteronomic prohibition on lending at interest to "thy brother" became canon law in Catholic Europe, its clean edges worn down by merchant evasion and clerical casuistry; Protestant theologians from Luther to Calvin shifted enforcement from civil law to individual conscience; and English statutory interest ceilings only ratcheted downward — from an 1488 call for outright extirpation to a 10 percent ceiling under Henry VIII and Elizabeth, 8 percent under James, and 6 percent by 1652 — rather than the prohibition simply vanishing. The productivity of capital investment in agriculture and industry, not any single legal repeal, is what finally undercut the old claim that money "could not earn more money."

**The Dutch, envied and inexplicable, forced English thinkers toward abstraction.** Dutch wealth had no treasure-fleet or conquest story behind it, only "broad-bottomed flyboats" and mundane trade — yet the Dutch had low interest rates, high land values, high wages, and high prices all at once, a bundle of contradictions that pushed English commentators to isolate variables like value, profit, and interest rates from the social detail around them and imagine the market as an impersonal system, aggregate demand acting, like Newtonian gravity, as "power exercised from a distance." This drive toward a legible, rule-governed model of the market economy resonates with [[modernity-machine]]-style abstraction, though Appleby keeps the frame historical rather than theoretical.

**The calico craze proved consumption itself could drive growth.** Cheap Indian cottons imported by the East India Company triggered a demand so elastic and so socially indiscriminate — servant girls dressed like their mistresses — that it forced a reconception of ordinary people as consumers with legitimate wants, not just producers to be disciplined. Wages rose, average workdays climbed from 250 to 300 over the eighteenth century, and defenders of the Company argued that stimulating wants, not hoarding bullion, was the real engine of national wealth — a direct challenge to mercantilist orthodoxy roughly a century before Adam Smith.

**The cumulative payoff was a new, supposedly invariant human nature.** By the end of the period, market participants had been redescribed as self-interested, calculating, and rational, displacing the older Christian and literary picture of impulsive, sin-prone humanity that had justified tight social control. Edmund Burke's praise of Smith's theory as resting on a human nature "which is always the same" captures how thoroughly this new anthropology had come to seem like discovered fact rather than a two-century argument won.

## Notable passages

The opening calico-craze quotation — "the Wants of the Mind are infinite" — is Appleby's cold open: ordinary consumers, not economists, first enacted the proposition that wanting more makes people work harder.

The clothiers' crisis passage shows the paternalist consensus meeting its limit in a concrete labor-market event, not an abstract debate.

Thomas Mun's "necessity beyond all resistance" is the discourse's pivot point, recasting trade as a law-governed system outside royal or moral control.

The Deuteronomic verse anchors the religious taboo that the rest of the unit shows being slowly, unevenly dismantled rather than formally repealed.

The Dutch "anti-fairy-tale" passage captures the puzzle — wealth without treasure or conquest — that pushed English writers toward abstract economic modeling.

The closing passage on market participants as "self-interested, calculating, and rational" states the discourse's cumulative achievement: a new, seemingly permanent theory of human nature.
