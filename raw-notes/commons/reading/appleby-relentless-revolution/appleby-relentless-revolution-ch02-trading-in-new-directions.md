# Trade Is Not Capitalism: Empire, Commerce, and Institutional Divergence, 1500–1700

The chapter follows two centuries in which Portuguese and Spanish voyages, then Dutch, English, and French rivals, linked the Atlantic, Indian Ocean, and Pacific into a single global trading system — spices, silver, tobacco, coffee, silk, disease, and slaves moving together — while insisting throughout that this vast commercial expansion did not, by itself, produce capitalism. Appleby uses the divergent fortunes of five states to isolate what actually mattered: Portugal and Spain turned conquest into royal bullion and aristocratic consumption without transforming their societies at home; the Dutch Republic built the era's most sophisticated commercial and financial institutions yet stalled short of industrialization; and England — its aristocracy structurally open to trade and weakened by civil war — emerged as the exception whose institutions would eventually let commerce feed into capitalism a century later.

## Themes

**Trade is a precondition of capitalism, not its cause.** Appleby's organizing claim, stated twice — once opening the analytical section and once closing the chapter — is that the "ancient practice of trading" must be kept separate from the "novelty of industrial production" that actually defines capitalism. Commerce had flourished for millennia before capitalism and, in her account, would have continued to flourish without it; the term "commercial capitalism" misleads by implying a seamless evolution that the historical record — Spain, Portugal, and even France and the Netherlands — does not support.

**Aristocratic contempt for commerce blocked economic transformation in Iberia and France.** Nobility that lived off rents, cultivated leisure, and treated "who you were" as more important than "what you did" actively discouraged the qualities — reinvestment, thrift, productive risk-taking — that economic development required. In Spain this hardened into hidalgos capping domestic prices and the crown taxing merchants, manufacturers, and farmers into stagnation even as bullion from Aztec and Incan mines poured in; in France, the concept of *derogation* meant a nobleman could lose his title simply by engaging in trade.

**The Iberian empires ran on force, bullion, and royal extraction rather than productive reinvestment.** Figures like Afonso de Albuquerque — "the Portuguese Mars" — and the conquistadors combined gold, silver, and naval force to seize control of the spice trade and the Americas, but the resulting wealth financed religious wars and royal grandeur (Charles V, Philip II) rather than new modes of production. Spanish industry peaked in 1560 and then declined as English, French, and Dutch artisans displaced it.

**England's institutional peculiarities let commerce reshape politics rather than the reverse.** A single-heir aristocracy (younger sons like Winston Churchill's ancestor became commoners, free to trade without losing status), a prolonged Civil War that weakened royal enforcement of monopolies, and financing novelties like joint-stock companies and the 1624 Statute of Monopolies together produced a political class with a direct stake in commerce — culminating in ventures like the Virginia Company's boom-and-bust tobacco economy.

**Commercial brilliance was not sufficient: the Dutch case shows a "provisioning mentality" can cap development.** Amsterdam became Europe's financial center (the Bank of Amsterdam, 1609), the Dutch built the era's largest merchant fleet, and joint ventures financed the Dutch East and West India Companies — yet the Republic never freed itself from dependency on imported food, and Appleby argues this vulnerability bred a security-first mentality that stifled the imagination needed for the leap into industrialization; Belgium, its "backward" neighbor, industrialized first.

**Unintended consequences reshaped economies more than anyone's design.** Piracy and privateering (Drake, Morgan, Kidd, Blackbeard) were transitional, old-order institutions that legitimate trade eventually pushed governments to suppress; the Virginia tobacco boom-and-bust cycle unintentionally widened consumer markets to ordinary people; and the global integration of silver (Spanish American mines feeding a Chinese silver standard) tied distant economies together well before anyone intended a "world system."

## Notable passages

The chapter opens its analytical core by insisting that **aristocratic values, not lack of opportunity, blocked capitalist development** in premodern Europe: nobles "cultivated leisure along with contempt for those who worked for a living" and got away with chronic indebtedness because merchants lacked the power to challenge them.

Appleby's **central thesis** appears almost as a methodological aside: the reason to separate ancient trade from industrial production is to isolate what actually caused capitalism, and "trade itself is not one of them" — merchants got rich buying low and selling high, but the wealth distinctive to capitalism came from producing things more efficiently.

A sharper, more concrete illustration follows in the account of **King James I's monopoly economy**, where a contemporary scholar catalogued a typical Englishman's life as built from "monopoly bricks," heated by "monopoly coal," dressed in "monopoly belts, monopoly buttons, monopoly pins" — a vivid picture of a commercial life mediated entirely by royal extraction, the very practice the Civil War would erode.

Sir Walter Raleigh's maxim — "Whoever commands the sea commands the trade; whoever commands the trade of the world commands the riches of the world and consequently the world itself" — crystallizes the operating logic that drove Dutch, English, and French challenges to Iberian naval supremacy.

The chapter's most pointed comparative payoff is the **Dutch puzzle**: why did the Republic, with superior commercial and financial institutions, fail to industrialize? Appleby's answer turns on chronic food dependency breeding "a provisioning mentality that prized security above risk taking" — a psychological/institutional limit that mere commercial success could not overcome.

The chapter closes by **restating its thesis directly**: commerce existed for centuries before capitalist practices appeared and would have continued without them; capitalism was not an extension of trade but required "a different set of attitudes and skills."

The Dutch Republic's list of institutional innovations — free ports, secure land titles, efficient lawsuit settlement, bookkeeping taught in schools, licensed marine insurance, the Bank of Amsterdam — reads as a catalogue of commercial [[legibility-as-machine-core]] infrastructure, the same genus of record-keeping and standardization (alongside Italian double-entry bookkeeping, cited later as a "propitious factor" rather than a cause of capitalism) that would come to define the [[modernity-machine]]'s scaling logic even though, per Appleby, it did not by itself cause capitalism to appear.
