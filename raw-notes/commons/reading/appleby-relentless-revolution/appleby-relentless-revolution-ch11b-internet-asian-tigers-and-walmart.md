# The Internet, the Four Little Tigers, and Walmart's Information Machine

This unit closes a chapter of *The Relentless Revolution* by tracing three late-twentieth-century engines of capitalism — the commercialization of the Internet, the export-led takeoff of Hong Kong, Singapore, Taiwan, and South Korea, and Walmart's logistics-driven retail empire — before a short coda on birth control, the Human Genome Project, and capitalism's fraught relationship with democracy and inequality.

## Themes

**The Internet's drift from military to commercial infrastructure.** Appleby narrates ARPANET's 1969 origin in the Department of Defense, its slow civilianization through university networks, and its eventual capture by commerce: Berners-Lee's Web, Mosaic/Netscape/Explorer/Firefox's browser wars, Bezos's Amazon, and Page and Brin's Google. The throughline is that a legibility-and-control project (DoD linking minicomputers) became, unplanned, the engine of a proliferating commercial ecosystem — retail, advertising, peer-to-peer lending, e-mail — echoing the [[divergence-machine]]'s logic of variety multiplying beyond any single sponsor's design. Silicon Valley's rise is credited partly to an "invisible institutional arrangement" — California's weak noncompete law versus Massachusetts's strong one — that let engineers defect and found rivals freely.

**Dependency theory refuted by the Four Little Tigers.** The note recounts modernization theory's rise and collapse, then dependency theory's claim (Prebisch, Wallerstein, Gunder Frank) that Third World underdevelopment was a permanent, engineered condition of the world system. Hong Kong, Singapore, Taiwan, and South Korea are presented as the empirical refutation: state-directed land reform, export promotion over import substitution, high domestic savings, and technocratic insulation from domestic politics produced self-sustained growth in thirty years that took Japan a century. Appleby is careful to note the authoritarian cost — repression tolerated by U.S. Cold War policy — alongside the economic success, and closes the section with Japan's 1990s stagnation and the 1997 Asian financial crisis as a counterweight, including the memorable line likening capitalism without bankruptcy to "Christianity without hell."

**Walmart as a legibility machine.** Sam Walton's chain is presented less as a retail story than an information story: bypassing wholesalers, networking every store, installing inventory control so granular that a single cash-register sale triggers a message to purchasing agents, store managers, and vendors, and later replacing damage-prone bar codes with RFID tags. This is [[legibility-as-machine-core]] made concrete at commercial scale — real-time, item-level tracking across seven thousand stores — in service of just-in-time restocking and margin extraction from suppliers. The unit pairs this efficiency with its labor cost: unlike the unionized automakers of a generation earlier, Walmart's low-wage, non-union employment model became emblematic of organized labor's broader collapse, driven by political opposition, the shift to services, and — most consequentially, Appleby argues — the American public's loss of sympathy for unions themselves.

**A closing ambivalence about capitalism's social bargain.** The final pages widen the lens: effective birth control as a driver of falling birthrates and out-of-wedlock births; the Human Genome Project as science absorbed into commercial genomics; and a meditation on capitalism's uneasy alliance with democracy, illustrated by a Russian woman's post-Soviet insistence that democracy meant nothing until her cupboard was full. Appleby ends on inequality as capitalism's persistent "shame," while resisting a simple verdict — the same era that produced Rust Belt decline also produced Seoul and Tokyo.

## Notable passages

The ARPANET-to-commerce passage frames the Internet's origin as a military-legibility project that outgrew its sponsor. The Four Tigers passage stages dependency theory's refutation as an empirical, not just rhetorical, event — "doing in thirty years what it had taken Japan a hundred to do." The Walmart passage renders Sam Walton's genius as an information architecture, not a merchandising one. The Asian-crisis passage supplies the chapter's most quotable line on the necessity of failure within capitalism. The labor passage sharpens the contrast between Walmart's non-union model and the mid-century auto industry it implicitly displaced. The closing Russian-woman anecdote crystallizes the unit's ambivalence about what "democracy" actually promises under capitalism.
