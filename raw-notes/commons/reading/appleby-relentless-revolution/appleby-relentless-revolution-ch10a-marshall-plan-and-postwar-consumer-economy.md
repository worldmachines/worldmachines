# Postwar Capitalism Remade: The Marshall Plan, European Integration, and America's Consumer Boom

Covering the quarter-century after 1945, this section follows the belligerent economies of World War II as they choose among three postwar paths—European state-guided planning, Soviet command control, and American reliance on market price signals—then traces the Marshall Plan, the Bretton Woods institutions, and the Schuman-Monet project for European unity as they rebuild trade and industry, before turning to the parallel story of Cold War rivalry and the credit-driven American consumer boom in cars, television, air travel, and plastics that defines the era's second half.

## Themes

**Three paths for rebuilding a shattered economy.** Appleby frames postwar reconstruction as a choice among three modes of coordinating recovery: the "indicative" path chosen by France, Sweden, and Britain, where governments used subsidies, nationalization, and welfare programs—Britain's National Health Service offered “cradle to grave” coverage—to point private enterprise in a direction without commanding it outright; the "imperative" path of Soviet central planning, extended coercively across a buffer zone of Eastern European satellites; and the "informative" path the United States chose, trusting the coded language of market prices over political direction. This tripartite framework organizes everything that follows.

**The Marshall Plan as leverage, not charity.** Facing slow recovery and rising sympathy for communism, Secretary of State George Marshall's 1947 program funneled eighteen billion dollars into Western Europe between 1948 and 1952, first for food and seed, then for capital goods and fuel. Appleby is emphatic that the plan's deepest effect was psychological and institutional as much as material: it bought confidence, eased national rivalries, and gave the United States leverage to push war-wracked countries toward free-market institutions, producing sustained ten-percent annual growth and an "economic miracle" first in West Germany, then across Western Europe.

**European integration came from individuals, not economic laws.** Against any theory that markets naturally produce cooperation, Appleby insists that the European Coal and Steel Community, the Common Market, and eventually the European Union were willed into being by particular people—Jean Monet and Robert Schuman chief among them—who had lived through two catastrophic wars and chose reconciliation over the vengeful nationalism of Versailles. Capitalism's shape, she argues throughout, is set by its participants, never by inexorable laws.

**Cold War rivalry under the shadow of mutual annihilation.** The Truman Doctrine, Churchill's “iron curtain,” and the 1949 Soviet atomic bomb turned ideological rivalry into a standoff neither side could safely escalate. Capitalism itself took on new moral weight as the signature economic system of the West, while the United Nations and Bretton Woods institutions (the IMF, the World Bank, GATT) supplied the deliberative machinery the interwar League of Nations had lacked.

**A credit-financed American consumer economy.** GI Bill education and mortgage benefits, the interstate highway program, and a newly assertive labor movement (the Wagner Act, the CIO's cross-racial organizing) built the income base for mass consumption, but Appleby locates the real institutional shift in credit: department-store charge accounts were reorganized after the war into a coherent national lending system, letting ordinary families buy cars, appliances, and eventually houses on installment even as creditors kept discriminating against Black and women borrowers.

**Technology reshapes daily life—and politics.** Television, commercial air travel, and plastics all trace their commercial takeoff to wartime research repurposed for peacetime markets. Appleby uses the television commercial to make a recurring point about capitalism: it "can't ignore outsiders" (as with self-taught inventor Philo Farnsworth) and it reshapes institutions nobody planned for, as when TV advertising rewired the financing of American political campaigns.

## Notable passages

The opening statement of the indicative/imperative/informative framework is Appleby's own organizing device for the whole postwar period, distinguishing how France, the Soviet Union, and the United States each coordinated economic recovery.

The passage announcing the Marshall Plan names its mechanics directly—loans and grants moving from food and seed toward capital goods and fuel—the pivot around which the European recovery narrative turns.

Appleby's declaration that European integration "came from individuals like Monet and Schuman, not from any economic laws" is her clearest statement that capitalism's cooperative turn after 1945 was a human choice, not a market law, directly continuing the anti-teleological argument that runs through the book.

The passage on department-store charge accounts becoming a coherent national credit system marks the institutional change that made postwar mass consumption on installment possible, not just rising wages alone.

The closing note that television commercials showed "commerce['s] power to shape institutions in unexpected ways" extends Appleby's argument about capitalism's unplanned social effects from the shop floor into the arena of American electoral politics.
