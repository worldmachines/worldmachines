# Capitalism's Imperial Turn: The Scramble for Africa and Japan's Meiji Counter-Model

In the last quarter of the nineteenth century, European governments—flush with capital accumulated over two centuries of market-driven growth—became "venture capitalists" in their own right, converting surplus money into state-directed empire-building across Africa and beyond; the unit traces this shift through David Livingstone's exploratory and humanitarian witness, Henry Morton Stanley's more predatory version of the same drive, King Leopold II's uniquely brutal Congo Free State, the wider scramble among France, Germany, Italy, Britain, and the United States (aided by innovations like the Maxim gun), the West's belated moral reckoning with imperialism's abuses, and, as a deliberate counterpoint, Meiji Japan's parallel modernization along Western capitalist and military lines without submitting to Western colonization—becoming an imperial power in its own right.

## Themes

**Empire as an extension of surplus capital, not a break from capitalism.** The unit's organizing claim is that late-nineteenth-century imperialism was not a departure from free-market capitalism but its logical, state-directed continuation: markets had generated more capital than private investors alone knew how to deploy, so "kings and statesmen became entrepreneurs," using state power to command foreign labor and resources for the market in ways private companies could not manage on their own. [[modernity-machine]]

**Livingstone and Stanley as two faces of the same capitalist psychology.** The unit draws a moral contrast between Livingstone's faith-driven, humanitarian exploration and Stanley's naked opportunism (his 190-man armed expedition, his view of the African continent as "empty of people, or at least of people who counted"), then notes that both men displayed the same "insatiable curiosity," tolerance for short-run discomfort in pursuit of long-run goals, and tenacity that define venture capital—implying that imperialism and capitalism share a psychological as well as an economic root.

**Leopold's Congo as the extreme case of a general pattern.** The Congo Free State—Leopold's personal fiefdom, run as a company and disguised as a humanitarian and scientific enterprise—functions as the unit's moral center of gravity: forced labor, village destruction, and coerced extraction of rubber and ivory, eventually exposed by missionaries and writers (Conan Doyle, Conrad, Twain) and absorbed into the Belgian state only at Leopold's death.

**A scramble enabled by technology and rationalized after the fact.** The Maxim gun's lopsided battlefield advantage (fifty British soldiers prevailing over five thousand warriors) made rapid conquest feasible; the 1884–85 Berlin Conference formalized the division of the continent; and colonizers reached for euphemisms ("mandates," "protectorates," "spheres of influence") and self-serving justifications—native "ignorance" or European cultural superiority—to launder what was, in the Hannah Arendt framing the unit invokes, a willingness to do abroad what would be intolerable at home.

**Japan as capitalism's imperial student who refused to become its subject.** Forced open by British bombardment and Commodore Perry's fleet, the Meiji Restoration deliberately borrowed Western industry, finance, and military organization—yet, unlike the rest of the non-Western world "brought into the Western orbit," it preserved political and cultural autonomy, kept economic development under state rather than free-market direction, and used the resulting strength to become a colonizer itself (Taiwan, Korea, and victories over China and Russia). ## Notable passages

- The opening reframing of imperialism as capitalism's own overflow: European governments, no longer content to "sit in the stands," turned to "reckless overseas adventures" once idle money became intolerable.
- The Congo Free State passage, which lays out the mechanics of Leopold's forced-labor regime in stark, unadorned terms.
- The Livingstone/Stanley pairing, which locates the "moral chasm" between humanitarian and predatory imperialism inside a shared venture-capitalist temperament.
- The Hannah Arendt aside, which supplies the unit's clearest statement of imperialism's core hypocrisy.
- The closing account of Meiji Japan, the one non-Western power that copied the West's economic and military tools "except its theories about free enterprise," escaping capture rather than succumbing to it.
