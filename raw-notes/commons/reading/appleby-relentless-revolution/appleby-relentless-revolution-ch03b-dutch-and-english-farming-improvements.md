# Priming the Pump of Capitalism: The Agricultural Revolution in England and the Netherlands

Dutch farmers broke a millennia-old bind between feeding people and feeding soil — abandoning fallow fields for four-part rotations of grain, turnips, hay, and clover — and English freeholders and improving landlords copied and extended these techniques across the seventeenth century, multiplying harvests, freeing labor from the land, and dismantling the fallow-and-famine cycle that had defined premodern Europe. Appleby traces both the agronomic mechanics of improvement and its slower, more contested social consequences: enclosure of the commons, the erosion of village-based communal farming, and a running argument — waged by contemporaries and later by Marxist historians alike — over who drove the change and why. She closes by arguing that the resulting market society was the offspring of agricultural transformation, not its coercive parent, and that this dry, unglamorous revolution in fields and hedgerows did more to make capitalism possible than trade in spices or the parade of industrial machines that gets the credit.

## Themes

**Breaking the fallow bind.** For most of agricultural history, restoring soil fertility meant either fallowing a third or a quarter of arable land each year or feeding animals whose manure required yet more land to produce. Dutch farmers escaped this trap by discovering that clover and other legumes fix nitrogen, and by rotating grain, turnips, hay, and clover through four-part cycles instead of leaving a third of the land idle — "the virtuous circle of growth replaced the vicious circle of decline." English farmers copied and adapted the method (four-field rotation in some regions, "up-and-down husbandry" in others), while also exploiting marl, sea sand, and ground seashells depending on local soil and geography.

**Why change was slow.** Appleby is emphatic that improvement was not simply opportunity meeting a natural human drive toward self-betterment. Custom, risk aversion, and even the "biorhythms of premodern men and women," unaccustomed to sustained daily labor, worked against adoption; being a rational economic actor "didn't make a lot of sense" when risk routinely outpaced reward. Only freehold farmers and landlords who had converted fixed rents into market-rate leases were positioned to gamble on new techniques, and their conspicuous success — bigger harvests, falling grain prices that punished the unimproved — is what eventually built market momentum for change.

**Against the Marxist causal story.** The chapter's argumentative spine is a direct reversal of the standard Marxist account, in which farsighted landlords coerce tenants into commercial leases to expose them to market discipline. Appleby holds instead that "new social relations were the consequence, not the cause" of agricultural transformation — an unplanned, multi-generational "slow, untidy sorting out" of successes and failures across the whole agrarian order, from cottagers to great lords, rather than a program imposed from above. This is a [[history-machine]]-shaped account of change: a paradigm assembling itself distributedly out of thousands of independent farm-level decisions, only becoming visible as a "revolution" in retrospect.

**Enclosure and the fracture of communal life.** Consolidating open-field strips into private, hedged farms increased productivity but dissolved the network of community obligations that had bound villages together, a rupture vivid enough to provoke pamphlet wars — the Reverend John Moore's charge that enclosure abandoned Christian duty to the poor, against the Reverend Joseph Lee's defense of private incentive ("the monarch of one acre will make more profit thereof, then he that hath his share in forty in common"). The Elizabethan Poor Laws are the institutional response to the same fracture, formalizing parish relief just as villages were losing the communal structures that had once absorbed the vulnerable.

**Escaping famine, building a national market.** By Appleby's account England and the Netherlands were the only European states to expand both population and income simultaneously in the seventeenth century, crossing a threshold — freedom from famine — that separated them from their own past and from the rest of Europe, where war, static technique, and, in Russia and Poland, serfdom kept agriculture stagnant. The unification of local markets into one national market, building [[modernity-machine]]-style legibility and interchangeability into food distribution, was itself disorienting to the people living through it, as one contemporary's lament over having to "carry our Corn God knows where" makes clear.

## Notable passages

The four-field rotation passage captures the mechanism at the root of everything else in the chapter: nitrogen-fixing clover breaking the soil-exhaustion bind, described by Appleby in almost mythic terms as farmers "taking the first permanent steps away from the age-old economy of scarcity."

The reversal of the Marxist thesis is the chapter's clearest statement of its historiographical stakes, insisting that market relations emerged from the ground up over "five or six generations" rather than being imposed by scheming landlords.

The Moore-Lee pamphlet exchange and the anonymous market lament both let contemporaries speak in their own voice about what was gained and lost — productivity and personal initiative against communal obligation and the security of a known, local market.
