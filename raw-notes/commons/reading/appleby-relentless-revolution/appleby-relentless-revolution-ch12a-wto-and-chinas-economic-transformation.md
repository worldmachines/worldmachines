# China's Market Transformation and the WTO's Fraying Consensus

Opening from the spectacle of the 2008 Beijing Olympics, Appleby surveys how China and India's return to economic weight—China's alone rising from roughly 5 to 12 percent of world purchasing power between 1989 and 2004—has upended Western assumptions about the necessary link between markets and democracy, then traces the fracturing of the postwar multilateral trade order (the WTO's failed 2008 Doha round) before giving a compressed narrative history of China's reform era from Mao's Great Leap Forward through Deng Xiaoping's special economic zones to the eve of the 2009 global recession.

## Themes

- **China and India's rise refutes dependency theory and the democracy-markets linkage.** Appleby frames both countries' growth as evidence that governments can engineer market transformation without following the Western liberal-democratic template, comparing it to the earlier shock the Four Little Tigers gave to dependency theory. The two countries, once nearly half of world income in 1820 and one-tenth by 1950, are forecast to reach one-third again by 2025.

- **The WTO's postwar consensus is fraying under new entrants' weight.** The Doha round's 2008 collapse—driven by China and India's break from Brazil over agricultural-tariff protections—echoes the beggar-thy-neighbor nationalism of the 1920s that Bretton Woods was built to prevent. Appleby treats the WTO itself as double-edged: a genuine near-universal forum, yet one where "the lion's share of wealth, experts, and hubris" lets rich members set terms, and where labor, environmental, and intellectual-property disputes remain unresolved.

- **Party control and market liberalization coexist through deliberate, reversible half-measures.** The Chinese Communist Party's approach—captured in the "bird cage economy" doctrine, where the central plan is a cage that must be swung wide enough to give the market-bird the feeling of space without letting it escape—recurs across every reform: dual-track pricing, the household responsibility system's leasing (not abolition) of collective land, and Township and Village Enterprises that could be relabeled "collective" to dodge discrimination ("wearing the red cap"). This gradualism, and the party's parallel monitoring apparatus (work-unit committees, residents' committees, household registration), reads as [[legibility-as-machine-core]] deployed to manage—not surrender to—a proliferating, locally improvised market economy that resembles the [[divergence-machine]]'s tolerance for non-uniform, bottom-up variation.

- **Each Chinese generation since 1949 has been defined by a discrete political rupture**, from the Great Leap Forward's famine and the Cultural Revolution's Red Guards through the June Fourth Incident at Tiananmen Square to the generation that came of age amid 10-percent annual growth. Appleby treats Tiananmen not as the era's turning point but as a warning shot the party absorbed without reversing course—material progress, not repression, became the party's answer to the demand for freedom.

- **Integration into the world economy has costs the party cannot fully suppress**: air pollution estimated at 8 percent of GDP, corruption absorbing 3 to 15 percent of a seven-trillion-dollar economy, censorship battles with Google and Yahoo, and press coverage the government can no longer contain, as with the 2008 tainted-milk scandal and the Sichuan earthquake's shoddy-school revelations.

## Notable passages

The unit opens its analytical argument with the claim that China and India's trajectory has "swept up the two largest countries in the world" and may "change the character of Western capitalism" itself—positioning this unit's material as continuous with the book's dependency-theory critique developed around the Four Little Tigers.

The Doha round's failure is read through Yogi Berra's "déjà vu all over again": Appleby draws a direct line from the tariff nationalism of the 1920s to 2008's breakdown, arguing that six decades of Bretton Woods discipline have not immunized the trading system against a resurgence of "the snake of national interests."

The "bird cage economy" doctrine is the unit's central governing metaphor for the party's whole reform program: control loosened just enough to let the market breathe, never enough to let it fly free—the clearest single image in the unit of legibility and capture managing liveness rather than eliminating it.

The Tiananmen Square passage is notable for what Appleby argues it did NOT do: rather than triggering harsh, sustained repression, the June Fourth Incident taught the party that the greater danger was its "best and brightest" connecting with the discontented working class—a lesson that shaped subsequent decades of Chinese governance more than the crackdown itself.

The closing image of privatization as "crossing the river by groping the stones," paired with the residents'-committee apparatus that still "influences a range of what would be private, individual decisions elsewhere," captures the unit's throughline: market transition in China has been a liminal, incompletely resolved passage between one order and the next—a state this era's own texture resembles the [[gramsci-gap]], the old command economy's legitimacy receding without the new market order's rules yet fully settled.
