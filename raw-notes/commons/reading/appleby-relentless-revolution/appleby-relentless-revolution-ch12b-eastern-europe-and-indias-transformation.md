# Eastern Europe's Shock Therapy and India's Long Road from Empire to Boom

The unit contrasts two very different transitions out of state-controlled economies: the botched "big bang" privatizations of Russia and Eastern Europe after 1991, and India's much slower, layered transformation from a deindustrialized British colony through Nehruvian socialism to Manmohan Singh's 1991 liberalization and the consumer boom that followed — read throughout against the parallel, and very different, path of Communist China.

## Themes

**Shock without therapy.** Western advisers, speaking through the World Bank and IMF, expected former Communist economies to free prices and privatize state assets and then quickly recover; instead production collapsed and inflation soared. Russia's transition was captured by the old nomenklatura and by organized crime faster than the weak transition state could respond, producing extreme inequality between new billionaires and the remaining poor, alongside a demographic collapse that will drop Russia from the ninth- to the fifteenth-largest country in the world. Poland, Hungary, and the Czech Republic managed the same dual transition — democratize the polity, dismantle the command economy — somewhat better than Russia did.

**Britain's deindustrialization of India, and the legibility infrastructure of empire.** English protectionist laws throttled Indian calico and gingham exports once English weavers had copied the designs, turning India from a manufacturer into a raw-materials supplier — "Britain's deindustrialization of India," a country whose textiles had been famous since Herodotus. The railways, canals, telegraph lines, and postal services Britain later built across India were framed as a civilizing gift, but were built to consolidate British political and military control — a textbook case of infrastructure built for imperial [[legibility-as-machine-core]] rather than for the governed. Dadabhai Naoroji's statistical "drain theory" made the extraction visible and legible in its own right, converting British rule from a naturalized fact of economic life into a contestable political choice — a move the unit frames as essential to any anticolonial critique of a system that insists it is merely natural law.

**Caste as capitalism's stubborn residue.** India's caste hierarchy — with untouchables at the bottom and Brahmins at the top — persists as a source of violence and, oddly, as a durable organizing tool in electoral politics (Uttar Pradesh's election of an untouchable woman, Kumari Mayawati). The unit treats caste, like the segregation that stunted the postbellum American South, as the kind of inherited status distinction that capitalism's homogenizing logic is "impatient with," even as caste networks like the merchant Vaishyas have paradoxically supplied some of India's commercial talent.

**Gandhi and Mao as rival templates for postcolonial modernization.** Both India and China found their traditions upended by a charismatic leader in the late 1940s who shaped the nation's subsequent relationship to capitalism. Gandhi built solidarity through civil disobedience and bequeathed India a rights-guaranteeing constitution and a tolerance for pluralism reflected in its many competing parties; Mao built the People's Republic through violent revolution and a totalitarian party structure that eradicated some social ills (foot binding, opium dens) while turning citizens into objects of state control. The unit's shorthand: Gandhi was an inspiration, Mao was an organizer.

**Dismantling the License Raj.** Manmohan Singh, as finance minister from 1991 and later prime minister, used an acute financial crisis to privatize state companies, welcome foreign investment, and abolish Nehru's License Raj — the Soviet-style five-year-plan bureaucracy that had throttled Indian enterprise since 1947. The result, paired with a huge English-speaking low-wage workforce, was India's rise as the world's back-office and call-center hub, a fast-growing service economy (50 percent of workers) contrasted with China's factory-floor industrial economy (50 percent in manufacturing).

**Growth's uneven and unfinished character.** The Green Revolution's high-yield seeds doubled Asian cereal production and rural incomes but favored farmers who could afford the new inputs, widening rural inequality, and later stalled as governments cut irrigation and research spending. Despite double-digit growth, a young, urbanizing population, and a fast-emerging consumer culture (Bollywood, cricket, mobile phones, Tata's cheap cars, billionaires like Mukesh Ambani), half of India's children remain malnourished, a quarter of the rural population lives in severe poverty, and farmer suicides have run into the hundreds of thousands.

## Notable passages

The Russia paragraph (c1) is the unit's clearest statement of shock therapy's failure mode: privatization outrunning the state's capacity to police it, producing oligarchy, inequality, and a shrinking population — a diagnosis the author explicitly contrasts with China's and India's more gradual paths.

Naoroji's drain-theory statistics (c2) matter because they convert imperial extraction from an assumed natural order into a quantified, contestable fact — the epistemic move the unit says was necessary before Indian reformers could "get outside the mind-set of their British rulers."

The 1973 bus story (c3) is the unit's most vivid illustration of caste's power: people choosing death over sharing a rescue rope with another caste.

The License Raj passage (c4) marks the hinge point of India's economic history — the 1991 crisis that let Singh dismantle four decades of command-economy bureaucracy.

The Gandhi/Mao comparison (c5) is the unit's organizing thesis for why India and China, despite similar starting points, produced such different societies.

The closing folk saying that India's "economy grows at night while the government is asleep" (c6) crystallizes the unit's picture of a vigorous private economy outrunning a slow, coalition-bound state.
