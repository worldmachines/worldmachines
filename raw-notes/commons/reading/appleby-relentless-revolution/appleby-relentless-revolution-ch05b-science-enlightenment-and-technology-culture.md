# How Science Became Machines: Invention, Factory Discipline, and the Enlightenment Case for Progress

This unit traces a single causal chain from the Scientific Revolution to industrial capitalism's mature ideology: Galileo and Bacon establish experiment over authority; Newton and Boyle turn that method into demonstrable laws of gravity, air pressure, and vacuum; blacksmiths and instrument-makers (Savery, Newcomen, Watt) convert those laws into the steam engine; entrepreneurs like Wedgwood and the textile inventors (Hargreaves, Arkwright, Cartwright, Crompton) convert the engine into the factory; workers meet the factory with the Luddite revolt and decades of direct action; and Enlightenment political economists (Adam Smith, Thomas Paine) supply the intellectual framework — natural liberty, unintended consequences, progress instead of cyclical time — that justifies and explains the whole system, a framework whose contradictions culminate in the chapter's closing turn to the entangled and then diverging histories of wage labor and Atlantic slavery.

## Themes

**Science met craft, and craft got a permanent supply of new principles.** Galileo's laws of motion and Bacon's insistence that "experiments, not theories" were the linchpins of natural philosophy created a culture in which knowledge was tested against nature rather than inherited from authority, then openly published rather than guarded as "a body of secrets to be passed on to a select group." The Royal Society (1662) institutionalized this: a community of philosophers "who read one another's writings, copied one another's experiments, and formed a consensus of experts," eventually running popular lecture circuits that carried Newtonian physics — and demonstration hardware like air pumps and model steam engines — out to Leeds, Manchester, and Birmingham.

**Invention had many parents and very uneven rewards.** Savery and Newcomen exploited atmospheric weight to pump water from mines; Watt, given a Newcomen engine to repair, spent a career solving its "terrible waste of steam" with a separate condenser. In textiles, four men working independently — Hargreaves, Arkwright, Cartwright, Crompton — produced the spinning jenny, the power loom, and the spinning mule, but "their differing success epitomizes the mixed fate of inventors": Arkwright prospered on better financial backing, Cartwright went bankrupt, and Crompton was too poor to even patent his own machine.

**Wedgwood industrialized discipline as much as clay.** Beyond inventing the pyrometer and perfecting creamware, Wedgwood's real innovation was organizational: separate rooms per production line, single-task specialization instead of the old all-round potter, and — most tellingly — bells, clocks, and exact record-keeping used to instill punctuality and identify "refractory employees." This is the factory floor learning to run on the same legibility logic (measurable, orderable, monitorable) that the Modernity Machine applies at civilizational scale — see `[[legibility-as-machine-core]]`.

**Machines met organized, sustained resistance, not passive acceptance.** Yorkshire Luddites smashed shearing frames named for Ned Lud; farm workers attacked the mole plow; the government sent more soldiers to quell the riots than Wellington took to fight Napoleon in Spain. Appleby counts "more than four hundred instances of direct action against the pace and scope of workplace changes" over the century, continuing through the Captain Swing threshing-machine protests of the 1830s — a rearguard action, in her telling, but one that regularly won short-term concessions.

**Enlightenment political economy gave capitalism its philosophy, including a new theory of human nature and of time itself.** Smith's "obvious and simple system of natural liberty" rested on a claim that the "uniform, constant, and uninterrupted effort of every man to better his condition" was a near-biological drive — a strikingly conatus-like account of a self-perpetuating human striving (compare `[[Conatus]]`) that Smith treats as the hidden engine of both private and national wealth. The Scottish philosophers' "conjectural history" — from hunters to herders to farmers to commercial society — replaced cyclical notions of rise-and-decay with cumulative, irreversible progress, a historiographical pivot with its own echo in how later frameworks (including this project's own Dawn-Day-Dusk cycles) narrate long-run change. Paine's *Common Sense* extended the attack from economics into government itself, denigrating the British constitution as "noble for the dark and slavish times in which it was erected" and casting commerce as a "pacific system" superior to war.

**Industrial progress and Atlantic slavery shared roots but diverged sharply in visibility and sensibility.** Both were driven by the same profit motive and, in American cotton, the same supply chains, but "industrial innovations took place at home in the midst of one of the most fertile intellectual periods in history; slavery flourished out of sight." Appleby credits the same climate of moral ferment and Enlightenment rights-talk that justified the American Revolution with waking the "sleeping conscience" that eventually produced Pennsylvania's 1780 abolition law — the first peaceful legislative abolition of slavery, and, in her framing, proof that ideas move the workaday world.

## Notable passages

- The Bacon/Royal Society passage (c1) captures the epistemic shift at the root of everything else in the unit: knowledge as tested hypothesis, openly published, rather than authority-derived secret.
- The Watt passage (c2) is the unit's clearest single image of science becoming machine: a named scientific property (atmospheric pressure) engineered into a specific mechanical fix (the separate condenser).
- The Luddite passage (c3) is the unit's most concrete rendering of organized resistance to mechanization, naming the movement's origin and its stakes ("venerable work routines," "comfort and conviviality").
- The Wedgwood discipline passage (c6) shows factory organization, not just machinery, as an invention in its own right — bells, clocks, and record-keeping as technologies of labor control.
- The Smith passage (c4) is the unit's fullest statement of the psychological premise underneath Wealth of Nations: a universal, lifelong drive to better one's condition, doing the same explanatory work classical economics and (in a different register) WMP's conatus concept both assign to a foundational striving.
- The slavery/industrialization passage (c5) is where Appleby explicitly connects this chapter to the book's larger argument about capitalism's Caribbean and Atlantic origins, marking the moment industrial and slave economies — sharing a profit motive — diverged in visibility and moral consequence.
