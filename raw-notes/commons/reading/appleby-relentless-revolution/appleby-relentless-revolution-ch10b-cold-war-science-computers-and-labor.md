# Sputnik, the Computer State, and the Unraveling of the Postwar Boom

This unit traces a single arc from 1957 to 1973: Sputnik panics America into mass-expanding its universities and funding a Cold War science-and-engineering complex (rocketry via Operation Paperclip, computers via ENIAC and IBM, pharmaceuticals via wartime penicillin research), all financed by a federal government that becomes, almost by accident, the country's largest venture capitalist; in parallel, organized labor rises to its postwar peak and then begins a long institutional decline; both threads converge in 1973, when the gold-dollar link breaks, the Yom Kippur War triggers an oil embargo, and stagflation announces that the "golden age" of postwar capitalism is over.

## Themes

**Sputnik converted higher education from a privilege into an entitlement.** The 1957 satellite launch was less a technological threat than a public-relations catastrophe that convinced American elites universities, not government labs, held the key to Cold War competitiveness. Clark Kerr's "multiuniversity" vision and California's 1960 Master Plan turned college into something close to a birthright, absorbing the GI Bill generation, growing numbers of women, and eventually a push for minority inclusion — but tenuring an intelligentsia inside the academy also drained, in Appleby's account, the acerbic outsider critique that in Europe had checked capitalism.

**Federal Cold War spending built the technical infrastructure of the postwar economy while incurring debts nobody had budgeted for.** Operation Paperclip imported German rocket scientists (some implicated in slave labor and war crimes) to found the American space program; army and navy contracts produced ENIAC and digital transmission; university, corporate (IBM, Bell Labs, Pfizer), and government R&D fused into a single legible apparatus for turning science into weapons and consumer goods — a system Eisenhower himself warned, on his way out of office, could let public policy become "the captive of a scientific, technological elite." The same spending spree produced hydrogen bombs, dams, and pesticides whose environmental costs seeded 1960s ecological consciousness (Carson's *Silent Spring*) well before 1973 made the bill due.

**IBM's rise from punch cards to the System/360 is the era's clearest legibility story.** Thomas Watson's ambition — to "confine all the elaborate collecting of information to one punch, which then could be stored, correlated, or printed" — is a small-scale instance of the same impulse that built the modernity machine: making populations, censuses, and corporate accounts machine-readable. The chapter does not flinch from the technology's darkest client (Nazi Germany's use of IBM cards to administer its census) even as it credits Watson's customer-relations culture with IBM's dominance and eventual $5 billion bet on the System/360.

**Organized labor won its postwar peak and then lost ground steadily.** Wartime and postwar unionization (sit-down strikes, the Wagner Act, Walter Reuther's UAW) delivered real wage compression, dignity at the bargaining table, and particular gains for Black and, later, female workers — but no European-style social safety net, because Reuther's pension/health-care ambitions were tarred as socialism. Taft-Hartley (1947), capital flight to the non-union South, immigration, service-sector jobs resistant to organizing, and a rhetorical shift from "working class" to "middle class" eroded union membership from a 1970 peak of 27 percent to one in eight workers by 2007.

**1973 is the hinge where the postwar order breaks.** Nixon's severing of the dollar from gold, the Yom Kippur War-triggered OPEC embargo, and the resulting stagflation ended two decades of shrinking income inequality and steady growth. Appleby frames this less as a single shock than a shift in perception — Rostow's confident modernization theory (that Western capital and technology would inexorably develop the Third World) proved wrong about the importance of labor and local skill, while Carson, Nader, and Harrington's critiques of environmental, consumer, and social costs, dismissed as marginal in the early 1960s, looked prophetic by decade's end. The unit closes by pivoting to the transistor and Silicon Valley — the technology that will define the next machine even as the old one, in Appleby's words, ends "with more of a whimper than a bang," a fair description of the interregnum WMP calls a [[gramsci-gap]]: the postwar order's legitimacy dissolving before anything is ready to replace it.

## Notable passages

**Sputnik as catalyst (c1).** The opening paragraph makes the case for why a beeping Soviet satellite mattered more to American capitalism than any domestic invention: it converted elite anxiety directly into a mandate that "American universities, not government research facilities, held the key" to Cold War competition — the founding move of the whole unit's argument.

**Paperclip scientists (c2).** The passage tracing German rocket scientists from surrender through State/War Department compromise to "paperclip scientists" screening shows Cold War science built on morally compromised imports, laying "the foundation for the American space program at White Sands, New Mexico, and Huntsville, Alabama."

**Eisenhower's warning (c3).** The farewell-address quote about the danger that "public policy could itself become the captive of a scientific, technological elite" is the chapter's clearest statement of the [[modernity-machine]]'s self-diagnosed risk: legibility and R&D funding scaling control past the point anyone intended, ending in his plea that "security and liberty might prosper together."

**IBM's punch card (c4).** Watson's data-consolidation ambition, and the unit's blunt aside that "the Nazis used IBM cards to code and manipulate the German census, with grim results," is the unit's starkest instance of [[legibility-as-machine-core]] — a technology for making populations countable, deployed toward both bureaucratic efficiency and atrocity.

**Labor's arc in miniature (c5).** The compressed history from Taft-Hartley to 2007 union density — beginning "When companies changed owners, contracts won were lost" and ending with legislative protection for public-sector workers that "foundered in the 1970s" — is the unit's clearest single-paragraph summary of organized labor's rise and fall.

**The golden age ends (c6).** The unit's own verdict on 1973 — that the shocks of that year meant the "golden age" was over, and that ultimately the greatest chapter in capitalism's history "ended with more of a whimper than a bang" — is the passage that names the transition the rest of the unit has been building toward.
