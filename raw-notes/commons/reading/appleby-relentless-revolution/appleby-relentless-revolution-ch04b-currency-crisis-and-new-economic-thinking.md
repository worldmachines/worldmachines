# The Recoinage Crisis and the Making of Economic Thought in England and France

A currency crisis in 1690s England — coin clipping, an undervalued mint ratio, and a wartime shortage of silver — forced a public reckoning over what gives money its value, pitting John Locke's insistence that coin has only intrinsic silver value against practical critics who saw legal tender as a socially useful medium of exchange; Parliament sided with the philosopher, and mass deflation followed. From there Appleby traces how England's post-1689 constitutional settlement, the Bank of England, and transparent taxation built the financial trust that outpaced France's more centrally managed and more volatile experiments (Colbert's dirigisme, John Law's Mississippi Bubble, physiocracy), closing with her broader argument that capitalism's emergence in England was a contingent cultural and intellectual achievement, not an inevitable one.

## Themes

**Locke's philosophically confident but factually wrong theory of money won the political argument anyway.** Locke held that silver coin had only intrinsic value and that no monarch or Parliament could alter it by decree — a claim inseparable from his political theory that property and money predate government and thus limit its power. His merchant and pamphleteer opponents, "Practical rather than philosophical," argued instead that legal tender itself added extrinsic value, evident in the fact that clipped coin circulated at face value as easily as unclipped. Locke had, in Appleby's judgment, "the worst argument in this controversy but the greatest influence": Parliament reminted at the old standard, coin continued to be melted and exported, and the resulting deflation rewarded landlords and creditors while pressing hardest on debtors, tenants, and the poor.

**Contemporaries were explicit that Locke's supposedly neutral theory carried class interests.** One critic charged that Locke "extends his care to creditors and landlords, not regarding the cases of tenants or debtors," who would be forced to repay clipped-money loans in full-weight coin, "perhaps of twice the weight." The recoinage debate was already understood by its participants as a distributional fight dressed in the language of natural value.

**Institutional trust and fiscal transparency, not risk-taking alone, built English financial power.** The Glorious Revolution settlement subordinated the crown to Parliament, which then monitored royal spending under uniform national tax rates: "The new transparency in tax collecting and budgeting enhanced certainty and predictability, both important to enterprise." The Bank of England (1694), with privileged knowledge of the government's tax stream, could keep interest rates low and helped finance Britain's eighteenth-century wars. This is the same move WMP names [[legibility-as-machine-core]]: making a state's finances measurable and predictable is what let capital treat the sovereign as a reliable counterparty.

**France's more centrally directed paths — Law's bank notes, Colbert's dirigisme, physiocracy — were more spectacular and less durable.** John Law's Mississippi Company financed Louisiana development by having "issued bank notes for the development of thousands of square acres," until oversupply triggered a crash so damaging it delayed French paper money for another seventy years. Decades later the physiocrats tried, by royal fiat under Turgot, to achieve what English farmers, merchants, and manufacturers had achieved through decentralized, unglamorous trial and error: "the physiocrats were attempting to do by command what English entrepreneurs had done for themselves." Appleby treats this as her central comparative case — authority-driven reform is fragile because it depends on rulers whose successors may not share their goals, while dispersed self-interested experimentation, though it "bungled as often as they hit their mark," changed ordinary people as it expanded and proved more enduring.

**A "new orthodoxy" on money congealed only after decades of pamphlet controversy — a small [[history-machine]] moment.** Out of the recoinage debate's welter of competing claims, David Hume's position that money's supply is unrelated to real prosperity gradually hardened into the classical position "articulated by David Ricardo and enshrined in the elegant writings of John Stuart Mill" — a paradigm assembling itself out of scattered argument rather than being handed down by any single authority.

**Capitalism required trust and intellectual openness as much as capital.** Appleby stresses England's relative religious and linguistic homogeneity, its culture of "vociferous, public debates" on everything from salvation to the manners of the poor, and its litigious-but-institution-trusting population as preconditions that let merchants extend credit to strangers. She contrasts this with Japan's twentieth-century difficulty adopting jury deliberation — evidence, for her, that "cultural traits" can obstruct economic institutions "at the outset" even where they pose no problem once capitalist practices are already established elsewhere to imitate.

**Capitalism's rise was not inevitable, and its aftermath was not innocent.** Appleby states her method plainly: "I want readers to resist the temptation to bring capitalism onto the historic stage as something inevitable, because it was not." Societies that resist industrial-economy adjustments today are evidence for her argument, not exceptions to explain away; the variety of arrangements even within capitalist economies "cautions against talking about universals and uniformities" — a claim that resonates with [[divergence-machine]]'s insistence on non-convergent, proliferating outcomes rather than one legible endpoint. Nor does she let capitalism off the hook once it wins: "Capitalism didn't eliminate oppressive upper classes. It just changed the basis upon which they stood," leaving old master-servant law and anti-union statutes intact under new management.

## Notable passages

The unit's argumentative spine runs through six passages. Locke's confident intrinsic-value claim — "He was wrong, but confident, as only a philosopher can be" — sets up the crisis's central irony: the losing argument became policy. A pamphleteer's rebuttal names the distributional stakes Locke's theory obscured, protecting landlords and creditors at debtors' expense. The account of Parliament's decision and its "drastic deflation" shows Appleby moving from theory to consequence, closing on soldiers unpaid and the poor rioting. John Law's Mississippi Company supplies the cautionary counter-case — a bubble so damaging its name became the generic term for sudden inflation and equally sudden collapse. The physiocracy passage states Appleby's comparative thesis most directly: French reformers tried to legislate top-down what English self-interested individuals achieved by accumulated trial and error. And the closing methodological passage — "I want readers to resist the temptation to bring capitalism onto the historic stage as something inevitable, because it was not" — is Appleby's clearest statement of the book's larger argument, of which this unit's currency crisis is one case study.
