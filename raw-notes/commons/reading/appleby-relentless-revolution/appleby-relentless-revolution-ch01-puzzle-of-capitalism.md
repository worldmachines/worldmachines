# Capitalism’s Puzzle: Why England, Why So Late

This is the introduction to Appleby’s *The Relentless Revolution*, framing the whole book around one question: not how capitalism eventually triumphed, but why humanity’s scarcity-bound, custom-governed traditional societies held almost unchanged for millennia before a contingent, non-inevitable break — occurring first and decisively in seventeenth-century England, not in wealthier or more cosmopolitan rivals, and not in Ming China, whose fifteenth-century voyages dwarfed the Portuguese’s — produced capitalism as a self-sustaining cultural system. Appleby surveys and positions herself against the field’s foundational explanations (Smith’s natural “truck and barter,” Marx’s coercive class formation, Weber’s Protestant-ethic account of an unnatural psychology, plus later material explanations from Diamond, Landes, Crosby, Lal, and Pomeranz) before closing with her own definition: capitalism as a cultural, not natural or destined, system.

## Themes

**The puzzle is delay, not arrival.** Appleby reframes the standard question: it isn’t a mystery that capitalism eventually emerged, but that “brilliant minds penetrated some of the secrets of the cosmos but couldn’t imagine how to combat hunger” for millennia beforehand.

**Traditional society ran on scarcity and status, not incentive.** She describes premodern societies worldwide — Egypt, Greece, Babylonia, Mongolia — as economies where upwards of 80 percent of labor went to producing food that still often fell short, where fear of famine legitimated authoritarian rule, and where tasks were allocated “through the inherited statuses of landlord, tenant, father, husband, son, laborer, wife, mother, daughter, and servant” rather than through individual choice.

**Contingency over inevitability: capitalism as rupture, not evolution.** Central to Appleby’s method is the claim that “a succession is not a process” — the many preconditions historians cite (printing, clocks, the New World, papal law) were present in places that never became capitalist, so no single-cause, teleological account works. Capitalism required the accidental convergence of “many elements, some fortuitous,” resolving into an irreversible break rather than a smooth developmental chain. This is close in spirit to the [[history-machine]] idea of a paradigm assembling silently from many independent, uncoordinated components until it lurches into visibility, against “great men” historiography — Appleby levels the same charge at Diamond’s geographic determinism, Landes’s blended-factors narrative with no interactive mechanism, and Crosby’s purely intellectual account.

**England’s singularity defeats every general theory.** Because the preconditions for capitalism (commerce, exploration, printing, scientific advance) were shared across Europe and Asia but the breakthrough happened only in England, Appleby rejects universal or material explanations (Diamond’s biogeography, Pomeranz’s “global economic parity” thesis) for a specific, non-repeatable convergence of timing in one country — capitalism as “a startling departure from the norms that had prevailed for four thousand years,” not a predestined chapter.

**Smith, Marx, and Weber, read against each other.** Smith naturalized capitalism by projecting a timeless “desire of bettering our condition” onto human nature, “turn[ing] an effect into a cause.” Marx supplied a coercive, class-based mechanism but assumed a market mentality that couldn’t yet have existed. Weber is the theorist Appleby credits most, for insisting pre-capitalist Europeans had none of the psychology later economists assumed, and for tracing “the spirit of capitalism” to the unintended anxieties of Protestant soteriology rather than to any natural human drive.

**Capitalism redefined as a cultural, not natural, system.** Appleby’s closing move, and the book’s thesis, is definitional: capitalism is “a cultural system rooted in economic practices,” not a freestanding, self-correcting mechanism or a law of human nature, and its history has to be told with attention to “culture, contingency, and coercion” together. This framing runs parallel to how [[world-machines]] treats an era’s deep infrastructure — interlocking institutions, technologies, and assumptions — as the real unit of historical analysis, rather than any single economic mechanism taken in isolation.

## Notable passages

The opening “detective story” framing sets the book’s entire question: not capitalism’s triumph but its four-thousand-year delay. The scarcity-and-status portrait of traditional society supplies the baseline against which any later “departure” has to be measured. The “dike” and “many elements, some fortuitous” passage is Appleby’s clearest statement of anti-teleological method, and the strongest resonance with the [[history-machine]] concept of contingent, distributed emergence. The extended engagement with Weber shows which of the three classical theorists most shapes her own approach, and why: his refusal to assume a ready-made capitalist psychology. Her closing definition of capitalism as “a cultural system” is the thesis statement the rest of the book argues for.
