# Friedman, Deregulation, and the Rise of Japan and the Personal Computer

The unit opens with 1970s stagflation shattering the postwar Keynesian consensus, traces how Milton Friedman's monetarism supplied the intellectual cover for Reagan- and Thatcher-era deregulation (airlines, trucking, banking, savings and loans) and the hollowing-out of the American Rust Belt, then pivots — via Tom Wolfe's remark about "the end of capitalism" — to argue that capitalism persists by continually reinventing itself along divergent local paths. It develops this through two case studies: Japan's century-long, state-guided, family-conglomerate-driven industrialization, from Sakichi Toyoda's looms through Toyota's lean production to Japan's postwar dominance in steel, autos, and consumer electronics; and the American personal computer, born when IBM built an open architecture from off-the-shelf Intel and Microsoft components that Apple, Compaq, and Dell would go on to compete over.

## Themes

**Stagflation broke the Keynesian consensus, but Friedman's monetarist alternative did not fully replace it either.** By the early 1970s "the facts no longer supported the original Keynesian proposition": government spending prescribed for recessions had never been withdrawn in good times, and rising unemployment alongside inflation contradicted the theory's own logic. Friedman's monetarism won the intellectual contest and the political patronage of Thatcher and Reagan, but in practice the Federal Reserve only ran on monetarist lines from 1979 to 1982, and even then "it failed to keep the country from sliding into recession" — a genuine interregnum between paradigms, the kind of gap WMP calls a [[gramsci-gap]]: the old orthodoxy's legitimacy gone before a working replacement is in hand.

**Friedman's ideas licensed a deregulation wave whose costs were real, not just theoretical.** Deregulation freed airlines and trucking for competitive pricing and, more consequentially, loosened accounting and down-payment rules for savings and loan institutions — "sailing faster with less ballast," as Appleby puts it, they "floated many more loans," and over seven hundred S&Ls collapsed within a decade at a cost exceeding one hundred billion dollars to taxpayers, without slowing the deregulatory movement itself. The same decade saw manufacturing flee to cheap-labor countries, turning America's steel corridor into the Rust Belt while opening a durable income split between low-wage service work and high-wage finance and tech.

**Capitalism's durability lies in adaptability to local conditions, not a single fixed formula.** Answering Tom Wolfe's claim that capitalism was ending, the unit insists it is "a system constantly reinventing itself" that can "take hold in diverse social contexts under government supervision and within communitarian cultures." The unit's own evidence — a paternalistic, holding-company Japan; a competitive United States; a corporate Europe — is presented as proof of this proliferation of viable institutional forms rather than convergence on one, the kind of divergent, non-convergent outcome WMP associates with the [[divergence-machine]].

**Japan's industrialization inverted the Western sequence and ran through a handful of family-controlled conglomerates.** Lacking raw materials, Japan financed heavy-industry imports with textile exports, let electric motors leapfrog steam power, and concentrated modernization in family pyramids like the Mitsuis, Mitsubishis, and Sumitomos rather than in managers risen from the ranks. Government's direct spending share of investment (7–11 percent) was far below the American figure, yet its influence on capital formation was outsized — a state-guided but not state-owned model exemplified by Sakichi Toyoda's loom business, whose failure to sell into Britain (because organized labor there blocked adoption of labor-displacing machinery) helped tip British textile market share from 82 to 27 percent while Japan's climbed to 39 percent.

**Postwar Japan's institutions were substantially engineered by the American occupation, then redirected by Cold War priorities.** MacArthur's staff wrote Japan's constitution, enfranchised women, and redistributed land, but the Korean War diverted American energy from democratization to rearming an anti-communist ally, cementing the Liberal Democratic Party's near-unbroken postwar dominance and a bureaucracy-business-party collusion that Appleby calls a largely "closed system." William Edwards Deming's quality doctrine and the wartime-necessity-born "just-in-time" system became the technical core of what outsiders later dubbed lean production — precision, small-batch, defect-intolerant manufacturing that Detroit's Big Three resisted copying even as their market share melted.

**The personal computer emerged when IBM chose an open architecture it could not ultimately control.** After hobbyists, Apple, Commodore, and Atari proved the market, IBM's 1980 task force recommended an autonomous unit that bought components — Intel chips, Microsoft's operating system — rather than building proprietary ones. That openness let IBM ship a PC every forty-five seconds within two years, but it also handed Intel and Microsoft the era's most lucrative franchises and, once clones proliferated, eroded IBM's own share from 50 to 22.3 percent by 1999. Japan, meanwhile, chose the opposite bet — continuing to build mainframes — which paid off unexpectedly when the Internet and corporate private networks revived demand for large systems.

## Notable passages

**The Keynesian consensus cracks (c1).** The unit's opening diagnosis — "the comfortable understanding among big business, big labor, and big government was coming apart" — states plainly why 1970s stagflation was a crisis of theory, not just of prices.

**Friedman's rise and monetarism's limits (c2).** The passage tracing Friedman from Nobel laureate to Thatcher-Reagan favorite closes on the sober coda that monetarism, even with Federal Reserve backing from 1979–1982, "failed to keep the country from sliding into recession" — the unit's clearest statement that the new orthodoxy did not simply solve what the old one couldn't.

**The savings and loan collapse (c3).** "Sailing faster with less ballast" captures, in one image, how loosened accounting and down-payment rules turned a deregulatory reform into a taxpayer-funded disaster of over seven hundred failed institutions.

**Capitalism as a work in progress (c4).** Answering Tom Wolfe's obituary for capitalism, the unit's claim that it is "a system constantly reinventing itself" and that success stories prove it is "in fact, always adapting" is the thesis statement that licenses the Japan and PC case studies that follow.

**The Toyoda loom and British labor rigidity (c5).** Toyoda's automatic loom failed to sell in Britain because organized labor blocked it, and "by acceding to them for short-run peace, the British industry lost its preeminence in the world market" — a compact case study in how resistance to disruption cedes ground to a more flexible competitor.

**IBM's open-architecture decision (c6).** The 1980 task force recommendation that IBM "buy the component parts for its microcomputers from those available on the market rather than create and patent its own" is the single decision the unit credits with creating both the PC industry's speed of growth and IBM's later loss of control over it.
