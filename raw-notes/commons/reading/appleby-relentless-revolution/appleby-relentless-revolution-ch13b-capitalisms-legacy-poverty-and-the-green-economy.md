# Capitalism's Legacy: Poverty, the Green Revolution, and the Book's Final Reckoning

This is the book's concluding chapter, run in five movements: a compressed recap of capitalism's history from sixteenth-century aristocratic contempt for merchants through the 2008 crisis; a taxonomy of capitalism's contemporary critics; a tour of practical anti-poverty thinkers and institutions (Yunus, Sen, De Soto, Barnes, Lappé, Bello, Patel); a short survey of an emerging green-technology response to capitalism's environmental toll; and a closing meditation on capitalism's fraught relationship with democracy and equality that ends on the book's title-referencing final line.

## Themes

**Capitalism was built, legitimized, and only later naturalized — it did not simply emerge.** The recap opens by insisting capitalism's flaws are visible in its history: commerce moved from the interstices of warrior societies to the center of modern ones, and its early proponents had to actively argue it into acceptance as "natural, liberating, progressive, and rewarding" before it could disrupt settled communities. Read against the World Machines framework, this is a textbook description of a machine's Dawn — new infrastructure (markets, factories, joint-stock ventures) assembling quietly for two centuries before becoming dominant, then running through Day into the stagflation and "perennial gale of creative destruction" (Schumpeter) that mark its most recent turbulence — a proliferation-and-churn dynamic close to what the project calls the [[divergence-machine]]. The unit's account of the 1970s fraying of the postwar big-business/big-labor/big-government consensus, with organized labor "losing its purchase on the popular imagination" just as employer power waxed, reads as a small-scale [[gramsci-gap]]: an old settlement dying with no replacement consensus yet in view.

**Capitalism's critics split into three camps, and Appleby favors the reformists.** She sorts opposition into an elite aesthetic distaste for vulgarity and materialism, an anti-globalization critique of multinational "rapacity," and a third group that works within capitalism's own logic to make it fairer — Yunus, Sen, De Soto, Barnes, Lappé, Bello, and Patel all belong to this last camp, which Appleby calls "the most constructive."

**Anti-poverty innovation redirects capitalism's own tools toward the excluded poor.** Collier's "bottom billion" — fifty-seven states trapped by civil war, resource curses, and bad governance — anchors the problem. Yunus's Grameen Bank answers it by extending small, collateral-free loans (starting with $27 of his own money) to women shut out of formal credit and charged 30 percent by loan sharks; De Soto answers it by fighting for legal land title so the poor's existing assets can serve as collateral; Sen reframes the goal itself, arguing that freedom and "social capabilities" — not just income — are the proper measure of development; Barnes proposes monetizing "the commons" (air, water, the corporate charter itself) to fund public goods, citing the Alaska Permanent Fund. All of this amounts to poor people's own striving for security and growth — a conatus-like drive — being unlocked once legal and financial infrastructure stops blocking it; where that infrastructure is captured by loan sharks, missing land titles, or bureaucratic licensing (De Soto counts seventy-seven procedures to register land in Egypt), that striving is suppressed exactly the way the World Machines framework describes [[capture-resistance]] and [[Conatus]] interacting. Critics of these programs note the same meritocratic bias runs through them as through capitalism generally: the most capable poor benefit, the least capable do not.

**A green-technology response is emerging, driven by price signals more than philanthropy.** Oil-price shocks, Al Gore's cultural moment, and cheap Rust Belt factory space are pulling venture capital into wind, solar, nuclear icebreakers, zero-energy German housing, and European bike-share schemes — presented as commercially self-interested, not charitable, and as necessary infrastructure if poverty itself is ever to be "confined to museums."

**Capitalism has no coordinating center, which is both its creative strength and the reason no one is accountable for its damage.** Appleby is explicit that capitalism is "not a unified, coordinated system" but a decentralized aggregate of self-interested actors — closer to the World Machines idea of [[Configurancy]] (non-teleological co-emergence) than to any designed institution — which is precisely why crises like the subprime meltdown happen without any single actor intending them.

**Democracy and capitalism are necessary but incompatible partners, and equality remains an unfinished project.** The two systems answer to different values — majority-rule morality versus market amorality — and Appleby warns, via Madison, that when government and the economy "read off the same profit sheet," the market's self-correcting mechanism is disabled; that collusion is itself a capture failure in the World Machines sense. Equality, she notes, is an Enlightenment-born ideal that remains "more ideal than real, but an ideal with legs."

**The closing verdict weighs Schumpeter's self-destruction thesis against capitalism's demonstrated capacity to learn.** Schumpeter thought capitalism might destroy the very institutions (stable families, honest auditors) that protect it; Appleby's rebuttal is that market participants' experience and expectations shift across generations, so societies keep modifying and monitoring their economies rather than simply repeating past failures — landing on the book's final formulation: "relentless," but "not mindless."

## Notable passages

The opening recap frames the whole chapter's thesis — that capitalism's problems are legible in its origin story of moving from society's margins to its center — before tracing the accumulation of "impressive savings" that funded the earliest foreign ventures.

Appleby's statement that capitalism has "no coordination from any center" is the unit's sharpest theoretical claim: it explains both capitalism's inventiveness and its liability for crises like the 2008 meltdown, since no actor designed the outcome.

The Yunus material supplies two load-bearing moments: the founding empirical insight (villagers paying loan sharks 30 percent interest because they lacked collateral) and his aspirational endpoint, the "poverty museum," explicitly modeled on the historical abolition of slavery.

The closing pages pivot to the book's real argument — that democracy and capitalism are "the couple that can neither live with nor live without each other" — before the final paragraph stages Schumpeter's pessimism against Appleby's own qualified optimism, ending on the line the book takes its title from.
