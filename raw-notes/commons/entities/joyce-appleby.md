# Joyce Appleby

Historian of capitalism; author of *The Relentless Revolution: A History of Capitalism* (Norton,
2010) — see [[the-relentless-revolution]]. This page records her method and her positions as they
appear across that book's reading notes.

## The signature move: refusing inevitability

Appleby's method is stated as an instruction to the reader: "I want readers to resist the temptation
to bring capitalism onto the historic stage as something inevitable, because it was not"
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch04b-currency-crisis-and-new-economic-thinking]]). The
methodological principle underneath it is compressed into a phrase in the opening chapter — "a
succession is not a process." The preconditions historians reach for (printing, clocks, the New
World, papal law) were all present in places that never became capitalist, so capitalism required
the accidental convergence of "many elements, some fortuitous" rather than any developmental chain
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch01-puzzle-of-capitalism]]).

She applies this consistently rather than only at the origin:

- **Against the Marxist causal story.** Where the standard account has farsighted landlords coercing
  tenants into commercial leases to expose them to market discipline, Appleby holds that "new social
  relations were the consequence, not the cause" of agricultural transformation — a multi-generational
  "slow, untidy sorting out" across the whole agrarian order
  ([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch03b-dutch-and-english-farming-improvements]]).
- **Against "Industrial Revolution."** She prefers "Industrial Evolution," reaching for Darwinian
  vocabulary — "replications," "random variations," "waste," "survival of the fittest" — to describe
  a century-plus of incremental trial and error, while conceding the conventional term is here to
  stay ([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch05a-slavery-labor-and-industrial-inventions]]).
- **Against determinist readings of American supremacy.** The "aura of an inexorable and optimal
  development generated from within the very logic of capitalist enterprise" is misleading;
  American dominance reflects specific political circumstances — weak government, cheap labour, a
  public receptive to standardized goods
  ([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch08b-corporate-restructuring-and-american-industry]]).
- **Against economic laws in postwar politics.** European integration "came from individuals like
  Monet and Schuman, not from any economic laws"
  ([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch10a-marshall-plan-and-postwar-consumer-economy]]).

The positive counterpart is her comparative thesis about how change actually happens: the
physiocrats "were attempting to do by command what English entrepreneurs had done for themselves."
Authority-driven reform is fragile because it depends on rulers whose successors may not share their
goals; dispersed self-interested experimentation "bungled as often as they hit their mark" but
changed ordinary people as it spread, and proved more durable
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch04b-currency-crisis-and-new-economic-thinking]]).

## What she takes from her predecessors

Appleby reads Smith, Marx and Weber against each other. Smith naturalized capitalism by projecting a
timeless "desire of bettering our condition" onto human nature, "turn[ing] an effect into a cause."
Marx supplied a coercive, class-based mechanism but assumed a market mentality that could not yet
have existed. Weber she credits most — for insisting pre-capitalist Europeans had none of the
psychology later economists assumed, and for tracing "the spirit of capitalism" to the unintended
anxieties of Protestant soteriology rather than to any natural drive. She rejects the later material
explanations in the same pass: Diamond's geographic determinism, Landes's blended-factors narrative
with no interactive mechanism, Crosby's purely intellectual account, and Pomeranz's global-parity
thesis ([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch01-puzzle-of-capitalism]]; cf. [[adam-smith]],
[[david-s-landes]]).

Her use of Schumpeter is two-sided. She adopts "creative destruction" as the deep logic under
railroad-building, telegraphy and industrial competition — "commercial objects don't grow old; they
just become obsolete when they are replaced by something better"
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch06b-banks-incorporation-and-the-1873-depression]]) — and closes
the book by arguing *against* his thesis that capitalism destroys the institutions protecting it:
because participants' experience and expectations shift across generations, societies keep modifying
and monitoring their economies
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch13b-capitalisms-legacy-poverty-and-the-green-economy]]).

## The moral ledger she keeps open

Appleby's anti-teleology is not a way of letting capitalism off. She refuses the conventional
separation of slavery from "the contributions of a free enterprise system," treating plantation and
factory as "twin responses to the capitalist genie," and argues racial ideology followed enslavement
rather than preceding it — "circular reasoning kicked in," whereby the traits slavery instilled were
then cited as reasons for bondage
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch05a-slavery-labor-and-industrial-inventions]]). She is equally
blunt about what the transition did not fix: "Capitalism didn't eliminate oppressive upper classes.
It just changed the basis upon which they stood," leaving old master-servant law and anti-union
statutes intact under new management
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch04b-currency-crisis-and-new-economic-thinking]]).

She also declines the easy version of the abolition story, flagging the historiographical debate
over whether British abolition reflected genuine moral outrage or the sugar industry's declining
profitability, and concluding the economic-decline thesis has been undermined by data showing West
Indian trade was still rising when Parliament acted
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch05a-slavery-labor-and-industrial-inventions]]).

Her final position sorts capitalism's critics into three camps — elite aesthetic distaste,
anti-globalization critique of multinational "rapacity," and reformers working within capitalism's
own logic (Yunus, Sen, De Soto, Barnes, Lappé, Bello, Patel) — and calls the third "the most
constructive." Inequality remains capitalism's persistent "shame"; equality is "more ideal than
real, but an ideal with legs"
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch13b-capitalisms-legacy-poverty-and-the-green-economy]]).

## Her epistemology of a system nobody runs

Two claims recur and belong together. First, capitalism is opaque to its own participants: dispersed
private decisions generate prices that "deliver information" nobody centrally interprets until a
crisis forces the question — "No one, as it were, is in charge"
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch06b-banks-incorporation-and-the-1873-depression]]). Second,
"the freer the market system, the more widespread are individual initiatives that pull along in
their train the unintended consequences of their actions" — her structural, non-moral reading of
2008 ([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch13a-the-2008-financial-crisis]]). Hence her closing
insistence that capitalism is "not a unified, coordinated system" and that its health depends on a
legal and civic substrate external to the market itself
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch13b-capitalisms-legacy-poverty-and-the-green-economy]]).

## Why she is useful to the World Machines project

Appleby is a working historian who arrives independently at several of WMP's structural commitments:
distributed, uncoordinated emergence made visible only in retrospect ([[history-machine]]);
proliferation of institutional forms rather than convergence ([[divergence-machine]]); and a
sceptical treatment of "progress" as a nineteenth-century ideological achievement rather than a
description — the Crystal Palace is where "progress" stopped meaning mere movement and became a
value-laden destination
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch06a-german-and-american-industrialization]]; compare
[[fiat-progress]], [[argument-of-progress]]). She is a useful check precisely because she is not
working in the frame and does not owe it anything.
