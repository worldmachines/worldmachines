# Thomas More

**Author of** [[more-utopia]], and — as "Mr. More" — a character inside its own dialogue.

Two figures share this name in the work: the historical author sketched in Henry Morley's editorial introduction, and "More" the persona inside Book 1, who argues the case *for* serving princes that the book's other voice, [[raphael-hythloday]], refuses.

## The historical More (per Morley's introduction)

From [[more-utopia-introduction]]:

- Born in Milk Street, London, in 1478; placed as a boy in Cardinal John Morton's household, where "the youth wore his patron's livery" and Morton predicted his greatness at table. Patronage is presented as "the ladder of Tudor advancement."
- Studied the new Greek learning at Oxford under Grocyn and Linacre, then law at Lincoln's Inn.
- In 1503 opposed Henry VII's marriage-subsidy in Parliament forcefully enough that "the House refused to grant it," costing him royal favour and nearly driving him from the country — an early political courage the introduction frames as the same willingness to needle power that animates *Utopia*.
- Served on the 1515–16 diplomatic embassy to the Low Countries with Cuthbert Tunstal, negotiated at Bruges, and befriended Peter Giles at Antwerp — documented fact right up to the point where it "slides into fiction with the finding of Raphael Hythloday."
- Composed *Utopia* back-to-front (Book 2 first, late 1515; Book 1 added early 1516); it was printed at Louvain, Basle, Paris, and Vienna but never in England in his lifetime.

## More the persona: the case for engagement

Inside the dialogue, "Mr. More" is the strongest voice for working within power rather than withdrawing from it:

- [[more-utopia-book1-02-serving-princes]]: the fountain metaphor — "the springs both of good and evil flow from the prince over a whole nation, as from a lasting fountain" — so influence at the source outweighs the discomfort of compromise.
- [[more-utopia-book1-07-philosophy-in-courts]]: his "civil philosophy" — a pliable philosophy that "knows its proper scene," plays its assigned part in the ongoing "comedy" of court life, and settles for making a bad situation "as little ill as possible" rather than ruining the play with an unwelcome tragic speech. He also invokes Plato's philosopher-kings ([[more-utopia-book1-05-jester-and-mock-counsel]]) to press Hythloday to serve.

## The withheld assent

[[more-utopia-book2-08-religions-04-conclusion]]: after Hythloday's full peroration, "More" the character finds much "absurd," judges Raphael too weary and too invested in his own wisdom to be safely contradicted, and closes the book by wishing "rather than hoping" to see Utopian laws followed at home. The structural irony runs the other way from the biography: the man who once opposed a king in Parliament and then served one as ambassador wrote a persona who lets the withdrawal-advocate have the last, unanswered word.
