# Raphael Hythloday

**The traveller-narrator of** [[more-utopia]] — the fictional Portuguese philosopher whose five years on the island supply the whole of Book 2, and whose refusal to serve a prince drives the whole of Book 1.

## Introduction and credentials

[[more-utopia-book1-01-meeting-hythloday]]: introduced to "Mr. More" by Peter Giles in Antwerp during a lull in the 1515 embassy. Weathered and bearded, he is read at first as "a seaman," then corrected by Giles into "a traveller, or rather a philosopher," learned in Greek. He gave up his inheritance to sail with three of Amerigo Vespucci's four voyages, had himself left behind at the edge of New Castile, and journeyed through equatorial wastes into a chain of well-governed civilizations. His interest — and the book's — is not monsters but "states that are well and wisely governed." He is the work's model of the traveller as an alternative epistemic authority (compare [[Explorer vs. Tourist]]): worth is detached from home, burial, or return, since "he that had no grave had the heavens still over him."

## The refuser of service

Across Book 1 he declines to become a prince's counsellor, and the refusal is a considered ethic, not modesty:

- [[more-utopia-book1-02-serving-princes]]: "The change of the word does not alter the matter" — to "assist" a king is to "enslave" oneself; having discharged his debts to friends and kin, "now I live as I will." Courts are closed flattery-loops that dismiss any better proposal with "such or such things pleased our ancestors." The notes read this as [[capture-resistance]] and Spinoza's [[Conatus]] avant la lettre — the striving to persist in one's own nature against an institution that would harness it to extrinsic ends.
- [[more-utopia-book1-07-philosophy-in-courts]]: against More's "civil philosophy," Hythloday holds that honesty forecloses the compromise, that proximity contaminates ("the ill company will sooner corrupt him than be the better for him"), and that Plato's rain-simile makes self-preserving withdrawal the last honest posture.

## The diagnostician of England

At Cardinal Morton's table he delivers the book's sharpest social criticism:

- [[more-utopia-book1-03-cardinal-morton-theft]]: idle retainers, standing armies, and enclosure ("sheep devour men") manufacture the thieves the law then hangs.
- [[more-utopia-book1-04-polylerits-punishment]]: restitution and marked penal labour instead of the gallows.
- [[more-utopia-book1-05-jester-and-mock-counsel]]: the Achorian fable against conquest.
- [[more-utopia-book1-06-treasure-and-corrupt-counsel]]: the five arts of fiscal extraction, answered by the Macarian oath capping royal gold.

## The witness of Utopia

[[more-utopia-book1-08-common-ownership]]: challenged on whether common ownership can work, Hythloday shifts registers from argument to eyewitness — "if you had been in Utopia with me... for the space of five years" — converting the rest of the book into travel report rather than speculation. He then narrates all of Book 2, closing with the "conspiracy of the rich" peroration ([[more-utopia-book2-08-religions-04-conclusion]]): every money-based government is "a conspiracy of the rich, who, on pretence of managing the public, only pursue their private ends."

## Name and function

[[more-utopia-introduction]] marks the finding of Hythloday as the exact hinge where documented fact "slides into fiction," and locates in the same passage the origin of the word "Utopian" as "an impracticable scheme." The narrator is himself part of the book's play between the plausible and the impossible: an eyewitness whose testimony is unanswerable precisely because it can never be checked.
