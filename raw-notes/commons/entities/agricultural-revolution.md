# The Agricultural Revolution (Appleby's account)

In *The Relentless Revolution* the switch from fallow-field farming to continuous crop rotation is
the causal keystone of capitalism's emergence — the change Appleby argues "did more to make
capitalism possible than trade in spices or the parade of industrial machines that gets the credit"
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch03b-dutch-and-english-farming-improvements]]). This page
collects that account across the notes that develop it.

## The trap it broke

Premodern Europe sat in a demographic-agricultural vise. Roughly 80 percent of the population was
needed just to grow food, leaving structurally too little surplus labour or spending money for
manufacturing, trade or luxury — disposable income reached perhaps 15 percent of society, and the
sixteenth century's occupational structure was in this respect no different from Rome's. Fear of
famine made grain the most surveilled commodity in the economy: English law criminalized
engrossing, forestalling and regrating, because "the growing and marketing of grains were as much
social as economic activities." Malthus later formalized the catch-22 — population's power
"superior to the power in the earth to produce subsistence" — while missing Europe's own quiet
counter-mechanism, a uniquely late marriage age that functioned as a voluntary population brake,
reconstructed only afterwards from parish registers
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch03a-basket-of-consumables-and-population]]).

The Columbian Exchange widened the "basket of consumables" — maize, potatoes, and above all sugar,
acting "like so many insurance policies against famine" — but unevenly and at a cost. The potato
drove earlier marriage and denser population in Ireland, Poland and China, then supplied a single
point of failure when the 1840s blight killed or displaced a third of Ireland's population; sugar
built a slave-labour plantation economy in the Caribbean before it reached the ordinary European
pantry ([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch03a-basket-of-consumables-and-population]]).

## The mechanism

Dutch farmers broke the bind between feeding people and feeding soil. Instead of fallowing a third
or a quarter of arable land, they discovered that clover and other legumes fix nitrogen and rotated
grain, turnips, hay and clover through four-part cycles — "the virtuous circle of growth replaced
the vicious circle of decline." English farmers copied and adapted it (four-field rotation in some
regions, "up-and-down husbandry" in others) while exploiting marl, sea sand and ground seashells
according to local soil ([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch03b-dutch-and-english-farming-improvements]]).

## Why it was slow

Appleby is emphatic that improvement was not opportunity meeting a natural drive to self-betterment.
Custom, risk aversion and even the "biorhythms of premodern men and women," unaccustomed to
sustained daily labour, worked against adoption; being a rational economic actor "didn't make a lot
of sense" when risk routinely outpaced reward. Only freehold farmers and landlords who had converted
fixed rents into market-rate leases were positioned to gamble on new technique, and it was their
conspicuous success — bigger harvests, falling grain prices that punished the unimproved — that
eventually built momentum
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch03b-dutch-and-english-farming-improvements]]).

## Consequence, not cause

The chapter's argumentative spine reverses the standard Marxist story. Rather than landlords
coercing tenants into market discipline, "new social relations were the consequence, not the cause"
of agricultural transformation — an unplanned, five-or-six-generation "slow, untidy sorting out"
running from cottagers to great lords. Enclosure raised productivity while dissolving the network of
community obligation that had bound villages, provoking pamphlet wars (the Reverend John Moore's
charge that enclosure abandoned Christian duty to the poor against the Reverend Joseph Lee's defence
of private incentive) and prompting the Elizabethan Poor Laws as the institutional replacement for
communal absorption of the vulnerable. Local markets unified into a national one — legible,
interchangeable food distribution that contemporaries found disorienting, as one lament over having
to "carry our Corn God knows where" records
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch03b-dutch-and-english-farming-improvements]]).

This is a [[history-machine]]-shaped account: a paradigm assembling itself out of thousands of
independent farm-level decisions, visible as a "revolution" only in retrospect. The grain-regulation
apparatus it displaced, and the national market it built, are both instances of
[[legibility-as-machine-core]] operating on food — first to prevent riot and starvation, then to
move a commodity.

## What it made possible

England and the Netherlands were, on Appleby's account, the only European states to expand
population and income simultaneously in the seventeenth century, crossing the threshold of freedom
from famine that separated them from their own past and from a Europe where war, static technique
and (in Russia and Poland) serfdom kept agriculture stagnant
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch03b-dutch-and-english-farming-improvements]]).

The industrial payoff is specific. The agricultural revolution cut England's farm workforce from
80 to 40 percent, driving up wages for the labour that remained; a domestic
[[putting-out-system]] was already dispersing capital and manufacturing skill through the
countryside; and abundant cheap coal substituted for depleting timber. High wages plus cheap fuel is
Appleby's stated arithmetic behind inventing "machines using fuel that could vastly increase the
output of human laborers"
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch05a-slavery-labor-and-industrial-inventions]]).

## A later, different episode

The twentieth-century Green Revolution is a separate event with a partly inverted moral. High-yield
seeds doubled Asian cereal production and rural incomes, but favoured farmers who could afford the
new inputs — widening rural inequality — and later stalled as governments cut irrigation and
research spending
([[appleby-relentless-revolution-ch12b-eastern-europe-and-indias-transformation]]). Where the
seventeenth-century transformation freed labour and capital for something else, this one distributed
its gains along an existing gradient of means.

## World Machines resonances

The pre-1650 pattern Appleby describes — population growth pressing against a food ceiling, then
collapse and recovery, with the Black Death killing roughly half the Continent — is the substrate
[[secular-cycles]] models; her account of the break is a claim about a cycle being exited rather
than completed. The energy framing (coal substituting for depleting timber, fuel-burning machines
substituting for human and animal muscle) connects to [[machines-as-energy-regimes]] and
[[vaclav-smil]].
