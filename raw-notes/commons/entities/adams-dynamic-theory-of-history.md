# Adams's Dynamic Theory of History

Henry Adams's attempt, built over roughly six years and stated outright in the last chapters of [[adams-education]], to make history a branch of mechanics: to define Progress as "the development and economy of Forces," to find a unit in which historical force could be measured, and to extrapolate a rate. It is the most sustained pre-twentieth-century attempt at the thing [[psychohistory-and-world-machines]] describes, made by a professional historian who wanted the deterministic version and could not get it.

## The Two-Book Frame

The theory's oldest component is a measurement scheme, not a philosophy. Adams's explanation appended to the *Education* and quoted by Lodge states it: "man as a force must be measured by motion from a fixed point," and he took the century 1150-1250 — Amiens Cathedral, the works of Thomas Aquinas — as the fixed unit, letting him "project his lines forward and backward indefinitely" ([[adams-education-editors-preface]]). *Mont-Saint-Michel and Chartres* supplies the thirteenth-century pole of Unity; the *Education* supplies the twentieth-century pole of Multiplicity; the theory is what you get by measuring the motion between them.

## Assembly

**The first sketch (1892), unrecognized.** The acceleration idea arrives long before the theory does, and arrives as a shipboard idle thought. Crossing on the *Teutonic* in [[adams-education-ch-21-twenty-years-after]], Adams turns the raw experience of a faster, more comfortable Atlantic passage into an implicit geometric progression — Niagara is to *Teutonic* as 1860 is to 1890, so what must 1890 be to "the next term"? The same instinct to quantify shows up in the same chapter when he measures American coal output (160 million tons) against the British Empire's (180 million) as the one place he can still locate the era's real motive force. Both moves are already the finished theory in miniature — a ratio, a proxy, an extrapolation — and Adams makes nothing of either for another decade.

**The turn (1899).** In [[adams-education-ch-24-indian-summer]] Adams, at sixty-one, "swept his mind clear of knowledge" and resolved to correlate twelfth- and twentieth-century political, social and scientific values "in some relation of movement that could be expressed in mathematics," modelling himself explicitly on Kepler and Newton and reaching for "a historical formula that should satisfy the conditions of the stellar universe" — down to seeking "a value, even infinitesimal," for the "attraction" of Congress. He found no teacher: Newcomb too orthodox, Gibbs unreachable, Langley sympathetic but suspicious of philosophy, leaving him Judge Stallo's neglected *Concepts of Modern Science* and his own isolation. The same chapter supplies the maxim that governs everything after: **chaos cannot be taught** — his explanation for why decades as a Harvard professor never produced "the working of law in history."

**The unit problem (1902).** [[adams-education-ch-29-the-abyss-of-ignorance]] is the search for a "unit of force" and its failure. Occult attraction, Pascal's ennui as "master-motor of man," the Virgin's Love, Aquinas's Multiplicity, the kinetic theory of gases, the new psychology of the divided mind — each promised Unity dissolves back into Multiplicity, until the kinetic theory makes "the scientific synthesis commonly called Unity" identical to "the scientific analysis commonly called Multiplicity" and Adams drops "the sounder into the abyss." Unable to settle the question, he converts it into method: fix two points, measure motion, and publish "subject to correction from any one who should know better."

**Politics as mechanics (1902).** [[adams-education-ch-28-the-height-of-knowledge]] states the doctrine in its political form. Domestic power is generated by "masses of mechanical power -- steam, electric, furnace" and merely channeled by the men who happen to run it, who become "forces as dumb as their dynamos." "Modern politics is, at bottom, a struggle not of men but of forces." Adams names the program in the same breath: "triangulate the future" and "fix the acceleration of movement in politics since the year 1200."

**Inertia (1903).** [[adams-education-ch-30-vis-inertiae]] is the vocabulary being built in public. Watching Russia roll toward the Pacific, Adams goes "to his books" for the dictionary's definition of mechanical inertia and translates it into a theory of mind: thought moving only along habitual "motives" is inertia, thought redirected by "newer or higher attraction" is force. Russia's "vis inertiae" is not policy at all — the Siberian Railway is less an instrument than the mechanism by which an already-moving glacier reaches the sea, and Hay's open-door policy is doomed by mass rather than by any error of statecraft. Adams then ranks inertias: "of all movements of inertia, maternity and reproduction are the most typical," the one force uniting history "in its only unbroken and unbreakable sequence." (On the "race-inertia"/"sex-inertia" framing and its handling, see [[adams-education]].)

**Instrumentation (1903).** [[adams-education-ch-31-the-grammar-of-science]] supplies the methodological posture. Adams foregrounds his "fatal handicap of ignorance in mathematics" and converts it into a privilege: he reads Pearson, Poincare and Mach not to judge their science but to track "the measurement of thought-motion as marked by the accepted thinkers," casting himself as "a mere instrument of measure, a barometer, pedometer, radiometer" seeking only "drift or direction" rather than truth.

**A companion theory (1893).** Adams was not working entirely alone: [[adams-education-ch-22-chicago]] records his brother Brooks supplying a rival law of history — that economic exchange, not politics or ideas, is the substrate driving historical instability, with "the speed of acceleration" outrunning any thinker's ability to keep pace, and predicting "not collectivism, but anarchism" from the capital-labor disequilibrium. It is systemic, non-biographical, and already worried about acceleration outpacing thought. Henry's own contribution is not the acceleration intuition, which was in the family, but the attempt to attach a unit to it.

**The decision (1904).** [[adams-education-ch-32-vis-nova]] is where he stops borrowing. Each day of the Russo-Japanese run-up is treated as "a measure of relative energy on the historical scale," the affair as "a Grammar of new Science quite as instructive as that of Pearson." Then, in Troyes, a street bulletin announcing Plehve's assassination collides with a day among twelfth-century glass, and the vertigo drives him back to his garret that autumn "to shape after his own needs the values of a Dynamic Theory of History."

## The Theory

[[adams-education-ch-33-a-dynamic-theory-of-history]] states it. Five moves:

1. **The inversion.** "A dynamic theory... takes for granted that the forces of nature capture man." Man is the captured particle, not the capturing agent — a spider in a web, an animal educated by fire, running water and grain, whose "body and thought are alike their product." Susceptibility to force, not mastery of it, is "the highest genius."
2. **Economy, not volume.** Most of recorded history before 1500 shows no growth in the volume of force available to man, only "economies of energy" — roads, coinage, law — applied to forces known since deep antiquity. Genuine historical rupture requires a new force entering the system, not a more efficient use of an old one.
3. **Institutions as overcapitalized trusts.** Constantine's Edict of Milan is read as speculation rather than piety: he "merged all uncertain forces into a single trust, which he enormously overcapitalized, and forced on the market," using the Cross as literal ordnance — "In hoc signo vinces!... a train of artillery."
4. **The Baconian hinge.** Bacon's demand to "try evolving thought from the universe" instead of "evolving the universe from a thought" is the pivot of modern history; after it, "thought did not evolve nature, but nature evolved thought." Newton, Franklin and Watt follow as conductors, not inventors.
5. **Decline as excess.** Rome collapsed from "excess... of action and reaction," not deficiency: the empire "should, as a problem of mechanics, have been torn to pieces by acceleration."

## The Law of Acceleration

[[adams-education-ch-34-a-law-of-acceleration]] supplies the rate. History is a mass drawn toward "a new centre, or preponderating mass, artificially introduced on earth," accelerating until it reaches a new equilibrium, like Newton's comet. Lacking any instrument for "social movement," Adams settles for what nineteenth-century society already measured — coal-power — and reads its doubling roughly every ten years between 1840 and 1900 as the growth of total historical force. He then carries the ratio backward through the mathematicians (Laplace, Newton, Descartes), the architects (St. Peter's, Amiens, Hagia Sophia) and finally prehistoric stone tools, conceding at each step that the numbers get less trustworthy while insisting the sequence does not. The claim hardens into law: "A law of acceleration, definite and constant as any law of mechanics, cannot be supposed to relax its energy to suit the convenience of man."

Two of the chapter's moves matter more than the arithmetic. First, Adams records a failed prophecy in his own field's recent memory: mid-century science, following John Stuart Mill, expected force to plateau, a conviction that "lasted through the eighties" until radium disproved it — a case study in how slow an institution is to see an acceleration already visible in its data. Second, he refuses smooth extrapolation. The closing image is thought as "common salt in indefinite solution" approaching a phase change: multiplicity outrunning the mind's capacity to unify it, forcing a break into "a new phase subject to new laws." The mind "would need to jump."

## The Forecast

[[adams-education-ch-35-nunc-age]] turns the theory into a dated, checkable claim: acceleration measured since 1800 would let "the mathematician of 1950" plot the human race's "past and future orbit... as accurately as that of the November meteoroids." The same chapter reads Roosevelt's antitrust campaign not as politics but "a measure of motion" — the Trusts "stood for the larger part of the new power that had been created since 1840" — and leaves the theory's real question open: whether the new forces can breed a "new man," since the old type "had plainly reached the end of his strength, and his failure had become catastrophic."

## Adams's Own Objections

The theory is not left undefended against itself, and the caveats are Adams's:

- **Chaos cannot be taught** ([[adams-education-ch-24-indian-summer]]). Adams separates the lived sense of "possible purpose working itself out in history" from any transmissible method: "This was history, not education."
- **Larger synthesis is suicide.** [[adams-education-ch-26-twilight]] closes on the warning that chasing a "larger synthesis" to unify anarchy repeats theology's mistake, and "a point must always be soon reached where larger synthesis is suicide." Its resolve is not prediction but a plan to "float with the stream" if one can learn which way the current runs.
- **Unity buys friction, not resolution.** The same chapter's central paradox — "the greater the unity and the momentum, the worse became the complexity and the friction" — undercuts the acceleration curve's implied convergence.
- **The scale is honest about its own cost.** The historian must "triangulate from the widest possible base" toward a horizon two generations off, and doing so leads into "a wilderness where twilight is short and the shadows are dense."

## Assessment

The theory never becomes testable. [[adams-education-ch-30-vis-inertiae]] shows the mechanism: Adams imports vocabulary from a dictionary definition and rides the analogy; he does not derive anything from it. What he has is a proxy series (coal) he admits he cannot validate, a rate he cannot explain, and a metaphor of attraction doing the work a mechanism would have to do.

What survives is the framing, and it is closer to [[world-machines]] than most later cyclical historiography: an era organized by an infrastructural force nobody chose ([[machines-as-energy-regimes]]), historical actors demoted to where the force happens to surface ([[history-machine]], [[witness-bias-toward-design]]), and a refusal to treat the transition to whatever follows as continuous or safe ([[gramsci-gap]]). Adams also earned an epistemic posture the framework had to argue its way to — see [[world-machines-as-interim-theory]] — by pure exhaustion: two fixed points, a measured motion, and a standing offer of correction to anyone who should know better.
