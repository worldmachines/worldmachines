# Protocol Gods and Platform Demons

## The Synthesis

[[The Small God of the Internet]] gives us a mythopoeic frame that generalizes beyond RSS: open protocols have theological virtues built into their architecture, while platforms are extractive demons that invert those virtues.

### The Protocol Virtues

| Virtue | RSS (ArrEsEs) | Principle |
|---|---|---|
| Patience | "He'll wait. He always has." | Pull-based; respects user attention |
| Order | Chronological | Reality has sequence; algorithms obscure it |
| Silence | "He does silence properly" | Absence is meaningful; platforms can't afford silence |
| Memory | "He remembers what you finished" | User-state belongs to the user |
| Non-intrusion | No "You Might Also Like" | No cross-subsidization of attention |

### The Platform Inversions

The [[The Gloomwheel|Gloomwheel]] systematically inverts each virtue:
- **Patience → Urgency**: platforms manufacture FOMO and "trending" to demand real-time attention
- **Order → Ranking**: chronological order is replaced by engagement maximization
- **Silence → Infinite scroll**: there is no bottom; silence is punished
- **Memory → Amnesia**: you can't reliably find what you saw before; your history serves their retargeting
- **Non-intrusion → Injection**: "You Might Also Like" is the canonical form of platform value extraction

## The Enclosure Argument

The RSS essay describes platforms as "throwing dams across [RSS's] tributaries and calling them platforms." This is a classic commons enclosure:
1. An open commons (the web, feeds, the River of Posts) is created through distributed effort
2. Platforms privatize the routing layer (the feed algorithm, the recommendation graph)
3. Good water (open signals) disappears behind walls; the rest is channeled into "ornamental channels that reflect only the palace"

This connects to the intellectual lineage of internet commons scholarship (Benkler, Zittrain — unacknowledged in Sathe's essay).

## The Persistence of Small Gods

Despite enclosure, the small gods persist in low-traffic places: "home pages with kettles on, personal sites that remember how to wave, gardeners who publish their lists of other gardeners." The personal web is the commons that survived enclosure — small, marginalized, but alive.

The small god's survival strategy: "If you invite him, tomorrow he will be there." Protocols survive not by competing with platforms but by being available when users turn toward them.
