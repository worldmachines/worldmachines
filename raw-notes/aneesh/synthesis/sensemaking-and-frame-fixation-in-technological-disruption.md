# Sensemaking and Frame Fixation in Technological Disruption

## The Central Synthesis

[[How to Make Sense of AI]] and [[How Experts Sensemake]] together constitute a two-layer argument about expertise and technological change:

1. **Practical layer** (Part 1): Filter AI content ruthlessly; consume only field reports; apply the [[Four Questions of Uncertainty]]
2. **Theoretical grounding** (Part 2): The [[Data-Frame Theory of Sensemaking]] explains *why* this works — and reveals what can still go wrong

The synthesis is: **the primary failure mode for experienced practitioners facing revolutionary technology is [[Frame Fixation]] — not ignorance, not lack of exposure, but the inability to let go of the expert frames that have made them successful.**

## Why Expertise Creates Vulnerability

Paradoxically, expertise creates a *specific* type of vulnerability during technological disruption:

- Novices don't have frames to protect; they absorb new information easily but lack the pattern libraries to do anything useful with it
- Experts have rich, productive frames honed over years — but those frames filter what counts as data; new capabilities that only make sense within a *new* frame are literally invisible to the expert with a fixed old frame

The NICU nurse (from Klein's examples) couldn't have spotted early sepsis without a "sepsis frame" available. By analogy: a senior software engineer who doesn't have a frame for "AI agents as primary developers" will literally not see — will not register as data — the field reports that indicate this is already happening.

## The "Keep an Open Mind" Trap

The conventional advice for navigating uncertainty — keep an open mind, don't jump to conclusions, reserve judgment — is precisely the *wrong* strategy according to NDM research.

Chin's synthesis of Klein et al: in naturalistic settings, "keep an open mind" strategies consistently underperform frame-first strategies. The reason: you cannot gather relevant data without a frame. An analyst without a committed frame doesn't see more — they see nothing coherently.

The paradox: the "open mind" strategy *feels* epistemically virtuous (humble, cautious, pluralistic) but produces the worst outcomes by preventing the committed elaboration cycle that generates real learning.

## The Correct Counter-Strategy

The Data-Frame theory prescribes:
1. **Commit early to plausible alternative frames** — even before you're sure
2. **Elaborate those frames** — seek confirming evidence (which is cognitively easier than seeking disconfirming evidence)
3. **Monitor vigilantly for anomalies** — maintain specific expectancies that can be violated
4. **Maintain fragment libraries** — the more cases you have, the cheaper it is to construct new frames

Chin's practical translation:
- Maintain a "possible frame" parallel to your operating frame: "maybe AI agents can actually ship good software without human review"
- Actively seek field reports that would confirm or elaborate this frame
- Know which specific data points would force a reframe (e.g., seeing quality collapse in these reports)

## Connection to Fast Adaptation

[[Fast Adaptation Under Uncertainty]] is the competitive stance that emerges from this synthesis. The implication is institutional as well as individual: organizations that structure themselves for fast reframing — information-sharing, experimentation culture, low cost of direction changes — will outperform organizations that optimize for accuracy of initial predictions.

The Walmart case study from Chin is the canonical historical fragment: Walmart didn't predict that PCs would create the world's most successful retailer. They coupled the technology to a new organizational form (decentralized logistics, cross-docking, supplier-facing inventory systems) in a way competitors couldn't easily copy. The sensemaking was in recognizing *that coupling* as the real opportunity.

## Essay Direction

**Seed**: "The Expert's Blind Spot: Why Experience Is the Enemy of Innovation"

The argument: the literature on expertise (NDM, [[Cognitive Task Analysis]]) shows us that experts have powerful frames that make them fast and accurate in familiar territory. But [[Belatedness]] in Bloom's sense and [[Frame Fixation]] in Klein's sense describe the same phenomenon from different angles — the trap of excessive investment in inherited structure. The essay could draw this parallel and propose that the cure for both is the same: deliberate cultivation of alternative frames, even before you believe them.
