# The Sensemaker and the Explorer

## The Identification

The [[Data-Frame Theory of Sensemaking]] describes expert cognition. [[The Explorer and the Tourist]] describes an epistemic posture. They are describing the same figure.

| Explorer (wiki figure) | Expert Sensemaker (Klein) |
|---|---|
| Navigates illegible terrain by creating personal landmarks | Constructs frames from a few anchors in ambiguous situations |
| Treats friction as signal | Treats anomalies as expectancy violations worth investigating |
| Builds shelters to convert space into place | Commits to frames to convert ambiguity into actionable understanding |
| Values breadth and serendipity | Maintains rich fragment libraries for rapid frame construction |
| Engages through play and curiosity | Uses abductive reasoning — inference to the best explanation |

And the negative mapping holds equally:

| Tourist (wiki figure) | Novice / Frame-Dependent Analyst |
|---|---|
| Consumes pre-made legibility | Relies on provided frames; few ready-made frames of their own |
| Treats friction as failure | Treats anomalies as noise or error |
| Inhabits places designed by others | Operates within frames given by training, authority, or convention |
| Values efficiency and completion | Seeks congruence (frame-data match) as quickly as possible |

## What Klein Adds to the Explorer

The explorer-tourist distinction in the wiki has been primarily *descriptive* — a recurring figure identified across sources. Klein's framework gives the explorer a *cognitive mechanism*.

The explorer is someone who:
1. **Commits early** to a provisional frame (builds a base camp)
2. **Elaborates** the frame by actively seeking data that fills its slots (explores from the base camp)
3. **Detects anomalies** because their frame generates specific, violable expectations (notices when the terrain doesn't match the map)
4. **Reframes** when anomalies accumulate (abandons the camp and builds a new one)
5. **Maintains a fragment library** — a repertoire of partial frames from prior experience that makes constructing new frames cheaper (has explored many terrains before)

The tourist, by contrast:
1. Accepts a provided frame (follows the guidebook)
2. Consumes pre-elaborated content (the curated experience)
3. Cannot detect anomalies because their expectations are vague or borrowed
4. Cannot reframe because they have no fragment library to construct alternatives from
5. Has few prior explorations to draw on

## The Paradox of the Explorer Revisited

The existing synthesis notes that the explorer is not a pure nomad — the explorer *builds*. Klein's theory explains why this must be so. The "keep an open mind" strategy — refusing to commit to any frame — is what Klein calls "diagnostic vagabonding." It is the pathology of refusing to build: you wander without landmarks, never generating the specific expectations that would tell you when something is wrong.

The explorer's commitment to building (shelters, thickets, studioli) is not conservative or limiting — it is the prerequisite for genuine discovery. You cannot detect the anomaly that triggers reframing if you never committed to a frame that generated testable expectations.

This resolves a tension in the wiki: the explorer seems to value both openness (breadth, serendipity, play) and commitment (building, dwelling, tending). Klein shows these are not in tension — commitment *enables* openness by generating the specific expectations whose violation constitutes discovery.

## Chin's "Field Reports Only" as Explorer Discipline

Cedric Chin's practical method in [[How to Make Sense of AI]] — ignore all opinion and analysis, consume only detailed field reports of use — is the explorer's information discipline translated to the AI era.

The tourist consumes opinions, predictions, and summaries (pre-made legibility). The explorer consumes field reports (raw terrain data) because only raw observations can serve as anchors for frame construction. An opinion is someone else's frame; a field report is data you can anchor your *own* frame on.

The [[Four Questions of Uncertainty]] (Vaughn Tan) are the explorer's compass: What new outcomes are possible? What actions can I take? What are the values of these outcomes to me? What causal relationships drive them? These questions are frame-construction prompts — they force the reader to build a frame rather than consume one.

## The AI Complication, Sharpened

The existing explorer-tourist page notes that LLMs can be either explorer tools or tourist infrastructure. Klein's framework sharpens this:

- **LLM as frame-provider**: the tourist use — asking the LLM for a summary, an analysis, a recommendation. The user receives a pre-constructed frame. They cannot detect anomalies within it because the frame is not theirs.
- **LLM as anchor-finder**: the explorer use — asking the LLM to surface specific data points, field reports, contradictions, or anomalies. The user constructs their own frame from the anchors the LLM helps locate.

The difference is not in the tool but in the [[Sensemaking Forms|sensemaking form]]: elaborating your own frame vs. consuming someone else's.

## Essay Seed

**"The Expert Explorer: On Navigation, Commitment, and the Cognitive Science of Not Keeping an Open Mind"**

Thesis: the counter-intuitive finding from naturalistic decision-making research — that experts who commit early to frames outperform those who "keep an open mind" — is the same insight the essays have been circling under the figure of the explorer. The explorer succeeds not through openness but through committed place-making that generates the specific expectations whose violation drives learning. The essay draws the practical implications for navigating the current AI transition.
