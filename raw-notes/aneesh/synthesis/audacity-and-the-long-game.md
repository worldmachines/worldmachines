# Audacity and the Long Game

## Three Forms of Anti-Shortcut Commitment

Across Aneesh Sathe's recent essays, a shared ethic appears: **the refusal to skip the hard foundational work in favor of the impressive-looking output**.

### Dwarf Fortress
"To play Dwarf Fortress is to experience audacity." The audacity is 20 years of building without commercial mandate, without institutional support, in a medium that rewards visible polish over simulation depth. The shortcut would have been a simpler, winnable, pretty game. DF refused the shortcut.

### Data Science Rigor
"Speed, habit, and misplaced trust" cause analysts to skip the [[Data Generation Process]]. The shortcut is the auto-chart, the one-click model, the pretrained-everything. [[Leo Breiman]]'s "two cultures" is a diagnosis of a field that chose the shortcut and is surprised when results don't hold.

### Socratic Inquiry
[[Agnes Callard]]'s distinction between problems and questions frames the Socratic method as the refusal to convert questions into problems prematurely. The shortcut is to "ask Google, get recipe, done" — to package the question as a problem so you can close it. Socratic commitment holds the question open until genuine orientation is achieved.

## The Common Structure

All three cases describe a choice point:
1. **The shortcut**: achieve a locally impressive outcome (a winnable game, a model that runs, a closed question) while bypassing the foundational work
2. **The long game**: do the foundational work (simulate the physics, trace the data generation, hold the question), accepting that the output will be slower and less immediately impressive

The long game is audacious not because it is heroic but because it is *sustained* — ordinary human life happening in parallel with extraordinary foundational commitment.

## Why Shortcuts Win (Usually)

The shortcut is easier to evaluate and reward:
- A working model is visually demonstrable; a rigorous DGP analysis is invisible
- A winnable game ships and sells; an open-ended simulation takes 20 years to become commercially viable
- A closed question is comfortable; a Socratic question creates productive discomfort

The institutions that select for shortcuts (funding cycles, publication pressure, commercial game studios, quick-turn consulting) systematically produce outputs that look impressive and fail at the foundation.

## Essay Seed

*The aesthetics of foundation*: what would it look like to build institutions and cultures that reward foundational work rather than visible outputs? What are the selection mechanisms that currently punish the long game, and can they be redesigned?
