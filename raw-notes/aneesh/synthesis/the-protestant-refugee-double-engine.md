# The Protestant / Refugee Double Engine

## The Connection No Single Chapter Makes

David Landes's *Revolution in Time* (1983) returns, again and again, to religion as a cause of Western horology — but he never assembles the full mechanism in one place, and the essays wiki had no canon page on religion as an engine of modernity at all (the nearest, [[psychological-modernity]], treats "secular pluralism in the wake of religious encounter and reform" as one cause among several, and [[dawn-day-dusk-machine-typology]] tracks Hume and Smith taking religion *out* of their work). This page collects the scattered passages into a single claim: **across Landes's book, religion drives modernity on both sides of the economic ledger at once — manufacturing the *demand* for time discipline and supplying the *skill* that met it.** The two engines run on the same fuel (Protestant conviction and the persecution it provoked) but push in opposite directions in the economy, and that is exactly why the connection is invisible chapter by chapter: the demand argument lives in the social-history chapters, the supply argument in the industrial ones.

## Engine One — Demand: Asceticism Manufactures the Want

Landes's deepest thesis is demand-pull — "the clock did not create an interest in time measurement; the interest in time measurement led to the invention of the clock" ([[rit-05-are-you-sleeping-brother-john]]). Religion is the original source of that interest, and it operates in two historical phases:

- **Catholic monastic prehistory.** Western Christianity, alone among the monotheisms, tied salvation to prayer at *set clock points* (not natural bands), performed *collectively and simultaneously*, including in the dead of night. Simultaneity was theology — chanting aloud "compelled simultaneity," and the whole was held greater than the sum. Punctuality became a *salvific obligation*, and the night office created the concrete engineering problem (waking reliably for matins) from which the alarm and then the escapement emerged ([[rit-05-are-you-sleeping-brother-john]]). The discipline preceded the device.
- **Protestant intensification and privatization.** When the clock miniaturized into the pocket watch, the same calculus migrated from the cloister into the lay individual, and here Landes makes the move he credits Max Weber with *missing*: "what the clock was to the cloistered ascetics of the Middle Ages, the watch was to the in-the-world ascetics of post-Reformation Europe" ([[rit-07-my-time-is-my-time]]). Watch production and watch-*wearing* were both disproportionately Protestant (Augsburg, 1600–1700: 165 of 189 master clockmakers of known affiliation were Protestant). Calvin's explicit acceptance of the watch let Geneva reconvert its banned jewellery trade into horology — Landes's pointed question, "if Calvinists were not interested in time and its measurement, who was?" ([[rit-17-multum-in-parvo]]). The English **"puritan watch"** is the cleanest demand artifact: plain, reliable, adopted by Calvinists as a *monitor* — a tool "of planning and self-discipline" rather than a bauble — and its makers, often Puritans themselves, competed on precision because unadorned products could be distinguished no other way ([[rit-08-of-toys-and-ornaments]]).

The demand engine therefore does more than sell watches: it shapes *design ideology*. Where the want is ascetic-Protestant, the trade is pulled onto the precision main line; where it is Catholic-courtly (French ornament, German complexity-toys), the trade diverts into "magnificent dead ends" ([[rit-08-of-toys-and-ornaments]]). Religion is selecting which branch of the technology gets developed.

## Engine Two — Supply: Persecution Scatters the Skill

The same religious conflict that built the demand also redistributed the *supply* of skill, by force. The hinge event is the **Revocation of the Edict of Nantes (1685)**, which expelled some 200,000 *réformés* from France — and it recurs, independently, in four of Landes's chapters as a *supply shock*:

- It "devastated French watchmaking and seeded the Swiss mountain industry" ([[rit-07-my-time-is-my-time]]).
- It drove France's best Protestant practitioners out, handing Britain its century of leadership under the five great London makers ([[rit-16-ups-and-downs-of-international-competition]]).
- Huguenot watchmakers fleeing Catholic persecution brought France's elite horological skills to tiny Calvinist Geneva, the engine of *Multum in Parvo* ([[rit-17-multum-in-parvo]]).
- Refugee inflows brought the commercial *networks* an export trade needed into the Jura, alongside the skills ([[rit-20-ah-but-he-could]]).

And the supply engine works at the level of individual decisive transfers, not just demographic flows. **Nicolas Facio** — "Protestant refugee of the Revocation" — brought ruby-jewelling to Britain, founding the jewelled-bearing technique and the ~100-year English monopoly that followed ([[rit-10-approaching-the-asymptote]]). One refugee, one technique, one national advantage.

A second supply mechanism is **Protestant literacy and numeracy as infrastructure**. The Swiss Jura's rise rested not only on refugee skill but on a culturally bounded swarm whose substrate was Protestant schooling: autodidact mountain libraries, the Le Locle bookshop, "a seedbed of Ben Franklins à la jurassienne" — and the telling geographic fact that watchmaking "stopped at the Catholic French border, except Protestant Beaucourt" ([[rit-20-ah-but-he-could]]). Religion here is not demand or migration but the literacy stock that lets a population *receive* a skilled trade.

## Why the Double Engine Matters

Two structural points fall out of assembling the engines together:

1. **Religion sits on both sides of the World Machine's books.** In the [[modernity-machine|Modernity Machine]] frame, the demand engine is a *pull* — a shared attractor (salvation through punctual, simultaneous, self-monitored time) drawing the invention and then the industry into being ([[pull-vs-push-machines]]). The supply engine is closer to a *push* — exogenous, violent, dispersive, scattering skill into whatever soil could receive it. A single theology produces both the convergent want and the divergent skill-flow. This is a sharper claim than Weber's: it is not only that the Protestant ethic motivated capitalist conduct, but that the *political reaction against* Protestantism (expulsion) was itself a productive force, relocating the means of production by persecution.

2. **The connection beyond Landes.** The double engine reframes the wiki's standing material on tools and worldview: the puritan watch is the strongest historical case for [[tools-and-worldview-commitment]] — adopting the watch commits its bearer to a worldview (time as spendable, wastable, sin-adjacent property). And it grounds [[anxiety-as-generative-force]]: the demand was charged by plague-era *memento mori* piety and salvation-accounting — anxiety, religiously framed, as the demand side of a civilizational tool. The page that this synthesis most directly antagonizes is [[clock-as-incomplete-discipline]]: if the discipline was manufactured by a salvation regime, its leaks (Saint Monday, the régleurs at funerals) are the points where the religious charge has drained out of the instrument, leaving only the habit.

## Tension / What Landes Does Not Resolve

Landes asserts the Protestant correlation but does not fully separate *causation* from *selection*. The expelled Huguenots were skilled because the French luxury trade had trained them — a Catholic-courtly demand engine — so the supply that seeded Switzerland was partly the *output* of the very ornament-line Landes treats as a dead end. The double engine is therefore not two clean independent forces but a single conflict whose demand and supply effects are entangled: Catholic patronage trained the artisans whom Catholic persecution then exported to Protestant cities that wanted a different (ascetic) product. A future essay could make this entanglement the point: modernity's skill base was *laundered through* a religious war.

## Essay Seeds

- **"Made by Persecution"**: an essay on expulsion as an underrated mode of technology transfer — the Revocation, the Sephardic diaspora, the Lyon silk weavers, partition-era migrations — arguing that states routinely manufacture their rivals' industries by exiling their own minorities.
- **"The Watch as Sin-Counter"**: on the watch as the privatized confessional — time as the one possession Petrarch said "can justify avarice," the minute hand as a ledger of "time used, time spent, time wasted, time lost."
