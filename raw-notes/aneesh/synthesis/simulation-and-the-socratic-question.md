# Simulation and the Socratic Question: Removing the Win Condition

## The Structural Parallel

[[Dwarf Fortress]] has no win condition. You cannot win; you can only play, lose, and play again. "Losing is FUN!" — failure is generative, not terminal.

[[Agnes Callard]]'s Socratic **questions** (from *Open Socrates*) have no win condition either. A Socratic question doesn't disappear when you answer it — it opens a larger horizon. You cannot "solve" justice or knowledge or the good life; you can only explore, probe, and discover new territory.

Both are designed systems that explicitly remove the terminal condition as a feature, not a bug.

## What the Design Achieves

**In DF**: removing the win condition means:
- Every fortress is a story, not a score
- Failure is narrative content, not narrative termination
- The game is indefinitely deep because there's no completion asymptote

**In Socratic inquiry**: removing the win condition means:
- Every answer opens territory rather than closing it
- Previous answers become places that orient further exploration ([[Space and Place Philosophy]])
- Knowledge accumulates rather than concluding

## The Depth Paradox

Both DF and Socratic questions are "44% complete" by design. DF has been in development for 20+ years and Tarn Adams estimates it's about 44% complete — and that number likely hasn't changed as new ideas expand the target. Similarly, philosophy's oldest questions remain open after 2,500 years of Socratic inquiry.

This is not a failure of completion. It is the appropriate structure for a simulation rich enough to generate genuinely new stories, and for a question profound enough to demand genuine orientation.

## The Win-State as Epistemic Closure

The win state in conventional games tells you when to stop playing. In epistemology, the equivalent is the **problem** (Callard's terminology) — it disappears when solved and releases you from engagement.

Designing out the win state (in DF, in Socratic philosophy) is a design choice that says: *the engagement itself is the value*. Depth, not completion.

## Essay Seed

*Designing against closure*: what happens when we remove win conditions from systems usually defined by completion? Applications: education (the lecture vs. the seminar), research programs (hypothesis-testing vs. question-holding), product design (feature completion vs. ongoing simulation). What does it mean to build systems for indefinite depth rather than achievable endpoints?
