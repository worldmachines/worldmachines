# Intent Collisions and the Internet of Beefs

## Two Regimes of Conflict

[[venkatesh-rao]]'s 2020 essay [[internet-of-beefs]] gave a stable-state account of conflict on social media. The Internet of Beefs was a low-grade civil war waged across platforms, conducted not to resolve disagreements but to transact identity and tribal belonging through them. Knights performed for mooks; beefs were the medium through which performance got monetised in attention. The system was *stable* in the technical sense: more knights, more mooks, more beefs all increased aggregate beef without congestion. Performance is non-rivalrous. My calling someone an idiot does not consume any of your supply of being able to call someone an idiot. The IoB had the economic shape of a public good, just an unpleasant one.

The Lightening of Intent essay introduces [[intent-collisions]] as the industrial successor. When two parties disagree in 2026, the first does not stop at posting. The first commissions an AI agent to write the counter-book in the disputed thinker's style, full length, with footnotes and an index, indistinguishable in production quality from a publisher's, in five days. The second deploys a different agent to write the refutation, also full length, also in five days, also indistinguishable. The intent to disagree no longer needs an audience. It produces *artefacts*, and the artefacts compete.

The two regimes share much: tribalism, identity production, the use of conflict as the substrate through which belonging gets transacted. They differ in one structural respect that determines everything downstream. The IoB was performance-driven. Intent collisions are production-driven.

## What Industrialisation Changes

Performance and production differ in scarcity geometry.

Performance is non-rivalrous. Two knights can perform the same beef in the same medium without diminishing each other's performance. The medium is attention; attention is finite but elastic; aggregate attention rises with engagement. The IoB ran on this elasticity for a decade.

Production is rivalrous in three ways simultaneously. First, the substrate has finite capacity: the GPUs that run the agents, the storage that holds their outputs, the network that distributes them. Compute is currently the binding constraint on intent-production at scale, and it is the most clearly rivalrous resource the substrate exposes. Second, attention remains finite at the consumption end and now also at the *evaluation* end: who reads the counter-book and the refutation? In a world of infinite plausible artefacts, the auteur reader's bandwidth becomes the binding constraint. Third, the dispute itself has a referee at the limit: reality, taste, or institutional sanction will eventually arbitrate which artefact stands. The IoB had no referee — that was its stability. Intent collisions all have referees, even if the referee is slow.

The shift from performance to production therefore moves the conflict regime from non-rivalrous and stable to rivalrous and competitive. Two intents collide in a way two beefs did not. The better-modelled, better-tasted, better-resourced intent wins. This is the structural source of the new regime's instability.

## What Stays the Same

Not everything changes. Three features survive the transition.

First, **tribalism**. The intents that collide are still organised by identity, lineage, and prior commitment. Agents are commissioned by parties who have already chosen sides; the artefacts they produce are extensions of those choices, not exits from them. The IoB's identity geometry persists into the production regime; what changes is the artefact density, not the identity density.

Second, **the artefact-as-token function**. In the IoB, posts, takes, and dunks were tokens that signalled membership. In the intent-collision regime, books, codebases, datasets, and proofs serve the same function with much higher production cost (formerly) and now near-zero production cost. The signalling work the IoB extracted from a tweet is now extracted from a counter-book. The economy of tokens is preserved; the unit cost has collapsed.

Third, **the Knight-Mook structure**. There are still producers and audiences. The auteur who commissions the agent is the new Knight; the readers and citers and re-circulators are the new Mooks. What has changed is that Mooks can now be Knights at low marginal cost. The barrier between performance and production has dissolved. Rao's IoB analysis predicted this implicitly: the Knight class would expand as the production cost of performance fell. What the analysis did not anticipate was that production would *replace* performance as the binding form of conflict, not just supplement it.

## What Will Settle Collisions

If intent collisions are rivalrous, something arbitrates which intent gets executed. The candidates:

1. **Taste.** The auteur regime ([[auteuring-and-taste]]) makes taste the production function, and the most discerning taste recognises the better artefact across the collision. This is the optimistic reading: the [[liveness-machine]] arbitrates collisions through cultivated discriminative skill at scale. It depends on whether taste cultivation institutions exist and operate at the relevant scale.

2. **Truth.** Disputes about empirical questions can in principle be settled by reality. In the intent-collision regime, the agent that produces the more reality-tracking artefact wins eventually. This is the technocratic reading: collisions get arbitrated through measurement, replication, and the slow correction of the world. It depends on whether the substrate produces or undermines the conditions for measurement and replication. Current evidence is mixed.

3. **Resources.** The agent with more compute, more storage, more distribution, more capital wins regardless of taste or truth. This is the pessimistic reading: the [[liveness-machine]] is a regime in which the better-resourced intent crushes the better-tasted one through sheer artefact density. The IoB's stability was partly that beefs were cheap; intent collisions are also cheap individually but expensive in aggregate, and aggregation is where capital wins.

4. **Capture resistance.** [[capture-resistance]] in Rao's sense — the live system's refusal to be harnessed for extrinsic purposes — is the long-run condition that determines which intents *survive* the collision regime, regardless of which ones win individual rounds. Intents that can be captured by the substrate's defaults will be diffused into the substrate's average; intents that resist capture will compound. This is the structural prediction: over a long horizon, the regime selects for capture-resistant intents and against captured ones, even if it does not select that way in any individual collision.

Most likely, all four operate in different domains and at different timescales. The Lightening essay's wager is that the *first generation* of intent-collisions — the next three to five years — will be settled mostly by resources, with taste lagging, truth lagging further, and capture resistance only beginning to be visible. The shape of the era will be set in that interval.

## Time Horizon

The IoB took roughly a decade (2010-2020) to mature into its stable form. Intent collisions are unlikely to take that long, because the production technology is mature on day one. The compounding is in the operational practices of agent commissioning, not in the underlying capability. By the essay's reckoning, three to five years separates the present from the first generation of collisions; by the end of the decade, intent collisions will be the dominant form of high-stakes disagreement in any domain where artefacts matter.

This compresses the political-economic adjustment window severely. The IoB had a decade for institutions to develop responses (and largely failed to). Intent collisions have a few years. Whatever institutional infrastructure is going to arbitrate them — courts, peer review, market mechanisms, taste networks — has to be built or repurposed in that window, or the resource-dominant arbitration becomes the default by inertia.

## Essay Seed

A follow-up essay could examine *historical analogues* of regime transitions from performance-driven to production-driven conflict. The most obvious: the printing press's transformation of religious dispute. Pre-print theological disagreement was largely performance-driven (sermons, public debates, councils); print made the counter-pamphlet and the counter-treatise the dominant form. Luther's 95 Theses were a performance gesture; what made the Reformation an irreversible regime change was the *production* of pamphlets at scale. The arbitration of those production-driven collisions was eventually settled by warfare (the Thirty Years' War) followed by territorial sortition (Peace of Westphalia). The intent-collision regime should be expected to follow a roughly similar pattern: an initial period of resource-dominant arbitration, an escalation into infrastructure-level conflict, and an eventual settlement on territorial-equivalent arrangements (epistemic, market, or geographic). The Lightening essay's three-to-five-year window is the equivalent of the 1517-1521 interval; the rest of the analogy is open.
