# Massey and Tuan: Place as Event vs. Place as Pause

**Sources**: [[doreen-massey]], *For Space* (2005); [[Yi-Fu Tuan]], *Space and Place* (1977/2002)
**See also**: [[massey-for-space-part-4-reorientations]], [[tuan-2002-space-and-place-ch1-3]]

---

## The Parallel Claim

Both Massey and Tuan agree that place is *not* a property of locations — not something a site simply has. Place is constituted through something that *happens*.

**Tuan**: "if we think of space as that which allows movement, then place is pause; each pause in movement makes it possible for location to be transformed into place" (Tuan 2002: 6). Place arises when movement stops; the pause is the constitutive act.

**Massey**: "Places not as points or areas on maps, but as integrations of space and time; as spatio-temporal events" (Massey 2005: 130). Place is "the coming together of the previously unrelated, a constellation of processes rather than a thing" (Massey 2005: 141). Place arises as a conjuncture — an event — in the intersection of trajectories.

Both are anti-essentialist: place is not a prior, fixed nature awaiting recognition. Both are processual: place is constituted through happening.

---

## The Key Divergences

### 1. The Subject of Place-Making

**Tuan**: the pausing agent is always human. Place is constituted in *experience* — in the felt accumulation of familiarity, value, and meaning by a human subject pausing in a location. The developmental account in *Space and Place* Ch. 3 grounds this phenomenologically: the child's first places are persons (mother), then large familiar objects (crib, high chair). Organisms pause; locations become places.

**Massey**: the event-character of place extends to *nonhuman* trajectories. Skiddaw's immigrant rocks — having migrated from the southern hemisphere over 500 million years — participate in the event of Keswick just as much as the weekend tourists or the hill farmers. Sarah Whatmore's "mobile lives" of animals, Clark's plant-migration, Latour's bacteria — all are trajectories in place-events. Place is not centred on human experience.

This is a deep divergence. Tuan's phenomenology is structurally anthropocentric — place requires a pausing *experiencer*. Massey's relational ontology is genuinely posthuman — place requires a conjuncture of trajectories, some of which are human, many of which are not.

### 2. The Temporality of Accumulation

**Tuan**: place accumulates through *repeated* pausing. Familiarity builds over time; the deep sense of home is the product of years, even generations, of repeated encounter. Place identity deepens with duration. There is a temporal arc: the more one dwells, the more intensely a location becomes place. Bachelard's poetics of intimate space ([[intimate-place-bachelard]]) extends this — the house as the accumulated shelter of being.

**Massey**: place is constituted at the *conjuncture* — the irreducible here-and-now of the current meeting of trajectories. Each meeting is unique and unrepeatable. "It won't be the same 'here' when it is no longer now" (Massey 2005: 139). The sense of continuity and return is itself a process (the swifts returning to Kilburn, Massey returning to south Lancashire), but each return is to a place that has moved on.

This creates a different account of return. For Tuan, returning home deepens one's relationship to an accumulating place. For Massey, returning home is meeting up with where the place's history has got to now — which may include finding that your mother has changed the cake recipe.

### 3. The Internal Coherence of Place

**Tuan**: place has an *interior* — the "inside" of dwelling, of warmth, familiarity, the gathering of meaning. This is why Bachelard, Heidegger, and Tuan all privilege the house. There is a phenomenological coherence to place — what it is like to be here, the accumulated identity of this location for this inhabitant.

**Massey**: place is "not capturable as a slice through time in the sense of an essential section. Not intrinsically coherent" (Massey 2005: 141). There is no presumption of pre-given coherence. The throwntogetherness of place consists of trajectories that may conflict, contradict, and resist resolution. The throwntogetherness demands *negotiation* precisely because coherence is not given.

---

## The Complementarity

Despite these divergences, the two accounts are more complementary than competitive. They describe different registers of place-constitution:

**Tuan** describes *how place feels from within* — the phenomenology of being at home, of dwelling in a familiar location, of the gradual deepening of spatial meaning through experience. This is indispensable for understanding the lived sense of place attachment.

**Massey** describes *what place actually is* — its ontological constitution as a conjunction of trajectories, its internal multiplicity, its political character as a node in power geometries. This is indispensable for understanding why place is contested, how globalisation works, and what responsibility a place bears for what it produces.

One might say: Tuan's pause *happens within* Massey's event. The human act of pausing, dwelling, and accumulating experiential meaning is itself one of the trajectories that constitutes the event of place. Massey's account is wider (includes the geological, the biological, the global) and more politically inflected; Tuan's account is deeper into the phenomenology of what it is like to be a pausing, feeling human in that event.

## The Sathe Connection

Sathe's "interruption" image ([[place-as-interruption-of-space]]) triangulates between Tuan and Massey:

- Like Tuan, Sathe's interruption is an *act* — it is something a subject does, a declaration of *here I am*.
- Like Massey, the interruption is constitutive (not a reception of a pre-given place but an active making) and it implies a relational context (interrupting *something*).
- Unlike both, Sathe foregrounds the *assertive*, even violent, dimension of place-making: "to build a home is to interrupt space; to be born is to interrupt infinity."

## Essay Seed

**"Three Theories of Place-Constitution"**: Tuan's pause (phenomenological accumulation), Massey's event (relational conjuncture), Sathe's interruption (assertive act). What does each account best, what does each miss? Does the difference between them track a difference between the *structure* of place (Massey), the *experience* of place (Tuan), and the *politics* of place-making (Sathe)?
