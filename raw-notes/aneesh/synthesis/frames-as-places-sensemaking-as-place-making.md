# Frames as Places, Sensemaking as Place-Making

## The Structural Mapping

The [[Data-Frame Theory of Sensemaking]] and the wiki's [[Spatial Epistemology — The Master Thread]] describe the same cognitive act from different disciplinary traditions. The mapping is not metaphorical — it is structural:

| Spatial Epistemology | Data-Frame Theory |
|---|---|
| Space (undifferentiated, disorienting) | Unframed situation (no frame available; "seeking a frame") |
| Place (bounded, inhabitable, orienting) | Frame (explanatory structure that filters data, generates expectations) |
| Place-making (drawing a boundary, saying "here") | Frame construction (anchoring on 3–4 data elements to build a frame) |
| Navigation (moving between places) | Sensemaking cycles (elaboration, questioning, reframing) |
| Landmarks (stable reference points within a place) | Anchors (the few key data elements that constitute a frame) |
| The shelter / base camp | The committed frame from which exploration proceeds |

Klein's description of what happens without a frame — "you cannot gather relevant data without a frame; an analyst without a committed frame doesn't see more — they see nothing coherently" — is identical to the spatial claim that space without places is disorienting. Data without frames is noise; terrain without landmarks is wilderness.

## Anchors as Landmarks

Klein et al. find that people use at most 3–4 anchors to build a frame. These anchors function exactly as landmarks in [[Space and Place Philosophy]]: they are the stable points from which the rest of the territory derives its meaning. An inaccurate anchor early in a sensemaking sequence corrupts all subsequent interpretation — just as a misplaced landmark corrupts navigation.

The weather forecasters in Klein's study used only 2–3 anchors. The fireground commanders oriented entire tactical responses around a single dominant anchor (the laundry chute, the Ohio River). This is not cognitive poverty — it is the necessary economy of place-making. You cannot orient by attending to everything. You orient by selecting, bounding, and committing.

This connects directly to [[Places, Problems, and the Act of Bounding]]: the act of selecting anchors is the act of drawing a boundary. It is constitutive (without it, nothing is tractable) and foreclosing (it makes other framings harder to reach).

## Reframing as Re-Place-Making

Klein's "reframing" — abandoning the current frame and constructing a new one — is the cognitive equivalent of what the spatial epistemology calls place-making in the strongest sense: not decorating an existing place, but carving a *new* inhabitable region from undifferentiated space.

The difficulty of reframing maps onto the difficulty of genuine place-making. It is easier to elaborate an existing frame (extend a place you already inhabit) than to construct a new one (build a new shelter in unfamiliar territory). Klein's "preservation cycle" — explaining away anomalies to protect the current frame — is the cognitive version of clinging to a familiar place when the territory has changed around it.

The explorer in [[The Explorer and the Tourist]] is the figure who reframes — who builds new places in unfamiliar space. The tourist is the figure who elaborates — who fills in the details of a frame provided by someone else (the guidebook, the algorithm, the pre-made legibility).

## The "Seeking a Frame" Form as Wilderness Navigation

Klein's seventh sensemaking form — "seeking a frame" when none is available — is the purest spatial situation: you are in undifferentiated space with no landmarks. The sensemaker searches for anchors from fragmentary data, trying to find enough stable points to construct an initial frame.

This is what [[The Shelter as Epistemic Engine]] describes as the primary function of shelter: not protection from weather, but the creation of a stable point from which space becomes navigable. The first frame, like the first shelter, converts overwhelming space into oriented territory.

## What This Mapping Illuminates

### For the Spatial Epistemology
Klein's empirical work gives the spatial epistemology a *mechanism*. The essays describe knowing as spatial in evocative, phenomenological terms. Klein provides the cognitive machinery: frames with slots, anchors that constitute frames, elaboration cycles that extend frames, preservation cycles that protect them. The spatial epistemology gains precision without losing its phenomenological force.

### For Sensemaking Theory
The spatial mapping gives Klein's somewhat technical theory an existential dimension. Sensemaking is not just information processing — it is *dwelling*. To commit to a frame is to inhabit a place. To reframe is to uproot yourself. Frame fixation is not just a cognitive error — it is the deeply human reluctance to leave home.

### For the Legibility Tension
The [[Legibility vs. Complexity — The Master Tension]] gains a new instantiation: frames are legibility apparatus — they make the world readable. But the world's complexity exceeds any frame, just as the territory exceeds any map. The sensemaker who over-commits to legibility (frame preservation) misses the anomalies; the sensemaker who refuses legibility (diagnostic vagabonding) sees nothing. The productive tension is Klein's prescription: commit to a frame, elaborate it, but maintain specific enough expectations that violations are detectable.

## Essay Seed

**"The Cartography of Understanding: Sensemaking as Spatial Practice"**

Thesis: cognitive science and phenomenological geography converge on the same insight — understanding is not accumulation of facts but construction of inhabitable places in epistemic space. Klein's data-frame theory provides the mechanism; Tuan and Heidegger provide the existential weight. The essay explores what it means to take "frames are places" literally: that expertise is a form of dwelling, that reframing is a form of displacement, and that the modern information environment is a crisis of place-making.
