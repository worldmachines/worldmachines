# The Taste Gap in AI Systems

## The Convergence

Two essays by Sathe approach the same problem from different angles:

**[[On Being Good vs. Knowing Good]]** (via [[Nick Cave]]): AI cannot create meaningful art because the process of imperfect striving is constitutive of the taste that makes great art recognizable. AI approximates outputs without having undergone the experience.

**[[Dancing on the Shoulders of Giants]]**: The gap between novice and expert is primarily a gap in the structure and density of knowledge networks. Expertise has two components: knowledge (approximatable) and taste (the judgment to navigate knowledge networks wisely — not yet approximatable).

Both essays arrive at: *AI can hold and retrieve knowledge; it cannot yet have taste.*

## What Taste Is

Synthesizing across both essays:
- Taste is the capacity to make good judgments in domains where the criteria for "good" are not fully explicit
- Taste develops through feedback loops between action and consequence — experience in the world, not processing descriptions of experience
- Taste is what allows an expert to say "this is the right approach" in genuinely ambiguous situations, and to know when the stated goal is the wrong goal
- Taste is why [[AI as Tool vs. Advisor]] matters: tools don't need taste; advisors do

## The [[Cognitive Task Analysis]] Problem

CTA (see [[Dancing on the Shoulders of Giants]]) attempts to extract tacit expert knowledge. But is taste a form of tacit knowledge that CTA could surface? Or is it more fundamental — not a set of heuristics but a calibration of judgment that can only be developed, not transmitted?

The [[Composite AI Architecture (Experts + LLM + KG)]] implicitly answers: taste stays with the human. The KG holds structured knowledge; the LLM navigates it; the human expert exercises taste over the outputs. This is the right division of labor *given current AI* — but it leaves open whether a sufficiently experience-rich AI could eventually develop something like taste.

## The Cultural Implication

Sathe's "new culture" prescription (from [[On Being Good vs. Knowing Good]]): we need to cultivate taste in AI users — the capacity to recognize when AI output is wrong. This is the epistemic version of Cave's artistic argument: you must develop your own judgment before you can use the tool productively. The tool does not develop judgment for you.

This creates a paradox: the users who most need AI assistance (those with the least domain expertise) are least equipped to audit AI outputs. The users who could most productively use AI (experts with developed taste) need it least for the basic tasks.

## Essay Seed

**Thesis candidate**: The most important consequence of the LLM era is not the jobs that AI will or won't eliminate, but the threat to the formation of taste. If AI handles execution before users develop judgment, we produce a generation of practitioners who know how to prompt but not how to think. The [[Lore vs. Search vs. LLM]] regression is one manifestation; stunted professional development is another.
