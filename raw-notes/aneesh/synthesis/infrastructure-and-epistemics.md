# Infrastructure and Epistemics

## The Hidden Claim in the Biotech Essay

[[Why Every Biotech Research Group Needs a Data Lakehouse]] is ostensibly a technical essay about data architecture. But it contains a deeper epistemic claim: **the infrastructure you use to store knowledge determines what knowledge you can produce.**

If data ends up on laptops, gets lost when people leave, or exists only as a pretty plot on a poster — *that data generates no downstream science*. The knowledge it could have enabled is foreclosed by the infrastructure failure.

## The General Pattern

This is a specific instance of a general principle: epistemic infrastructure shapes what epistemic work is possible.

Other instances:
- **The printing press**: made knowledge portable, reproducible, and checkable. Enabled the Scientific Revolution by allowing researchers to build on shared texts.
- **The internet**: made knowledge globally accessible. Enabled the current pace of science, but also the current pace of misinformation.
- **Open formats vs. proprietary formats**: knowledge locked in proprietary formats is epistemically inaccessible to anyone without the right license.
- **Time-travel and lineage in data systems**: makes claims checkable. Without it, you cannot verify whether an analysis was honest or cherry-picked.

## The Compliance-as-Epistemics Angle

The essay argues for [[Data Lakehouse]] features (time-travel, lineage) partly on compliance grounds (FDA, EMA audits). But compliance is actually enforcing epistemic norms: it requires that claims be checkable by third parties who didn't make them.

This reframes regulatory compliance as applied epistemology: the audit requirement is a structural enforcement of the basic scientific norm that results must be reproducible.

## Connection to Open Formats

[[Open Data Formats]] are not just pragmatic (avoid vendor lock-in) — they are epistemically significant. Knowledge in open formats is knowledge that can be verified, extended, and built upon. Knowledge in proprietary formats is epistemically quarantined.

## The Wagon Connection

There is a unexpected structural parallel between the biotech essay and the creative practice essays:

- In [[On Protocols, Wagons, and Associated Acrobatics]]: falling off the wagon loses the accumulated noticing. *Getting back on* preserves continuity of the creative record.
- In biotech: losing data to a laptop breaks the scientific record. Infrastructure (the lakehouse) is what *preserves continuity* across personnel changes and time.

In both cases, the protocol/infrastructure serves the same function: ensuring that the accumulated work of the past remains accessible to the future self (or future researcher).

## Thesis Candidate

**The tools we use to store knowledge — whether databases, filing systems, or personal writing practices — are not neutral infrastructure. They are epistemic choices. The choice of open, reproducible, lineage-preserving systems is the choice to keep knowledge available for inquiry. The choice of proprietary, non-reproducible, loss-prone systems is the choice to foreclose it.**
