# Zoom Ethics and the Panglossian Trap

## The Problem

All four texts in this cluster circle a common problem: **how should one act when the macro-level trajectory is unclear or horrifying, and the micro-level demands immediate attention?**

This is a problem of **zoom ethics**: at what scale do you make moral judgments?

## Three Responses

### 1. Panglossian Optimism: Zoom Out
[[Horto]]'s argument in [[The Best of All Possible Worlds]]: zoom out far enough (to the species, to history, to Moore's Law) and every local harm becomes a stepping stone. The right ethical stance is macro-consequentialism: evaluate everything by its contribution to the largest possible good.

Problems:
- The zoom level is chosen strategically, not principled
- The endpoint ("humanity reaching the stars") is assumed, not argued
- Local suffering isn't actually halved by macro progress — it's just made invisible from the chosen zoom level
- José Antunes's life *looks* like a Panglossian trajectory but wasn't lived that way

### 2. Kulturkritik: Zoom to Diagnosis
[[Kulturkritik]] (Rousseau, Schiller, Goethe) operates at an intermediate zoom level — not as large as Panglossian optimism (it does not argue for cosmic inevitability) but not as local as the thicket gardener. It asks: what does civilization do to human wholeness, and how might art/nature/the garden restore it?

Problem: it can't actually deliver the restoration it promises. Every cure is homeopathic. "Have we ever really left the French garden?" The diagnostic zoom level is productive (it generates genuine insight into self-alienation) but the therapeutic zoom level is delusional.

### 3. The Thicket: Zoom In
[[Florian Lohse]]'s thicket gardener in [[The Best of All Possible Worlds]]: "Nothing here moves like that. The walnuts fall and stay where they fall." The ethical stance is entirely local: tend what is in front of you, avoid legibility, "find your own thicket first."

Connection to Voltaire: "*il faut cultiver notre jardin*" — Candide's final verdict is also a zoom-in. After all the philosophical debates, you just tend the garden.

Problem: the thicket offers no account of how to act in the world beyond the thicket. It can't tell you what to do about Trumpism or climate change or child labor in chip supply chains. It's an answer to a different question.

## The Divergence Machine as Framework

[[venkatesh-rao]]'s taxonomy in [[The Divergence Machine]] offers a way of holding these zoom levels together without resolving them:

- **Late modern** behavior: Panglossian optimism in its most energetic zombie form — enormous resources deployed to maintain a crumbling narrative (the best of all possible worlds; progress is real; the current suffering is temporary)
- **Postmodern** analysis: Kulturkritik — the intelligent ghost critique of modernity's wound; still running intellectual patterns that are brilliant but no longer generative
- **Metamodern** response: the thicket impulse — sincere, locally cultivated, but ultimately a form of ennervated necromancy (trying to get what the old world delivered but from within personal-scale retreats)
- **Divergent** liveness: something none of these texts quite articulates — a practice that has the energy of late modernity without its zombie canonicity; the irony of postmodernity without its negative-sum entanglement; the concern of metamodernity without its gloomy interiority

## The Open Question

None of the texts answers: what does the divergent ethical stance actually look like in practice?

The closest candidates:
- Lohse's Leibniz: pragmatic, honest about its own limitations, buying time until something better arrives
- Goethe's *Steigerung*: accepting that nature and culture never synthesize, but their dynamic opposition produces ever-higher energies
- Rao's true-north question: "Does this embody new forms of liveness being newly and generatively turned on in the world?"

## Essay Seed

**"The Ethics of Scale"**: An essay on zoom level as an ethical choice — how the selection of which scale to evaluate from already determines what conclusions you'll reach. Pangloss and the thicket gardener are not disagreeing about facts; they're choosing different scales to inhabit. The essay would ask: is there a principled way to choose, or is scale-choice itself a form of the shitty castle?
