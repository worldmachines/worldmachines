# Progress as a Non-Stationary Argument

## The Core Reframe

[[venkatesh-rao]]'s Feb 2026 essay introduces a conceptual move with significant downstream implications: *Progress* is not a debate between optimists and pessimists; it is an *argument* in the mathematical sense — a function y=f(x) where x (the scope of experienced reality) keeps expanding, f (the sense-making framework) must be perpetually rebuilt, and y (value) is an emergent product of the process, not a pre-given destination.

This reframe has a precise target: all "Progress Studies" and optimism/pessimism discourse — from Whig history to a16z American Dynamism — is treating the argument as if x were constant and f were received. This is the [[fiat-progress]] structure: a degenerate case of the [[Argument of Progress]] where the expansion of reality has been suppressed.

## Why This Matters for Intellectual History

The Spinoza-Leibniz encounter is Rao's showcase case for what this reframe implies:

- **Leibniz**: a brilliant genius who nonetheless reduced the Argument of Progress to "best of all possible worlds." He was confronting the same expanding reality (Galilean astronomy, new world explorations, microscopes, new social forms) but tried to fit it within Aristotelian and Christian frameworks. He *committed prematurely* to existing scaffolding.
- **Spinoza**: a lens grinder who accepted that the categories of good/bad needed reconstruction from scratch. He did not try to fit the new x into old f. He chose ontological doubt over premature resolution.

One generation later: Hume and Smith are intellectual descendants of Spinoza, with Leibniz (as philosopher) already irrelevant. Two generations: Darwin completes the project by making variety and divergence *load-bearing features of reality itself*.

The pattern is: the [[Argument of Progress]] advances by a minority tradition of thinkers who resist the Leibniz error, while the majority continues operating on Fiat Progress narratives. The minority was enough to keep the tradition alive for 400 years; Covid + AI finally mainstreamed it.

## The Hume Connection to AI

Rao's claim that Hume is "the original philosopher of the AI age" deserves unpacking. The "bitter lessons" of machine learning (Rich Sutton's framing, though unacknowledged) are exactly the pattern Hume established: simple empirical models that learn from vast amounts of data repeatedly outperform models built on human-specified priors and theories.

Hume was "only willing to treat the log files of reality as reality. Everything else was made up human conceits." This is the epistemological stance that deep learning embodies: don't hand-engineer features; learn from raw experience data.

If Hume is the original AI philosopher, then AI itself is a continuation of the Argument of Progress — a *tool for expanding x* that simultaneously makes Fiat Progress narratives untenable (AI-generated outputs don't respect received categories of value) and provides new instruments for sense-making.

## The Polycentric Narrative Structure

Rao's characterization of the Leibniz-Spinoza relationship is worth preserving: "two important centers in a polycentric narrative with a gradually moving 'average' state. This was neither a negotiated consensus, nor a cleanly partitioned dissensus. Rather, it was like gradually rising zeitgeist temperature."

This is a model for how intellectual progress actually happens — not through debate and resolution, not through paradigm shifts (Kuhn), not through dialectical synthesis (Hegel), but through a polycentric process where multiple centers push in loosely correlated directions and the "average" gradually shifts. No individual center needs to "win."

This is a meta-level application of the Argument of Progress itself: the process of understanding Progress is itself non-stationary, polycentric, and incompletely narratable.

## Tensions and Open Questions

1. **The Habermas/Lyotard problem**: Rao is covering terrain that postmodern and critical-theory thinkers have mapped extensively. Lyotard's "incredulity toward metanarratives" is structurally similar to the shift from Fiat Progress to the Argument of Progress. Why doesn't Rao engage with this tradition? Is the Divergence Machine just "postmodernism with better periodization"?

2. **The normative gap**: Rao insists the Argument of Progress is not normative doctrine. But the divergentist move — accept ontological dread, avoid premature commitment, keep participating — is itself a normative stance. The difference between tolerance-as-necessary-for-divergence-machine and tolerance-as-intrinsic-good is thin.

3. **Who can afford the dread?**: Ontological dread as a productive state requires material security, social shelter, and time — the conditions Spinoza had in Amsterdam, that Hume had with his institutional position. The politics of who can afford to "keep the game going" rather than resolving dread into optimism/pessimism is absent from Rao's account.

## Essay Seeds

1. **"The Argument of Progress in the Age of AI"**: Extend Rao's framework to the present. If Hume was the first to treat the log files of reality as primary, what does an LLM's relationship to its training data mean for the Argument of Progress? Is AI the first *non-human* participant in the Argument?
2. **"The Modernist Error in Politics"**: Apply the Leibniz pattern to contemporary political discourse. Where are the most damaging premature commitments to existing good/bad scaffolding? What would the Spinozan move look like in political practice?
3. **"Polycentric Progress"**: The Leibniz-Spinoza model as an alternative to Kuhnian paradigm shifts and Hegelian synthesis. What intellectual history looks like if you model it as a polycentric non-stationary argument.
