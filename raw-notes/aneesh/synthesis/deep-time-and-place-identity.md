# Deep Time and Place Identity

**Sources**: [[doreen-massey]], *For Space* (2005); [[Yi-Fu Tuan]], *Space and Place* (1977); John McPhee, *Basin and Range* (1980)
**See also**: [[massey-for-space-part-4-reorientations]], [[the-lake-district-as-meeting-place]], [[immigrant-rocks]]

---

## The Problem

The most persistent objection to relational or constructionist accounts of place is the appeal to *nature*: whatever the flux of human social relations, the mountains endure. Skiddaw stood before the Romans and will stand after us. The land grounds place identity even as human societies shift and change.

Massey's immigrant rocks argument is a direct response to this objection. But it connects to a broader set of questions that appear across sources in this wiki: what happens to place identity when the natural foundations are themselves revealed to be processes, trajectories, events?

---

## Massey: The Mountains are Immigrants

The Skiddaw slates (Lake District) were deposited in the southern hemisphere 500 million years ago and have been migrating northward ever since. They will continue to move. Continental drift is ongoing; erosion is ongoing; the landscape is constitutively unstable at timescales that dwarf human history.

The consequence: "If we can't go 'back' home, in the sense that it will have moved on from where we left it, then no more, and in the same sense, can we, on a weekend in the country, go back to nature. It too is moving on." (Massey 2005: 136)

Nature cannot serve as the stable foundation for place identity. If you want a foundation, you need to go to a timescale at which things are (provisionally) stable — and then justify why *that* timescale is the appropriate one for grounding place.

---

## John McPhee: Geology as Narrative

McPhee's *Basin and Range* (1980) — a founding text of popular geology — makes the same move for the American West. The Basin and Range province (Nevada, Utah) was produced by the stretching and faulting of the continent over the past 20 million years. The Great Basin is not a stable landscape but the current result of ongoing tectonic processes. The mountain ranges are separated by valleys because the crust between them dropped.

McPhee's narrative technique: he interweaves the geological timescale with the human timescale, using "deep time" as an uncanny distance from which human history appears as a thin film of recent events. This is exactly Massey's move, but McPhee performs it stylistically where Massey performs it philosophically.

The connection to place: the Nevadan rancher who grounds her identity in "the land" is grounding it in something that was an ocean floor in the Cambrian. The permanence she perceives is real at human timescales but illusory at geological ones.

---

## The Question of Timescale Selection

Both Massey and McPhee raise, but do not fully resolve, a further question: if everything is process at some timescale, does this mean we can never legitimately appeal to natural stability?

Possible responses:

**1. Pragmatic stability suffices.** The mountains are stable enough for human purposes — they are not going anywhere within any human political timeframe. This is true but doesn't rescue the philosophical point: the claim is that nature provides *eternal* foundation, and eternal it is not.

**2. The provisional conjuncture is enough.** Massey's own response: "here is no more (and no less) than our encounter, and what is made of it" (Massey 2005: 139). The here-and-now conjuncture is real and significant, even if it is not eternal. The event of place is genuine; it just doesn't last forever. This is actually a more honest — and in some ways richer — account of place than the eternalist one.

**3. Different timescales, different stabilities.** A place may be geologically unstable (over millions of years) while being humanly stable (over centuries) while being socially unstable (over decades). The question is which timescale is politically relevant. Massey's point is that political claims about place often implicitly invoke the "eternal" timescale without admitting that they are doing so.

## Tuan: The Cultural-Historical Complement

Tuan's approach to place identity is cultural-historical rather than geological. His attention to mythic space, to cosmological orientation, to the ways civilisations locate themselves in landscapes — all of these are processes of stabilisation within human history. The Chinese cosmological landscape, the Australian Aboriginal songlines, the Greek sacred geography — these are all human-temporal stabilisations of the natural, ways of making nature legible and place-constituting.

Tuan does not make the geological move. His places are constituted by human meaning, and while he acknowledges that physical features shape place identity, he does not question the foundational character of geography the way Massey does.

The interesting synthesis: Tuan shows how *human culture* stabilises and gives meaning to what Massey reveals to be physically unstable. Culture is a way of making provisional conjunctures feel permanent. This is not deception — it is how place works. But it does mean that the cultural work of place-making is always working against an underlying impermanence, and that cultural stabilisations can be challenged by revealing the impermanence they conceal.

**"Everything Was Somewhere Else"**: on the politics of geological time as a challenge to place identity. Massey's immigrant rocks, McPhee's deep time, the Aboriginal use of geological features as songline landmarks (which assumes their stability over Dream Time).
