# Cost Disease and the Retreat from Public Life

## The Argument

[[Alex Mayyasi]]'s essay in [[Baumol Cost Disease and Toxic Solitude]] makes a structural economic argument for a phenomenon usually explained in cultural or technological terms: the retreat from public and communal life in America.

The standard narratives — screens, suburbanization, individualism — are not wrong. But they miss the economic layer: under [[Baumol's Cost Disease]], the profit motive *systematically* favors businesses that keep people apart over businesses that bring them together. This is not a cultural pathology; it is a structural feature of any growing economy where productivity gains are unevenly distributed.

## The Mechanism, Restated

1. Economy-wide productivity growth raises wages
2. Scalable digital products (streaming, delivery apps, social media) can absorb higher wages because they serve more users without proportional labor increase
3. [[Third Places]] and social experiences cannot scale — they are irreducibly labor-intensive
4. Result: social experiences become relatively more expensive; digital isolation becomes relatively cheaper
5. Capital flows toward scalable products; social spaces are starved of investment
6. The surviving social businesses achieve efficiency gains by becoming *less social* (kiosks, fast-casual, algorithmic content)

## Connection to Other Wiki Themes

### The Lore Regression (Sathe)
[[My Grandfather Had Lore]] traces a regression in information access: from expert lore → Google → LLMs → possibly back to lore. The cost disease analysis offers a parallel: from communal life → suburbanized isolation → digital isolation → possibly back to informal social networks as the only accessible remaining option.

Both are stories of degradation in infrastructure (epistemic and social) that falls through the productivity/scalability gap.

### Algorithmic Curation as Place-Making
[[Algorithmic Curation as Place-Making]] (existing concept) explores how digital platforms simulate the functions of physical places. Cost disease provides the economic explanation for *why* this substitution occurs: physical places get expensive, digital simulations get cheap. The simulation takes over not because people prefer it but because the structural economics favor it.

### The Universal Library Parallel
In [[The Universal Library in the River of Noise]], the tension between inclusivity (noise, abundance) and quality (curation, islands of meaning) mirrors the cost-disease tension: the scalable option (the internet, digital entertainment) wins by volume but degrades quality. The labor-intensive option (curated library, sit-down restaurant, theater) maintains quality but becomes unaffordable.

## The Policy Question

Mayyasi asks whether social spaces should qualify for the same kind of public subsidy that already applies to performing arts organizations and public libraries. This is a genuinely difficult policy question, but the framing matters: once you see social isolation as a *structural economic outcome* rather than a *cultural failure*, the policy toolkit expands.

The question is not "how do we get people to value community again?" — it is "how do we make community economically viable in a cost-disease world?"

## Essay Seeds

1. *If social isolation is an economic problem, not a cultural one, what does that mean for the "loneliness epidemic" discourse?* The discourse tends to pathologize individuals or generations (millennials, Gen Z) — cost disease suggests the right unit of analysis is market structure.

2. *Is there a Baumol effect for attention?* Just as labor-intensive services get relatively more expensive, effortful cognitive activities (reading a book, having a sustained conversation) compete against cheap, scalable dopamine (social media, streaming). Is attention cost-diseased?
