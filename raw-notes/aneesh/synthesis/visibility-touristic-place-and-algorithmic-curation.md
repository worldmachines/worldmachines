# Visibility, Touristic Place, and Algorithmic Curation

## The Tuan Mechanism

[[visibility-and-the-creation-of-place]] (Tuan, *Space and Place* Ch. 12) gives the wiki something it had only intuited: an explicit mechanism for how place-visibility is *manufactured*. The wiki's [[insta-places]] and [[algorithmic-curation-as-place-making]] describe the contemporary effects; Tuan supplies the longer history and the structural typology.

> "Place is whatever stable object catches our attention." (Tuan 2002, p. 161)

But "stable" and "attention" are not given. They are produced. Tuan inventories the mechanisms:

1. **Naming** — scientists pointing official fingers at one body of water rather than another, creating "the source of the Mississippi" as a specific visitable place (p. 162).
2. **Monumentalization** — Wallace Stevens's jar that "took dominion": "The wilderness rose up to it, and sprawled around, no longer wild" (p. 162). Architectural prominence catches and holds attention.
3. **Civic ritual** — beating the bounds, the Chinese marketing-area procession, the Roman patriarch circumambulating his fields. Repeated bodily action converts abstract territory into felt place (pp. 165–168).
4. **The Baroque *place royale*** — each with the monarch's statue at center, streets radiating outward (p. 163, Fig. 18). Deliberate place-construction as political statement.
5. **Outsider naming** — derogatory labels like "Back of the Yard" can impose a place-identity on residents who had none (p. 172).
6. **Nationalism's manufactured place** — the nation-state is too large to be known personally; "symbolic means had to be used to make the large nation-state seem a concrete place" (p. 176). History books, school atlases, civic shrines, the French Legislative Assembly's 1792 decree that every commune build an altar to la Patrie (p. 177).

Tuan's compressed summary:

> "Human places become vividly real through dramatization. Identity of place is achieved by dramatizing the aspirations, needs, and functional rhythms of personal and group life." (Tuan 2002, p. 178)

## Tuan's Crucial Distinction: Manufactured Visibility vs. Lived Place-Attachment

Within Ch. 12, Tuan distinguishes two registers that the algorithmic age conflates. *Manufactured visibility* is what monumentalization, naming, ritual, and tourism produce: the place that is photographable, recognizable, dramatized for an audience of outsiders. *Lived place-attachment* is what Ch. 10's intimate places describe: the trough of dust under the playground swing, the heirloom corner, the home as "a place where every day is multiplied by all the days before it" (Tuan 2002, p. 144).

The two registers are not enemies — most enduring places have both. Beacon Hill is both monumentally visible and intimately inhabited. But they can come apart, and the distinction is critical for diagnosing algorithmic place-making.

> "Deeply-loved places are not necessarily visible, either to ourselves or to others. Places can be made visible by a number of means: rivalry or conflict with other places, visual prominence, and the evocative power of art, architecture, ceremonies and rites." (Tuan 2002, p. 178)

The algorithmic feed manufactures visibility at industrial scale. It does not — *cannot* — manufacture intimate place-attachment.

## The Contemporary *Places Royales*

The Baroque *place royale*, as Tuan describes it, is a deliberate construction:
- A monarch's statue at center.
- Streets radiating outward to make the statue visible from many approaches.
- A choreographed approach that dramatizes the experience of arrival.
- An audience whose attention is harvested for the political legitimation of the monarch.

Now read this against the contemporary instances:

| Place royale (Baroque) | Contemporary instance |
|---|---|
| Monarch's statue at center | Pinned location, "trending now," "must-visit" |
| Radiating streets | Algorithmic recommendation paths |
| Choreographed approach | The recommended itinerary, the "20 things to do" listicle |
| Audience attention harvested for legitimation | User attention harvested for engagement / ad revenue |

Sathe's [[insta-places]] are exactly the contemporary instances: cognitive shortcuts that present themselves as solid places worth visiting, regardless of whether the place has any intimate-attachment density. The Instagram pin, the Maps annotation, the Airbnb Experience, the TikTok-discovered cafe — each performs the place royale's manufactured-visibility function at platform scale. The wiki's [[algorithmic-curation-as-place-making]] correctly identifies what the algorithm does: it makes a place by catching and holding attention, regardless of whether the place has lived attachment behind it.

Tuan supplies the missing piece: this is not new. *Place-manufacture has always been political-economic technology.* The Baroque court and the Bonapartist Republic and the contemporary platform are all in the same business — converting attention into legitimacy and revenue through the deliberate construction of visible place. What is new is *scale and rate*: the platform manufactures place-visibility at sub-second rates and global scale.

## The Tourist Universalized

[[the-explorer-and-the-tourist]] in this wiki distinguishes two postures: the explorer who navigates illegible terrain by creating personal landmarks, and the tourist who consumes pre-made legibility (guidebooks, algorithms, summaries).

Tuan's Ch. 10 distinction between intimate place and touristic place gives the explorer/tourist distinction its foundational geographical instantiation. Tuan, citing Pirsig: "Quality is what you see out of the corner of your eye" (Tuan 2002, p. 147). The intimate place is what is perceived peripherally, in passing, in the act of dwelling. The touristic place is what is named, monumentalized, dramatized, *attended to deliberately*. The tourist's failure is not lack of attention; it is lack of *peripheral, unmarked, sustained dwelling*.

Apply this at platform scale. The algorithm pre-names the world. Every cafe is a "must-visit"; every neighborhood has a "vibe"; every trail has a Strava segment. The result is that *everyone is made a tourist* by the algorithm's pre-naming, even in their own city. There is nothing left to discover by peripheral attention because the peripheral has been absorbed into the manufactured visibility regime.

This is the dark corollary of [[naming-as-anchoring]]. Naming is power; naming creates place. When naming is industrialized, the *intimate-place register* — the corner of the eye, the un-named, the dwelt-in — is foreclosed in advance. The tourist has no inside left to retreat into.

## Why Manufactured Visibility Is Not the Whole Story

Tuan is careful not to demonize visibility. Ch. 12 acknowledges that monumentalization, naming, and civic ritual are constitutive of communal place-attachment at the larger-than-intimate scales. The home is intimate place; the city requires manufactured visibility to be a city. The neighborhood acquires identity precisely through external naming and rivalry (Boston West End, Beacon Hill, pp. 169–172). Nationalism's manufactured place, problematic as it is in some forms, also enables collective political action.

The algorithmic regime's failure is not visibility per se. It is the *scale-amplification of manufactured visibility while simultaneously eroding the intimate-place register*. The platform pins everywhere as a place; the platform's economic logic also dissolves the conditions for intimate dwelling (mobility expectations, screen-time, the substitution of platform-mediated socialization for un-named local sociability). Both edges of the scissor: more manufactured place than ever, less intimate place than ever.

The wiki's [[the-architecture-of-resistance]] / [[The Thicket]] / [[the-cloister-web]] arguments are responses to this scissor. The thicket is the deliberately *un-named* habitat — the place that resists manufactured visibility *and* cultivates intimate dwelling. The cloister web is semi-private deliberate cultivation — the small commons in which dwelling can happen below the visibility threshold of the manufacturing regime. Both are Tuan-inflected resistance strategies in Bryant's terminology: alternative gravitational fields ([[the-gravity-of-infrastructure]]) constructed to allow temporal disengagement ([[temporal-topology-and-the-viscous-frontier]]) and intimate place-attachment to survive.

## Essay Seeds

**"Everyone Is a Tourist Now"**
A short, sharp essay developing the claim that the algorithm's pre-naming makes everyone a tourist in their own city. Use Tuan's intimate vs. monumental distinction (Ch. 10 vs. Ch. 12) to diagnose what is lost. Pair with the wiki's [[the-architecture-of-resistance]] to argue for deliberate cultivation of un-named, un-visible dwelling.

**"The Algorithmic Place Royale"**
A long essay tracing the historical lineage from Baroque *places royales* to Bonapartist civic monumentalization to the contemporary algorithmic feed. The thesis: manufactured visibility has always been political-economic technology; the platform scale-amplifies a centuries-old practice. The diagnostic question is who controls the manufacture, who benefits from it, and what counter-architectures (intimate place, slow takes, small webs) can survive at sub-visible rates.

**"Outside the Pin"**
A practice-piece exploring what it means to deliberately seek the *unpinned* — places, foods, experiences that the algorithm has not yet named. Argue this is not nostalgia but a Tuan-grounded epistemic practice: peripheral attention, intimate dwelling, the corner of the eye. Connect to [[curiosity-as-ego-dissolution]] and [[unselfing]].
