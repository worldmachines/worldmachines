# Composability and Inequality

## The Tension

Two sources in this wiki make incompatible-looking claims:

1. [[The Arrival of Composable Knowledge]]: composability has steadily democratized knowledge — each technological leap (writing, printing, internet) made knowledge more accessible to more people.

2. [[Jan. 3, 2025]] (via Eric So et al.): *"AI widens the knowledge-driven disparities between sophisticated and unsophisticated participants."*

The first claim is historical. The second is empirical and current. Are they contradictory?

## Resolution: Multiplier vs. Leveler

The apparent contradiction resolves if you distinguish two mechanisms:

**Leveling**: A tool that raises the floor — it helps those with less more than it helps those with more. The printing press may have been partially leveling; cheap books helped those who previously had no access to books.

**Multiplying**: A tool that scales with existing capability — it helps sophisticated users proportionally more. AI summaries appear to be multiplicative: the more you know, the more precise query you can write, the more precise output you get.

These are not mutually exclusive. A tool can raise the floor AND widen the gap — it can help everyone while helping the skilled more. Whether *net* inequality increases or decreases depends on which effect dominates.

## The Jargon Mechanism

See [[Jargon as Compression]]. The AI democratization gap operates primarily through query quality:
- Access to AI is (increasingly) near-universal
- The bottleneck is knowing what to ask and how to ask it
- Domain expertise compresses into jargon, which compresses into better queries, which extracts better output
- There is no substitute for this accumulated expertise

This means the composability upgrade provided by LLMs is *structurally* multiplicative. It rewards the same thing the previous infrastructure rewarded: deep domain knowledge.

## Historical Re-read

Did earlier composability leaps also multiply rather than level? Possibly:
- The printing press helped those who could read (literacy was stratified) more than those who couldn't
- The internet helps those who can evaluate sources more than those who can't
- Search engines help those who can formulate good queries more than those who can't

Each technology has its jargon equivalent — a set of skills that determines how much of the tool's capability one can actually access. Composability may always have been multiplicative; we retrospectively read it as leveling because the long-run effect is broadly positive.

## What Would Genuine Leveling Look Like?

The Shah & White [[Sims (AI User Simulation)]] proposal is relevant here: a Sim that holds intimate knowledge of a user and can formulate precise queries *on their behalf* could, in principle, close the query-quality gap. Whether it would actually do so depends entirely on who trains the Sim and whose interests it represents (see [[Principal-Agent Problem in AI]]).

Similarly, [[Sparing and Freeing]] in interface design: if good interfaces can reduce the burden of query precision — helping users ask better questions without requiring full domain fluency — this could partially rebalance the multiplicative effect.

## Essay Seed

**Thesis candidate**: The history of composable knowledge is not a history of democratization but of multiplication. Each composability upgrade — writing, print, internet, LLMs — has been most valuable to those who were already most knowledgeable. The gains are real and broadly positive, but the inequality signature is consistent across centuries. This doesn't mean the upgrades are bad; it means "democratization" is systematically the wrong frame.

**Complication**: This thesis could be wrong. It's possible that some composability leaps *have* been genuinely leveling (universal literacy, for example), and that what we observe with LLMs is a temporary phase before the leveling effects kick in.
