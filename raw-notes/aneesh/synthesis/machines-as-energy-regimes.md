# Machines as Energy Regimes

## The Parallel

Each world machine is not just an institutional or epistemic arrangement — it is an energy regime. The transitions between machines are energy transitions. The intellectual and cultural superstructure that defines a machine is downstream of (and co-constituted with) the energy surplus that makes it possible.

## Energy Regimes by Machine

### Pre-Axial (Palace Economies)

Solar-agricultural surplus, centrally redistributed. Energy capture ~10,000–20,000 kcal/day/capita. The machine optimized for administrative control of surplus — the palace economy is an energy management system before it is anything else. Writing itself emerged to track grain stores.

### Axial Age

Same energy base as the pre-axial, but a massive institutional innovation: schools, legal codes, universal ethical systems, new channels for surplus deployment. The Axial machine did not increase energy capture. What it increased was the *information content per unit of energy* — more structured knowledge per calorie of surplus. This is the first historical demonstration that civilizational transitions need not be energy transitions; they can be information transitions riding the same energy base.

### Medieval

Wind power, water power, the horse collar, the heavy plow, crop rotation. Still solar-based but with significantly expanded mechanical conversion. Berman's Papal Revolution created the institutional pluralism (competing jurisdictions: church, crown, city, guild) that enabled competitive innovation. Monasteries functioned as knowledge infrastructure — libraries, scriptoria, agricultural laboratories. Energy capture rising but still fundamentally solar.

### Modernity Machine

Fossil fuels. Coal to steam to oil to electricity. Energy capture explodes: ~50,000 to 200,000+ kcal/day/capita. The MM's pull toward Progress — its canonical direction, its legibility drive, its scaling imperative — was *powered* by fossil energy surplus. Without coal, there is no industrial revolution; without oil, there is no globalizing modernity. Legibility optimized the deployment of fossil-fuel energy: standardized measures, national grids, supply chains, accounting systems all served to channel energy flows more efficiently.

### Divergence Machine

Inherits fossil infrastructure wholesale, adds a digital/information layer on top. Energy capture still rising but EROI (Energy Return on Investment) declining: from ~30:1 for conventional oil to ~18:1 for oil/gas overall and dropping. The DM's push forces — pluralism, divergent intent, individual agency — are informationally powered, riding the energy infrastructure the MM built. The DM is, energetically, a parasite on the MM: it could not exist without the fossil substrate, but its distinctive features are informational, not energetic.

This raises a hard question: can the DM survive the MM's energy base declining? Or is divergence a luxury good of high-EROI fossil civilization?

## Tainter's Complication

Joseph Tainter's core insight: complexity is not chosen — it is *compelled* by problem-solving, and it drives resource use in turn. The MM's energy surplus did not *cause* complexity. Complexity *demanded* energy. Each problem the MM solved (disease, famine, logistics, governance at scale) added a layer of administrative and technical complexity that required more energy to maintain.

The energy-complexity spiral explains why the MM could not reform itself. Every attempt to address the problems generated by fossil-fueled complexity required *more* complexity, which required *more* energy. The system became locked in. Collapse, for Tainter, is not a catastrophe — it is an economizing measure, a return to a lower-complexity, lower-cost configuration.

Applied to the world machines: the MM-to-DM transition is either (a) Tainter-style simplification, where the DM represents a lower-complexity arrangement riding informational efficiency instead of energetic brute force, or (b) a genuine phase transition to a new complexity regime. The answer may depend on whether the DM finds a new energy base or remains parasitic on fossil infrastructure.

## Constructal Law Perspective

Adrian Bejan's constructal law: flow systems evolve toward tree-shaped architectures that maximize access for what flows. Each machine's infrastructure evolves toward flow-maximizing configurations — road networks, river trade, rail systems, power grids, fiber optic networks.

When a machine's flow architecture hits diminishing returns (Tainter), it doesn't simply fail — it collapses and a new architecture emerges. The MM's flow architecture (centralized grids, hub-and-spoke logistics, hierarchical institutions) is being superseded by the DM's (distributed networks, mesh architectures, flat organizations). The constructal law predicts this: when tree-shaped flows saturate, the next architecture is a network, because networks access more of the available space.

## Maximum Power Principle

Howard Odum's maximum power principle: natural systems — and civilizations — optimize for maximum power (energy throughput per unit time), not for efficiency. This has profound implications:

- The cost of action drops across machines not because *efficiency* improves but because *total power* rises. More energy flows through the system per unit time, making each individual act proportionally cheaper.
- This explains the Jevons Paradox at civilizational scale: efficiency gains don't reduce total consumption — they increase it, because the system optimizes for power, not conservation.
- Tim Garrett's empirical finding: ~7.1 watts per $1000 of accumulated civilizational wealth, constant for decades. Wealth and energy throughput are linearly coupled. The economy *is* a heat engine.

The maximum power lens reframes the world machines: each machine is not a cultural or institutional arrangement that *happens* to use energy. Each machine *is* an energy dissipation regime — a way of organizing maximum power throughput at a given scale of complexity.

## Implications for the Lightening of Intent

The [[the-cost-of-intent-across-machines|cost-of-intent thesis]] gains thermodynamic grounding here:

- Intent-execution cost falls because total system power rises (maximum power principle)
- Each machine's infrastructure is a flow architecture optimized for throughput (constructal law)
- The DM inherits the MM's energy infrastructure but may not be able to sustain it (EROI decline)
- If the energy base contracts, does the cost of intent rise again? Or has the informational layer decoupled enough to sustain cheap execution on a thinner energy base?

This last question may be the decisive one for the DM's long-term viability.

## Adams's Coal Dynamometer: A Primary Source Inside the Fossil Transition

This page's Modernity Machine section argues that the MM's pull toward Progress was *powered* by fossil surplus, and that legibility served to channel energy flows. [[adams-education]] supplies a witness who reached substantially that thesis from inside the transition, published it in 1904, and tried to attach a number to it. The value is not that Adams was right — it is that the energy-regime reading is available as contemporaneous testimony rather than only as retrospective interpretation.

**Coal as the unit of historical force.** [[adams-education-ch-34-a-law-of-acceleration]] is the measurement program. Lacking any direct instrument for "social movement," Adams settled for what nineteenth-century society already measured — coal-power — and treated its doubling roughly every ten years between 1840 and 1900 as a stand-in for the growth of total historical force. This makes the coal series a dynamometer rather than an economic statistic: the claim is not that coal correlates with history but that coal *is* the readable face of the era's motive power. He then pushes the ratio backward through the mathematicians, the architects, and prehistoric stone tools, conceding at every step that the numbers get less trustworthy while insisting the sequence does not.

The habit predates the theory by a decade. [[adams-education-ch-21-twenty-years-after]] already has him reaching for the same instrument on instinct, measuring American coal output (160 million tons) against the British Empire's (180 million) as the one place he can still locate the era's real motive force — and noting, pointedly, that it yields him no new education, since the railway system, unlike thought, offers "no chance for future profit" to a mind already made. Coal was legible to him as the era's power long before he tried to make it a unit.

**The regime named outright.** [[adams-education-ch-27-teufelsdrockh]] gives the empire its emperor. Descending into the industrial Rhineland in 1901, Adams finds the old Asia-Europe trade routes erased and replaced by a single infrastructure — Cologne, Birmingham and Pittsburgh producing "the same power... the same mind... the same impulse" — which he names "one great empire... ruled by one great emperor--Coal," continuous from Hammerfest to Cherbourg to Halifax. That is this page's thesis stated as geography by someone standing in it, and it comes with the legibility claim attached: the empire's mark is that it is the same everywhere.

**The counter-case, which is the more useful half.** Adams identifies what stands outside the coal empire: Russia, "resting on inertia rather than coal," read not through politics but as "a substance whose energies had been sucked out--an inert residuum--with movement of pure inertia," against America's "hasty and unsure acceleration." [[adams-education-ch-30-vis-inertiae]] develops this into a full contrast — Russia "must fatally roll--must, by her irresistible inertia, crush whatever stood in her way," with the Siberian Railway less an instrument of policy than the mechanism by which an already-moving glacier reaches the sea, and no administrative skill from de Witte or Khilkoff able to "lift such a weight." This is a contemporary applying an energy-regime typology to a live geopolitical rival: mass without throughput, set against throughput without mass. It is the maximum power principle's shadow — a polity organized around neither efficiency nor power, only momentum — and Adams treats the resulting foreign policy as mechanically determined by that fact.

**Adams's economy/volume distinction, and why it matters to the Axial Age section.** [[adams-education-ch-33-a-dynamic-theory-of-history]] makes a distinction this page arrives at independently. Adams insists that most of recorded history before 1500 shows *no growth in the volume of force available to man* — only "economies of energy" (roads, coinage, law) applied to forces known since deep antiquity: fire, muscle, wind. Genuine historical rupture, in his scheme, requires a new force entering the system, not a better use of an old one. That is the same move as this page's Axial Age claim that a civilizational transition "need not be an energy transition; it can be an information transition riding the same energy base" — reached by a different route and stated as a general law rather than a single case. Adams's version is stronger and probably too strong: it makes pre-1500 history definitionally incapable of rupture, which his own Constantine analysis then has to work around by recasting the Cross as a force being *concentrated* rather than discovered.

**The MM's construction burst, first-hand.** [[adams-education-ch-16-the-press]] is an eyewitness account of the buildout. Returning in 1868, Adams finds coal, iron and steam have given "a distinct superiority in power over the old industrial elements--agriculture, handwork, and learning," and that his generation is "already mortgaged to the railways": having settled the Union question by "sheer force," the country turns to building "capital, banks, mines, furnaces, shops, power-houses, technical knowledge, mechanical population, together with a steady remodelling of social and political habits, ideas, and institutions to fit the new scale." Institutions being remodelled *to fit the energy scale* — with everyone alive mortgaged to a project none of them individually chose — is this page's co-constitution claim, reported by someone who watched it happen in one decade.

**The blind spot, dated.** [[adams-education-ch-02-boston]] records how invisible the transition was to the class best positioned to see it. Unitarian Boston in the 1850s had reduced faith to mild moralism, considered "Suffrage, Common Schools, and Press" sufficient to guarantee social perfection, and "had solved the universe." The one man Adams says saw what was actually coming was Marx — and what Marx saw was not a political argument but a quantity: sixty or seventy million tons of coal and nearly a million steam-horsepower "just beginning to make itself felt."

**Where Adams stops.** He has a proxy he admits he cannot validate, a doubling rate he cannot explain, and no mechanism connecting energy throughput to the historical motion it is supposed to measure — the gap [[energy-rate-density]] later tries to close with a defensible quantity, and that Tainter, Bejan and Odum close from the theory side. What Adams has that they do not is the view from inside: a professional historian in 1904, reaching for the nearest available number, and landing on coal because it was the only thing in his century that was obviously getting bigger.
