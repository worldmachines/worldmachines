# Play as Navigational Instrument

## The Problem of Direction

In creative and intellectual life, there is rarely a clear destination. External goals ("become a published novelist," "get tenure") are often arbitrary or misaligned with what actually motivates you. The question is: how do you navigate without a fixed destination?

Three concepts from this corpus offer complementary answers:

## Three Instruments

### 1. Ludic Feedback Loops (Cadence)
**From**: [[Work or Play? Ludic Feedback Loops]], [[venkatesh-rao]]

The question: *at what rate does this work reward me?*

[[Ludic Feedback Loops|Ludic frequency]] gives you information about whether the *cadence* of a domain is right for you. Fast feedback on meaningful problems = sustainable engagement. This instrument tells you when to stay and when to leave a domain.

### 2. Infinite Game (Continuity)
**From**: [[Infinite Game]], [[Robert Peake]], [[James Carse]]

The question: *am I still playing?*

The infinite game framework reframes success: you are not trying to win, you are trying to keep playing. Falling off is not failure; re-entering is the move. This instrument gives you permission to persist imperfectly and prevents catastrophic quitting after a single miss.

### 3. Maximal Interestingness (Direction)
**From**: [[Maximal Interestingness]], [[On Protocols, Wagons, and Associated Acrobatics]]

The question: *which way is most interesting from here?*

When cadence is right and you're staying in the game, you still need to pick a next move. Interest is the most reliable signal when destinations are unknown. This instrument gives you direction at each decision point.

## How They Work Together

| Instrument | Question | Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Ludic frequency | Should I be doing this kind of work at all? | Felt reward cadence |
| Infinite game | Should I quit after this failure? | Is there still a game to play? |
| Maximal interestingness | Which direction now? | Felt sense of curiosity |

A fully instrumented navigation:
- Choose domains with the right ludic frequency (stay if rewarded fast enough on meaningful problems)
- Persist through gaps with infinite game thinking (getting back on the wagon is the move)
- Pick next steps by pointing toward maximal interestingness (curiosity as compass)

## The Montaigne Connection

Montaigne, in [[Looking Forward to Montaigne]], operated by something like this framework: he wrote when interested, on whatever interested him (maximal interestingness), continued for decades (infinite game), in a form that rewarded his curiosity with immediate satisfaction (adequate ludic frequency).

His essays are the output of someone well-calibrated on all three instruments.

## Thesis Candidate

**Play is not the opposite of work — it is the navigational system for work without a predetermined destination. Ludic frequency, infinite game thinking, and maximal interestingness are three instruments that together constitute a complete navigation system for creative and intellectual life.**
