# Documentation as Gift Economy

## The Thread

Three essays ([[Divine Documentation]], [[A Good Dictionary]], and implicitly [[Hack Hacky Hacker]]'s "evil twin" framing) share an underlying structure: **someone in the past did the hard work so you don't have to**, and the quality of intellectual life depends on whether those gifts are maintained.

- [[William Zinsser]] wrote *On Writing Well* so future writers don't have to discover prose mechanics the hard way
- [[John McPhee]] demonstrated dictionary use so future writers know what the tool is for
- [[James Somers]] updated the 1913 Webster's installation instructions so future Mac users can access it
- The author of [[A Good Dictionary]] updated those instructions again, five years later

This is a **relay of gifts**, each writer extending the chain by a few more years into the future.

## The Gift Economy Structure

A gift economy differs from a market economy: goods are given without immediate reciprocity, and status accrues to those who give most. Documentation culture, personal blogging, and open-source software all operate on gift-economy logic:
- You give knowledge to strangers who will never repay you directly
- The gift compounds when recipients extend it to the next generation
- The system breaks when people stop extending gifts and only consume

The [[Small Web]] lament is partly a lament for a failing gift economy: social media platforms have captured the attention that used to flow back into the gift chain (personal blogs, updated documentation, extended instructions).

## The Counter-Force: Decay

Documentation decays. [[James Somers]]'s instructions were "a little out of date." Corey Ward's were "mostly still work." The author had to update them again. This is the default trajectory: gifts depreciate without ongoing maintenance.

The Nakatomi Tunnel approach applies here too: each person who encounters outdated documentation and updates it for the next person is being *hacky* about the documentation ecosystem — lateral, low-effort, high-value.

## Essay Direction

**Seed**: "The Documentation Chain: Knowledge as Gift, Decay, and Relay"

The argument: knowledge transfer is a gift economy with a decay problem. The internet promised to solve this by making gifts permanent and searchable, but in practice platforms accelerated decay by redirecting attention away from maintenance. The essay could trace the Webster's chain as a concrete example and argue for documentation culture as civic duty.
