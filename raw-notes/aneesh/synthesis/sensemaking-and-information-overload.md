# Sensemaking and Information Overload

## The Paradox

The standard remedy for poor decisions is better information. More data, faster data, cleaner data. Decision support systems are justified on this basis; intelligence spending is justified on this basis; the entire DIKW (data-information-knowledge-wisdom) pyramid is built on this assumption.

Klein et al. (2007) subvert this directly: accuracy increases with data elements up to a point (~8-10 elements) and then **asymptotes**, while **confidence continues rising** (Oskamp, 1965). Lanir's (1991) study of intelligence failures (Yom Kippur War) found that decision makers already had sufficient data in every case — their difficulty was analysis, not acquisition.

Army officers commented to Klein et al. that information technology was *slowing* the decision cycle, not speeding it — soldiers delaying decisions with each new data element received, trying to figure out its significance.

## Why More Data Can Hurt

The data-frame theory explains the mechanism. Data are not perceptual primitives; they are constructed through frames. A frame can only process data it has slots for. Presenting data without the frame context to interpret them does not improve understanding — it creates noise that the sensemaker must either explain away (frame preservation) or be paralyzed by (no available frame).

The frame bottleneck: people use at most 3–4 anchors to build a frame. Additional data elements beyond the anchoring set don't contribute proportionally to frame quality. They may actively interfere by competing for anchor status with more relevant elements, or by demanding explanation within an already-specified frame.

**The equivocality problem** (Weick 1995): the problem in complex sensemaking situations is not *uncertainty* (insufficient information) but *equivocality* (too many possible meanings). More data increases equivocality. Resolving equivocality requires values, priorities, and frame clarity — not additional data points.

## The DIKW Critique

The data-information-knowledge-wisdom hierarchy is the implicit architecture of most decision support systems and information management frameworks. Klein et al. argue it is "misleading" — it "enshrines the information-processing approach to sensemaking, and runs counter to an ecological approach that asserts that the data themselves need to be constructed."

The DIKW model treats data as the raw material that processing converts into knowledge. The data-frame model inverts this: frames construct data. What counts as a datum depends on the frame in use. A different frame would make different aspects of the environment visible. The DIKW pyramid cannot account for the constructive direction.

## What Actually Helps

Klein et al.'s DSS recommendations reorient from data provision to frame support:
1. **Track available anchors** — since people use so few, help them identify which data elements can serve as anchors
2. **Support abductive inference** — not just logical inference chains
3. **Support anomaly detection** — make violated expectancies salient (the frame-questioning trigger)
4. **Don't restrict to logical inferences** — abductive speculation is often the appropriate mode
5. **Avoid fusion algorithms that obscure logical bases** — opaque data fusion compounds the equivocality problem

## Connection to Intelligence Analysis

Heuer (1999) made the same point from the intelligence analysis side: analysts need help evaluating and interpreting data, not acquiring more of it. Patterson et al. (1998) found that initial misleading intelligence articles distorted all subsequent analysis — because the initial frame (anchored on the first articles) guided all subsequent search. This is a pure data-frame dynamic: early anchors corrupt late interpretation.

The intelligence policy lesson: the problem is frame quality and anchor reliability, not intelligence volume. Surge collection without frame improvement produces more noise to preserve incorrect frames against.

## Synthesis Across Sources

This synthesis connects:
- **Klein et al. (2007)** on the frame construction of data and anchor economy
- **Weick (1995)** on equivocality as the core information management problem
- **Heuer (1999)** on the intelligence analysis failure mode

The common thread: decision quality in complex environments is a sensemaking problem (frame quality), not an information problem (data quantity). This should substantially reorient how we design decision support, train analysts, and evaluate intelligence systems.
