# Places, Problems, and the Act of Bounding

## The Common Act

Across four of Aneesh Sathe's essays, a single constitutive act recurs: **drawing a boundary**.

- **[[Space and Place Philosophy]]**: A space becomes a place the moment a boundary is drawn — "here." The place is the bounded zone; the space is what the boundaries define negatively.
- **[[Problems vs Questions]]**: A problem is a bounded question — tractable, named, solvable with established methods. Drawing the boundary is what converts an open question into a closed problem.
- **[[Data Generation Process]]**: Feature extraction, experimental design, and measurement all involve drawing boundaries — deciding what counts as data, what constitutes a valid comparison, what the unit of observation is.
- **[[Antimeme]]**: Administrative structures and legal frameworks bound jurisdiction, responsibility, and interpretive authority. The boundary around an official's role determines what they can be held accountable for.

## Bounding as Constitutive and Foreclosing

The act of bounding is **constitutive**: without it, nothing is tractable, knowable, or governable. You cannot analyze what you haven't measured; you cannot solve what you haven't bounded; you cannot govern what you haven't structured.

But it is also **foreclosing**: the boundary creates the tractable zone by excluding everything outside it. Confounders are variables that matter but fall outside the experimental boundary. Antimemetic power operates in the spaces between jurisdictions — in the gaps the boundaries leave.

## The Scientific Case

[[Beyond the Dataset|Beyond the Dataset]] is centrally about bounding discipline: understanding exactly what your measurements bound (the instrument's resolution, the experiment's randomization) so you know what your conclusions can and cannot extend to. A crack in the boundary — a confounder, a sensor drift — propagates upward into every conclusion built on the bounded zone.

## The Philosophical Case

[[Problems vs Questions|Problems are Places, Questions are Spaces]] argues that premature bounding (converting a question into a problem too early) forecloses exploration. The Socratic method is a discipline of *refusing* to bound — holding the question as a space rather than packaging it as a place.

## Essay Seed

*The ontology of the boundary*: a unified account of how drawing boundaries constitutes tractable objects in governance, science, and epistemology — and what it means to bound well vs. bound badly. What are the criteria for a good boundary? When should you refuse to draw one?
