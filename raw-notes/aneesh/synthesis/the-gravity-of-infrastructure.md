# The Gravity of Infrastructure

## The Reframing

Bryant's choice of "gravity" over "power" is rhetorical *and* substantive. Power and force, in Bryant's diagnosis, are tautological terms in the social sciences: invoking them explains nothing because they merely re-describe the phenomenon to be explained (Bryant 2014, pp. 187–88). Worse, they are anthropocentric — they direct attention to discourse, narrative, and ideology while leaving the plane of content (rivers, currents, mountain ranges, fossil-fuel reserves) analytically invisible.

> "The rhetorical advantage of the concept of gravity over terms like power and force is that it gives an all-purpose term capable of straddling both humans and non-humans, the social and the natural, so that we avoid fixating on the cultural." (Bryant 2014, p. 188)

The Einsteinian move is the substantive one. Bodies don't *attract* one another; they travel curved paths produced by mass. The bright object curves the field of action; the satellite has not been seduced or coerced — it has simply been given a topology in which its possible trajectories are pre-shaped.

Read this against what the wiki has already argued and the convergence is total.

## Oil as the Brightest Object

Bryant's most concentrated example is oil:

> "Perhaps the brightest object of all today is oil. There is nothing on the planet that is not somehow caught in its gravitational pull and transformed into a satellite by it... In one way or another, all life on the planet is currently structured by paths created by oil." (Bryant 2014, p. 204)

Two consequences for the wiki cluster on energy and machines:

**First**, [[solar-as-flow-vs-fossil-as-stock]] is doing gravitational physics, not just energy economics. The fossil regime is a gravitational topology: it pre-shapes what jobs exist, what cities can be inhabited, what foods can be eaten, what political coalitions are possible. The solar transition is not a fuel substitution; it is the construction of an *alternative gravitational field* in Bryant's strict sense — alternative bright objects (utility-scale solar, distributed batteries, EV charging networks) capable of capturing satellites that are currently in fossil orbit. [[swansons-law]] is the cost-curve at which the alternative field becomes practically constructible.

**Second**, the wiki's [[the-cost-of-intent-across-machines]] thesis is a gravitational claim: each civilizational machine produces a different topology of possible action, and "execution cost" is the energetic price of moving through that topology. The Modernity Machine subsidized convergent intent by curving the field toward Progress. The Divergence Machine inherits the infrastructure but no longer subsidizes any particular direction; the field is locally flat in many places, which means producing direction is now the expensive metabolic act — see [[viscous-frontier-and-cost-of-intent]].

## The Politics: Light Machines vs. Heavy Machines

This is where the synthesis becomes a self-criticism of the wiki.

[[heavy-vs-light-machines]] notes the implicit Bryant distinction: heavy machines (infrastructure, energy regimes, supply chains, monetary systems) exercise gravitational power that does not depend on belief; light machines (ideologies, narratives, cultural norms) exercise power through signification.

The company-scrip example (Bryant 2014, pp. 203–204) is the sharp instance. Scrip works *not* because workers believe in the company's ideology of natural capital-labor relations. It works because it controls access to food, clothing, shelter, and energy. You can debunk the ideology without freeing anyone from scrip. You cannot debunk the scrip; you can only build an alternative payment system or pass legal-tender laws.

The wiki has historically over-emphasized light-machine politics. The essays on [[AI Homogenization]], the [[the-architecture-of-resistance]], the [[The Thicket]] — all describe valuable strategies of cultural-discursive resistance. They are not wrong. But they are the politics of the *light machine layer*, where the gravitational pull is exerted by signs, norms, and cultural averaging. The wiki has done less work on heavy-machine politics: who builds the alternative infrastructure, who pays for it, what regulatory and material conditions enable it, how solar, distributed compute, federated protocols, mutual-credit networks, and cooperative ownership become viable bright objects.

Bryant's political prescription is unambiguous:

> "The question therefore is one of *building* alternatives that might generate lines of flight or paths of escape from these orbits." (Bryant 2014, p. 205)

This is the [[from-artifact-to-process]] thesis applied to politics: don't perfect the critique; build the heavy machine. [[live-zones]] in Sathe's vocabulary are exactly this — accumulating structures with non-retractable artifact requirements that produce alternative gravitational fields. Anne Carpenter's twenty-year arc on Cell Painting ([[the-viscous-frontier]]) is heavy-machine construction in biology. The Hōkūleʻa voyage was heavy-machine construction in cultural memory. Both succeeded because they built infrastructure, not because they criticized the existing field.

## Both, Not Either

The synthesis is not "abandon ideology critique for infrastructure construction." Bryant explicitly resists this:

> "The concept of gravity proposed here includes all of the discursivist and practice-based elements of concepts of power proposed by theorists such as Foucault and Bourdieu, while also helping us to recognize the power that things themselves exercise." (Bryant 2014, p. 194)

Light-machine politics matters; the gravitational field has a discursive component. But the wiki has been disproportionately occupied with the light layer. The Bryant ingest is an opportunity to broaden — to ask, of every essay topic, *what heavy machines exercise gravity here, and what alternative heavy machines could be built?* This is the question [[live-zones]] and [[artifact-requirement]] partly answered for individual practice. Bryant lets us ask it at infrastructural scale.

## Algorithmic Feeds as Gravitational Attractors

Apply the framework to the wiki's existing platform critique. The algorithmic feed is:

- **A bright object**: it organizes the "field of choice" of users (Bryant 2014, pp. 202–205).
- **A heavy machine**: switching cost is prohibitive (network effects, accumulated content, social embedding).
- **A gravitational attractor with pre-delineated options**: users do not choose among all conceivable content; they choose among options the algorithm makes available.
- **An asymmetric relation**: platforms depend on users in aggregate but no individual user; users depend on the platform almost completely for the type of social access it organizes.

The political-strategic implication is direct. Boycott individuals does not work — the heavy machine is not affected by isolated departures. Ideology critique of "engagement maximization" does not work — the algorithm does not require user belief. What works: alternative bright objects ([[blogrolls-and-the-personal-web]], [[rss-as-protocol-theology]], federated protocols, mutual-credit-style content economies). This is the [[protocol-gods-and-platform-demons]] thesis in Bryant's vocabulary. The synthesis: *protocols are the heavy-machine alternatives that platforms cannot dislodge once they reach gravitational sufficiency.*
