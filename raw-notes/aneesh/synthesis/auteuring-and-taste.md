# Auteuring and Taste

## The Two Threads

The first thread runs through the wiki's existing material on taste. [[taste-as-epistemic-capacity]] frames taste not as preference but as a discriminative skill that maps onto truth — a way of knowing that is metabolically expensive to develop, refused by easy reduction to rules, and dependent on long exposure to honest feedback from a domain. The companion essay summary [[on-being-good-vs-knowing-good]] reads Nick Cave on the distinction: knowing the good is not the same as being good, and taste sits closer to the second than the first. The summary [[the-tortured-artist-is-so-yesterday]] extends the argument to the engineered-vs-cultivated divide: the things worth caring about cannot be optimised into existence; they must be raised.

The second thread is new. [[auteuring]] names a mode of work in which the human exercises extreme control over a project driven primarily by their taste, while the model executes everything below that control. The bench scientist receives a thousand candidate molecules and her job is to pick. The essayist receives ten variants of a paragraph and her job is to keep one. The founder receives a complete codebase from a coding agent and her job is to architect what it should have been. In each case the model executes, the auteur picks, and the product is the auteur's taste rendered through capacity that could not exist without the substrate.

This page argues that the two threads are not parallel — the second loads the first.

## From Knowing to Picking

In the [[modernity-machine]] regime, taste was an ornament on production. The bench scientist generated her own candidate molecules; the essayist wrote her own first draft; the founder typed her own code. Taste was needed to sift and refine, but the bottleneck was generation. A generation of pedagogy in every domain optimised for *the production of competent generators*. Schools taught how to write a paragraph, code a function, design a synthesis. The taste to choose between them was ambient — important, but not the binding constraint.

The Lightening of Intent essay's claim is that the binding constraint has moved. When the model executes and the human picks, the production function is no longer "human generates competently". It is "human picks correctly". Cave's argument that taste cannot be engineered — that knowing the good cannot be reduced to a rule — was always true. In the MM regime it was true but inert: the heavy work was in generation, where rules and pedagogy got a long way. In the post-DM regime, where generation is free, Cave's argument becomes the load-bearing observation about how value is produced.

The shift is from *knowing the good* to *picking the good*. The two are related but not identical. Knowing the good is propositional: I can articulate why this paragraph works. Picking the good is operational: I can identify it among ten near-misses in the time it takes to read them. Auteuring requires the second. The model has eliminated the labour cost of generating the near-misses; what remains is the perceptual cost of distinguishing them.

## The Generalisation Across Domains

The essay's structural move is to claim that auteuring is not confined to art. It is the regime of work in any domain where execution has been ephemeralised below the perceptual cost of judgement.

- **Science.** The bench scientist who once spent six months synthesising one candidate now receives a thousand in twenty minutes. Her contribution is no longer the synthesis. It is the pick — the judgement of which candidates are worth the wet-lab time, against an evaluation function that has not yet been formalised because if it had been, the machine would already be running it.

- **Writing.** The auteur essayist's contribution is no longer the sentence. It is the keep, the cut, the recombination, the framing. The sentences are cheap; the architecture is expensive; the architecture is taste.

- **Design.** A designer who can specify a brief well enough for a model to produce twenty variants is performing the same act as the auteur picking the take. The variants are free; the brief is the artifact; the brief is taste rendered as constraint.

- **Founding.** The founder who can architect a product without typing the code is performing taste at the level of system design. The codebase is downstream of the architecture; the architecture is downstream of taste about what should exist.

In each case the same structural pattern: the model executes, the human picks, the picking is taste, and taste is what the substrate cannot do for you because taste is the discriminative skill the substrate was trained on (averaged) rather than the discriminative skill the situation demands (specific).

## Why Taste Becomes Scarce

Three reasons taste does not just stay scarce but becomes *more* scarce in the auteur regime.

First, the generative substrate provides a flood of competent near-misses. The discriminative load on the auteur grows with the size of the candidate pool. A scientist filtering ten candidates uses different skills than a scientist filtering ten thousand. The latter requires either a much sharper taste or the courage to pick fewer in less time.

Second, the substrate trains on the average of taste, not its frontier. Every model is a regression toward the mean of its training set. The picks that constitute live taste are by definition off-distribution from the substrate's modal output. Auteur taste must be cultivated *against* the gravitational pull of the substrate's defaults. This is harder than cultivating taste against the absence of substrate — the absence is silent; the defaults are loud.

Third, taste is not engineerable. Cave's argument applied to the auteur regime: there is no curriculum, no optimisation procedure, no scaling law that produces taste in a population. Taste comes from long exposure to honest feedback in a domain, and the substrate has shortened the loops through which that feedback used to arrive. The bench scientist who never had to spend six months on a single synthesis may never have developed the taste that distinguishes the molecules worth those six months.

The substrate makes taste both more central and harder to acquire. This is the structural source of the scarcity.

## What This Predicts

Three predictions follow.

First, the distribution of returns to work in any auteurable domain becomes more taste-weighted. Returns to mid-tier execution converge toward zero (the model does it). Returns to top-tier taste rise sharply (the model amplifies it). The middle of the distribution evacuates. This is visible already in writing, design, and software; it is arriving in science.

Second, the institutions that produce taste become structurally more important than the institutions that produce execution. Apprenticeship, long-form mentorship, place-based scenes — anything that can plausibly transmit taste — gains in value. Credential-based pedagogy that produces competent generators loses. The PhD as currently structured is a competent-generator production system, not a taste-cultivation system, which is why its returns are softening even as research output rises.

Third, the [[the-cost-of-intent-across-machines]] thesis acquires a sharper political economy: the auteur regime is paradise for those whose taste was already cultivated and a treadmill for those whose taste was not. The Lightening of Intent essay's wager is that the political economy of the [[liveness-machine]] is determined by what is done about this asymmetry. If institutions of taste cultivation are built — funded, staffed, permitted to operate at the slow timescales taste actually requires — the auteur regime broadens. If they are not, the auteur regime narrows to a small caste with cultivated taste and a large population whose intent-production has been captured by the substrate's defaults.

The substrate cannot solve this for itself. The auteur regime is the regime in which taste is the production function, and the production function is, by [[crystallized-imagination|Hidalgo]]'s reading, the one thing that cannot be produced by the system itself — only by the slow accumulation of imagination in individuals.

## Essay Seed

A follow-up essay could be a quantitative inversion of the standard productivity story: rather than measuring output per hour of human work, measure *picks per hour* across pre- and post-substrate regimes. The hypothesis is that picks-per-hour has held roughly constant for individual auteurs across a hundred years, while output per pick has risen by orders of magnitude. The implication: the rate-limiting step is and has been perceptual judgement, and the substrate has done nothing for that step. Industries where this metric reveals the actual bottleneck — film direction, scientific research, founding, editorial work — should already show the auteur pattern. Industries that do not yet show it are industries where the substrate has not yet penetrated below the perceptual cost of judgement, and they will be the next to convert.
