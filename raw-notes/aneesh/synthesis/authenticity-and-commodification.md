# Authenticity and Commodification

## The Problem

[[Byung-Chul Han]] argues in *The Crisis of Narration* that storytelling has become storyselling — narrative optimized to manufacture consumers. The mechanism is the attention economy: platforms reward engagement, and engagement rewards emotional manipulation, not truth-telling. The result is a world where every story is a pitch.

## The Structural Question

Is authentic expression structurally possible under current conditions, or does the infrastructure of attention-economy publishing inevitably commodify even well-intentioned writing?

Three positions:

1. **Hard structuralism (Han)**: the platform optimizes for engagement, which is incompatible with authentic expression. Writing for an audience on social media is always already storyselling.

2. **Soft resistance**: certain forms are more structurally resistant than others. The [[Personal Essay as Form|personal essay]] — meandering, non-teleological, frank — is harder to optimize for conversion. It can survive on the margins.

3. **Individual heroics**: authenticity is a matter of intention and practice, not infrastructure. Some writers are authentic on Instagram; some are dishonest in personal essays.

The author of [[Looking Forward to Montaigne]] takes position 2: [[Michel de Montaigne]] as evidence that the form itself resists commodification.

## The Montaigne Test

What specifically makes Montaigne's writing resistant to storyselling?

- **No audience optimization**: he wrote for a coterie of friends, not for conversion.
- **No product**: he wasn't selling anything, had no subscription, no brand. His economic position (aristocrat on retirement income) removed commercial pressure.
- **Meander as signal**: meandering is *expensive* for a salesperson — it breaks the conversion funnel. It's a costly signal of non-commercial intent.
- **Provisional conclusions**: "what do I know?" is bad marketing. Confident claims sell; epistemic humility doesn't.

## The Old Internet Parallel

[[The Old Internet]] had similar structural properties: most writers had no monetization, no brand, small audiences. The structural condition of non-commerciality enabled authenticity. When the platforms arrived with advertising models and follower counts, the same structural commodification pressure Han describes took hold.

This suggests authenticity is less a virtue than a structural property — it thrives in low-commercial-pressure environments and withers under optimization.

## Thesis Candidate

**Authentic expression is not primarily a moral achievement but a structural one. To write authentically in the current environment requires either (a) economic independence from audience, (b) choosing forms that are structurally resistant to optimization, or (c) writing for small audiences where performance pressure is low. Montaigne had (a) and (b). The old internet had (c). Social media has eliminated all three for most writers.**
