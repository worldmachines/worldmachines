# Frames as the Unit of Cognition

## The Convergence

From very different traditions, a cluster of 20th-century thinkers arrived at the same structural insight: **the primary unit of cognition is not the inference, the belief, or the proposition — it is the frame.**

| Thinker | Domain | Term | Key Claim |
|---------|--------|------|-----------|
| Klein et al. (2007) | Naturalistic decision-making | Frame | Data-frame mutual construction; frames determine what counts as data |
| Karl Weick (1995) | Organizational theory | Sensemaking | Organizations exist as sensemaking processes; plausibility over accuracy |
| Erving Goffman (1974) | Sociology | Frame | Social life organized by culturally relative frames that organize experience |
| Marvin Minsky (1975) | Artificial intelligence | Frame | Knowledge represented as data structures with slots and default values |
| Thomas Kuhn (1962) | History of science | Paradigm | Normal science operates within paradigms; anomalies accumulate until paradigm shift |
| Gregory Bateson (1972) | Anthropology/communication | Frame | Metacommunication ("this is play") provides frames that determine message meaning |
| Piaget (1952/1954) | Developmental psychology | Schema | Cognitive development proceeds through schema construction, assimilation, and accommodation |

These are not the same concept. But they share a family resemblance: **a structure that precedes and shapes perception**, determining what is noticed, what counts as relevant, and what action is appropriate.

## What the Convergence Means

### Against Atomic Empiricism
The convergence is a collective refutation of the view that experience presents itself to us as neutral data which cognition then processes. From Minsky's AI frames to Kuhn's paradigms to Klein's sensemaking frames, the argument is the same: there is no data without a frame that makes it data. The frame is not layered on top of raw experience; it is constitutive of experience.

This is the deep implication Klein et al. spell out: "The data identify the relevant frame, and the frame determines which data are noticed. *Neither of these comes first.*"

### The Construction Problem
If frames construct data, then sensemaking failures are not primarily failures of information quality or reasoning accuracy. They are failures of frame quality. You can have all the information in the world and miss the key pattern if your frame doesn't make that pattern visible.

This reframes (pun structural) the entire project of improving decision-making:
- **Classical rational choice**: improve options enumeration and expected value calculation
- **Heuristics and biases**: identify and correct cognitive distortions from the correct baseline
- **Frame-based view**: improve the frames through which data are constructed; expand frame repertoire; train anomaly detection

### The Preservation Problem
Every frame has a self-stabilizing mechanism. Frames direct attention toward confirming data and away from disconfirming data. This is functional (frames need stability to operate) and pathological (frames can persist past their useful life). The mechanisms of preservation — knowledge shields, explaining away, confirmation-seeking — are the same in individual cognition (Klein), organizational routines (Weick/Vaughan), normal science (Kuhn), and AI overfitting.

The question is what makes a frame vulnerable to replacement. Klein: violated expectancies generated by a sufficiently differentiated frame. Kuhn: accumulation of anomalies past a threshold. Weick: dramatic events that force organizational identity revision (Mann Gulch).

## The Minsky-Klein Connection

Minsky's (1975) AI frames were designed to represent stereotyped knowledge structures with "slots" that could be filled by observed data. Klein et al. are aware of this: they cite Minsky explicitly and note that frames can "fill slots" (Minsky's term) through elaboration. The connection is not incidental — Klein's human sensemaking frames and Minsky's AI frames are attempts to solve the same problem: how does a knowledge system use context to select and organize relevant information?

What Klein adds that Minsky's frame theory lacked: the reciprocal construction of data. Minsky's frames accept data; they don't construct it. Klein's frames construct what counts as data in the first place.

## The Kuhn Connection

Kuhn's scientific paradigms function exactly as Klein's sensemaking frames: they direct attention, determine what counts as anomaly, guide research programs, and resist replacement until a sufficiently dramatic accumulation of anomalies forces a shift. The "frame preservation" behavior Klein describes in individual sensemaking is the same as Kuhn's "normal science" — explaining anomalies away, attributing them to measurement error, invoking special cases.

The difference: in science, paradigm replacement is social and generational (old scientists die; new ones are trained in the new paradigm). In individual sensemaking, reframing can happen in minutes.

## Tensions and Open Questions

- **Frames and creativity**: if frames are necessary for perception, how is genuinely novel insight possible? Klein's "seeking a frame" form gestures at this but doesn't resolve it.
- **Frame relativism**: if different frames make different things visible, can we compare frames for accuracy? Klein says yes (some frames are better than others), but the mechanism of frame evaluation is underspecified.
- **Cultural frames vs. cognitive frames**: Goffman's frames are culturally constructed; Klein's are individually compiled from experience. The interface between these levels is unexplored.
