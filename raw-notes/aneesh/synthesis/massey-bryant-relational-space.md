# Massey and Bryant: Relational Space — Two Versions

**Sources**: [[doreen-massey]], *For Space* (2005); [[levi-bryant]], *Onto-Cartography* (2014)
**See also**: [[massey-for-space-part-4-reorientations]], [[onto-cartography-bryant-ch6-topologies]]

---

## Bryant's Own Citation

Bryant explicitly draws on Massey in *Onto-Cartography*, Ch. 6, citing *For Space* as a near-ally. His characterisation: her three theses on space (product of interrelations; sphere of contemporaneous plurality; always under construction) align closely with his topological conception. His reservation: Massey associates space too closely with narrative and expression rather than with machinic-material processes.

This is a productive disagreement: Bryant is right that Massey's language tends toward the humanistic (stories, trajectories with authors), while his own language is more formally ontological. But Part Four of *For Space* — especially the geological argument about immigrant rocks — shows Massey extending her account to include precisely the nonhuman, material, machinic processes Bryant is interested in.

---

## The Structural Convergence

### 1. Space as Product of Relations, Not Container

Both reject the container model of space:

**Massey**: space is "the product of interrelations; as constituted through interactions, from the immensity of the global to the intimately tiny" (Massey 2005: 9). The space between London and the Lake District is constituted by the train, the geological formations, the social relations of tourism and work, the migratory paths of swifts — not by a neutral Euclidean grid into which these are inserted.

**Bryant**: space arises from the paths of machines through their phase-spaces. Topological distance is not Euclidean distance but relational nearness-or-farness constituted by the interactions of machines. Two machines may be Euclidean-adjacent but topologically distant if they interact minimally; they may be Euclidean-distant but topologically near if they are intensely coupled.

### 2. Multiplicity of Co-existing Trajectories/Paths

**Massey**: space is "the sphere of the possibility of the existence of multiplicity in the sense of contemporaneous plurality" (Massey 2005: 9). The slice through space at any given moment is full of holes, disconnections, tentative encounters — a genuinely multiple simultaneity, not a coherent totality.

**Bryant**: there is no single all-containing space. Different machines produce different spatial fields; there is a *plurality of spatial fields*, each constituted by different machine-interactions. The concept of a single universal container-space is precisely what Bryant is fighting.

### 3. Space as Always-Under-Construction

**Massey**: "It is always in the process of being made. It is never finished; never closed" (Massey 2005: 9).

**Bryant**: topological fields are milieus of becoming, never static. The assemblage of machines is always being reconfigured as machines change, as new machines enter, as existing connections are broken.

---

## The Key Divergences

### 1. Human/Nonhuman Agency

**Bryant**: more formally posthumanist. The category of "machine" is explicitly non-anthropocentric — it includes physical objects, biological organisms, social institutions, texts, and ideas. Agency is distributed across the network of machines without privileging human intentionality.

**Massey**: her language of "trajectories" and "stories-so-far" tends toward a humanistic register (stories have authors; trajectories suggest agency). However, *For Space* Part Four extends this explicitly to geological processes, animal migrations, and plant movements. The immigrant rocks passage shows Massey reaching for the same posthumanist scope as Bryant. This is a difference of emphasis and vocabulary more than of substance.

### 2. Political Inflection

**Massey**: power-geometry is central. The question of who controls trajectories, who is moved by others' decisions, which places are nodes of production and which are victims — this is integral to the spatial analysis. Space is not politically neutral.

**Bryant**: power appears in *Onto-Cartography* through the concept of gravity (which entities have greater influence over the configuration of worlds), but it is less politically specific than Massey's feminist/postcolonial analysis. Bryant's framework can accommodate power-geometry analysis but does not foreground it.

### 3. Narrative vs. Formal Ontology

**Massey**: her account is developed through extended examples — train journeys, geological narratives, stories of home. The philosophical claims emerge from and remain anchored to these concrete cases. This makes her account more accessible and more politically vivid.

**Bryant**: his account is more formally systematic — phase-spaces, attractors, topological distance, gravity fields. This makes it more precise at the cost of requiring more philosophical background to read.

---

## Where They Genuinely Disagree

Bryant's reservation about Massey's reliance on narrative/expression is a genuine critique. Massey's language of "stories" risks suggesting that trajectories require narrating subjects — that place is constituted by the stories people tell rather than by the material processes that constitute it. Her geological argument somewhat corrects this (rocks don't narrate their stories), but the vocabulary can mislead.

Massey might respond to Bryant: his language of "machines" and "phase-spaces" risks making spatial analysis too abstract, losing the political texture of who controls which machines, whose trajectories are constrained, whose are free. The power-geometry analysis requires a vocabulary that can attend to these differentiations.

---

## The Essay Seed

A productive essay could use Massey's power-geometry and Bryant's gravity together: gravity (which entities have greater influence over spatial configurations) is the ontological structure underlying what Massey's power-geometry is politically analysing. The distribution of gravity in a machinic assemblage tracks, and may explain the persistence of, the unequal power-geometries Massey is concerned with.
