# Counter-Enlightenment and Nationalism — The Dangerous Slide

## The Puzzle

[[Isaiah Berlin]] admires the [[Counter-Enlightenment]] thinkers while documenting their catastrophic political legacy. The puzzle he leaves partially unresolved: how did insights that are essentially correct — cultures are incommensurable, there is no view from nowhere, the Enlightenment's universalism is really provincial French bourgeois culture imposed globally — generate the most murderous political movements of the 20th century?

## The Two Paths from the Same Insight

The counter-Enlightenment's core claim — "each culture has its own irreducible centre of gravity" — is Herder's. Herder himself drew a pacifist conclusion: therefore all cultures should flourish side by side; therefore we should condemn Roman imperialism, Christian missionary coercion, and French cultural hegemony alike.

The same insight, in the hands of Maistre, Bonald, and 19th-century nationalists, drew a militarist conclusion: therefore each culture must defend its organic integrity; therefore "unhistoric" peoples who lack a national spirit may be absorbed or destroyed; therefore the executioner is civilization's foundation.

The insight is the same. The conclusions are opposed. Why?

## Berlin's Answer (Partial)

Berlin offers several strands without fully integrating them:

**1. The collectivization of the Romantic self.** The Romantic doctrine that values are created rather than discovered — "I create because it is mine" — started as individual self-expression. When the "self" in question is identified with the nation, class, or race rather than the individual, the same logic generates collective chauvinism. See [[Romanticism and Political Danger]].

**2. The Maistre contamination.** Maistre's strand of the counter-Enlightenment was authoritarian from the start — it never passed through Herderian pluralism. Men are evil and destructive; therefore authority must be absolute; therefore the organic nation is the vessel of authority. This is not a distortion of Herder; it is a parallel development that merged with cultural nationalism in the 19th century.

**3. Seeds already in Herder.** Even in Herder, the logic of cultural self-defence is present: if cosmopolitanism is "the shedding of all that makes one most human," then cultural contamination is a kind of death. The movement from "cultures should be protected" to "cultures must be defended by force" requires only the addition of perceived threat — which political entrepreneurs in suppressed nationalities readily supplied.

## What Berlin Does Not Fully Explain

The account remains genealogical rather than logical. Berlin shows that the ideas flowed from counter-Enlightenment pluralism to fascism. He does not show exactly *which premiss* makes the valid pluralist insight yield the invalid political conclusion, or how to block that inference without abandoning pluralism itself.

The missing premise: something about the *priority of individual rights over collective organic integrity*. If individual persons have rights that no organic collectivity can override, the Herderian insight becomes compatible with liberalism. But this priority itself is a universalist claim — which is in tension with the particularism of value pluralism. This is the permanent tension in Berlin's thought.

## The Relevance to Contemporary Politics

The pattern Berlin describes — valid pluralist critique of universalism hijacked into aggressive particularlism — recurs:

| Historical case | Counter-Enlightenment move | Political hijack |
|---|---|---|
| German nationalism | Herder's cultural pluralism | Ethnic nationalism, Holocaust |
| Anti-colonialism | Legitimacy of non-Western cultures | Authoritarian postcolonial states using culture as cover |
| Contemporary populism | Legitimate democratic resentment of elite cosmopolitanism | Nativist exclusion |

The structure is consistent: a genuinely valid critique of imposed universalism gets collectivized and weaponized by political entrepreneurs who add the premise that the organic community is a unit whose interests override those of the individuals within it.

## The Berlin Resolution (and its limits)

Berlin's resolution: value pluralism is correct, but it is consistent with a minimum floor of basic rights that all cultures must respect. The Herderian insight does not license the torture, enslavement, or extermination of persons in the name of cultural purity.

This is a principled position but it requires exactly the kind of cross-cultural universal claim that value pluralism tends to undermine. It is, in the end, a liberal addition to pluralism rather than something that follows from it — which is why critics like Charles Taylor argue that Berlin's liberalism and his pluralism are in permanent, productive, unresolved tension.

## Essay Seed

*Can pluralism license limits on itself? That is: does the incommensurability of cultural values entail that some cultural practices are forbidden? And if so, on what grounds — which must themselves be universal — does the line get drawn? Berlin draws a line but cannot fully justify it within his own framework. This is the gap worth writing into.*
