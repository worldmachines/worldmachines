# DEC 05 — Day the Fourth

Day Four opens with something rare in the *Decameron*: Boccaccio stepping out of the frame to defend himself against critics who say he courts women too assiduously, wastes his maturity on frivolities, and neglects his Muses. His rebuttal takes the form of a half-finished tale — the hermit's son Filippo Balducci, raised in total ignorance of women, who instantly desires one the moment he sees them ("Father mine, I prithee get me one of these green geese"). The moral is pointed: nature is stronger than wit, and anyone who blames Boccaccio for writing about women understands neither nature nor themselves. Filostrato then assumes the governance of the day and prescribes the theme: loves that ended unhappily. Under this dark mandate the brigata produces ten tales, ranging from the grand (Tancred and Ghismonda, the eaten heart of Guillaume de Guardestaing) to the intimate (Lisabetta's pot of basil, Simona and the poisoned sage-bush), with a comic outlier at the end — Dioneo's story of Ruggieri in the chest — that breaks the gloom by design.

## Themes

**Tragic love as structural necessity.** Filostrato chooses unhappy endings not at random but because they mirror his own condition; he is suffering from unrequited love and wants communal acknowledgement of the damage love does. The day demonstrates that love is fatal when authority — paternal, fraternal, social — overrides the lovers' own legitimacy.

**The defence of desire and authorship.** The author's prologue is an extended argument that writing for women and desiring them is natural, literary (Dante and Cavalcanti did it), and consistent with serving the Muses. "The Muses are women," Boccaccio writes — and women have been "the occasion of composing a thousand verses." This is simultaneously a gendered compliment and a defence of vernacular, secular literature.

**Merit vs. birth.** Ghismonda's great speech to Tancred argues from first principles that worth (virtue and nobility of soul) was prior to and greater than heritable status. "Worth it was that first distinguished between us, who were all and still are born equal." Guiscardo is more noble than the courtiers simply because he *behaves* more nobly. This argument recurs structurally: nearly every tragic couple involves a cross-class pairing that society destroys.

**Fortune's caprice.** The Day repeatedly invokes Fortune as the agent that turns happiness into mourning (Tancred's accidental discovery of the lovers; the storm-becalmed ship that lets Gerbino attack; the toad under the sage). The characters are not always culpable; the world simply does not hold still.

**Eros and death intertwined.** The Day's most extreme tales literalise this: Ghismonda drinks poison from a cup containing her lover's heart; Guillaume de Roussillon's wife literally eats her lover's heart and then jumps to her death. The body of love and the body of death merge. Girolamo *wills* himself dead in Salvestra's bed. Simona accidentally dies proving her innocence by repeating the exact action that killed her lover.

## Notable stories

**Story 1 — Tancred and Ghismonda.** The defining tale of the Day. Prince Tancred of Salerno discovers his widowed daughter Ghismonda has taken a low-born lover, Guiscardo, through a secret tunnel. He has Guiscardo strangled and sends his heart in a golden bowl to Ghismonda. She pours the poison water she has already prepared over it, drinks it down, and dies on her bed holding the heart. The story's power lies in Ghismonda's set-piece speech vindicating herself: she refuses to apologise, argues from natural philosophy that nobility is earned not inherited, and denies her father the satisfaction of her weeping. Entirely self-possessed to the end, she grants Guiscardo a more honourable sepulchre than most nobles receive.

**Story 5 — Lisabetta and the pot of basil.** Three merchant brothers in Messina murder their sister's secret lover Lorenzo and bury him; he appears to her in a dream to show her the place. Lisabetta digs up the corpse, cuts off the head, and buries it under a basil plant she tends with her tears. The brothers eventually take the pot; she dies of grief. The folk-song quoted at the end ("Alack! ah, who can the ill Christian be / That stole my pot away") was already current when Boccaccio wrote — he is collecting living oral culture. The story was later immortalised by Keats as *Isabella, or the Pot of Basil*.

**Story 4 — Gerbino.** A love story conducted entirely by reputation and messenger: Gerbino and the Tunis princess fall in love without ever meeting. When the King of Tunis ships her to marry the King of Granada, Gerbino attacks the vessel against a pledge of safe-conduct his grandfather King Guglielmo gave. The Saracens murder the princess before Gerbino's eyes and throw her into the sea; he slaughters them; his grandfather — choosing honour over posterity — executes him. The pathos is double: two people destroyed without ever having touched.

**Story 9 — The eaten heart (Guillaume de Roussillon).** The Provençal troubadour legend, retold straight: Roussillon kills his friend and wife's lover Guardestaing, extracts his heart, has it served to her as a ragout. She eats it, learns what she has eaten, proclaims no other food could follow such "noble meat," and jumps to her death through the window. The tale is the Day's most operatic expression of love-as-consumption and Boccaccio signals its legendary status in a footnote linking Guardestaing to Guillem de Cabestanh.

**Story 10 — Ruggieri in the chest (Dioneo).** The deliberate comic break. A doctor's neglected wife keeps a disreputable lover, who accidentally drinks an opiate left out for a surgical patient, is mistaken for dead, stuffed into a chest by the terrified women, and the chest stolen by moneylenders during the night — whereupon Ruggieri wakes up, knocks the chest over, and is arrested for burglary. The maid's quick-witted confession to the prefect (sleeping with him first to smooth her argument) saves everyone. Dioneo explicitly frames it as relief from the day's sorrows.

## Quotables

"Love can far more than either you or I." — Guiscardo to Tancred, on being accused of dishonour (Story 1).

"Worth it was that first distinguished between us, who were all and still are born equal; wherefore those who had and used the greatest sum thereof were called noble and the rest abode not noble." — Ghismonda to Tancred (Story 1).

"God forfend that ever other victual should follow upon such noble meat as the heart of so valiant and so courteous a gentleman as was Sir Guillaume de Guardestaing!" — the lady to Roussillon before leaping to her death (Story 9).

"Father mine, I prithee get me one of these green geese." — Filippo Balducci's son, on encountering women for the first time (Author's prologue).

"The Muses are women, and albeit women may not avail to match with them, yet at first sight they have a semblance of them." — Boccaccio's self-defence (Author's prologue).

## WM resonances

**[[The Thicket]]** — The brigata itself is a curated refuge from the plague city, and within this Day the thicket concept is under deliberate pressure: every secret space the lovers construct (Ghismonda's cave tunnel, Lisabetta's pot, Andrevuola's garden, the hermit's mountain retreat) is eventually found and breached by authority. The Day dramatises what happens when a thicket fails — when the portal is discovered and the terroir offers no further opacity. Boccaccio's author's prologue also functions as a meta-thicket argument: he is defending the right to a protected space of writing in which natural desire is recognised rather than suppressed.

**[[capture-resistance]]** — Ghismonda's speech is a sustained instance of capture-resistance logic: the force that would make her legible and governable (paternal love transmuted into social control) is met with a refusal to be reduced to its terms. She does not petition; she argues from prior principles that the regime of birth-based legibility is itself a corruption of natural equality. The Day as a whole tests whether love, as a form of autonomous self-organization, can survive the extraction attempts of fathers, brothers, and husbands — and mostly finds that it cannot, but documents the refusal.

**[[Conatus]]** — The structural motor of the Day is Spinoza's conatus made lethal: each lover's drive to persist and expand (to love freely, to maintain the relationship) collides with an equally driven suppressive force, and the resulting annihilation is the Day's repeated cadence. Girolamo literally wills himself dead by straitening his vital spirits — conatus inverted.
