# DEC 04 — Day the Third

Day the Third is governed by Neifile, who sets the theme as wit and industry in the service of desire: every story must show a character acquiring "some much desired thing" or recovering "some lost good" by dint of diligence. The brigata has moved from Pampinea's first estate to a second, more splendid palace with an Eden-like walled garden — the opening description of the garden (fountain, espaliered vines, roses and jasmine, free-roaming animals) is among the most lush set-pieces in the whole work, and it frames the day's stories as taking place within a curated paradise. The ten tales told this day are the ribaldest in the first three days; the erotic content is not incidental but structurally necessary: desire is the engine that activates wit.

## Themes

**Wit as the master virtue.** The Day's design argument is that cleverness — not birth, piety, gender, or rank — is what actually governs outcomes. A peasant (Masetto), a horsekeeper, a merchant's widow (Gillette), a young noblewoman (the wool-monger's wife in III.3), and a naïve desert girl (Alibech) all succeed or are overcome by the same mechanism: information advantage, disguise, or controlled narrative. The theme is not immorality for its own sake but a philosophical claim that intellect outruns institutional constraint.

**Anticlericalism as structural critique.** More than half the tales involve clergy as gullible dupes or cynical exploiters: an abbess who beds her gardener, a friar weaponized as go-between (III.3), a pious dolt's wife enjoyed by a monk (IV), a Tuscan abbot who drugs a peasant and impersonates him for ten months (VIII), and Rustico the hermit (X). Tedaldo's long speech in III.7 (speaking as a fake pilgrim) is the Day's explicit ideological set-piece: a sustained indictment of contemporary friars as greedy hypocrites who terrorize women with hellfire imagery to control their behavior. Boccaccio is doing something more than anti-clerical comedy: he is arguing that the institutional church manufactures guilt in order to extract both sex and money.

**The body as honest, institutions as dishonest.** Across the Day, natural physical desire is consistently aligned with candor and institutional religion with deception. Masetto's stratagem works because the nuns' desire is real; the abbess's theology is the false thing. Alibech's guilelessness is the foil for Rustico's manipulative piety. Ferondo's "purgatory" is a drug-induced imprisonment; the abbot's saintly reputation is the real fiction.

**Fortune and patience.** A secondary current — especially in III.7 (Tedaldo) and III.9 (Gillette) — counterweights comic ingenuity with something more patient and costly: exile, years of wandering, the slow accumulation of proof. Gillette fulfills an apparently impossible condition (two sons by the husband who rejected her, his ring on her finger) through sustained, austere ingenuity across years.

**Gender and agency.** Women in this Day are unusually active: the wool-monger's wife engineers her own affair via three expertly staged confessions; Gillette heals a king, administers a county, and executes a bed-trick; Ferondo's wife negotiates with the abbot as a rational agent. Even the nuns in III.1 form a collective, quietly redistributing access to Masetto. The one case of female passivity — Catella in III.6 — is the Day's most troubling tale, and Boccaccio does not sanitize it: Ricciardo's reveal and emotional coercion of Catella into acquiescence is presented with obvious relish but also with enough detail about her distress that an analytic reader notes the violence.

## Notable stories

**III.1 — Masetto the mute gardener.** The paradigm case for the Day's theme. Masetto feigns deafness and dumbness to enter a convent as gardener; all eight nuns and the abbess eventually use him; when his strength fails he reveals he was never truly mute (claiming miraculous restoration), becomes bailiff, and departs old, wealthy, and full of unofficial children. The comedy is frank and unapologetic; the joke is entirely at the expense of the belief that monastic vows extinguish nature.

**III.3 — The wool-monger's wife and the dull friar.** A Florentine gentlewoman of high spirit who despises her craftsman husband constructs an elaborate three-stage manipulation of a credulous friar to communicate, receive gifts from, and finally bed her chosen lover — all while the friar believes he is protecting her virtue. The story showcases information warfare: the woman alone holds the full picture; every other character is working from a partial false model. The closing line is Filomena's prayer that God conduct her to the same situation.

**III.7 — Tedaldo in pilgrim's disguise.** The longest and most morally complex tale of the Day. Tedaldo Elisei returns after seven years' self-imposed exile (disguised as a pilgrim), talks his estranged mistress Ermellina through her guilt by demolishing the friar who originally persuaded her to drop him, saves her husband Aldobrandino from false execution for Tedaldo's murder (the actual corpse is a Lunigiana soldier who happened to resemble him), reconciles feuding families, and is reunited with his mistress. The embedded anti-clerical sermon is some three thousand words — a pamphlet within the fiction. Structurally this tale treats wit as a form of justice: disguise enables Tedaldo to operate as a truth-speaker outside the identity-constraints that would otherwise silence him.

**III.8 — Ferondo in "purgatory."** An abbot with a powder from "the Old Man of the Mountain" (hashish, via Marco Polo) drugs the jealous simpleton Ferondo, entombs him apparently dead, imprisons him in an underground vault for ten months (beaten twice daily by a monk playing an angel), and beds his wife the whole time. Ferondo emerges cured of jealousy, believing he has risen from the dead; the abbot's sanctity is amplified. The tale is a high-concept farce about the manufacture of religious experience from secular chemistry and theatrical staging.

**III.9 — Gillette de Narbonne.** The Day's most structurally ambitious story. Gillette cures the King of France of a fistula using her physician father's knowledge, demands Bertrand de Roussillon as her reward, is given him against his will, administers his county competently in his absence, and eventually fulfills his impossibly vindictive conditions (his ring, two sons by him) via a bed-trick arranged with a Florentine gentlewoman whose daughter Bertrand is pursuing. The tale sets patience and sovereign competence beside erotic cunning: Gillette is perhaps the Day's most fully realized agent.

**III.10 — Alibech and Rustico ("the devil in hell").** Dioneo's tale, which he holds as his privilege to tell last. Alibech, a fourteen-year-old Barbary girl seeking Christian piety, is taught by the hermit Rustico that the holiest service is "putting the devil in hell" — the extended sexual metaphor is explained partly in untranslated Italian in the 1886 Payne edition. Alibech becomes more ardent than Rustico can sustain; she is eventually retrieved, married, and returns to normal life still describing the practice in literal theological terms to the laughter of the city. The tale is bawdy but also — like III.1 — a philosophical fable: the "most acceptable service to God" joke circulates precisely because it exposes the pretensions of ascetic theology.

## Quotables

"I was indeed dumb, not by nature, but by reason of a malady which bereft me of speech, and only this very night for the first time do I feel it restored to me, wherefore I praise God as most I may." — Masetto, dropping his deception (III.1)

"Friars of old were very pious and worthy men, but those who nowadays style themselves friars and would be held such have nothing of the monk but the gown." — Tedaldo's anti-clerical sermon in pilgrim's guise (III.7)

"So that in whichever one of these three things aforesaid you have committed a far greater sin than in your converse with him." — Tedaldo (as pilgrim) arguing that withdrawing love is a greater sin than fornication (III.7)

"Madam, I am your Ricciardo." — The reveal in III.6, after Catella has spent several paragraphs congratulating herself on punishing her husband.

"Be it whensoever it pleaseth thee" — Alibech consenting, in good faith, to Rustico's theological proposition (III.10)

"We shall presently see if the wolf will know how to govern the ewes better than the ewes have governed the wolves." — Neifile crowning Filostrato at the Day's close, setting up Day Four's theme of ill-fated loves.

## WM resonances

[[The Thicket]] — The walled garden described at the Day's opening is the clearest architectural instance so far of a curated interior space defended from the world outside: "all walled about," full of roses and jasmine "on the sides," inhabited by tame animals, centered on a fountain whose water eventually exits to turn mills. The brigata quite literally inhabits a designed enclosure that separates an interior of pleasure and free discourse from the institutional world whose corruptions fill the day's stories. The curated refuge enables the storytelling frame.

[[capture-resistance]] — The Day's ten tales are uniformly about escaping capture by institutional systems — religious vows, jealous husbands, social hierarchy, false accusations of murder, arranged marriages. The ingenuity celebrated is specifically the capacity to move through a legibility system (convent records, confession, legal proceedings, royal decrees) while maintaining an opacity the system cannot penetrate. Masetto is never identified; Tedaldo's disguise works because his family and the courts cannot read through it; Gillette's bed-trick exploits the darkness of an unlighted room. The Day proposes that wit is capture-resistance made operational.

[[legibility-as-machine-core]] — Tedaldo's anti-clerical sermon in III.7 explicitly describes how the church manufactures legibility over bodies and souls (confession as an extraction mechanism, hellfire as a disciplinary threat) and argues that this apparatus serves institutional self-interest rather than genuine spiritual governance. The observation that friars "decry lust in men, in order that… the latter may be left to the chiders" is a precise description of how a legibility apparatus captures what it formally prohibits.
