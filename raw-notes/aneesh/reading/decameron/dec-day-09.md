# DEC 10 — Day the Ninth

Day Nine is the single day in the Decameron with no prescribed topic: Queen Emilia grants the company full liberty to narrate whatever most pleases them. The result is a deliberate miscellany — yet it coheres around recurring preoccupations: female resourcefulness in the face of male appetite or institutional authority, the credulity of fools exploited by clever companions, and a handful of darker tales (a prophetic dream fulfilled; a wife beaten into compliance) that complicate the Day's overall comic tone. The frame opens in a coppice where the brigata encounters deer and wild goats standing fearless — animals grown bold because the plague has emptied the hunting world — a rare, fleeting re-entry of the pestilence into what is otherwise a pastoral bubble.

## Themes

**Female wit and institutional cover-up.** Three stories (I, II, VI) hinge on women who use quick-wittedness to neutralise a scandal that threatened to trap them — or who manipulate an entire inn full of sleepers with midnight cradle-moving. In each case the woman's intelligence is both admired and quietly subversive of official order. Story II is the sharpest: the abbess, caught wearing her priest-lover's breeches on her head, cannot sustain her moral authority and capitulates to pragmatic tolerance.

**Calandrino as epistemological cautionary figure.** Stories III and V return to the painters' favourite dupe. In III, a false pregnancy is sold to Calandrino with the connivance of the comically named "Doctor Simple-Simon"; he pays in capons and coin for a cure to a condition he never had. In V, a magic talisman produces a brief amorous encounter before Calandrino's own wife arrives and administers physical correction. The two episodes together chart the full arc of his credulity: first he is deceived about his body; then about desire itself.

**Knavery, tit-for-tat, and proportionality.** Story IV (Cecco Fortarrigo and Angiolieri) and Story VIII (Ciacco and Biondello) both explore revenge as calibrated social mechanism. Fortarrigo's brazen inversion — stripping his victim while crying "thief!" to the peasants — is almost surrealist in its audacity. Ciacco's revenge is more elegant: he engineers a proxy beating for Biondello through Messer Filippo's explosive temper, matching injury to insult with exact measure.

**Prophetic knowledge and wilful disbelief.** Story VII (Talano and Margarita's wolf dream) is the Day's one genuinely ominous tale. The wife dismisses her husband's warning as a trick to keep her from the wood where he presumably keeps a mistress. Her cynical, correct-seeming suspicion turns out to be catastrophically wrong; the wolf is real and mauls her exactly as dreamed. The story does not gloat but it leaves a mark.

**Obedience and the Solomonic question.** Story IX is the Day's most ethically loaded piece, narrated by the queen herself with a preamble endorsing female submission that sits ill with much of what the Day has otherwise shown. Solomon's two-word answer ("Love" / "Goosebridge") is terse wisdom, but the second answer is demonstrated at a wife's expense. Boccaccio is not straightforwardly endorsing domestic violence; the story is framed as counsel — but framed approvingly enough to be genuinely uncomfortable.

**Bawdry and the limits of enchantment.** Dioneo's closing tale (X) — the mare-conjuration that fails because Pietro cannot stay silent — is the Day's most openly obscene piece. Dom Gianni's "conjuration" is a barely veiled sexual act; Pietro's jealous interruption ("I won't have a tail there!") wrecks the pretence. The laughs are at Pietro's expense, and his wife's exasperated final line ("Dolt that thou art") echoes Dame Tessa's furious scolding of Calandrino — women the sharper judges of practical reality.

## Notable stories

**Story I — Madam Francesca and the tomb.** A Pistoia widow, importuned by two Florentine exiles, devises an impossible test — one must lie in a dead man's tomb, the other must carry him out — as a way to rid herself of both. Both men attempt it; an accidental police patrol disperses them before the task completes. The widow watches from her window, laughs, and discharges them both for technically failing. A cleanly engineered comic escape; the dead man (the villainous "Scannadio," or "Murder-God") adds appropriately gothic atmosphere.

**Story II — The abbess and the priest's breeches.** The classic story of institutional hypocrisy undone by a single detail: the abbess rushing to condemn a nun accidentally dons her priest-lover's breeches instead of her veil. When the accused nun quietly points this out ("Madam, tie up your coif"), the abbess is defeated without a word of accusation. The Day's funniest and most economical story. The abbess concludes that "it is impossible to withstand the pricks of the flesh" and changes her tone entirely.

**Story III — Calandrino's false pregnancy.** The triple conspiracy of Bruno, Buffalmacco, Nello, and the compliant physician convinces Calandrino he is with child — "Woe's me! Tessa, this is thy doing, for that thou wilt still be uppermost." He hands over capons and five silver crowns for a cure. The cure is a glass of spiced clary wine. Dame Tessa "smoking the cheat" is the story's telling coda: she sees clearly what the men around her pretend not to.

**Story VI — The inn at Mugnone.** A young man (Pinuccio) engineers a night with an innkeeper's daughter by staging an accidental lodging; a displaced cradle in the dark causes a chain of wrong-bed encounters. The innkeeper's wife ends up with the visitor; Pinuccio ends up confessing to the wrong man. The wife recovers the situation with a sustained lie so well-constructed that her husband scolds Pinuccio for dreaming. The story's admiration is entirely for female pragmatic intelligence.

**Story X — Dom Gianni's mare-conjuration.** Dioneo's finale: a priest claims he can turn his mare into a woman at night; the curious gossip Pietro asks for the same for his wife; the "conjuration" (a complete undressing and tactile transformation-spell) is interrupted at the last moment; the wife scolds her husband for wrecking the deal. The story closes the Day on maximum obscenity and maximum laughs, with Boccaccio noting through Dioneo that the women in the audience "understood it better than he willed."

## Quotables

"It is impossible to withstand the pricks of the flesh; wherefore she said that each should, whenas she might, privily give herself a good time, even as it had been done until that day." — Story II (the abbess's revised doctrine)

"Woe's me! Tessa, this is thy doing, for that thou wilt still be uppermost; I told thee how it would be." — Story III (Calandrino on discovering his pregnancy)

"She who had been with him was the wife of the master of the house" — Story V (Calandrino's desperate excuse to his wife, after she has clawed his face raw)

"Dolt that thou art, why hast thou marred thine affairs and mine? What mare sawest thou ever without a tail?" — Story X (Gemmata to Pietro)

"A black crow amongst a multitude of white doves addeth more beauty than would a snow-white swan" — Dioneo's preface to Story X, his habitual apologia for licence

## WM resonances

**[[The Thicket]]** — The brigata's coppice-walk at the Day's opening is one of the text's most explicit thicket images: a small interior space where wild animals have become tame because the hunting world outside has been emptied by pestilence. The enclosure is literally portal-bordered (they enter and exit), defended by the de facto opacity that plague has granted it, and the animals within operate by different rules than outside. This is not metaphor — it is the structural logic of the brigata itself, spelled out spatially.

**[[legibility-as-machine-core]]** — Story IX (Solomon/Goosebridge) is an exercise in the gap between opaque counsel ("Get thee to Goosebridge") and its legible enactment. Solomon's wisdom is deliberately illegible until the travellers witness the muleteer; the moment of legibility (recognising the place-name) produces understanding. Boccaccio is half-serious about this as an epistemology: meaningful knowledge resists transparent transmission and only becomes actionable through concrete encounter.

**[[capture-resistance]]** — The women across Day Nine (Francesca, Isabetta, the inn-wife, Dame Tessa) consistently evade capture — by suitors, by institutional authority, by scandal — through improvised opacity rather than frontal resistance. The abbess's story is the starkest: the accusatory apparatus of the convent chapter is neutralised not by argument but by a single detail that makes the accuser illegible to herself. This is capture-resistance operating at the level of the situation's framing.
