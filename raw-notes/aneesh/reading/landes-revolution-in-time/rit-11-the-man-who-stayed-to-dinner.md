# RIT 11 — The Man Who Stayed to Dinner (ch. 9)

This is Landes's Harrison chapter, and its distinctive move is to honor the legend while dismantling its melodrama. John Harrison — carpenter's son from Barrow-on-Humber, self-taught, building precision clocks out of oak and lignum vitae — did beat the Newton-backed astronomical establishment to the £20,000 longitude prize (12 Anne c. 15, 1713–14). But Landes refuses the standard David-and-Goliath reading on three counts: the Board of Longitude's stickiness had real legal and epistemic grounds (a prize-worthy "solution" must be reproducible and accessible, not a one-off secret masterpiece); Maskelyne's championing of lunar distances was intellectual conviction that only later curdled into mutual animus; and, the chapter's sting, Harrison's machines are *not* the ancestors of the modern marine chronometer — of all his inventions only the maintaining power survived. His contribution was demonstrative, not genetic: he proved the job could be done.

## Key arguments

- **The establishment bet on astronomy, the craftsmen bet on clocks.** Newton (1721) held that longitude "is not to be found at sea by any method by which it cannot be found at land," and that improvement must come from "the ablest Astronomers," not watchmakers. The clockmakers disagreed so strongly that the best of them abandoned lucrative civilian horology for the quest — and a rural autodidact won.
- **Lunars were workable in principle, crippled in practice.** The method's four requirements (instrument, deck watch, predicted tables, "clearing the distance") were each eventually met, but the procedure demanded four simultaneous observers and ~four hours of calculation by someone as practiced as Maskelyne himself. Harrison was right on the merits; Landes insists he was wrong about Maskelyne's motive.
- **The Board's strictness was partly wisdom, not just spite.** The original Act was vague because nobody expected a horological answer. Confronted with H.4, the board "wisely insisted on the submission not of a method that worked once but of one that would work again and again and be accessible to all" — hence 5 Geo. III c. 20 (1765): explain the mechanism, surrender the machines, and withhold half the prize until others could build "timekeepers of the same kind."
- **Simplicity with high performance beats virtuoso complexity.** Harrison's grasshopper escapement, gridiron pendulum, and roller pinions were all superseded by simpler solutions; Graham's plain dead-beat regulator with mercury pendulum became the observatory standard even though one Harrison long-case clock didn't vary a minute in ten years. The same selection pressure shaped the chronometer lineage.
- **Openness as national advantage.** Halley sent the nervous provincial to George Graham ("Speak to the point"); Graham, once convinced, talked with him from ten in the morning into the evening and lent funds without security or interest. Landes: the clockmakers of Paris would have been less generous — and the French, first to publish marine-ready lunar tables (Connaissance des temps, 1761), "never carried through" on either lunars or the chronometer.

## Evidence & specifics

- **Crackpot baseline:** the 1687 "powder of sympathy" proposal (after Sir Kenelm Digby) — a wounded dog aboard ship, its bandages dipped in powder back in London every hour, yelping the time across the ocean.
- **The wooden clocks:** with brother James, Harrison built two precision clocks of oak plates and wheels, boxwood arbors, self-lubricating lignum vitae bushings, anti-friction rollers, grasshopper escapement, brass-steel gridiron pendulum; he claimed a second a month — if true, unmatched until Riefler's regulator of 1889. He checked them by stellar transits without observatory equipment, having copied Saunderson's Cambridge lectures *in extenso*.
- **The series:** H.1 (six years; 3 ft cube, 71 lb; Lisbon trial corrected the landfall reckoning by ~90 miles), H.2 (four years), H.3 (seventeen years), H.4 (completed 1759: 5.2 inches, a coach watch — near-dead-beat verge, remontoir, diamond pallets, bimetallic curb; Gould: "the most famous timekeeper which ever has been or ever will be made").
- **Trials:** Jamaica (Nov 1761–Mar 1762, Harrison aged 68) clouded by the after-the-fact rate; Barbados (1764): mean error 39 seconds over five months = 9.75′ of longitude = 11.2 miles at the equator, a tenth of a second a day, against a 30′ statutory limit.
- **Lunars:** moon moves ~33″ of arc per minute of time; Hadley's reflecting quadrant (1731) replaced the 5–6′ back-staff; Newton's 1713 lunar tables cut moon-position error from 10–15′ to 5–6′ (still 2.5–3° of longitude); Flamsteed, Halley, Lemonnier, Cassini observations plus Clairaut and Euler analysis fed Tobias Mayer's tables (sent to the Board 1755; Brittany trials 1757–9, within 37′); Maskelyne got within 1.5° at St Helena (1761), published The British Mariner's Guide (1763) and the first Nautical Almanac (for 1767). Newton on the moon: "the only problem that ever made my head ache."
- **The bitter end:** Harrison built H.5 at seventy-seven; King George III ("By God, Harrison, I'll see you righted!") forced the payout through Parliament, yet the board still "bilked" (Gould's word) £1,250 from the sum. Died 1776; the Gentleman's Magazine gave the "most extraordinary autodidact in the history of arts and crafts" a few lines, against a column and a half for Graham in 1751.

## Quotables

- "This improvement must be made at land, not by Watchmakers or teachers of Navigation or people that know not how to find the Longitude at land, but by the ablest Astronomers." (Newton, 1721; ~p. 156)
- "There is neither any other Mechanical or Mathematical thing in the World that is more beautiful or curious in texture than this my watch or Timekeeper for the Longitude." (Harrison on H.4; ~p. 160)
- "The board chose to construe the Act strictly and wisely insisted on the submission not of a method that worked once but of one that would work again and again and be accessible to all." (~p. 164–5)
- "His contribution, then, lay not in his devices but in the simple fact of their effectiveness: he demonstrated that the job could be done." (~p. 166)

## WM resonances

- The Board's admission criterion — not a secret marvel that worked twice, but a method explained, surrendered, and replicable by other hands before the machine pays out — is the legibility regime's gate in miniature: singular genius only counts once it is rendered transferable and standardizable ([[legibility-as-machine-core]]).
- The aftermath is a clean episode of standardization pruning variety: Harrison's idiosyncratic lineage (wood, grasshopper, gridiron) goes extinct but for one trait (maintaining power), while "simplicity with high performance" — Graham's dead-beat, the simplified chronometer — becomes the reproducible main line. The suppressed alternatives mark what the pull toward a single metric discards ([[divergence-machine]]).
