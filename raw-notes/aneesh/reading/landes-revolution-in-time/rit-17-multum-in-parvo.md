# RIT 17 — Multum in Parvo (Ch. 15)

"Much in little": Landes's chapter on Geneva, a hundred-square-mile republic of 12,500 people (mid-1500s; ~25,000 two centuries later, versus London's 700,000), that became "person for person... probably the most productive and creative city in Europe." The engine was religious refuge: Huguenot watchmakers fleeing Catholic persecution brought France's elite horological skills to a city whose Calvinist regime had killed its jewellery trade but exempted watches — "if Calvinists were not interested in time and its measurement, who was?" The chapter's deeper argument is institutional: Geneva's greatness came not from technical genius (that was London and Paris, book chs. 9–11; notes rit-11–rit-13) but from organizational innovation — the *établissage* putting-out system, a class-and-geography division of labor that pushed low-wage work outward to immigrants, women, and Savoyard peasants while citizens kept the lucrative finishing — plus merchant intermediaries and bank credit. And his "law of nemesis": every monopoly rent invites imitators, and the system Geneva used on its hinterland was eventually used against it by the Neuchâtel Jura.

## Key arguments

- **Guild exclusion + booming demand = structural evasion.** From 1601 the guild reserved the trade to *citoyens* and *bourgeois*, excluding *habitants*, *natifs* (even Geneva-born), and all women. Masters bypassed their own rules by splitting production into specialties: an *établisseur* gave out materials to subcontractors, advanced wages, collected components, supervised finishing, and marketed the result — expanding capacity "without diluting the highly profitable privilege of mastership."
- **Regulation failed at the border.** The guild's reiterated interdictions are themselves evidence of "the limits of control in an area of highly fragmented political sovereignty" — the Arve River, five tram stops from downtown, was the frontier; Carouge was foreign soil. Renegade lists were paralyzed because successful masters needed the bootleg country movements to fill orders.
- **Unequal exchange, anticipated.** A late-18th-century French document reads like modern dependency theory: Geneva "paid six days of an ébaucheur with one day of a finisher," which is why 25,000 Genevans had more money than 450,000 Savoyards and 800,000 in neighboring departments.
- **Middlemen earned their keep.** Against the perennial prejudice (peasant, aristocrat, socialist alike) that traders are parasites, Landes insists the merchant — who knew markets, bore road-and-sea risk, commanded bank credit — was as constitutive of the fabrique as the craftsman-artist.
- **Nemesis.** Statist transplants failed (Waterford's "New Geneva"; Constance 1785–87, dead by 1800; Revolutionary Besançon 1793–94, dependent on Geneva parts). What worked was the Jura's organic rural fabrique: lower wages, longer hours, more women and children, cheaper watches — driving Geneva up-market into volatile luxury demand just as the 1790s wars hit.

## Evidence & specifics

- Origins: Charles Cusin (arrived 1574) traditionally credited as founder, but Antony Babel found sixteen earlier watchmakers; guild incorporation mooted by the 1580s. Specialties: form watches (skulls, animals), prohibited-but-profitable crucifix watches, lunar-movement watches for the Ottoman market ("business is business," twice).
- Turkish trade: 1632 compact, Pierre Cuper of Blois with Anthoine Arlaud of Geneva, mixed lots shipped via Marseilles to Galata; Abraham Arlaud later contracts for "as many lunar-movement watches as he can make in the space of one year." Pastor Leger, 1652: Genevan watchmakers in Constantinople living "in complete licence, without religion or any restraint." Isaac Rousseau, Jean-Jacques's father, was *horloger du sérail*.
- Refugee politics: post-Revocation flood; Philippe Savyon's six-count xenophobic bill of charges; the 1696–97 committee heard the witnesses, then kept hands off and reprimanded Savyon — unlike Zurich, Geneva never expelled foreign craftsmen.
- Etablissage data: a 1654 contract for twenty-one rough movements (*montres blanches*) when output averaged one watch per man-month; mercury-gilding women "paid for their jobs with their health and life."
- Scale: 1685 — ~100 masters, 300 workers, ~5,000 watches/year; 1760 — 800 masters, ~4,000 city workers plus more outside; peak (Sismondi) ~85,000 watches/year 1781–86; collapse to ~60,000 by 1801–02, workforce from ~5,000 to under 2,000 by 1803.
- Commerce: trade ran "by leaps and fairs" (Leipzig, Beaucaire); a 1780s suicide study tied the boom-slump rhythm to watchmaker depression; Dufalga's 1767 inventory of 120,000 florins ≈ forty years of a master's salary; Achard father and son's 1806 counterfeits of Higgs & Evans sold at 115–130 francs against the genuine 200; truck payments left "poverty covered with silk and velvet" (Cornuaud); J. B. Vacheron's 1818 Italian journey past brigands' limbs nailed to posts — Constantin took over the traveling in 1819.

## WM resonances

- [[legibility-as-machine-core]] — the guild's regulations are a legibility apparatus that fails at the territorial edge: the fabrique grew precisely in the space (Carouge, Savoy, the Jura) that Geneva's authorities could neither read nor reach, and the regulators' "reiterated interdictions" are Landes's own evidence of the limits of control.
- [[divergence-machine]] — the fabrique behaves like a push machine: refugee inflows, opportunistic proliferation of forms for every market (lunar watches for Ottomans, skull watches, counterfeit "Higgs y Evans"), no shared attractor. The pull-style state transplants (Waterford, Constance, Besançon) all died; the unplanned Jura push won.
- [[The Thicket]] — the transplant failures are a terroir argument: "attempts to transplant a shoot of this flourishing tree to other climes quickly failed" because Geneva's conditions (refugee networks, credit, skills ecology) were non-portable.
- [[clock-as-incomplete-discipline]] / [[untimely]] — the makers of time-discipline's instrument were themselves undisciplined by it: work ran "by leaps and fairs," the moody *cabinotier* was "independent in his dependence," and the 1794 satirist mocks Genevans' ten-o'clock breaks against the Jura's twelve-hour days.
