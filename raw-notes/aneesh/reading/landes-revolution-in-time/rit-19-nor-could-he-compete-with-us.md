# RIT 19 — "Nor Could He Compete with Us" (ch. 17, pp. 293–309)

The chapter title is Edward Dent's famous last words: the smug 1842 assertion that no foreign maker could ever rival the British quality watch. Landes uses it as the epitaph for a national industry that destroyed itself at the precise historical moment when demand exploded. While Britain was becoming "workshop of the world," its watch trade — taxed by Pitt, undercut by Swiss costs, debased by its own cost-cutting, and protected from innovation by its own guild — shrank from roughly 200,000 to 169,000 pieces a year even as world output rose nearly tenfold (c. 350,000–400,000 at the end of the eighteenth century to 2.5 million three-quarters of a century later). The deeper irony: the unification of time itself — railway timetables, telegraphic time signals, Greenwich standard — multiplied the value of the watch and created the mass market the British were structurally unable to serve. The Swiss took two-thirds of world output by value (c. 1870); "the watch trade gave warning of things to come."

## Key arguments

- **Policy and war whipsawed demand.** Pitt's 1797 use tax on clocks and watches drove timepieces "into hiding"; repealed within a year, but the trade had already shed stock and orders. War with France then protected Britain from Swiss imports and revived the industry — making the post-1815 peace, with demobilization and renewed competition, that much more brutal.
- **The micro/macro trap.** Facing Swiss low-cost competition, individual British makers cut wages and quality — rational per firm, fatal for the branch, because the whole British position rested on the *claim* to superior quality. Landes explicitly invokes Thomas Schelling's micro-motives/macro-results contradiction. The era of "two-year watches," sham second hands, and cases with only the (assayed) back cover in true eighteen-carat gold.
- **Protection couldn't work.** Watches were the perfect contraband — small, dense in value, trivially hidden. Organized smuggling insurance ran ~10% of value against a nominal duty of ~25%+; the 1817 Parliamentary witnesses doubted any Swiss watch in Britain had legally passed customs. The watch trade demanded higher barriers at the exact moment Britain's general policy moved toward free trade.
- **Scapegoating instead of diagnosis.** The 1817 committee asked leading anti-Semitic questions about "inferior watches manufactured by the Jews"; most witnesses conceded Jews and non-Jews alike sold cheap work. Landes mines this for con-game anecdotes (the mock auction, the "personated Jew" sting on sailors) and notes the stereotype itself became a tool for fraud.
- **The Ingold failure as hinge.** Pierre Frédéric Ingold's 1840s scheme for machine-made watches with uniform, interchangeable parts — beyond Japy's "similar, not uniform" ébauches — was exactly what Britain needed. A prospectus promised 30% cost cuts and 200–300 movements a day (vs. a few thousand per year for the largest merchant-manufacturers); practical watchmakers vouched for the prototypes; the British Watch and Clockmakers' Company formed with £250,000 nominal capital. The Worshipful Company of Clockmakers killed the parliamentary charter. Ingold left for America; Landes finds *no* evidentiary link from him to Waltham, against Swiss chronicle tradition.
- **Time unification made the watch necessary.** Railways could not run on a "multitude of local times"; the telegraph (first tried 1837, London & North Western) let one exact minute be broadcast network-wide; the Railway Clearing House recommended Greenwich time on 22 September 1847; Airy's "galvanism" synchronized national clocks; US time zones (1883) and the International Meridian Conference (October 1884) globalized the grid — with France holding out until 1911, then legislating "Paris Mean Time, retarded by 9 minutes 21 seconds" rather than say "Greenwich."
- **Standardization bred resistance.** Scots, Irish, and West-of-England conservatives denounced "railway-time aggression" as "usurping the power of the Almighty"; daylight saving (a WWI fuel measure) ran into milking cows, Orthodox Sabbath logistics in Israel, and Japanese workers who experienced summer time as an extra bind, repealing it in 1952 the moment the American occupation ended.

## Evidence & specifics

- Goldsmiths' Hall assay figures: gold watch cases peak 1809, fall 27% in 1811; silver down nearly a third from the 1808 peak by 1813. Witnesses to the 1797 tax inquiry: Gregson (Bruton Street), Grant, Greenwell.
- Coventry as the logical extreme: 102 subdivided specialties, no journeymen, seven-year "apprentices" discarded for newcomers; John Nightingale, "a very good workman at the watch trade," reduced to emptying privies and physically unable to finish the job.
- Jura unemployment after the blockade: authorities tried converting watchmakers to instrument makers and failed — "the local industry had been built on private initiative and did not take kindly to development from above."
- Counter-evidence to Dent's complacency: Jules Jürgensen at Le Locle making lever watches "as good as any English maker could make" (without a fusee), plus Courvoisier, Louis Audemars, Charles Jacot producing high-grade work in quantity.
- Growth map: Besançon ~395,000 pieces by 1874 (~12% of world output); Neuchâtel ~280,000 (1844) nearly tripling in a decade; Bern, Solothurn, Fribourg, Vallée de Joux outgrowing the old core; Geneva static at 100,000; Lange's artisanal Glashütte lever in the hundreds of units; US factory production only a few thousand in its first decade.

## Quotables

- "Such a watch cannot be supplied by the foreign manufacturer under any circumstances; nor, if he were to make the experiment, could he … compete with us." — Dent, 1842 pamphlet (p. 299; source of the chapter title)
- "In a timekeeping society, we are all linked by habit and mind-set to the standard of measurement (which is one reason why political authority has always sought to put its stamp thereon), and there are always some who resist chronometric changes as though they were violations of identity and self-esteem." (p. 304)
- Pitt on his own tax: "evasion, fraud and meanness which have struggled to defeat the operation of the assessed taxes." (p. 294)
- "The economic pundits should have been paying attention. The watch trade gave warning of things to come." (p. 309, chapter close)

- [[legibility-as-machine-core]] — the railway/telegraph/Greenwich sequence is the Modernity Machine compressing local times into one national, then planetary, grid: a single broadcast minute enabling "ordering of movement and multiplication of activity." Landes notes political authority *always* stamps the standard of measurement.
- [[clock-as-incomplete-discipline]] / [[untimely]] — standardization secretes its own remainder: "railway-time aggression," defenders of "the hour of their locality, or even of a given clock," French clocks set an hour off Greenwich, counties refusing daylight saving, Japanese workers repealing summer time. Resistance is experienced as defense of identity, not mere inconvenience — disciplinary time never fully governs its subjects.
