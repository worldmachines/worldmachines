# RIT 21 — Not One in Fifty Thousand (Ch. 19)

The chapter takes its title from the verdict of a Le Locle master adjuster who dismantled a fifth-grade Waltham watch that Edouard Favre-Perret had pulled blind from a factory safe in 1876: "one would not find one such watch among fifty thousand of our manufacture." Landes's argument is that the American watch industry won not by craft but by a different production ontology — the "American system of manufacture": machine-made, standardized, interchangeable parts. The governing variable is *tolerance*: stamping (coinage) and casting (Chou bronzes) had produced *like* objects since antiquity, but articulated moving parts demand worked metal shaped by machines that are "smart," clean, and hard — and the rigor required scales inversely with the permitted margin. Europe invented every piece of this (Polhem, Japy, Blanc, Maudslay) but never generalized it; the United States, lacking Europe's pool of cheap skilled cottage labor, made it "a general principle of fabrication" — first in wooden clocks, where tolerances were loose, then, after agonizing failures, in watches, where parts had to be machined to 1/5,000 of an inch.

## Key arguments

- **Tolerance is the key concept.** Traditional techniques sufficed wherever play was acceptable (musket balls, window panes). Identity in moving parts required machining: no hand-guided tool can repeat the same pass indefinitely; only machines with templates, fixed stops, and hardened cutters can.
- **Europe had precursors, not a principle.** Polhem's cog-wheel cutter (a "technological isolate"), Japy's machine-made ébauches, Blanc's gunlocks (the likely inspiration, via Jefferson, for Whitney's 1798 musket contract), Maudslay's mechanized Bramah lock (1790–91) — each "remained the exception, without impact on its own, let alone other branches of production."
- **Culture over cost calculus.** Interchangeability became "a familiar, comfortable, even proper response" for American technicians; Landes argues "the cultural matrix is at least as important as cost calculations." Even Whitney is demoted by recent research — he produced *similar*, not matching, parts; true interchangeability came with John H. Hall and Simeon North, and required go/no-go gauges, because "without testing, there could be no substantial identity of shape and size."
- **The deep innovation was engineering out human judgment.** Waltham's second-generation machines were heavy single-purpose presses and lathes with fist-size levers, templates, and fixed stops "to minimize the need for human judgement"; the watch's 150+ irregular parts made arsenal-style gauge assortments impossible, so the universal lever-magnifying micrometer replaced them; and where Europe hand-fitted balance to hairspring, Americans mass-produced both, sorted them into bins by weight and force, and *matched* them — "just choose a target and let statistical distribution take care of the rest."
- **Scale jumped by orders of magnitude — the third tenfold jump in a century.** Landes's four-stage ladder: craft shops (serials in the thousands per lifetime) → établissage (tens of thousands per lifetime) → mechanized putting-out (tens of thousands per year, e.g. Jules Huguenin of La Chaux-de-Fonds) → Waltham (serial number one million by 1877, ten million by 1901).
- **The British failure pattern repeats.** As with Swiss watches, British clockmakers dismissed Jerome's "Yankee clocks," retreated into superb bracket clocks and regulators, and became antiques; Germany's Junghans (founded 1864 as a *Fabrik amerikanischer Uhren*) chose the other path and became one of the world's largest makers.

## Evidence & specifics

- Eli Terry (1772–1852): c. 1802–3 contracted for a thousand-plus wooden clocks from a water-powered shop in Plymouth, Connecticut; the 1807–8 four-thousand-clock venture took one year to build machinery, one for the first thousand, one for the rest. Trained Seth Thomas and Silas Hoadley — "teacher to the trade," as Maudslay was for British machine tools.
- Chauncey Jerome (1793–1868): the 1837 cheap brass clock; first run of forty thousand movements at $1.40 each; shipped to England invoiced at $1.50 to retail near $20; British customs twice seized cargoes as undervalued, paying cash plus 10% — "very satisfactory to us" — then let the Yankees be.
- Waltham's gestation: Dennison, Edward Howard, and financier Samuel Curtis founded the firm in 1850; three years to the first watches, only ~1,000 in the four years after. Machine-tool makers could not meet the tolerances, so the watch firms invented their own. By c. 1880 one man with a twenty-ton punch blanked ten thousand wheels a day; micrometers were issued at 1/250, 1/1,250, and 1/17,000 inch.
- The Civil War as accelerant: "large compartmentalized structures such as armies depend on a knowledge of time to organize and synchronize action." Waltham's 14,000th watch in 1858, its 118,000th by 1864; the *Ellery* "soldier's watch" was 44.7% of unit sales in 1865; the last war year returned ~58% on sales, 164% on capital.
- The industry: Howard (1857), Elgin (1864), Illinois (1869), Hampden (1877), Waterbury (1879), Hamilton (1892), plus ~sixty entrants — ~120 million jewelled watches in 75 years, at least twice as many dollar watches; Elgin ~50M and Waltham ~40M (an effective duopoly); Ingersoll a million watches in 1898 and fifty million in its first quarter-century ("The watch that made the dollar famous"); Ingraham ~80M after 1911.
- The Swiss reckoning: exports to the US fell from 18.3M francs (1872) to 4.8M (1876) — far beyond the post-1873 depression. Favre-Perret's report from the Philadelphia Centennial, delivered at La Chaux-de-Fonds, is "the most famous speech in horological history": Swiss makers had dumped "the worst trash" on Civil War soldiers; a new Elgin lever movement cost 19 francs against several times that for the Swiss equivalent; his random 75-franc Waltham varied 30 seconds over six days from Paris to Le Locle.
- Stray but vivid (from the plate captions bundled into this section): casemaker Georges-Louis Favre of La Chaux-de-Fonds, still working alone in the late 1970s against Third-World factory wages, "a die-hard, for whom work and enterprise are also a way of life" — his son calls visiting the shop going "chez Edmond Dantès."

## WM resonances

- [[legibility-as-machine-core]] — this chapter is the Modernity Machine's standardization drive operating at the scale of the artifact itself: go/no-go gauges making "substantial identity" testable, the micrometer converting microscopic difference into a readable scale, machines built "to minimize the need for human judgement," and statistical sorting that regulates a watch "without having seen it" — tacit craft knowledge replaced by measurement plus distribution. A wry coda: the one thing that defied mechanization was the porcelain dial, and its machine-stamped metal successor was "very handsome, very fashionable, very durable, but less legible" — production legibility purchased at the cost of display legibility.
- [[clock-as-incomplete-discipline]] — two genuine touches. Dennison's portrait of English outwork — pieceworkers who, "if sober enough to be at their places," drift to the pub and unfit themselves for the day — and Mercer's tale of the craftsman who tied string round his vice, "master of his time, not amenable to discipline": the ungoverned time of dispersed manufacture is exactly what the factory system routed around rather than reformed. And the Civil War demand spike makes the watch the soldier's interface to synchronized institutional time — armies as the paradigm "compartmentalized structures" that run on shared time.
