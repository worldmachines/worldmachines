# RIT 01 — Prefaces & front matter

This unit holds the tail of the Preface to the second edition (signed Cambridge, Massachusetts, September 1999), the full original Preface (1983), the acknowledgments, and the front-matter epigraphs. Together they do two jobs: the 1983 preface tells the origin story — an economic historian of the Industrial Revolution discovers, via an impulse watch purchase, that "few historians had" ever studied time measurement — and lays out the book's three-panel design; the 1999 preface audits what changed in the intervening sixteen years, both in scholarship (atomic timekeeping, Swiss historiography) and in the watch industry itself, which Landes reads as a compressed parable of globalization.

## Key arguments

- **Low-frequency clocks made modernity, not high-frequency ones.** Harvard Nobelist Norman Ramsey objected that high-frequency (atomic/molecular) timekeeping gained more precision in thirty years than mechanical clocks did in seven hundred, and deserved more than a few pages. Landes concedes the science but holds the line: for "time and the making of the modern world," the instruments that mattered are "the clocks we put on our mantelpiece or carry in our pocket or wear on our wrist" — the devices that disciplined ordinary conduct, not laboratory standards.
- **The triptych.** The book is (1) cultural history — why was the mechanical clock invented in Europe?; (2) history of science and technology — how did crude timekeepers become precision instruments?; (3) social and economic history — who made them, how, who used them, why. In 1983 Landes claims this was "largely uncharted territory" for general historians, "a rare strike" in an old discipline; "This book, then, is only prologue."
- **Watchmaking as globalization in miniature.** By 1999 the industry has trifurcated: Switzerland lives on luxury (Swatch's prices doubled and tripled; even a mass-production firm finds "it pays to appeal to the lovers of the different and the particular"); digitals for the masses go to whoever combines low wages with high skills — Hong Kong, China, Malaysia displacing even Japan; the US (Timex's postwar cheap-but-good mechanical watch) and Britain (reduced to commemorative reproductions — Harrison chronometer copies, Congreve rolling-ball clocks) have exited. The rule: "look for the labour and leave it when it gets too good and costly."
- **Precision without punctuality.** Cheap radio-synchronized watches mean "people's knowledge of time far exceeds the timeliness of action." Punctuality decays into mere promptness (earliness); airlines define large lateness margins as "on time"; air traffic control holds planes at will; Angelenos waste ~40 hours a month — a week's work — commuting. Measurement has saturated; coordination has not.
- **Church and science.** Clerical astronomy was genuinely progressive, yet results contradicting orthodoxy met "overt and covert hostility, passionate indifference and tenacious contradiction" — symbolized by the statues flanking Saint Peter's: Paul III (encouraging useful technique) vs Urban VIII (repressing subversive cosmology).

## Evidence & specifics

The 1983 origin story is precise: a Tuesday-evening wine tasting in Berkeley ~1958 where Carlo Cipolla produced a chiming gold "striker" (repeater) watch, before colleagues Henry Rosovsky and Albert Fishlow; the 1972 purchase of unsigned "Superwatch" at R. Laforet's Au Vieux Cadran, rue Bonaparte, Paris; the chance meeting with Adolphe Chapiro, CNRS chemist and horologist ("You must never do that... buy an expensive watch without knowing anything about watches"); Sotheby's London, where dealers run a ring with private "knock-out" auctions after the sale. Landes's self-indictment is the historiographical hook: he had stood inside Abraham Darby's 1709 blast furnace — "the brick cradle of the Industrial Revolution" — yet never asked how his own watch worked. Milestones: a 1978 seminar at Zurich/ETH (possibly the first such course in Swiss higher education), the Richard Lectures at Virginia, a first manuscript in spring 1982, reworked through lectures at Geneva and the EHESS. The inquiry's itinerary previews the book's range: the Great Khan's court, the Manila galleon, chronometric trials at Kew, Geneva and Neuchâtel, the Waltham and Elgin factories, Southeast Asian horological sweatshops. The 1999 additions: Swiss chronometers for marine navigation flourished in a landlocked country; Seiko's design-competition winners "disappoint," so rich Japanese buyers come to Geneva; Lange of Dresden (Glashütte) revives in complicated wristwatches; Los Alamos scientists now model traffic instead of Star Wars.

## Quotables

- "The clock, not the steam-engine, is the key-machine of the modern industrial age... it marks a perfection toward which other machines aspire." — Lewis Mumford, *Technics and Civilization*, epigraph (front matter, paired with "The clock is not merely a means of keeping track of the hours, but of synchronizing the actions of men.")
- "In so far as we are concerned with 'time and the making of the modern world', it is the low-frequency pieces that made the difference — the clocks we put on our mantelpiece or carry in our pocket or wear on our wrist." (Preface to the second edition)
- "Watches as much as any other manufactured commodity embody the patterns of contemporary globalization: look for the labour and leave it when it gets too good and costly." (Preface to the second edition)
- "People's knowledge of time far exceeds the timeliness of action. Even for those for whom time matters, punctuality tends to become promptness (earliness)." (Preface to the second edition)

## WM resonances

- The Mumford epigraph is nearly a thesis statement for [[legibility-as-machine-core]]: the clock as the [[modernity-machine]]'s key device — synchronization, standardization, automatic action — "a perfection toward which other machines aspire."
- Landes's 1999 punctuality passage is an unwitting case study in [[clock-as-incomplete-discipline]]: universal cheap precision produces *knowledge* of time without *governed* time. The slack pools in airline "on-time" margins, ATC holds, and LA congestion — ungovernable temporal surplus that disciplinary time secretes but cannot absorb, adjacent to the [[untimely]].
- Swatch, "conceived for mass production," finding profit in "the lovers of the different and the particular" is a small but genuine [[divergence-machine]] datum: standardization's flagship bending toward variety and proliferation.
