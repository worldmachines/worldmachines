# RIT 24 — The Quartz Revolution, II (ch. 21, second half + conclusion)

The book's final stretch moves from the culture war over quartz (collectors' letters for and against), through the statistical and human anatomy of Swiss collapse, to the Swatch counterattack and the luxury-mechanical renaissance, ending with Landes's revised verdict on seven hundred years of mechanical horology. The through-line: quartz devalued Switzerland's trump — accumulated skill — "overnight," putting the Swiss where Britain stood two hundred years earlier (how do you compete with low wages *plus* up-to-date technology?); survival came not from reclaiming precision, which was now a commodity, but from re-founding the industry on meaning, fashion, and prestige.

## Key arguments

- **An inversion of the hierarchy of tasks.** The knowledge-intensive work migrated upstream (circuit design, photochemical preparation of crystals and modules) while assembly and casing were drastically deskilled — "there is no repassage with digitals." So the old anti-*chablonnage* policy of hoarding finishing work collapsed: Swiss exports of unassembled movements/components rose from 1.8M pieces (1971) to 32.7M (1980), and "the meek have inherited the horological earth." Advanced nations may defend themselves better on anonymous components (cf. Britain's crucible-steel quasi-monopoly) than in cost-conscious finished goods.
- **Rationality has a price.** The Swiss married quartz to analog dials (over 90% of their quartz watches) because they make the world's best wheel trains and analog allows elegance — rational, says the economist, but it ceded the fastest-growing segment, quartz "complicateds," where digital displays add functions at negligible marginal cost. Nearly all utilitarian demand went elsewhere.
- **Leadership churns; present history is dangerous.** Japan tripled output 1970–80 (23.8M → 87.9M units; exports 11.4M → 68.3M, 48% → 78% of output), held first place barely three years, then was supplanted by Hong Kong — itself threatened by cheaper labor and the colony's settled political future. "The quartz watch is not the last word."
- **The Swatch was institutional, not a beach epiphany.** Landes explicitly revises the "too neat and inspired" myth of Jacques Müller's idea: Ernst Thomke (ex-apprentice watchmaker via pharma, ETA CEO from 1978, who rejected "the purgative therapy that had sunk Waltham") had already *routinized creativity* — brainstorming, idea premiums, cross-hierarchy communication — and the Delirium ultra-thin module (1981: 5,000+ sold at avg. $4,700) proved the weld-module-to-case technique. The Swatch spec was written to force new technique: under 10 SFr cost falling to 6, 10M units in three years, throwaway quality.
- **Precision as cost-saver, obsolescence as strategy.** Parts cut from 91 to 51 (Roskopf's move repeated), plastic injection molding, tolerances under 1/200 mm making parts interchangeable without adjustment, ultrasonic fixing to a single plane, robotic lines; "Quis" quality-information systems cut the initial 70% reject rate to under ~5% and became a sellable product (Bosch, videotape makers). Six-year durability "did not suit the convenience of the manufacturer," so new models several times a year made old ones *aesthetically* obsolete. Results: 1.2M (1983), 3.2M (1984), 8.3M (1985) — "the Timex of the 1980s, writ larger."
- **Survival by meaning, not measurement.** Swatch saved prestige, not employment: a few hundred jobs against an industry that fell from 89,000 workers (1970) to 32,000 (1985) and from 1,620 firms to 600. The top end thrives on Veblen logic — Patek's slogan shifted from precision to eternity ("custodian for one's children"); the tourbillon "doesn't even make sense in a wristwatch" yet is prized as highest artistry; Blancpain boasts of never making quartz on a fudged 1735 pedigree. Japan can copy every technique but not the aesthetic: "every object has its own way of speaking and shining." By 1985 Switzerland made 10% of the world's watches but nearly half the value.
- **The closing verdict.** Landes retracts his first-edition obituary for the craftsmen — artistry continues — but they no longer give us time. That task belongs to physicists and engineers; the best ordinary timekeeper is already a sub-$100 radio receiver tuned to an observatory signal: "No movement; no module. Just a receiver." And do we *like* knowing time so precisely? Apparently not (alarms ring twice for the unwilling riser) — yet time is "a deep habit, the price of modernization." *Tempus vitam regit.*

## Evidence & specifics

- Swiss output: peak 87M pieces of a 227M world total (1974); still largest in 1978 (63M of ~265M); houses fell from a peak 2,332 (mid-1950s; OCR-mangled year) to under 900 by 1980; bench workforce down to 26,228 (from ~55,320; OCR-mangled).
- The Jura: jobs in 1982 half their level a decade earlier; income per head from 42% *above* the national average (1950) to ~25% *below* (1977); regional income grew 3.1× vs. the national 7.4× — "a half-turn of the wheel of fortune." Simone Oppliger's *Quand nous étions horlogers*: Renan (Vallon de Saint-Imier) fell from 1,094 to ~845 inhabitants in 1970–78; school entry class from twenty to six. The union, unable to fight structural change, bribed meeting attendance with clothes-hangers; the bankrupt leading manufacturer of Renan: banks "had already decided in high places that we were beyond rescue."
- The luxury pole: NYT, Dec. 1980 — Patek/Audemars perpetual calendars at ~$15,000; a Vacheron-Constantin diamond "watch" over $4M sold with a promised 20%/yr capital gain ("It was no watch... It was an investment"); Patek's sniff: "You can take any movement and encrust it with diamonds."
- Personalities: Müller and Elmar Mock ("moderate, Swiss version of the rebels of '68"; Mock a plastics specialist) vs. the counter-case of Max Hetzel, tuning-fork inventor turned away by every Swiss house. Seiko's Suwa plant sits in a valley "uncannily similar to the watch country of Neuchâtel" — a nearly humanless robotic line, cleaned by local women, not *Gastarbeiter*.
- The culture war: collector Daniel Wardin unsettled by "an oscillator... vibrating at the obnoxious rate of 32,768 times a second strapped to our wrists"; physicist-romantic Leonard X. Finegold answering that his quartz watch gives him "a connection with the cosmos through the universal force of gravity."

## Quotables

- "There has been, in other words, an inversion of the traditional hierarchy of tasks within the industry, and the meek have inherited the horological earth." (~p. 383)
- "We attached too much importance to moral values. That's not the language you have to use in the business jungle." — the bankrupt Renan manufacturer (~p. 386)
- "For most of us, time is a deep habit, the price of modernization, productivity, potential affluence. Who are we to deprecate what we live by and, living, have got rich by?" (~p. 395)
- "The mechanical clock and watch may go, like the clepsydra and sundial before. Even quartz clocks and watches may shrink drastically in usefulness and importance. The timekeeper remains." (final lines, ~p. 395)

## WM resonances

- [[clock-as-incomplete-discipline]] / [[untimely]]: the book's last page states the thesis almost verbatim — time as "deep habit" and "price of modernization," yet alarms must sound *twice* for the unwilling riser, and a "timeless vacation" is impossible without renouncing the time-bound media of modern life. Discipline is near-total at the infrastructure level and still leaks at the level of bodies.
- [[legibility-as-machine-core]]: Landes names precision time as the substrate of "modernization, productivity, potential affluence"; on the shop floor, the Quis devices show legibility turned on manufacture itself — continuous in-line measurement is what makes the 5% failure rate (and the throwaway watch) possible.
- [[divergence-machine]]: once quartz commoditized accuracy — the very metric seven centuries of horology had optimized toward — value migrated to proliferation: Swatch's deliberate aesthetic obsolescence and collector runs, tourbillons prized precisely for their functional senselessness, Patek selling eternity instead of precision. The industry survived the perfection of its own legibility metric by switching to variety and meaning.
