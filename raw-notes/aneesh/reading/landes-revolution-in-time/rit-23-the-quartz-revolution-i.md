# RIT 23 — The Quartz Revolution, Part I (Ch. 21)

This first half of the chapter stages two successive challenges to Swiss supremacy and argues they were different in kind. The Timex assault was *quantitative* — "the extreme expression of older developments," two centuries of standardization, interchangeability, and deskilling pushed to their logical conclusion — and the Swiss could study and copy it (the Fédération Horlogère literally wrote a monograph, "The Timex Formula"). The quartz revolution was *qualitative*: a new physics of timekeeping that made the product "still look like a watch but in reality a new product," whose learning-curve economics ("exponential, cascading downwards") the Swiss had never seen. Landes's central claim is an indictment: the Swiss "should have known" by 1968, had every scientific asset needed, and failed anyway — because decades of sheltered monopoly rents had destroyed their *Neuerungsfreudigkeit*, their joy in innovation. Science had defeated art, and the Swiss had the science but not the will. (My text ends with Landes's elegy for the mechanical movement; the chapter continues beyond this unit.)

## Key arguments

- **Timex won on cheap time, not better time.** Accurate to only a minute or two a day — good enough in a world of radio and telephone time — it sold through a quarter-million non-traditional outlets (drugstores, supermarkets, tobacconists), riveted shut, unrepairable, disposable. Its real foundation was worker productivity: armalloy parts interchangeable *among* plants, automation "to reduce the human element to a minimum."
- **High frequency is the whole secret of quartz.** Every stable frequency is a clock if you can count the beats; quality (Q = natural frequency ÷ resonance-curve width) rises with frequency and slow decay. A good quartz crystal is "a one-metre pendulum that took a month rather than a day to lose half its amplitude."
- **The warnings were legible and ignored.** Quartz dated to the 1920s, atomic clocks to the late 1940s; Bulova's Accutron (1960) — a tuning-fork watch invented by Swiss engineer Max Hetzel, rejected by Swiss makers, triumphant in America — guaranteed a minute a month for life. The Swiss licensed it back grudgingly, crediting Bulova in microscopic type.
- **The chronometry competitions died of measurement.** Once quartz entries scored two orders of magnitude better in six years, the contest "lost meaning, both for spectators and for participants" — and threatened to become "a vehicle for their overthrow" by the Japanese.
- **Collective research without individual enterprise fails.** Japan and the US moved via single firms betting big (Seiko, Hamilton/Pulsar), drawing on existing electronics sectors (Sharp, Casio, TI, Intel, Fairchild). Switzerland moved collectively (CEH), and a "critical gap" yawned between R&D and production: "Watch companies are like horses: you can lead them to know-how, but you can't make them use it."
- **Dying technologies bloom under sentence of death.** The Swiss answered quartz by perfecting mechanicals — "the finest days of the sailing ship came after the advent of steam."

## Evidence & specifics

- Timex: Waterbury Clock Co. revived by Norwegian refugee Joakim Lehmkuhl (1942, fuse production; postwar sales crash $70m → $300,000); first Timex 1949 at $6.95–7.95; 8 million units in 1960 (more than all Japan, 3× US jewelled output); 1 in 3 US watches by 1962; ~45% of US market and 86% of domestic production by 1973; 30 million pieces, $200m sales, 17,000 employees, 20 plants by 1972–3. Expansion: Canada, Britain, South Africa, France (Besançon plant, "Kelton" brand), Germany (jewellers wooed; 500,000+ units by 1967–8).
- Quartz lineage: Curie's piezoelectricity; 1920s broadcast-frequency crisis; Horton & Marrison lab prototype (US, 1928); Greenwich's first quartz (1939) at 2 ms/day; ovened, closed-loop lab clocks reach 0.01 ms/day — one second in 275 years. First clocks filled 3 × 2.5 × 1 m cabinets.
- Early quartz watches (1968–9): $1,000+ (≈5× in today's money), push-button displays, crystals drifting after a year — "For the price of a Timex one could throw away one's watch and start again. But not at $1,500 a copy!" Gillette entered assuming watches sell like razors; return rates hit 40% (J.C. Penney, 1976, one supplier).
- Neuchâtel observatory scores (0 = perfect): 1961 quartz marine chronometer 1.2 vs 2.8 best mechanical; then 0.8, 0.13, 0.0099 by 1967; CEH's small-calibre quartz watch 0.152 vs a record 1.73 mechanical. Wristwatch competition suspended 26 April 1968, never revived; the regleurs now meet "once a year for dinner; also at funerals."
- Swiss scientific assets: CEH Neuchâtel; Institut Battelle → Omega analogue quartz 1970; FASELEC Zurich (1967; Philips, Brown Boveri, FH, Landis & Gyr); Ebauches SA + Hughes Aircraft ICs at Marin; Texas Instruments + Ebauches LCDs for Longines/ASUAG (1972). ASUAG made modules and components — not watches — and so sold abroad, seeding competitors.
- The curve: 1970 FH reassured itself 98% of sales were still mechanical; even Japan was <6% quartz in 1974 — then 1.8m → 19.7m electronic watches 1974–78. At the 1979 Geneva congress Seiko showed current draw down 60%+, thickness from 5+ mm to under 1 mm, prices down to 2–3% of decade-start levels. By 1983: $100 solid-state watches outperforming the finest detent chronometers; Sharp's $100 talking watch (1981); Seiko's $425 TV wristwatch (Dec. 1982) and 8-minute watch-recorder (May 1983).
- Swiss mechanical rearguard: calibres cut from 550–600 to 440–60 (1970–73); output per head 723 → 1,305 (1966–76); only ~2.5% of sales to R&D, two-thirds of it on the old technology; 36,000-bph fast-beat watches as "the mechanical answer to the Accutron"; George Daniels's new escapement unable to find a Swiss manufacturer.

## WM resonances

- [[legibility-as-machine-core]] — Timex is the Modernity Machine's production logic at its asymptote: parts interchangeable among plants, automation eliminating "petty troublesome adjustments," the human element minimized. Landes's own framing — "the culmination of two centuries of striving" — reads as a machine completing its optimization target. The Q coefficient and observatory scores likewise render accuracy itself fully legible, a single number that kills an entire craft contest.
- [[gramsci-gap]] — the 1968 suspension is a textbook interregnum move: rather than cede its legitimating institution to the new order, Swiss horology abolishes it. The old (regleur artistry, chronometer certification) is dead; the new (quartz mass production) cannot yet be born in Switzerland — the science exists, the modules exist, but the "critical gap" between R&D and production stays uncrossed, and the combines export know-how to their own future conquerors.
- [[dawn-day-dusk-machine-typology]] — "once-dominant technologies make some of their greatest improvements under sentence of obsolescence; the finest days of the sailing ship came after the advent of steam": dusk-phase brilliance, exactly — calibre rationalization, doubled productivity, and fast-beat virtuosity all arrive *after* the verdict.
- [[divergence-machine]] (lightly) — the early quartz scramble is push-machine proliferation: horological innocents (Gillette, Intel, Fairchild) flood in, quality control is abandoned ("cheaper to let the public screen the product"), brand names multiply and die in a continuing shake-out, and "national as well as professional lines blurred as knowledge was traded for profit across frontiers."
