# RIT 20 — "Ah, But He Could!" (ch. 18)

The chapter title answers the British verdict on Ingold-style machine watchmaking: he could, and the Swiss did. Landes traces how the Swiss industry, scorned by the British for its cheap cylinder watches, overtook Britain in quality as well as quantity between roughly 1790 and 1907 — through observatory time trials, emulation societies, partial mechanization, product innovation (above all stem winding), and a cultural infrastructure of literacy and dynastic networks. He then turns the comparison into a methodological polemic: against neoclassical "New Economic History," he insists British failure was a failure of imagination and enterprise, not fate — the opportunity to "alter the parameters rather than adjust the variables" existed and was seized elsewhere (the United States, foreshadowed here).

## Key arguments

- **Measurement infrastructure drove quality.** Swiss prize competitions (from 1790/1792, verified against a sundial at noon) evolved into continuous observatory trials at Geneva (1829) and Neuchâtel (1858) — observatories built largely *for* the watch industry — standardized into annual published competitions in the 1870s that "made and unmade reputations." Greenwich trials (from the early 1830s) tested only marine chronometers for the Royal Navy and were actively conservative: they entrenched the idea that "chronometer" meant detent escapement, whereas the Swiss defined chronometer by performance, learning that accuracy could be had in cheaper, mass-producible forms.
- **Mechanization preceded interchangeability — and shouldn't be confused with it.** Ébauche-making mechanized first (Japy, 1770s–80s; Fontainemelon, 1793); Geneva resisted ("the Genevans did not take to machine-made products, much less to machines"). Leschot's pantograph machines for Vacheron & Constantin (from 1839) produced *similar*, not interchangeable, parts: soft steel, tempering distortion, 25%+ rejects, hand-fed. True interchangeability awaited American heavy presses and single-purpose tools in the 1860s–70s.
- **British decline was not for want of talent.** Britain had inventors (Prest's stem-winder from 1820, Nicole's 1844 patent) and the world's best finishers; what it lacked was commercial responsiveness. Merchants (Sewill) refused to invest in manufacture; producer-établisseurs were "prisoners of the past"; workers defeated even trivial economies (Usher & Cole's unpolishable arbors). Change had to come from outside the industry or country — and insiders could not conceive it.
- **Swiss success demands a collective-cultural explanation.** Roy Church's textile-substitution thesis is factually wrong (textiles were German-Swiss, watches French-Swiss); export dependence is real but insufficient. Landes's answer: a culturally bounded swarm — dynasties (Favre, Piguet, Audemars, Le Coultre), Protestant geography (watchmaking stopped at the Catholic French border, except Protestant Beaucourt), and above all Protestant *literacy and numeracy* — autodidact mountain libraries, the Le Locle bookshop, "a seedbed of Ben Franklins à la jurassienne" — institutionalized by schools, societies, museums, and exhibitions that "socialize recruits."

## Evidence & specifics

- Société des Arts of Geneva (1776, de Saussure + Faizan): mechanics courses (Pictet, 1781), prizes for matching British crucible steel, first enduring watchmaking school (1823). Young Louis Agassiz proposed libraries/museums/schools to Neuchâtel's Patriotic Society of Emulation (founded 1791).
- Price collapse: a Japy verge ébauche fell from 6.50 francs (late 1700s) to 1.5–2 francs by 1815; finishing accounted for 90%+ of a 20-franc watch. Division of labor: ~100 *parties brisées* in 1870 vs. 54 in 1830. By 1870, one-tenth of the workforce was in "protofactories"; the industry spilled into low-wage German-speaking districts (Grenchen, Solothurn), tapping women and children.
- Stem winding: Adrien Philippe (1842) + Comte de Patek → Patek Philippe's ascent; Antoine LeCoultre's form won out. Britain clung to keys for decades — even adding an extra wheel to make keyless movements wind counterclockwise "as before," at cost to price and performance; repairers sabotaged adoption.
- The coup de grâce: Paul Ditisheim of La Chaux-de-Fonds won Kew in 1903 with a record 94.9/100 — "an outlier, four or five standard deviations above the mean" — and from 1907 Swiss makers (Patek Philippe, Vacheron, Zenith, Movado, Longines, Omega) took first prize every year.
- British labor's endgame: Hampshire fusee-chain makers earning twopence farthing an hour in the 1890s; the *Horological Journal* (1891) lamenting prices "a bicycle repairer would have a good laugh at"; Alun Davies's craftsmen "trapped by their own skills."

## Quotables

- "Of genius in this fabrication, nothing; but lots of order, supervision and, with these, very good profits." — Élie LeCoultre on Jacottet's rough-parts production (p. 313)
- "There is no watchmaker who would not rather wind three watches of ordinary construction than one keyless watch!" — British repairers on stem winding (p. 316)
- "Thus was British watchmaking robbed of its final consolation, stripped of its last illusion." — after Ditisheim's 1903 Kew victory (p. 318)
- New Economic History's "motto is 'Whatever has been, had to be.' The market is perfect; businessmen know their best interest and are rational in their choices; and there is no such thing as failure, only fate." (p. 318)

## WM resonances

- [[legibility-as-machine-core]]: the observatory trials are a legibility apparatus in miniature — performance rendered into certificates, points out of 100, published rankings that "made and unmade reputations." Note the two modes: Britain's *categorical* standard (chronometer = detent escapement) froze practice, while the Swiss *metric* standard (chronometer = whatever measures well) opened the design space for mass production. Legibility-by-measurement outcompeted legibility-by-category.
- [[divergence-machine]]: Landes explicitly rejects individual-talent explanations and demands "a model of collective effort" for "the swarm of people and shops" — many independent local makers, emulative and competitive, proliferating variant escapements and winding schemes against a British industry locked to one canonical form. A push-machine pattern beating a pull toward the traditional attractor; though the Swiss swarm's own drive toward normalization shows divergence feeding a new standardization.
- A minor [[gramsci-gap]] echo at industry scale: the old order could not birth the new from within — "it could not have occurred to them" — and Lancashire's attempt to hire the old artisans only "brought the enemy within the gates"; the Swiss trained fresh workers "which hadn't got the tradition."
