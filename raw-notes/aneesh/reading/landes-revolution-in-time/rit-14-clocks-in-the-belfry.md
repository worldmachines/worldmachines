# RIT 14 — Clocks in the Belfry (Ch. 12)

Economic history at ground level: what it took, in money, men, and iron, to build and keep a fourteenth-century turret clock. Landes squeezes three documentary survivals — the Perpignan account book of 1356, a 1379 contract from the village of Nieppe, and the Cambrai cathedral accounts — for everything they yield about clockmaking as a trade. The composite overturns the lone inventor-artisan image: early clock "manufacture" was construction work, done on site by ad hoc teams of up to a hundred under a versatile contractor-engineer, and the finished machine was only the beginning of a lifetime of expensive care. The closing verdict doubles as thesis: whoever built a clock gave a hostage to fortune.

## Key arguments

- **Early clockmaking was building, not manufacture.** Teams travelled to the site, forged there, and were paid partly in kind. Talent came from blacksmiths, braziers, millwrights, and above all the Church — mining-and-metallurgy monasteries were "probably the largest body of mechanics," diffusing best engineering practice across Europe.
- **Reputation created a profession.** As makers acquired renown (the Jewish instrument-makers of Aragon; the Dondi of Padua; Henry de Vic, imported for the Paris palace clock), the surname "del Orologio" granted to Dondi père announced a new occupation.
- **Service was as valuable as sale.** Rapid wear and obsolescence meant constant reordering and repair; a maker who built for a king often found himself permanently engaged — and lendable like any other royal asset.
- **A standard product already existed by 1379.** The Nieppe contract specifies no design at all, only iron weight, price, installation, and arbitration "by good workers expert and knowledgeable in the matter" — evidence of a trade standard and a recognized craft, even in a village that already had an earlier clock.
- **Maintenance rivaled construction.** Nearly as much effort went into upkeep as into building; Cambrai's accounts record a century of overhauls, retainers, and (1435) one of the earliest technical consultations on record.
- **Machines and humans failed in alternation.** When the Perpignan clock wore out, King John I substituted hired bell-ringers at a penny a day — who also failed. Landes's dry pivot: people are not more reliable than machinery; "that, after all, was why the clock was invented in the first place."

## Evidence & specifics

- **Perpignan, 1356**: notary Ramon Sans, commissioned by Peter IV of Aragon, recorded every sou across 77 folio sheets (Archives of Aragon, Barcelona) — the earliest detailed record of a clock build, rescued from oblivion by C. F. C. Beeson and Francis Maddison.
- **The build**: nine months, 1,667 livres net — roughly $1 million today (top wage 4 sous/day ≈ $100–150). Over a sixth went to contractor Anthony Bonelli, *plomberius* to Pope Innocent VI at Avignon, with ten clocksmiths. Clock ~2 tons, bell twice that, lead driving weights only 460 kg; a castle corner became forge and machine shop; iron tilt-hammered to wooden patterns (19 cwt at 36 sous/cwt); costly steel used "stingily" — verge, pallets, files. Spectators required a barrier; the bell-casting earned a feast; finishing took two more months.
- **Decline**: trouble from 1379 (age ~23 — "a fair lifetime"); 1387, human ringers hired (tailors or furriers would do), Barcelona's cathedral endowed with sand-glass watchmen; 1399, royal inquiry into negligent ringers; by the French occupation the clock had vanished — bell (and, Landes conjectures, clock) recast as cannon.
- **Nieppe, 1379**: Pierre Daimleville of Lille contracted by the Countess of Bar for a 301-pound clock at 11 gold francs, defects corrected at his own cost, plus an annual tunic of officers' cloth as maintenance retainer.
- **Cambrai**: astronomical show clock, pilgrim lure and revenue source (legend: built by a shepherd, then blinded — false, but telling of relic-style monopoly logic); accounts from 1318; 1348 overhaul ~300 livres, half to the master; 1396–98 rebuild by Mahieu of Soingnies, 143 livres — "over a thousand days of skilled labour" — with subcontracted scribe, painter, organ-builder; 1401, Mathieu gets 16 livres against under 2 for the artists; 1435, the clock "in great ruin," Lille smith Jacques Yolens paid for advice, Colard Lefevre's overhaul 120 livres.

- [[clock-as-incomplete-discipline]] — incompleteness at the level of the *instrument itself*: the Perpignan clock disciplined nobody for long, decayed within a generation, and was replaced by unreliable humans who were in turn the reason clocks existed. Cambrai's market and work bells show church time infrastructure already serving secular labour coordination.
