# RIT 09 — My Time Is Your Time (ch. 7)

The chapter title is the chapter's thesis: before the 1650s every timekeeper kept its own idiosyncratic time; after the pendulum (Huygens, 1656) and the balance spring (Huygens, 1675), clocks could agree — with each other and with an abstract standard, mean time. Landes opens with the diagnostic insight that early verge timepieces were deceptive because daily averages concealed wild hour-to-hour swings; the real task was an oscillator with its own inherent stable frequency, isolated from the wheel train. He then runs the precision revolution as a chain of derived demand ("innovation begets innovation"): pendulum → circular error → cycloidal cheeks → anchor escapement → the verification problem → equation tables → the triumph of mean time over the sun itself. Threaded throughout are horology's signature priority wars (Galileo vs Huygens, Hooke vs Huygens, Thuret vs Huygens), each treated as evidence that the key inventions were "in the air."

## Key arguments

- **Averages deceive.** Verge clocks variance: ~15 min/day for clocks, ~30 min for watches — tolerable as a daily mean, useless hour to hour, and inadequate for astronomers and navigators. The fix was not better trains but a new architecture: a self-frequencied oscillator (pendulum for standing clocks, *spiral réglant* for portable ones) decoupled from train irregularities.
- **Anticipation is not innovation.** Jost Bürgi's cross-beat escapement plus remontoir (1584) gained one to two orders of magnitude — "practically another Archimedes," said his patron William of Hesse — yet is forgotten because nobody built on it: too hard to make, too hard to adjust. Lineage and uptake, not ingenuity, make an invention count.
- **Precision created its own verification crisis.** Once a clock beats within seconds, what checks it? Bellair to Huygens (1659): two converted seconds-pendulum clocks could not be kept sounding together four days running. Sundials read no finer than ~5 minutes; minute-reading dials (c. 1700) arrived after clocks had outrun them.
- **Mean time conquered solar time culturally, not just technically.** Sun time runs up to 16 min ahead and 14 min behind mean time, coinciding four times a year. Equation tables were pasted inside clock-case doors; then the inversion: sundials acquired conversion scales so people *corrected the sun* rather than setting by it — "one more step towards that emancipation from the natural diurnal rhythm." Adoption was uneven: British makers geared equation clocks to mean time; the continent lived by solar time into the nineteenth century.
- **The balance spring, not quartz, was the socially radical revolution.** Quartz's later gain (minute/day → minute/year) is two orders of magnitude but a "psychic luxury"; the balance spring's gain (half-hour/day → ~5 min/day) crossed the threshold at which life and work could be ordered by the watch. It "revolutionized time discipline."
- **Dials index public time literacy.** Minute numerals at five-minute intervals → fifteen-minute (third quarter of 18th c.) → none (end of century) → markless 20th-c. dials. Display variety (wandering-hour, sun-and-moon, differential dials) collapsed onto concentric hands; purists like Henry Sully (Paris, 1717) damned the rest as horological foolishness.

## Evidence & specifics

- **Pendulum:** conceived by Galileo 1637 or earlier; realized by Huygens with clockmaker Salomon Coster, late 1656. Performance: ≥15 min/day variance → 10–15 sec/day; existing clocks converted *en masse*. Italian outcry (Leopold de' Medici, the Accademia del Cimento, 1657–67); Leopold's disclaimer came only after the *Horologium Oscillatorium* (1673). Galileo's unbuilt escapement was the first free/detached design — Huygens underrated it.
- **Circular error:** the cycloid is the tautochrone; semiarc growth 1°→2° adds 4.95 sec/day, 5°→6° adds 18.09. Huygens's cycloidal cheeks (*Horologium*, 1658) — he was prouder of the geometry than of the clock — lost out to small arcs plus the anchor escapement (William Clement 1671 vs Hooke post-1666 claims). The "royal pendulum" held 10 sec/day; Greenwich ran 13-foot pendulums beating double seconds. French patent thrice refused — Chancellor Séguier feared "all the master clockmakers of Paris crying after him."
- **Balance spring:** Huygens's "Eureka" notebook entry 23 January 1675; cipher to the Royal Society, solution 20 February ("the arbor of the moving ring is fixed at the centre of an iron spiral"); watch by Isaac Thuret shown to Colbert. Counterclaims: Thuret (forced into written apology); Hooke's engraved "R. Hooke invenit 1658, T. Tompion fecit 1675," backed by Moray's 1665 letter to Huygens. A. R. Hall: Hooke had conceived spring + constant-force + detent escapement ("trigger"/"trigg") for longitude by 1660 — a century before Pierre Le Roy — but buried it when backers refused a £2,000 confidentiality bond. "His own worst enemy."
- **Aftermath:** instant obsolescence of the world's watch stock; conversions proved inferior, driving new purchases; first special-purpose tools, standard calibres, batch production, division of labour. Seconds hands from the 1690s ("doctors' watches" — mostly proof the watch was going); centre-seconds with stop levers, 1740s; Pouzait's independent seconds train, 1776; fifths-of-seconds dials by 1770, a British specialty serving owners and trainers of race horses.

## Quotables

- "It is in the nature of an average — such as time over a twenty-four-hour period — to conceal the range of deviation of its components." (~p. 127)
- "Soon sundials were built with conversion scales for equation of time: instead of setting by the sun, people corrected the sun." (~p. 134)
- "The balance spring thus laid the material basis for what even we would consider punctuality." (~p. 139)
- "'Tis with our judgements as our watches, none / Go just alike, yet each believes his own." (Pope, 1711, quoted ~p. 142)

## WM resonances

- Correcting the sun is the legibility move in its purest form: an abstract, uniform, machine-defined scale (mean time) displaces the local natural referent, and the referent itself is then re-labelled as deviation. Mean time operates as a shared attractor pulling every private timekeeper into agreement — "my time is your time" as Modernity-Machine pull dynamics ([[legibility-as-machine-core]], [[pull-vs-push-machines]]).
- Landes's own opening point — the average conceals the range of deviation of its components — is the legibility critique stated from inside horology: the metric that makes performance readable is the same one that hides variance ([[legibility-as-machine-core]]).
- The chapter documents time discipline being *materially constituted* (the balance spring as the substrate of punctuality) while showing the discipline never closes: the best clocks still drift apart, Bellair's pair can't hold synchrony four days, and Pope's everyone-believes-his-own-watch persists. Synchrony is an asymptote, not an achievement ([[clock-as-incomplete-discipline]]).
- The brief riot of display forms — wandering-hour, sun-and-moon, differential dials — collapsing onto the concentric two-hand standard, with novelty watches surviving only as ornament, is another miniature of variety pruned by a single legible convention ([[divergence-machine]]).
