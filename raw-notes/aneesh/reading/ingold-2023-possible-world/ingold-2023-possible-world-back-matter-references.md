# Back Matter: Acknowledgements, Notes, and References

The closing pages of Ingold's "On not knowing and paying attention" are not argumentative but they are informative about the essay's origin and lineage: the Acknowledgements disclose that the piece is a collage of fragments from three of Ingold's own earlier books, a Note dates it to a 2020 keynote address at University College Cork, and the References list the phenomenological, process-philosophical, and anthropological sources the essay draws on throughout. No conflicts of interest or funding are declared.

## Themes

**Provenance as collage, not a paper written fresh.** The Acknowledgements state plainly that the text is assembled from previously published fragments, "most notably my books The Life of Lines (2015), Anthropology and/as Education (2018) and Imagining for Real (2022)." This matters for downstream reading: recurring formulations in the essay (on lines, correspondence, education) are not new arguments but restatements drawn from that trilogy, which is where the fuller versions live.

**Origin as a keynote address on walking and attention.** Note 1 identifies the essay's occasion: a keynote to the workshop "Walking Conversations: The Art of Paying Attention, or, Non-Procedural Methodologies," held in March 2020 under the auspices of University College Cork. This explains the essay's exploratory, essayistic register and its direct address to an audience concerned with non-procedural (i.e. non-method-driven) ways of knowing.

**A citation lineage spanning phenomenology, process philosophy, and new materialism.** The reference list is dominated by Merleau-Ponty, Bergson, and Serres (phenomenology/process philosophy); Gibson, Myers, and Ingold's own prior books (ecological/anthropological approaches to perception, skill, and place); Dewey, Rancière, and Masschelein (pedagogy of attention and "poor pedagogy"); and Barad, Manning, and Harney and Moten (new materialism/posthumanist theory). This is the same lineage that supplies the essay's working concepts of correspondence, wayfaring, and attention, and is the natural place to look for primary-source elaboration of any of them.

## Notable passages

The Acknowledgements' admission that the essay is stitched from fragments of Ingold's own books is the single most load-bearing sentence in the back matter for anyone tracing where a given argument in the essay first appeared. The Notes entry on the workshop's title and date fixes the essay's occasion and register. The remaining Notes and References are standard scholarly apparatus (an ORCID iD, a Rilke citation via Macy and Barrows, and an alphabetical bibliography) and are not further discussed here.
