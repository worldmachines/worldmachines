# Abstract: Attention, Not-Knowing, and the Wisdom of a Possible World

This unit is the published abstract and keyword list for Tim Ingold's essay "On not knowing and paying attention: How to walk in a possible world," prepared for the volume *Walking Methodologies: Sociological Research on the Move* and appearing in a special issue of the *Irish Journal of Sociology*. In under a page it states the essay's entire argument: knowledge and wisdom are opposed orientations to the world, not degrees of the same thing; wisdom's attentiveness to an irreducibly uncertain world is what makes anticipation (and life itself) possible; and treating "not-knowing" as equivalent to "ignorance" is a category error that education, science, and the state have institutionalized.

## Themes

**Knowledge and wisdom face opposite directions.** Wisdom means turning toward the world and paying attention to what is found there; knowledge means turning away from it in pursuit of certainty and predictability. But a fully "joined up," certain world is one in which "nothing could live or grow" — life depends on the very uncertainty that knowledge tries to eliminate.

**Uncertainty is the condition of possibility, not a deficit.** Because a world of life is necessarily uncertain, it is also necessarily open to pure possibility. Reaching that possibility requires rethinking the relation between doing and undergoing — between intentional and attentional models of action.

**Attention and intention are differently shaped, not just differently valued.** Attention "cuts a road longitudinally" through the "transverse connections" that intention draws to its objects — a directional, almost topological image of two distinct ways action can relate to the world. From this follows the essay's central pairing: intention is predictive, attention is anticipatory.

**Ignorance and not-knowing are not synonyms, and collapsing them is the error to correct.** If prediction's failure mode is ignorance, anticipation's counterpart is simply the possibility of not knowing — and modern institutions have built authority on erasing that distinction. Ingold names the offenders directly: "Education, science and the state are powerful machines for the production of ignorance." His closing counter-claim: not-knowing is not ignorance but "the wisdom that lies in attending to things."

## Notable passages

- The founding opposition (c1) sets up knowledge-as-certainty against wisdom-as-attention and states the paradox that a fully certain, "joined up" world could not sustain life or growth.
- The conceptual pivot (c2) moves from that opposition to the essay's operative distinction — intentional/predictive versus attentional/anticipatory models of action — via the image of attention cutting a longitudinal road through intention's transverse connections.
- The payoff (c3) delivers the essay's corrective claim: ignorance and not-knowing are different things, institutions manufacture the former, and not-knowing is itself a form of wisdom.

The keyword list (attention, generations, knowledge, possibility, wisdom) includes "generations," which is not developed anywhere in the abstract text itself — a signal that the full essay likely opens onto an intergenerational dimension (transmission, apprenticeship, or upbringing) invisible from this unit alone.
