# Generations as a Braid, and Astonishment Without Surprise

Ingold asks how the infinitude of life — lived, as he has argued elsewhere in this essay, as a "one-possibility thing" that carries on for an Aboriginal-cosmology "everywhen" — can be reconciled with the plain fact that every mortal being dies, and answers by rejecting the common-sense picture of generations as discrete layers that inherit from and replace one another in favour of an intergenerational braid whose overlapping strands keep the life-process going without any single strand being "inherited." A childhood anecdote — meeting an "aged, bearded moon-watcher" while walking home from school — then pivots the unit into its second argument: that astonishment (the stance of those genuinely open to the world) is not the same thing as surprise (what happens to prediction when it fails), and that the not-knowing on which astonishment depends is categorically different from ignorance.

## Themes

**Generations as an overlapping braid, not a stack of layers.** The dominant modern belief is that life is lived *within* generations but does not *flow between* them: each generation receives a "heritage or inheritance" of information and resources, uses it up, and passes on only "the discards." Ingold traces this to the same "fulfilment of potential" logic he rejects earlier in the essay, and names its social-imaginary correlate: "stratigraphic thinking," which equates generations with "layers of sedimentation," "deposits in the occupation of a site," "documents in an archive," even "consciousness in the human mind." Against this he proposes Figure 2's second image — "every particular life" as "one strand of an intergenerational braid" — where strands give out and new ones are paid in, transmission can break without extinction, and (quoting Bergson) each generation is pictured "leaning over the generation that shall follow," a figure of care and even love rather than replacement.

**The stack metaphor manufactures a rhetoric of extinction.** Ingold is explicit about the stakes of the metaphor choice: stratigraphic, layered thinking "feeds directly into a rhetoric of extinction that wonders whether the coming generation, or any after that, might be the last," and is "the reason why we feel ourselves facing a future blighted by uncertainty." Reimagining generations as a braid is offered as a way "to lift the curse of uncertainty, and to restore a sense of possibility" — the essay's move from a descriptive metaphor to a normative, even therapeutic, one.

**Astonishment vs. surprise, anticipation vs. prediction.** The second half of the unit draws a parallel Ingold has made before (citing his own 2011 work): astonishment relates to anticipation as surprise relates to prediction. Prediction's failure mode is ignorance; anticipation's counterpart is "the possibility of not-knowing" — and Ingold insists not-knowing and ignorance "are entirely different." Ignorance presumes things must be *explained* to us; prediction presumes things are *explicable*; both, he says, are "two sides of the same coin" underwriting "the rhetoric of normal science." He glosses this via Popper's conjecture-and-refutation model, where progress is a "cumulative record of predictive failures" lifting inquiry from ignorance to "knowledgeable enlightenment" — and contrasts it with a different relation to the world entirely: science's methodology is a device for holding the world "at a distance," a set of protocols meant to "immunise the investigator from any infection arising from too close a contact with the phenomena."

**Openness as strength, not naivety.** The unit closes by cashing out the distinction morally as much as epistemically. Scientists "are surprised, but not astonished, when their predictions turn out to be wrong," because prediction rests on "the conceit that the world can be held to account." By contrast, "indigenous peoples" and children whose minds are "yet to be subdued by adult disciplinary oppression" are "perpetually astonished" but "never surprised," precisely because they never assumed the world was predictable in the first place. Their "openness, or exposure, renders them vulnerable, but it is also a source of strength, resilience and wisdom," allowing "ongoing responsiveness." This equation of vulnerability-through-openness with resilience, set against a science that seeks to *immunise* itself from contact with its object, echoes — without being identical to — the logic of [[capture-resistance]]: closure buys predictability at the cost of the very responsiveness that keeps a living process alive.

## Notable passages

**The braid figure and Bergson.** The essay's key image-substitution: generations as "one strand of an intergenerational braid" rather than "layers in a stack," anchored to Bergson's picture of "each generation leaning over the generation that shall follow" as an act of care. This is the constructive half of the unit's argument — see the first curated passage.

**The critique of stratigraphic thinking.** Immediately preceding the braid figure, Ingold names what he's arguing against: a "stack" model that licenses easy equations with sedimentary layers, archaeological deposits, archival documents, even the layered structure of consciousness itself — the second curated passage.

**The moon-watcher anecdote.** A short, self-contained childhood memory — encountering "the aged, bearded moon-watcher" on the walk home from school, an observation that "coupled the myopia of the peering eye with the intimate immensity of the cosmos" — serves as the essay's hinge between the generations argument and the astonishment/surprise argument. **Not-knowing, ignorance, and the methodology of distance.** The unit's central conceptual move: astonishment is to surprise as anticipation is to prediction, and not-knowing is to ignorance as anticipation is to prediction's failure — with Popper's conjecture-and-refutation model named as the disciplinary machinery that keeps ignorance and prediction locked together, and scientific "methodology" glossed as a set of protocols for holding the world at a distance rather than opening oneself to it.

**Scientists vs. the perpetually astonished.** The unit's closing contrast: scientists are surprised but not astonished because they believe the world can be "held to account," while indigenous peoples and children — not yet disciplined out of openness — are astonished but never surprised, their vulnerability doubling as strength, resilience, and wisdom, ending on the rhetorical question that gives the essay's larger argument its emotional close: why gaze at the moon at all, "if it not because it is so astonishing?"
