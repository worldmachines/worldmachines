# The Two Sides of Attention: From Exposure to Falling Forwards

Ingold argues that attention has two complementary sides — exposure and attunement — and traces how they alternate, in one fixed direction, through the practical case of walking on two feet, using that alternation to distinguish the open-ended “anticipation” proper to a life lived forwards from the closed logic of prediction.

## Themes

**Attunement is a perceiver catching up with a world already laid out.** Drawing on James Gibson's ecological psychology, Ingold describes attunement as the skilled uptake of affordances — information a stable environment already offers, learnable “for as long as life goes on.” The carpenter reading the grain of wood, the smith reading the ductility of iron: skill is a perceptual system becoming “attuned to information of a certain sort,” an “education of attention.” But this picture, Ingold notes, puts the momentum entirely on the side of the perceiver, as if the things to be perceived were already there merely awaiting notice.

**Exposure is the perceiver's submission to a world that is not already there.** Against that settled backdrop, Ingold poses a world “restless and ﬂuid, bustling with life” — weather, tides, rivers, growth. Here attention runs the other way: not extraction of information but waiting on the world, abiding with it. Drawing on Jan Masschelein's etymology of exposure (Latin ex-positio, meaning “pulled out of position”), Ingold casts this as vulnerability — solid ground shaken, understanding displaced by “undergoing” — and reads Merleau-Ponty's account of moonlit gazing as its paradigm: a symbiotic “linking up” with the phenomenon rather than a detached apprehension of it.

**The two sides alternate, and the alternation runs one way only.** Walking is Ingold's exemplary case: every step falls forward into momentary jeopardy (exposure) before footwork restores balance (attunement), in a cycle repeating for as long as the walk continues. He generalizes this to all life: submission always leads and mastery always follows, never the reverse. The turn from one to the other — from undergoing into doing — is what Ingold calls, borrowing from Erin Manning, a moment of inflection: not a movement itself but a variation in how movement moves, arising from within “the cleave of the event.”

**Anticipation opens a path; prediction closes one.** Ingold distinguishes aspiration/anticipation — an active “taking in” that, per Dewey, summons energy and casts the past forward — from prediction, which belongs to the logic of certainty, uncertainty, and probability. Anticipation, following Derrida's gloss that to anticipate is “to take the initiative, to be out in front,” does not fix a future state but improvises a passage through an as-yet-unformed world: “a seeing into the future,” not “not to ﬁx an end point.” This open-ended, non-determining relation between a moving life and an unformed world is close kin to [[Configurancy]] — a non-teleological process by which agent and world co-emerge, rather than a plan executed against a fixed target.

## Notable passages

**On attunement (Gibson).** The unit opens by naming “two sides to attention: exposure and attunement,” then unpacks attunement through Gibson's ecological perception — affordances learned through craft, culminating in the “education of attention” that turns a novice's blindness into a carpenter's or smith's fine-grained sensitivity.

**On exposure (Masschelein).** Ingold turns the picture around: if the world itself is unfinished — “restless and ﬂuid” — then attention must instead wait on it. The Masschelein-derived etymology of exposure as being “pulled out of position” grounds a reading of attention as vulnerability rather than mastery, ending in a state that “opens to pure possibility.”

**On the moonstruck gaze (Merleau-Ponty).** The passage beginning “And this is just what happens as we gaze at the moon” supplies exposure's paradigm case: perception as symbiotic communion with a phenomenon rather than a subject's detached uptake of information, ending in a world that “takes on a different sheen.”

**On falling forwards (walking).** The passage running from “Let us take as an example one of the most ubiquitous of all human activities” through the closing image of an “alternation that continues for as long as the walk goes on” is the essay's title image: every step is a controlled fall, exposure resolved into attunement and then risked again.

**On anticipation versus prediction (Dewey, Derrida).** The final curated passage, from “Literally, to aspire is to draw breath” to Ingold's insistence that anticipation means “to look where you are going, not to ﬁx an end point,” is the unit's clearest theoretical payoff: it separates a forward-looking, improvisatory orientation to an open future from the probabilistic closure of prediction.
