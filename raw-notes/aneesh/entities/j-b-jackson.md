# J. B. Jackson (1909–1996)

American landscape critic, writer, and cultural geographer. Founder and editor of *Landscape* magazine (1951–1968), which opened the American vernacular landscape to serious humanistic analysis. Taught at Berkeley and Harvard. Widely considered the founding figure of landscape studies as an independent humanistic discipline in the United States.

## Key Works

- *Landscapes: Selected Writings of J. B. Jackson* (ed. Ervin Zube, 1970).
- *American Space: The Centennial Years* (1972).
- *The Necessity for Ruins* (1980).
- *Discovering the Vernacular Landscape* (1984).
- *A Sense of Place, A Sense of Time* (1994).

## Core Contributions

### The Vernacular Landscape

Jackson insisted that the ordinary, undesigned, economically motivated landscape — strip malls, trailer parks, gas stations, dirt roads, farm fields — deserved the same analytical attention as designed parks and monuments. The vernacular landscape is a record of social priorities, mobility patterns, and temporal relationships with land.

This argument is the cultural-geography complement to [[Yi-Fu Tuan]]'s phenomenological argument: where Tuan explains how *experience* converts space into place, Jackson reads the landscape as a *text* of the values and practices that have shaped it. Both are fundamentally anti-formalist: what matters is meaning, not design.

### Roads and American Space

Jackson was fascinated by the road as a shaping force of American landscape and culture. The American preference for movement over settlement — space over place, in Tuan's terms — is expressed in road culture: the drive-in, the roadside strip, the motel, the mobile home. See [[openness-and-freedom-tuan]].

The road is a linear form that embodies route knowledge ([[route-vs-survey-knowledge]]): it encodes a sequence of movements, a direction of travel, a set of landmarks. It is place in process of formation.

### The Necessity for Ruins

Jackson's essay on ruins makes a distinctly temporal argument: places require destruction and renewal to remain alive. The deliberate creation of ruins — the demolition that precedes reconstruction — is what creates the new beginnings that give places their vitality. A landscape without ruins is a landscape without time; it has become museum.

This resonates with Tuan's [[place-as-pause]]: place is not permanent stasis but a pause in movement. The ruin marks where the pause ended.

### Democratic Landscape

Jackson was committed to the democratic dimension of the American landscape: the vernacular landscape is *made by ordinary people* through ordinary economic decisions, not by designers or planners. The suburb, the trailer park, and the shopping center are as authentically American as the national park or the town square.

This democratizing impulse has an affinity with Tuan's attention to everyday spatial experience — his phenomenology is equally interested in the child's experience of a flight of stairs as in the sacred geography of ancient cosmologies.

## Relation to Tuan

Jackson and Tuan were contemporaries and colleagues in the project of humanizing geography. Both published in *Landscape* magazine's orbit; Tuan drew on the tradition Jackson established. Where Jackson reads landscapes as cultural texts, Tuan reads them as experiential fields. Together they define the humanist geography of the 1960s–1980s.
