# Vaclav Smil

## Overview

Vaclav Smil (b. 1943, Plzeň) is a Czech-Canadian environmental scientist and distinguished professor emeritus at the University of Manitoba, where he taught from 1972 until his retirement. He is the author of more than forty books on energy, food, materials, technology, the environment, and population — a corpus assembled with unusual quantitative rigour and a refusal to write either techno-optimist or declinist polemic. Bill Gates has cited him repeatedly as one of his most important influences and reads everything Smil writes.

## Method

Smil's signature is the systematic quantification of physical throughputs at civilisational scale: how many calories, joules, tonnes of cement, kilograms of nitrogen, hectares of cropland a society actually moves through itself. He works from primary statistical sources (FAO, IEA, national accounts, historical reconstructions) and is sceptical of any claim — about decarbonisation, growth, AI, or collapse — that does not survive contact with the numbers.

He is famously uninterested in narrative. His books are dense with tables and unit conversions and avoid the redemption-arc structure typical of popular non-fiction. The implicit argument is that the scale and inertia of material systems is the binding constraint that most public discussion either ignores or magic-thinks past.

## Key Findings on Energy History

Smil's historical reconstructions of per-capita energy use are the standard source for the long-run curve:

- **Palaeolithic foragers**: ~2,000 kcal/day, all of it food.
- **Early agriculturalists**: ~12,000 kcal/day with fire and draft animals.
- **Pre-industrial societies (e.g. eighteenth-century England)**: ~50,000–60,000 kcal/day with biomass and water/wind.
- **Industrial societies**: an order-of-magnitude jump with coal.
- **Modern United States**: ~230,000 kcal/day per capita across all uses.

Each step up corresponds to a new prime mover (fire, agriculture, animal traction, water and wind, fossil fuels) creating surplus that builds infrastructure. Smil also tracks the calorie-return-on-labour shift: pre-industrial agriculture as a net source (~10:1 food-out per labour-in), modern agriculture as a net sink (~13:1 fossil-fuel-in per food-out).

Adjacent contributions:

- **Nitrogen** — *Enriching the Earth* (2001): the Haber-Bosch process and the dependence of half the world's protein on synthetic fertiliser.
- **Materials** — *Making the Modern World* (2013) and *How the World Really Works* (2022): the "four pillars of modern civilisation" (cement, steel, plastics, ammonia) and their stubborn resistance to rapid decarbonisation.
- **Innovation pace** — *Growth* (2019): why technological transitions take longer than enthusiasts predict.

## Influence

Smil's data appears throughout serious work on energy history and civilisational complexity. In ["The Lightening of Intent"](https://aneeshsathe.com/) his per-capita energy figures and his EROI accounting underpin the section on the system burning hotter ([[machines-as-energy-regimes]], [[eroi-thresholds]]). He is in conversation, methodologically, with [[eric-chaisson]] (cosmic-evolution energy-rate-density) and Earl Cook (1971's per-capita energy curve), although Smil is more empirically conservative than either.
