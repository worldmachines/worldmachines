# Ian Morris

Ian Morris is a British classical historian, archaeologist, and Willard Professor of Classics at Stanford University. His work bridges ancient history and macrohistorical comparison, using quantitative methods to address questions traditionally handled through qualitative narrative.

## Key Works

**Why the West Rules — For Now** (2010) asks why Western Eurasia rather than East Asia came to dominate the modern world, rejecting both cultural-essentialist and pure-accident explanations in favor of a geography-and-ecology framework. Morris argues that similar social development trajectories in East and West were shaped by different geographic constraints, producing different timings of key breakthroughs.

**The Measure of Civilization** (2013) provides the methodological backbone: the [[social-development-index]], a composite quantitative measure tracking energy capture, organization (city size), information technology, and war-making capability across 15,000 years. The index reveals lead-changes between East and West — the West led until the 6th century, the East led until the 18th, and the West regained the lead thereafter.

## Significance

Morris's contribution to the [[world-machines]] framework and comparative civilizational history is primarily methodological: he demonstrates that long-run historical comparison can be made quantitative without being reductive. The Social Development Index connects to [[energy-rate-density]] (Chaisson's universal complexity metric) — both treat energy flow as the master variable, though at different scales.
