# Mahatma Gandhi

**Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi** (1869–1948), known as the Mahatma ("Great Soul"), was the preeminent leader of the Indian independence movement. He developed and practiced nonviolent civil disobedience as a political strategy, leading campaigns that eventually resulted in India's independence from Britain in 1947.

## Role in the Intellectual/Revolutionary Typology

In the [[Cloister Web]] essay's historical typology of [[Intellectual vs. Revolutionary]] dyads, Gandhi is singled out as the **exceptional case**: he uniquely embodied both roles simultaneously.

Where [[Rabindranath Tagore]] and [[Sri Aurobindo]] were the intellectual framework-builders and Gandhi was expected to be the mobilizer, Gandhi in practice both articulated philosophical frameworks (on nonviolence, *swaraj*, civil disobedience) *and* directly mobilized mass action (the Salt March, the Non-Cooperation Movement).

This makes Gandhi a key data point for the claim that the intellectual/revolutionary distinction is contingent rather than necessary — and thus that LLMs might make the split role more available to others.

## Intellectual Contributions

- Theory and practice of *satyagraha* (truth-force/nonviolent resistance)
- *Hind Swaraj* (1909) — critique of modern civilization and argument for self-rule
- *An Autobiography* (1927) — "The Story of My Experiments with Truth"
