# Thomas Nagel

## Biographical

Thomas Nagel is an American philosopher, emeritus professor at NYU, working in philosophy of mind, ethics, and political philosophy. Best known for the 1974 paper "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?"

## Key Work Referenced

### "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" (1974)

**Core argument**: There are facts about subjective experience — what it is *like* to be something — that cannot be captured by any objective, third-person physical description. Even a complete neuroscience of bat sonar would not tell you what it is like to experience the world as a bat.

This establishes the "hard problem" of consciousness: the explanatory gap between physical description and first-person phenomenal experience.

**The bat specifically**: Bats use echolocation — a sensory modality so different from human vision that we cannot even form a concept of what bat experience is like from the inside.

## Sathe's Use

In [[Chatbots, Bats & Broken Oracles]], Nagel's bat serves as an illustration of **Socratic inquiry on an impossible question**:
- The question "What is it like to be a bat?" cannot be solved with current technology.
- Yet engaging with it is valuable: clicking in a dark room, hanging upside down, generate crude approaches that create *surface area for criticism and further inquiry*.
- Even a crude method is better than nothing if the goal is inquiry.

The bat example also grounds the claim about why LLMs (not bats) can serve as Socratic partners: bats lack sufficient shared worldview; LLMs trained on human knowledge do not.

## Connection to Sathe's Broader Framework

The bat paper is implicitly a case of a **question** (not a **problem** in Callard's sense):
- No established method for solving it.
- Approaches generate understanding without dissolving the question.
- The question reveals something about the limits of third-person description of first-person experience.

See: [[Problems vs. Questions]], [[Socratic Thinking]]

## Potential Tension

Sathe uses Nagel's example to argue LLMs are viable Socratic partners because they have "enough humanness" (trained on human language). But Nagel's argument implies that *linguistic* humanness may not be sufficient: what matters is first-person experience, which LLMs plausibly lack.

This tension — shared language vs. shared experience as the condition for genuine Socratic exchange — is not resolved in the essays.
