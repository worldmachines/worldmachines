# Ilya Prigogine

**1917–2003.** Belgian-Russian physical chemist. Nobel Prize in Chemistry, 1977, "for his contributions to non-equilibrium thermodynamics, particularly the theory of dissipative structures." Major works: *From Being to Becoming* (1980), *Order Out of Chaos* (with Isabelle Stengers, 1984), *The End of Certainty* (1997).

## Core Contributions

**Dissipative structures**: Far-from-equilibrium open systems that maintain complex, organized states by continuously dissipating energy. Classic examples: Bénard convection cells (organized rolls in heated fluid), the Belousov-Zhabotinsky reaction (chemical oscillator), living organisms. These structures appear to violate the Second Law locally (they are highly organized, low entropy) while actually satisfying it globally (they export entropy to their environment). See [[far-from-equilibrium-systems]].

**Non-equilibrium thermodynamics**: Extension of thermodynamics beyond near-equilibrium regimes where linear approximations hold. Prigogine showed that far from equilibrium, systems can undergo spontaneous symmetry-breaking, generating new organized structures at *bifurcation points* where small fluctuations determine which of multiple possible structures emerges.

**Irreversibility and the arrow of time**: Prigogine argued that irreversibility is a fundamental feature of reality, not an approximation. Classical mechanics is time-reversible; non-equilibrium thermodynamics reveals a genuine arrow of time embedded in physical law. This resolves the apparent paradox between the time-symmetrical laws of mechanics and the directional character of macroscopic processes (including life and evolution).

**The end of certainty**: Prigogine's late work extended these ideas to argue that determinism is an idealization — not because of quantum uncertainty, but because of the instability and sensitivity to initial conditions that characterizes far-from-equilibrium systems. The world is fundamentally a world of probabilities, becoming, and irreversible time.

## Philosophical Significance

Prigogine and Stengers argued in *Order Out of Chaos* that the new thermodynamics represents a fundamental shift in the scientific worldview: from a world of being (stable, deterministic, reversible) to a world of *becoming* (unstable, probabilistic, irreversible). This aligns with process philosophy (Bergson, Whitehead) and is explicitly welcomed by Prigogine.

The key philosophical lesson: complexity, organization, and life are not anomalies that resist physical explanation but are themselves consequences of physical law — specifically, the non-linear dynamics of far-from-equilibrium open systems.

## Relation to Bryant

Bryant references Peirce on entropy (1980: 78–106) rather than Prigogine directly in Chapter 4, but Prigogine's dissipative structures framework is the physical underpinning for Bryant's entropy/negentropy account. When Bryant argues that machines must draw energy flows from their environment to maintain their organization, he is describing the thermodynamic situation of dissipative structures. The city example — requiring ongoing flows of food, fuel, and materials to sustain its low-entropy social organization — is a sociological dissipative structure.
