# Michel de Certeau (1925–1986)

French Jesuit historian, social theorist, philosopher of religion. His most-cited work for cultural studies is *The Practice of Everyday Life* (1980; English 1984), in which the famous distinction between **strategies** (the moves of the powerful within their proper place) and **tactics** (the on-the-wing moves of the weak who lack a proper place) is developed.

## Core Distinction

> "A strategy assumes a place that can be circumscribed as proper (*propre*) . . . The 'proper' is a victory of space over time. On the contrary, because it does not have a place, a tactic depends on time — it is always on the watch for opportunities that must be seized 'on the wing'." (de Certeau 1984: xix, quoted in Massey 2005: 45)

Strategies are spatial (they own and stabilise a place); tactics are temporal (they exploit moments of opportunity). The distinction underwrites a generation of cultural studies work on resistance, the everyday, and the agency of the dominated.

## Massey's Critique

[[doreen-massey]]'s Part Two argument against de Certeau (chapters 2–3 of *For Space*, 2005) makes three points:

### 1. The strategy/tactic binary instates a power/resistance dichotomy

The distinction "overestimate[s] the coherence of 'the powerful' and the seamlessness with which 'order' is produced [and] reduces (while trying to do the opposite) the potential power of 'the weak'" (Massey 2005: 45).

### 2. It re-installs the space/time binary it tries to escape

By labelling strategies "spatial" and tactics "temporal," de Certeau ironically traps space in the role of static structure and time in the role of mobile resistance — the very dichotomy that structuralism had bequeathed and that de Certeau is otherwise trying to disrupt. The result: "His [de Certeau's] move from summit to street involves a troubling reinscription of a theory/practice opposition — semantically projected as 'high' versus 'low' . . . 'static' versus 'dynamic' . . . 'seeing' versus 'doing'" (Meaghan Morris 1992a, quoted 46). Massey adds: and *spatial* versus *temporal*.

### 3. Space-as-representation again

In de Certeau's framing, "scientific writing ceaselessly reduces time, that fugitive element, to the normality of an observable and readable system" (de Certeau 1984: 89, quoted Massey 2005: 26). Massey identifies this as another version of the space-as-representation conflation. See [[space-as-representation-critique]].

## Why de Certeau matters anyway

Massey grants that de Certeau is "widely cited in the literature on spatiality, particularly urban spatiality" (45) and that the framework is generative for thinking about resistance and the everyday. Her objection is conceptual, not dismissive: the spatial side of the framework deserves the same liberation that the tactical side received.
