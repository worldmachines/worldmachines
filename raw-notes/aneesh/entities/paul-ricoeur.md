# Paul Ricoeur (1913–2005)

**Field**: Hermeneutics, phenomenology, philosophy of narrative
**Nationality**: French
**Key works**: *Freedom and Nature* (1950/1966); *Symbolism of Evil* (1960/1967); *Time and Narrative* (3 vols., 1983–85); *Oneself as Another* (1990/1992)

## Role in Tuan's *Space and Place*

Tuan cites Ricoeur in Ch. 2 on the nature of feeling. The passage (Tuan 2002, p. 9):

> "Feeling is . . . without doubt intentional: it is a feeling of 'something' — the lovable, the hateful, [for instance]. But it is a very strange intentionality which on the one hand designates qualities felt *on* things, *on* persons, *on* the world, and on the other hand manifests and reveals the way in which the self is inwardly affected."

This is Ricoeur's account of feeling as **double-directed**: outward (feeling reveals qualities of the world — this place is welcoming, that landscape is melancholy) and inward (feeling reveals how the self stands affected). Feeling is not merely subjective projection; it has genuine intentionality, tracking real qualities. But it is also not purely objective description; it simultaneously discloses the self's state.

## Why This Matters for Place

Tuan uses this to explain how places can be felt as havens, threats, sacred zones, or home. The feeling is not simply "in the subject" (projected onto a neutral location) nor "in the object" (a measurable property of the space). It is a relational quality that emerges from the encounter — which is why two people can feel the same location very differently, yet neither is simply wrong.

This Ricoeurian frame also explains why the experiential approach to place cannot be reduced to either subjective psychology or objective measurement. Place-feeling is a third thing: a real relational quality accessible only through experience.

## Ricoeur's Broader Contribution

Ricoeur's hermeneutics is concerned throughout with the mediation of self and world through symbols, narratives, and texts. His account of narrative identity — we are the stories we tell about ourselves — is relevant to [[symbolization-and-place]]: the places that matter most are the ones embedded in narrative, not just in coordinates. Jerusalem is a place partly because of the stories told about it.
