# Byung-Chul Han

**Born**: 1959, Seoul, South Korea
**Works at**: Universität der Künste Berlin
**Key Works**: *The Burnout Society* (2010), *The Transparency Society* (2012), *In the Swarm* (2013), *The Crisis of Narration* (2024)
**Intellectual tradition**: Continental philosophy; influenced by Heidegger, Nietzsche, Benjamin

## Intellectual Character

Han writes short, dense diagnostic essays. He identifies pathologies of contemporary life — the compulsion to optimize, the collapse of privacy into transparency, the erosion of the "other" — without offering prescriptions. His books are widely read but criticized for being overly pessimistic and lacking solutions.

## Key Concepts

- **Burnout Society**: the achievement society demands self-optimization; subjects exploit themselves freely.
- **Transparency Society**: radical transparency destroys trust, intimacy, and ritual.
- **Narrative crisis**: storytelling has been colonized by commerce — [[Storyselling vs. Storytelling]].

## *The Crisis of Narration* (2024)

The most relevant work in this corpus. Core claim:
- Stories historically created shared reality and transmitted culture.
- Under capitalism and social media, narrative has become storyselling: optimized to manufacture consumers.
- We have lost genuine storytelling capacity.
- Han offers no solution (noted critically in [[Looking Forward to Montaigne]]).

## Criticisms

- Diagnostic without prescriptive — he names the disease but not the cure.
- Arguably nostalgic for a past that may not have existed as cleanly as he imagines.
- Short books read more as extended essays than sustained arguments — evidence is often impressionistic.

## Relation to Montaigne

In [[Looking Forward to Montaigne]], the author reads Han as naming a problem that [[Michel de Montaigne]] inadvertently solves: the personal, frank, meandering essay is structurally resistant to storyselling. The juxtaposition produces the tentative thesis that Montaigne-style writing is the antidote Han doesn't offer.
