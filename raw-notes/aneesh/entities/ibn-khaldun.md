# Ibn Khaldun (1332-1406)

## Standard Reading vs. Rao's Reading

**Standard reading**: Ibn Khaldun as proto-sociologist, pioneer of social science, ahead of his time — the *Muqaddimah* as inaugurating a new science of society.

**Rao's reading** (from *Modernity Machine III*): "Ibn Khaldun: An Intellectual Biography should not be read as the story of a proto-sociologist ahead of his time. That role is largely retrofitted by modern interpreters."

In his own context, Ibn Khaldun appears more plausibly as "a kind of depressed Arab Petrarch: a brilliant chronicler of decline and defender of tradition, lamenting the absence of an Islamic renaissance rather than inaugurating one."

## The Diagnostic Reading

His cyclical theory of dynasties (*asabiyyah* — group cohesion — rising, peaking, and declining) "does not launch a new science of society; it records the exhaustion of an old civilizational form."

His importance in Rao's framework is **diagnostic, not genealogical**: "He documents a world failing to enter the Modernity Machine at all, despite being in possession of many of the necessary components."

This is a pointed claim: the Islamic world of the 14th century had the raw materials for modernity (cities, trade networks, sophisticated legal systems, philosophy, mathematics) but something prevented assembly. Ibn Khaldun's cyclical pessimism is *evidence of that failure*, not a contribution to its solution.

## Intellectual Lineage

- Compare [[Giordano Bruno]]: another figure retrofitted by modernity as an early-modern prophet, but actually a *terminal medieval outlier*
- Compare [[Petrarch]]: the Petrarch parallel is apt — Petrarch also looked back at a classical golden age with longing, constructing the "dark ages" concept in the process
- In Rao's Spinoza essay: Adam Smith's approach to economics was "ontologically different from (say) the approach of Ibn Khaldun" — Ibn Khaldun worked within an existing cosmological ontology; Smith worked from noisy empirical reality data

## What's Missing from Rao's Account

- No engagement with Marshall Hodgson's account of Islamic civilization's structural constraints
- The reason *why* the Islamic world possessed the components but failed to assemble them is left unexplained in Rao's framework — he gestures at "delayed arrival and diffusion of print" as a factor in the Indian case, but the Islamic case is left open
- The *Muqaddimah*'s sociology of knowledge and concept of *asabiyyah* are more sophisticated than a simple "cyclical history" framing suggests

## Relevance to Royalty and Administration

Ibn Khaldun's cyclical theory directly informs Sathe's essay on [[royalty-administration-and-antimemetics|Royalty, Administration, and Antimemetics]]. The *asabiyyah* cycle — desert cohesion → state-building → luxury → diffusion → collapse — maps onto the observation that antimemetic bureaucracies are a late-cycle phenomenon: the machinery outlasting the original driving force that built the state.

Robert Irwin's *Ibn Khaldun: An Intellectual Biography* is cited as a source in Sathe's essay.
