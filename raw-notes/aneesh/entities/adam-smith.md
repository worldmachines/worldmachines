# Adam Smith (1723-1790)

## Biographical Context

- The "professor" in *The Infidel and the Professor* — held an institutional position while remaining an undeclared agnostic
- Close friend and intellectual collaborator of [[david-hume]]
- Key figure of the Scottish Enlightenment, along with Hume
- More diplomatically circumspect than Hume — took religion and theology out of his intellectual work quietly rather than combatively

## Rao's Reading

Smith is best understood as an intellectual descendant of [[baruch-spinoza]], with [[gottfried-wilhelm-leibniz]] (as philosopher) already faded into irrelevance by his time. He represents "an equally radical continuation of Spinoza's thought in an entire new reality domain that was just beginning to acquire modern contours — economics."

His *approach* to economic phenomena was "ontologically different from (say) the approach of Ibn Khaldun, whose life and ideas we encountered in last year's book club. Or even the approach of the Venetian merchants who inaugurated the reality-scope expansion that eventually required an Adam Smith to make sense of."

## The Invisible Hand as Empirical Stance

The "invisible hand" is not primarily a claim about markets or efficiency — it is an epistemological stance. The discipline of economics that Smith inaugurated was "fundamentally *about* noisy, messy reality data, with only weak edifices of emergent constitutive laws built on top. The opposite of divine design and direction."

This is the Spinozan move applied to economic life: don't look for divine design or moral order in economic outcomes; look at what actually happens and build weak heuristics from the data.

> "A unified Hume-Smith theory of reality would be: Shit happens, but it's not entirely unpredictable and disorderly. There are laws, but they're just handy, contingent heuristics, not 'reality' itself."

## The Hume-Smith Collaboration Pattern

Where Spinoza and Leibniz were in a partially adversarial relationship, Hume and Smith were "collaborating allies who influenced each other in their campaigns on different parts of the frontier of expanding reality." This is a different mode of participating in the [[Argument of Progress]] — collaborative frontier expansion rather than adversarial divergence from a common starting point.

Both were "primarily oriented towards reality rather than theology, compared to their predecessors." Both paid attention to moral sentiments — "rugging religious or institutional understandings of morality" — continuing Spinoza's project to separate secular and religious philosophy.

## The Darwin Connection

The Hume-Smith assault on traditional moral and natural philosophy represents "the beginnings of the intellectual iconoclasm that reach its peak with Darwin." Darwin's *real* accomplishment was not disproving literalist religion (Hume had done that effectively) but "assaulting the *secular* philosophical foundations of the modernity machine" — making divergence and variety load-bearing features of reality itself.
