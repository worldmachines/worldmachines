# Novalis

Novalis (Friedrich von Hardenberg, 1772-1801) was a German Romantic poet and philosopher, a core member of [[The Jena Circle]]. His early death at 28 cut short one of the most original philosophical projects of the Romantic era.

## Key Works

- *Hymns to the Night* (1800)
- *The Allgemeine Brouillon* (Universal Notebook, 1798-99)
- *Heinrich von Ofterdingen* (unfinished novel)

## Critique of Enlightenment Rationalism

Novalis argued that Enlightenment philosophers "cling to definitions, neat categorizations, and the rules of logic" and have "adopted a mechanistic attitude towards thinking." In *Hymns to the Night*: "The gods disappeared with their following — nature stood forlorn and lifeless. Arid count and strict measure bound her with iron chains."

But Novalis did not reject system entirely. He quoted [[Friedrich Schlegel]]'s warning: "Just as a fully systematic philosophy might be an illusion, a completely unsystematic philosophy would instantly kill off every intellectual ambition." Philosophy should be "open-ended; it should develop in close interaction with the natural sciences as well as the humanities."

## The Allgemeine Brouillon and Encyclopedistics

The *Allgemeine Brouillon* (1798-99) is Novalis's largest theoretical work: over 1,100 notebook entries constituting a "Romantic Encyclopaedia" or "Scientific Bible." It covers philosophy, poetry, natural sciences, fine arts, mathematics, mineralogy, history, and religion.

Crucially, this was *not* the Enlightenment encyclopedic project of reducing knowledge to a single rational framework. Instead, it preserved disciplinary distinctiveness while revealing underlying methodological parallels through what Novalis called "the magic wand of analogy" — using musical terminology to illuminate physiology, chemical language to describe religion, mathematical concepts to model non-mathematical contexts.

Key formulations:
- "All good researchers...proceed like Copernicus — they turn the data and methods around" (517)
- "Philosophy is really homesickness — the desire to be everywhere at home" (857)
- "Idealism is nothing but genuine empiricism" (402)
- "Philologizing is the true scholarly occupation. It corresponds to experimenting" (724)
- Philosophy as "a science of science" (886) that "cannot bake bread — however, it can provide us with God, freedom and immortality"

His encyclopedistics was a method of orientation, not a system of answers — "an impetus to revision and improvement" rather than conclusive knowledge.

## The Central Distinction: Homogenization vs. Free Interconnection

Novalis's most important formulation for contemporary debates:

> "Before abstraction everything is one...after abstraction everything is unified again, but this unification is a free interconnection of independent, self-determined beings."

This distinguishes between **homogenization** (forced unity that destroys difference) and **integration** (free interconnection that preserves autonomy). The emphasis on "self-determined" places the onus on the participants in the network, not just the network's architecture.

## Symphilosophie

Novalis theorized the Jena circle's collaborative practice: "Every person who consists of persons is a person to a higher power, a person squared." See [[Symphilosophie]].
