# David Harvey

**Born**: 1935
**Field**: Human geography, Marxist political economy, urban theory
**Positions**: Johns Hopkins University, CUNY Graduate Center
**Key works**: *Social Justice and the City* (1973); *The Condition of Postmodernity* (1989); *Monument and Myth* (1979, article in *Annals of the AAG*)

## Relevance to Language and Place

Tuan cites Harvey's 1979 article "Monument and Myth" (*Annals of the AAG* 69: 362–81) as an example of a geographer using language to transform the perceived character of a place — specifically, to *undermine* the innocent eye's view of Sacré-Coeur in Paris.

Sacré-Coeur is "a white, shining Byzantine-Romanesque basilica — a tourist if not a religious attraction of considerable drawing power." Harvey's excavations into its history — its origins in the reaction to the Paris Commune, its financing, its role in French political Catholicism — "give[s] a more sombre tone" to the building (Tuan 1991, p. 693). After reading Harvey, a visitor cannot easily experience Sacré-Coeur as merely a beautiful or spiritually moving structure; the scholarly language has infiltrated the perceptual field.

This makes one "wonder whether, to anyone who has read the article, it can still be the same edifice" (p. 693). The building is physically unchanged; its perceived character is altered by scholarly narrative.

## The Geographer as Place-Transformer

Harvey illustrates the second of Tuan's two kinds of geographer-effects: not the practical effect (communicating procedures to enable collective action) but the *perceptual* effect — words drawing attention to aspects of reality hitherto invisible. Harvey's article makes visible the political and historical forces embedded in Sacré-Coeur's gleaming white stone, forces that the tourist's innocent eye screens out.

This is also an illustration of the *destructive* potential of language: Harvey's eloquence diminishes a place's reputation (at least in certain registers of appreciation), just as malicious gossip can diminish a person's. Tuan does not present this as straightforwardly problematic — there may be a moral obligation to expose the uncomfortable history — but it demonstrates that language's place-making power is morally ambiguous.

## Harvey in Broader Context

Beyond this specific citation, Harvey is one of the central figures in twentieth-century human geography. His introduction of Marxist political economy into geographical analysis (via *Social Justice and the City*) transformed the discipline's understanding of how capital shapes urban space. His later work on postmodernity and time-space compression extended this framework to cultural and architectural analysis. He represents the "sociolinguistic" approach to language and place that Tuan describes — concerned with language as power, epistemology, and social hierarchy — though Harvey's language-focus is usually instrumental rather than constitutive in Tuan's sense.

## Relation to Massey

Massey's *Space, Place and Gender* (1994) contains a sustained feminist critique of Harvey's *The Condition of Postmodernity* (1989). Harvey's analysis of time-space compression — the acceleration and spatial restructuring of capitalism in the postmodern period — is, Massey argues, blind to its own power-geometry position. Harvey describes the disorienting mobility of postmodern capital as if it were universally experienced, when in fact "different social groups, and different individuals, are placed in very different ways in relation to these flows and interconnections" (Massey 1994: 149). The jet-setter's experience of time-space compression is not the refugee's experience of it.

This is the origin of Massey's concept of [[power-geometry]]: a corrective to Harvey's tendency to describe spatial change from an implicitly universalist (and gendered, racially unmarked) subject-position.

Despite this critique, Massey's and Harvey's projects are broadly allied in their Marxist-inflected political-economic geography, their insistence on the social production of space, and their opposition to political-philosophical treatments of space as neutral or given.

## Sathe's Use in "We Need Homes in the Delta Quadrant" (2025)

In §III, Sathe deploys Harvey's analysis of uneven spatial production to argue that place-making is never politically neutral — capital concentrates selectively, producing certain places for flourishing and sacrificing others. For Sathe, Harvey provides the macro-economic structural account of a violence that Mbembe addresses at the racial-political extreme: together they show that placelessness is produced by identifiable mechanisms, not by accident or insufficiency of effort. Harvey's argument motivates the essay's turn to custodianship as a counter-ethic: if place is systematically commodified by capital, then ethical place-making must deliberately resist commodification, attending to use-value rather than exchange-value.
