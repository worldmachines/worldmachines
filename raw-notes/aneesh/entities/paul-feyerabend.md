# Paul Feyerabend (1924–1994)

Austrian-born philosopher of science, most associated with his 1975 book *Against Method*, which argued that there is no single scientific method that accounts for all productive scientific discovery — hence his motto: "anything goes."

## Core Argument

Feyerabend examined the history of science and found that every methodological principle invoked as necessary for good science had been violated at key moments of genuine discovery. Galileo, for instance, relied on rhetoric, aesthetics, and psychological persuasion as much as on evidence to advance heliocentrism. The lesson Feyerabend drew: the best scientists are epistemological opportunists, not methodological purists.

This is not a relativist claim that all views are equally valid — it is a claim that no rule about *how* to pursue knowledge is universally productive, and that the creative scientist must maintain methodological flexibility.

## Relevance to the Curiosity Typology

Sathe (see [[Dancing on the Shoulders of Giants]]) invokes Feyerabend's "anything goes" as the epistemological correlate of high forward flow — the [[Hunter vs. Dancer Curiosity|dancer style]] of intellectual exploration. The dancer moves across conceptual distance without predetermined method; Feyerabend validates this as not merely acceptable but historically necessary for breakthrough discovery.

The hunter style, by contrast, corresponds to the methodological orthodoxy Feyerabend opposed: tight, goal-directed, rule-following inquiry that is efficient within a paradigm but misses cross-paradigm connections.

## Tensions and Critiques

- The "anything goes" slogan has been widely misread as advocating intellectual chaos. Feyerabend's actual position is more subtle: *ex post*, what worked was always something different from what the ruling methodology prescribed.
- Does not address how to distinguish productive methodological flexibility from mere inconsistency.
- The argument is primarily historiographical — whether it translates into prescriptive guidance for practitioners is unclear.
