# Jakob von Uexküll

Estonian-German biologist (1864–1944). Founder of biosemiotics and a central figure in the philosophy of biology. Uexküll's key contribution: each organism inhabits its own *Umwelt* (surrounding world) — a subjective universe defined by the organism's perceptual apparatus and action possibilities. The tick, the sea urchin, and the human all inhabit the same physical world but entirely different phenomenal worlds.

## The Umwelt Concept

The Umwelt is not the objective environment but the organism's *operative world* — the subset of the physical environment that the organism can perceive and act upon. Uexküll's famous example: the tick is sensitive only to light (for position), warmth (for finding a host), and butyric acid (for initiating feeding). Everything else in the world is simply absent to the tick. The tick's Umwelt is strikingly impoverished compared to human perception, yet perfectly adequate for tick survival.

Key text: *A Foray into the Worlds of Animals and Humans* (1934/2010), cited in Bryant's *Onto-Cartography* p. 62.

## Relation to Bryant

[[levi-bryant]] treats Uexküll's ethology as a direct precursor to [[alien-phenomenology]]. Uexküll shows *how* to practice second-order observation — asking what the world is like for another organism — and provides techniques for investigating the perceptual apparatus and action possibilities of non-human animals.

Bryant's modification: Uexküll's ethology is *vitalist* — it restricts Umwelt analysis to living organisms. Bryant's post-vitalistic alien phenomenology extends the same investigation to all machines: rocks, cameras, institutions, mathematical equations. As Bryant writes: "alien phenomenology is 'post-vitalistic' ... not an ethology that *excludes* the living, just as post-humanism does not exclude the human. Rather, alien phenomenology holds that we can also engage in second-order observations of how non-living, non-human machines such as carbon atoms, cameras, computers, rocks, etc., are structurally open and operationally closed to the world about them." (*Onto-Cartography*, p. 64)

## Influence

Uexküll was influential on Heidegger (who discusses the tick in *The Fundamental Concepts of Metaphysics* as an example of an organism's "poverty in world"), on Deleuze and Guattari (who use the tick-horse-grass example in *A Thousand Plateaus* to illustrate assemblages), and on biosemiotics more broadly (Jesper Hoffmeyer, Kalevi Kull).
