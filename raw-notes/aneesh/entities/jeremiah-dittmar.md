# Jeremiah Dittmar

## Overview

Jeremiah Dittmar is an American economic historian who has held positions at the London School of Economics (Centre for Economic Performance) and American University. He works primarily on the economic and informational consequences of media technologies in early modern Europe, with the printing press as his central case. His method combines hand-collected city-level data, econometric identification strategies adapted from labour and development economics, and close attention to the mechanics of the technology itself.

## Key Findings on Print Economics

Dittmar's most-cited paper, "Information Technology and Economic Change: The Impact of the Printing Press" (*Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 2011), establishes that European cities that adopted print between 1450 and 1500 grew measurably faster than otherwise comparable non-adopting cities over the following century. The identification strategy uses distance from Mainz (Gutenberg's home) as an instrument for early adoption, exploiting the geography of how the technology diffused.

Subsequent work (with co-authors and in working papers through the 2010s and 2020s) extends the empirical picture:

- **Book prices fell roughly 2.4 percent per year** for over a century after the introduction of moveable type, an unprecedented sustained decline for a knowledge good.
- **Each additional printer in a city** lowered local book prices by roughly another **quarter** (~25%), evidence of competitive market structure rather than monopoly rents.
- **Doubling time for European book production** collapsed from approximately **104 years before 1450** to about **43 years after** — Gutenberg accelerated not just output but the *rate of change* of output.
- City-level human-capital effects: print adoption is associated with the rise of merchant manuals, business mathematics, and a reorganisation of commercial practice.

These figures are the empirical backbone of the printing-press section of ["The Lightening of Intent"](https://aneeshsathe.com/) and of related accounts in the [[modernity-machine]] literature.

## Method

Dittmar's contribution is to treat the printing press as an *information technology* in the modern econometric sense — a general-purpose technology whose effects can be identified by comparing adopters to non-adopters with appropriate controls and instruments — rather than as a cultural-historical event whose effects are inferred narratively. The work sits in the lineage of Joel Mokyr (knowledge and growth) and Acemoglu/Robinson (institutions and growth) but is unusual in the granularity of its city-level data and its willingness to put hard numbers on a phenomenon usually discussed qualitatively.

## Influence

Dittmar's print-economics figures have become the standard quantitative reference point for arguments about general-purpose information technologies and growth, including parallels to the internet and to AI. They are routinely cited in:

- AI policy and macroeconomic-impact papers (Brynjolfsson and others).
- Long-run growth and unified-growth-theory work.
- Popular accounts of the printing press as a precedent for digital media transitions.
