# Joseph de Maistre (1753–1821)

**Born**: Chambéry, Savoy, 1753
**Died**: Turin, 1821
**Key works**: *Considérations sur la France* (1796); *Du Pape* (1819); *Les Soirées de Saint-Pétersbourg* (posthumous 1821); *Étude sur la souveraineté* (posthumous)

## Intellectual Profile

Maistre was a Catholic Savoyard aristocrat who became the most formidable intellectual opponent of the French Revolution and the Enlightenment tradition that produced it. Unlike Vico or Herder, Maistre is not an empathetic pluralist — he is a ferocious authoritarian who uses the counter-Enlightenment's insights about human irrationality to justify absolute power. [[Isaiah Berlin]] regards him as "one of the darkest of the reactionary forms of the fight against the Enlightenment, as well as one of the most interesting and influential."

## Key Ideas

### Radical Anti-Optimism about Human Nature

The Enlightenment conception of man as naturally disposed to benevolence, co-operation, and peace is, for Maistre, "shallow and false." History and zoology are the most reliable guides to human nature — and they reveal "a field of unceasing slaughter." Men are by nature aggressive and destructive; they rebel over trifles but go obediently to war. When the destructive instinct is evoked, men feel exalted and fulfilled. Rousseau's declaration that man is born free is "the opposite of the truth."

### The Executioner as Social Foundation

In a striking image that Berlin dwells on: "all social order in the end rests upon one man, the executioner." Nobody wishes to associate with this hideous figure — yet so long as men are weak, sinful, unable to control their passions, all peace and all society depend on him. The notion that reason is sufficient to control the passions is ridiculous. Repression, censorship, absolute sovereignty, and judgements from which there is no appeal are the only methods of governing human beings.

### Irrationality as Social Necessity

Reason is the thinnest of walls against violent emotion — no permanent structure can be built on it. Irrationality, far from being an obstacle, has historically led to peace, security, and strength. Rational institutions — republics, elective monarchies, democracies — collapse soonest; authoritarian Churches and hereditary monarchies persist. The Catholic Church must never explain or justify itself rationally, for what one man can demonstrate another can refute.

### Organic Nationalism and Race

"Nations are born and die like individuals; they have a common soul, especially visible in their language." Nations are organic wholes that should endeavour to remain racially pure. His ally Bonald similarly regretted that the French nation had abandoned its ideal of racial purity. This mystical organicism, which transcends all forms of discursive reasoning, is the intellectual ancestor of 20th-century fascism.

### *Bâtonocratie*

Maistre's coinage: rule by the stick. Military dictatorship built by individual human hands is "brutal force without spiritual power" — only time, only history, can create authority men can truly worship and obey. Napoleon, therefore, predicts his own end.

### The Best Government

The best government the world has ever known was that of the Romans — "they were too wise to be scientists themselves." Dark instincts govern men and societies; only elites who understand this can maintain order. Excessive secular education makes people over-critical and discontented — too much reason destroys social cohesion.

## Berlin's Assessment

Berlin takes Maistre seriously as a diagnostician: his account of human irrationality and the limits of reason is penetrating. But his prescription — authoritarianism, repression, organic mysticism — is for Berlin a catastrophic wrong turn. Maistre is the cautionary tale of what counter-Enlightenment insights become when stripped of Herder's empathy and Vico's historical imagination.

The political genealogy Berlin draws is explicit: Maistre's doctrines, combined with Romantic heroism and the historic/unhistoric nation distinction, "duly inspired nationalism, imperialism, and finally, in their most violent and pathological form, Fascist and totalitarian doctrines in the twentieth century."

## Contrast with Herder

Where Herder argued that different cultures should flourish side by side like flowers in a garden, Maistre argued that men must be kept under the heel of an authoritarian Church or they will tear each other apart. Both attack Enlightenment universalism; only one anticipates liberalism, the other fascism.
