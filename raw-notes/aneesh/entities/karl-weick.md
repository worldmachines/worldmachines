# Karl E. Weick

## Biographical Sketch

Karl E. Weick is a professor of organizational behavior and psychology at the University of Michigan (Ross School of Business). One of the most influential organizational theorists of the 20th century.

## Core Intellectual Contributions

### Sensemaking in Organizations
Weick's signature contribution: organizations exist primarily as processes of *sensemaking* — making retrospective sense of enacted environments. Key properties of sensemaking: grounded in identity construction, retrospective, enactive of sensible environments, social, ongoing, focused on extracted cues, driven by plausibility rather than accuracy.

### Loose Coupling
Organizations are "loosely coupled systems" — elements affect one another but retain identity and separateness. This is adaptive: failures don't cascade; local adaptation is possible. Also explains why organizations are hard to change.

### Enactment
Organizations enact their environments rather than passively perceiving them. The environment an organization faces is partly constituted by the organization's own actions.

## Relevant Work for the Essays Wiki

### "What Theory Is Not, Theorizing Is" (ASQ, 1995)
A response to Sutton and Staw's "What Theory Is Not." Key arguments:
- Most organizational theory is approximation, not full-blown theory
- The five elements that look like "not theory" (references, data, lists, diagrams, hypotheses) can all serve as stages in a theorizing process
- Theory is a continuum, not a dichotomy
- The key question is whether something is an *interim struggle* moving toward theory
- Belated theorizing (from data to theory) is common and valid

This piece is directly relevant to the [[world-machines-framework]] as an explicit interim struggle — "sensemaking triage, not a Grand Unified Theory."

### "The Collapse of Sensemaking in Organizations: The Mann Gulch Disaster" (ASQ, 1993)
Analysis of the 1949 Mann Gulch wildfire in which 13 smokejumpers died. Key thesis: **sensemaking collapses when role structures collapse**. When the fire behavior became unpredictable, the smokejumpers' organizational structure dissolved, and with it their capacity to improvise collectively. Freud had made the same observation 70 years earlier.

The Mann Gulch paper exemplifies Weick's own "data as symptoms, theory last" methodology — illustrating the principle he would articulate in the 1995 ASQ piece.

## Intellectual Position

- Phenomenological / interpretive tradition in organizational theory
- Skeptical of positivist models that treat organizations as machines
- Sees narrative and storytelling as primary organizational cognition, not an afterthought

## Weick and Klein

Klein et al. (2007) explicitly build on Weick's sensemaking theory, citing his definition of sensemaking as the core cognitive function practitioners must perform. Key Weick claims adopted and extended:
- Sensemaking is initiated by surprise — "the breakdown in expectations" (Weick 1995) — though Klein et al. add proactive sensemaking without surprise
- Effective sensemaking relies on plausibility and reasonableness rather than accuracy
- A person's stance (commitment) affects how events are understood
- During reframing, previously discarded data become significant

Key difference: Weick's sensemaking is primarily organizational and retrospective ("How do I make sense of what I just did?"). Klein's is primarily individual and prospective ("What is happening, and what should I do?"). Weick's enactment thesis — that people actively constitute their environments — maps onto Klein's claim that data are frame-constructed, not given.
