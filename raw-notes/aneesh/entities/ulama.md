# Ulama

The **ulama** (singular: *alim*) are Islamic religious scholars who serve as the custodians and interpreters of Islamic law (sharia) and theology. Across the pre-modern Islamic world — al-Andalus, the Ottoman Empire, the Safavid Empire, the Mughal Empire — the ulama held a distinctive form of [[Antimeme|antimemetic]] power.

## The Mechanism of Antimemetic Power

Monarchs in Islamic states issued decrees. The ulama *interpreted* and *applied* those decrees as law in daily life. This interpretive authority was:

- **Continuous**: not episodic legislation but constant, subtle application
- **Unattributable**: "the king decreed" — the interpretation was invisible
- **Adaptive**: allowed legal systems to evolve through interpretation without visible legislative acts
- **Profound**: shaped marriage, inheritance, commerce, criminal punishment, and public morality

As Sathe notes: "This interpretive authority, operating subtly within the legal and religious bureaucracy, allowed for continuous adaptation and influence without the visible, attributable acts of formal legislation, making it profoundly antimemetic."

## Examples Across Empires

- **Al-Andalus**: ulama shaped legal norms and public morality under the Umayyad caliphate
- **Ottomans**: the *mufti* and *qadi* systems applied sharia locally; the Şeyhülislam (Grand Mufti) was enormously powerful
- **Safavids**: Shi'a ulama were central to state legitimacy and legal enforcement
- **Mughals**: ulama interpreted Mughal decrees through Islamic jurisprudence in a predominantly non-Muslim empire

## Comparison to Other Antimemetic Structures

Unlike the [[Ming Dynasty Grand Secretaries]] (who were antimemetic by design — appointed specifically to be invisible), the ulama's antimemetic quality was structural: they occupied the interpretive layer between law and life, a layer that is by nature less visible than the legislative layer.
