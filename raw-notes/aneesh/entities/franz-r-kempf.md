# Franz R. Kempf

## Identity

Literary scholar affiliated with Bard College. Author of "Noble Savages and English Gardeners: Kulturkritik from Rousseau to Goethe," *Philosophy and Literature*, Vol. 39 No. 2 (October 2015), pp. 422–442. DOI: 10.1353/phl.2015.0055.

## Key Contribution

Kempf's central intervention against existing scholarship on [[Kulturkritik]]: most treatments assume a binary nature-vs-culture opposition and a straightforward critical project. Kempf argues instead that Kulturkritik's rhetorical strategy posits a **reciprocity** of nature and culture — the two poles are not opposed but mutually constituting, and the critical work depends on this mutual constitution rather than on choosing one over the other.

This reframing makes Kulturkritik not a critique of culture in favor of nature, but a **regulative mode of experimental thinking** — a dialectical procedure that keeps both poles in productive tension, enabling self-reflection through the mutual mirroring of self (culture) and other (nature).

## Method

Kempf traces the Kulturkritik tradition through its horticultural manifestations — the French garden and the English garden — as a way of bringing the aesthetic and the philosophical dimensions of the argument together. The garden is not incidental but central: it is the site where Kulturkritik's paradoxes are most clearly enacted and most honestly admitted (Julie confessing she created only "the illusion of natural wilderness").

## Intellectual Lineage Mapped

Kempf charts a precise intellectual genealogy:
- [[Jean-Jacques Rousseau]] → initiates the project; the bête and the bourgeois as rhetorical extremes
- [[Friedrich Schiller]] → responds to the French Revolution going awry; shifts cure from nature to art (aesthetic education)
- [[Johann Wolfgang von Goethe]] → the most skeptical; rejects the English garden; proposes Steigerung (intensification) over synthesis

He also notes extended resonances with Diogenes, Freud, Adorno, *The Matrix*, Frank Lloyd Wright's Fallingwater, and Tom Stoppard's *Arcadia* — suggesting Kulturkritik's logic extends well beyond its 18th-century instantiation.
