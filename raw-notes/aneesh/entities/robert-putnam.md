# Robert Putnam

**Born**: 1941
**Affiliation**: Harvard Kennedy School
**Key works**: *Bowling Alone: The Collapse and Revival of American Community* (2000); *Making Democracy Work* (1993); *Our Kids* (2015)

## Intellectual Profile

Putnam is a political scientist whose work focuses on social capital — the value that derives from social networks, norms of reciprocity, and civic engagement. His empirical and interpretive work documented the broad decline of these resources in American life from roughly the 1950s onward.

## Bowling Alone (2000)

The central claim: virtually every indicator of civic engagement and social connection in America — participation in clubs, unions, churches, political parties, informal socializing — declined substantially between 1950 and 2000. The metaphor: more Americans bowl than ever before, but league bowling has declined — people bowl alone.

Putnam's explanation emphasizes four causes:
1. Pressure of time and money (especially two-career families)
2. Urban sprawl and commuting (substituting private car time for social time)
3. Electronic entertainment (especially television)
4. Generational change (the "civic generation" of WWII-era Americans is dying out)

The technological and values-based explanations — screens and individualism — that [[Alex Mayyasi]] characterizes as the dominant narrative of American social decline draw heavily on Putnam. Mayyasi's contribution is to add the economic mechanism ([[Baumol's Cost Disease]]) that Putnam underweights.

## Social Capital

Putnam distinguishes:
- **Bonding capital**: ties within a group (family, ethnic community) — strong but potentially insular
- **Bridging capital**: ties across groups — weaker but more valuable for civic life and information flow

[[Third Places]] are primarily generators of bridging capital — the kind most under threat.
