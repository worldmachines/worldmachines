# Claude Lévi-Strauss (1908–2009)

French anthropologist, founder of structural anthropology. His major works — *Tristes Tropiques* (1955), *Structural Anthropology* (1958), *The Savage Mind* (1962), and the four volumes of *Mythologiques* (1964–71) — established the project of reading myths, kinship systems, and cultural practices as systems of binary oppositions with their own structural logic.

## Why He Matters for the Wiki

[[doreen-massey]]'s Part Two of *For Space* (2005) treats Lévi-Strauss as the originator of the structuralist move at the centre of her diagnosis. The genealogy:

1. Structural anthropology arose in part to refuse Western developmental narratives that treated other societies as "primitive" — stages in a single history. Lévi-Strauss insisted each society had its own coherent structure to be analysed in its own right.
2. The vehicle for this insistence was the **synchronic** analysis: examine the structure at a moment, not its sequence in time.
3. Synchrony was then equated with **space** — on the bare assumption that whatever is not temporal must be spatial.
4. The result: structuralist "space" became a-temporal, internally interlocked, closed. See [[prison-house-of-synchrony]].

## Massey's Critique

> "His [Lévi-Strauss's] ruse is to substitute diachrony for history. That sleight of hand is supported, much like the diversions all illusionists try to create while operating their magic, by directing the reader's attention to something else, in this case to the 'opposition' of Space and Time." (Fabian 1983: 54, quoted Massey 2005: 38)

Massey notes that what Lévi-Strauss calls "space" is "actually a taxonomic space, indeed a map" — not the lived space of human geographers but a conceptual schema purged of temporality. Space here is "real space," in scare quotes, confused once again with representation. See [[space-as-representation-critique]].

The famous Lévi-Straussian "third term" that bridges synchrony and diachrony — mana, myth, facial paintings among the Caduveo — gets read by Massey as a symptom: the impossibility of bridging the gap is the symptom of having made the wrong cut in the first place.

## What Survives

Massey is not anti-structuralist. The project of refusing developmental narratives is one she endorses (it underwrites her Part Three critique of [[aspatial-globalisation]] and the [[cosmopolitan-time-queue]]). What she rejects is the *spatialisation* of that refusal — the move that left "space" as the prison-house of structure.
