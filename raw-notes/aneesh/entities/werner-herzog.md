# Werner Herzog

## Overview

Werner Herzog (b. 1942) is a German filmmaker known for *Aguirre, the Wrath of God* (1972), *Fitzcarraldo* (1982), *Grizzly Man* (2005), and dozens of other films and documentaries. In [[venkatesh-rao]]'s work, he appears not as a filmmaker but as a philosopher of nature — specifically, as the articulator of what genuine wildness looks like and why civilization's aestheticizations fall short.

## The Key Monologue

Delivered during the filming of *Fitzcarraldo* in the Amazon (documented in *Burden of Dreams*, Les Blank, 1982):

> "Taking a close look at what is around us, there is some sort of harmony. It is the harmony of overwhelming and collective murder. And we in comparison to the articulate vileness and baseness and obscenity of all this jungle, we in comparison to that enormous articulation, we only sound and look like badly pronounced and half-finished sentences out of a stupid suburban novel, a cheap novel."

Herzog continues: there is no harmony as civilization has conceived it; there is overwhelming misery, fornication, growth, and lack of order. Even the stars look like a mess.

## What Rao Takes from Herzog

1. **Nature is murder**: The intense liveness of the jungle is *inseparable* from its murderous competitiveness. These cannot be separated by aesthetic preference.

2. **Civilization is "badly pronounced and half-finished"**: Human attempts to aestheticize nature — including stylized Darwinism, Bloodsport Planet aesthetics — are feeble. They "can rule a stadium, not a live planet."

3. **Love against better judgment**: Herzog says "I love it against my better judgment." This models the intellectual posture Rao advocates: accepting wildness and liveness even when it offers no comfort.

## Herzog as Counter-Model to Stylized Darwinism

Rao explicitly contrasts Herzog's genuine Darwinian vision with the "theater of stylized Darwinism" — Trump-era bloodsport aesthetics featuring legibilized fitness hierarchies. Herzog's jungle has *illegible* fitness forces — mussels on a piling, not gladiators in an arena.

## Intellectual Position

Herzog is a consistent anti-idealist in his treatment of nature:
- Nature is not benign, purposeful, or directed toward human ends
- Harmony, where it exists, is the harmony of collective killing
- Human meanings are provincial, "suburban," inadequate to nature's scale

This makes him a useful corrective to both:
- Solarpunk / humanist visions of technology-nature harmony
- Bloodsport aesthetics of legibilized competition
