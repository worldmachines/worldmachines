# Rabindranath Tagore

**Rabindranath Tagore** (1861–1941) was a Bengali polymath: poet, novelist, playwright, composer, painter, and philosopher. He was the first non-European to win the Nobel Prize in Literature (1913). He is the primary intellectual figure in the [[Cloister Web]] essay's typology of Indian independence, where he represents the **intellectual** pole against Gandhi's dual intellectual-revolutionary role.

## Role in the Intellectual/Revolutionary Typology

Tagore shaped notions of Indian identity, spirituality, and humanist nationalism through his writing — particularly his poetry (*Gitanjali*) and essays. He did not lead mass political movements but provided the intellectual and cultural vocabulary that shaped Indian political consciousness.

This positions him as the archetype of the **intellectual** in the [[Intellectual vs. Revolutionary]] distinction: conceptualizing and articulating new frameworks at some remove from mass political action.

## Key Tensions with Gandhi

Tagore and Gandhi had a famous and productive intellectual relationship marked by significant disagreements:
- Tagore criticized Gandhi's emphasis on *charkha* (spinning wheel) as symbolic nationalism, arguing it fetishized simplicity
- Gandhi respected Tagore's universalism but thought it insufficiently grounded in mass political reality
- The tension between Tagore's cosmopolitan humanism and Gandhi's strategic nationalism maps onto the intellectual/revolutionary distinction

## Intellectual Contributions

- *Gitanjali* (1910) — devotional poetry; Nobel Prize text
- *The Home and the World* (1916) — novel exploring nationalism, terrorism, and personal ethics
- Shantiniketan — experimental school/university embodying his educational philosophy
- Essays on nationalism, art, education, and Indian cultural identity
