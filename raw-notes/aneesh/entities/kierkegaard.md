# Kierkegaard

**Søren Aabye Kierkegaard** (1813–1855) was a Danish philosopher, theologian, and proto-existentialist. He is one of the earliest thinkers to make *subjectivity* — the individual's relationship to their own existence — the central problem of philosophy.

## Key Concepts (as used here)

### Anxiety (Angst)
In *The Concept of Anxiety* (1844), Kierkegaard defines anxiety not as fear of a specific object but as the "dizziness of freedom" — the vertiginous recognition that you can choose, and therefore must choose. Anxiety arises from confronting the abyss of your own possibilities.

This is the foundational concept in [[Anxiety as Generative Force]] and [[The Plato Plateau]].

### The Leap
Kierkegaard argued that genuine commitments — ethical, religious, personal — cannot be reached by reason alone. They require a "leap" — an act of will in the face of uncertainty. This connects to [[Identity Crash]] as a site of forced leaps.

### Stages of Existence
Kierkegaard's three stages (aesthetic → ethical → religious) are a precursor to [[Levels of Philosophical Engagement]], though organized around spiritual and ethical commitment rather than identity-maintenance function.

## Intellectual Position

Kierkegaard is explicitly anti-Hegelian: he rejected systematic philosophy as a betrayal of the individual's lived experience. His irony, pseudonymous authorship, and indirect communication are all devices to force the reader to *appropriate* the idea rather than merely comprehend it.

## What's Missing from the Sources

The essay uses Kierkegaard's anxiety concept but doesn't engage with his religious framework (anxiety as the condition that makes sin — and redemption — possible). The secular reading of anxiety as identity-motor strips out the theological stakes.
