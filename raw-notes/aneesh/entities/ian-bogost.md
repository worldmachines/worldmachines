# Ian Bogost

## Biographical

American media theorist, video game designer, and philosopher. Professor at Washington University in St. Louis (and previously Georgia Tech). Known for his work on procedural rhetoric and the study of video games as a medium.

## Major Works

- *Unit Operations* (2006) — early OOO-adjacent work on procedural units in games and literature.
- *Alien Phenomenology, or What It's Like to Be a Thing* (2012) — the key OOO text. Argues for "carpentry" (making things to think with) and develops the concept of alien phenomenology.
- *Persuasive Games* (2007) — on how games argue through procedure.

## Alien Phenomenology

Bogost's concept: an alien phenomenology is an observation of how another entity observes or interacts with the world about it. Rather than asking how humans experience things, alien phenomenology asks: what is it like to be a rock? A circuit board? A mosquito? Not as anthropomorphic projection, but as a rigorous inquiry into the operations and interactions of non-human entities.

Bryant adopts the concept for Ch. 3 of *Onto-Cartography*: "In Chapter 3, I argue that we must engage in 'alien phenomenology' to understand how machines interact with other entities in the world about them." (Bryant 2014, p. 9)

## Relation to Bryant on Media

Bryant's footnote at the end of Ch. 1 credits Bogost as a co-developer of the extended media concept: "This conception of media as extending the sense-organs or modifying the activity and becoming of any other entity and not just humans arose out of discussions between Ian Bogost and myself." (Bryant 2014, p. 36, fn. 6)
