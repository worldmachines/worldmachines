# Alexander Herzen

**Alexander Herzen** (1812–1870) was a Russian political thinker, writer, and social critic, often called the "father of Russian socialism." He is cited in the [[Cloister Web]] essay as the archetypal intellectual in the Russian revolutionary context — providing the ideological groundwork that [[Vladimir Lenin]] later channeled into mass revolutionary action.

## Role in the Intellectual/Revolutionary Typology

Herzen represents the **intellectual** pole in the Russian case of the [[Intellectual vs. Revolutionary]] distinction. His writings — particularly from exile in London, where he published *The Bell* (*Kolokol*), the first free Russian press — laid the intellectual foundations of Russian populism (*narodnichestvo*) and broader socialist thought.

He conceptualized and articulated the frameworks; Lenin (four decades later) provided the organizational structure and revolutionary will to realize a version of them.

## Key Works

- *My Past and Thoughts* — autobiography and political memoir; considered one of the great Russian prose works
- *The Bell* (*Kolokol*) — London-published free Russian newspaper, 1857–1867
- *From the Other Shore* — philosophical reflections on the failure of the 1848 European revolutions

## Intellectual Position

Herzen was notably pessimistic about teleological history — he rejected the idea that history inevitably moves toward a goal. This distinguished him from Marxist determinism and positioned him as a tragic liberal rather than a revolutionary ideologue. His influence on Lenin was indirect: the populist tradition he helped create became the soil in which Bolshevism grew, even as Lenin rejected much of Herzen's specific politics.
