# Jean Baudrillard

**Dates**: 1929–2007
**Field**: Sociology, philosophy, cultural theory
**Key works**: *The System of Objects* (1968); *Simulacra and Simulation* (1981); *The Consumer Society* (1970)

## Core Ideas

**Simulation and Hyperreality**: Baudrillard argued that in late modernity, representations of reality have replaced reality itself. Signs refer to other signs rather than to any underlying referent — "the map precedes the territory."

**Consumer Identity**: In *The System of Objects* and *The Consumer Society*, Baudrillard analyzed how objects in consumer capitalism function primarily as signs within a code of social distinction. We do not consume objects for their use-value but to construct and signal identity.

The historical shift he identifies:
- **Pre-industrial**: technology personalized for us (a craftsman's tool, a tailor's suit)
- **Industrial/post-industrial**: we choose technology and objects to show *who we are* — actively constructing a consumer identity

## Sathe's Use

In [[The End of Identity: AI, Plasticity, and the Divergence Machine]], Sathe uses Baudrillard's consumer identity analysis to describe a specific moment in the history of [[Legibility]]:

Under the [[divergence-machine|modernity machine]], individuals cling to consumer-constructed identities to remain **legible** to a system exhibiting "zombie persistence." They are performing a known role in a dying system.

This is the layer of identity that AI begins to strip — along with the underlying layer of professional know-how.

## Relation to Other Thinkers in This Wiki

- **vs. Malabou**: Baudrillard's "flexible" consumer is Malabou's flexibility without plasticity
