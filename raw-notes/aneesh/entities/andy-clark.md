# Andy Clark

**b. 1957.** British-American cognitive scientist and philosopher. Professor at Edinburgh (now Sussex). Major works: *Being There* (1997), *Supersizing the Mind* (2008), *The Experience Machine* (2023). With David Chalmers: "The Extended Mind" (Analysis, 1998 / 2011).

## Core Contribution: The Extended Mind Hypothesis

Clark (with Chalmers) argues that cognitive processes are not confined to the brain but can be *constitutively* distributed across brain, body, and external physical media. This is stronger than the claim that external tools are useful aids: Clark claims that under the right conditions (active availability, ready access, reliable trust), external media become genuine components of cognitive processes — not in a metaphorical sense but in the sense that the cognitive process *is* the brain-body-world system, not merely the brain part of it.

The Inga/Otto case: Inga has a normally functioning memory and can recall exhibition addresses from internal memory. Otto has Alzheimer's and keeps a notebook. Clark and Chalmers argue that Otto's notebook and Inga's brain are *functionally equivalent* as memory systems — both store beliefs that can be accessed when needed. Otto's beliefs are in the notebook; the notebook is part of his extended mind.

**Implications**:
- Mind is a *relation* between brain, body, and physical world — not a substance inside the skull
- "Cognition that takes place with pencil, paper and Arabic numerals is *different* from cognition that takes place purely inside the head." (Bryant, p. 87, summarizing Clark)
- External media (paper, calculator, internet) are not merely convenient but constitutive of certain types of mind

## Bryant's Use

Bryant uses Clark as the exemplary case of the machinic-assemblage account of mind. Clark shows that the minimal unit of cognitive analysis is not the brain or even the brain-body, but the *assemblage* of brain, body, and media. Different assemblages (different media couplings) constitute genuinely different cognitive systems with different powers.

This has political implications Bryant draws: assemblages with access to different media (internet, libraries, calculators, guns) are not equal in their powers, and a political theory based on abstract equality of "humans" misses the genuine inequalities produced by differential media access. "The engineer armed with a complex calculator is able to do things that the engineer with a slide rule is not." (p. 92)

Bryant also extends Clark's framework to affects and beliefs: the Žižek example of canned laughter as externalized affect — the television show does the feeling for you, and this is no less your affect for occurring outside your skull.

## Relation to Maturana/Varela

Clark's extended mind and the structural coupling concept are complementary. Structural coupling is the mechanism by which minds extend into their media environments; the extended mind hypothesis is the cognitive-scientific articulation of what structural coupling means for mind.
