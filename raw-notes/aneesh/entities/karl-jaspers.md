# Karl Jaspers

Karl Jaspers (1883–1969) was a German-Swiss philosopher and psychiatrist whose work spans existentialism, the philosophy of history, and philosophical anthropology.

## Key Work

*The Origin and Goal of History* (*Vom Ursprung und Ziel der Geschichte*, 1949) introduced the [[axial-age]] concept: the claim that between roughly 800 and 200 BCE, Chinese, Indian, Persian, Judean, and Greek civilizations independently achieved a breakthrough from mythical embeddedness to reflexive, theoretic consciousness. Confucius, the Buddha, Zoroaster, the Hebrew prophets, and the Greek philosophers were all working on structurally similar problems — the nature of the good, the status of tradition, the possibility of universal truth — without contact with one another.

Jaspers offered the Axial Age not as an explanation but as a *fact requiring explanation*. He explicitly refused to reduce it to material causes and treated it as evidence for a deep unity of human historical existence — a philosophical claim rather than a historical-sociological one.

## Broader Contributions

Before turning to the philosophy of history, Jaspers made significant contributions to psychiatry (*General Psychopathology*, 1913) and existential philosophy (*Philosophy*, 3 vols., 1932). His existentialism is less well-known than Heidegger's or Sartre's but distinctively emphasizes *communication* — Jaspers held that authentic existence is impossible in isolation and requires the encounter with other free subjects.

His relationship with Heidegger — once a close colleague, broken by Heidegger's embrace of Nazism — is one of the defining intellectual-biographical narratives of 20th-century German philosophy.
