# Gilles Deleuze

**1925–1995.** French philosopher. Major works: *Difference and Repetition* (1968), *The Logic of Sense* (1969); with Félix Guattari: *Anti-Oedipus* (1972), *A Thousand Plateaus* (1980); *Foucault* (1986); *The Fold* (1988); *What is Philosophy?* (1991).

## Core Positions

**Philosophy of difference**: Against the representationalist tradition (which makes difference secondary to identity), Deleuze argues that difference is primary and identity is derivative — produced through repetition, not given in advance. Reality is a field of differentiation, not of pre-given stable identities.

**Immanence**: No transcendent principles (God, Subject, Idea) govern being from outside. Being is immanent — it is the plane of immanence, the field of differential forces.

**Assemblage theory** (with Guattari): Entities are not unified substances but assemblages (*agencements*) of heterogeneous elements. Assemblages have two dimensions: machinic (content, bodies) and semiotic (expression, enunciation). The assemblage concept allows analysis of entities at any scale — from bacteria to civilizations — without privileging any level.

**Individuation by powers/affects** (Spinozist): Following Spinoza, Deleuze argues that entities should be individuated by what they can do (their affects, powers, capacities) rather than by their qualitative appearance or genus/species membership. "What can a body do?" is the central ethological question.

**Territorialization/Deterritorialization/Reterritorialization (with Guattari)**: See [[territorialization]]. Assemblages stabilize (territorialize) and destabilize (deterritorialize); political and creative life involves managing these movements.

## Key Texts in Bryant's Usage

- *A Thousand Plateaus* (with Guattari, 1987 English trans.): source of assemblage theory, the latitude/longitude of bodies, the man-horse-stirrup-lance example, and the T/D/R vocabulary
- *Difference and Repetition*: ontological background for individuating by powers rather than qualities

## Relation to Bryant

Deleuze is one of Bryant's two principal theoretical debts (alongside Harman's object-oriented ontology). Bryant's machine concept is a restatement of the assemblage in materialist terms, and his insistence on individuation by powers directly follows Deleuze and Guattari's Spinozism. Bryant differs from Deleuze in: (1) using "machine" rather than "assemblage" as the primary term; (2) integrating entropy and thermodynamics into the account; (3) maintaining a stronger realist commitment to machines as genuine individuals rather than processes of individuation.
