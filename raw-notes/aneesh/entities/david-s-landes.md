# David S. Landes (1924–2013)

Economic historian at Harvard, known for sweeping histories of technology, capitalism, and industrial development. Author of *The Unbound Prometheus* (1969, on European industrialization) and *The Wealth and Poverty of Nations* (1998).

## Key Work Referenced

**"What Do Bosses Really Do?"** (1986), *Journal of Economic History*, Vol. 46, No. 3, pp. 585–623.
- [JSTOR link](https://www.jstor.org/stable/2121476)

Landes argues against the view that factory owners and bosses were merely exploiters who could be eliminated. Instead, he shows that the "boss" performed an **essential economic function**: organizing production, connecting producers to markets, and solving coordination problems (embezzlement, shirking, quality control) that guild systems and putting-out arrangements could not solve at scale.

## How Sathe Uses Landes

In [[What Do Platforms Really Do?]], Sathe transposes Landes's framework to platform economics:
- Platforms perform the same functions as Landes's factory owners — market creation, labor specialization, management
- They solve the same problems through [[Algorithmic Management]] instead of overseers
- The [[Putting-Out System]] structure persists in how platforms shift creative risk onto creators
- The [[Specialization of Ideas]] mirrors Landes's analysis of industrial task disaggregation

## Key Concepts Borrowed

- **Disaggregating a productive process**: the advantage of dividing production into narrow tasks that can be matched to precise labor quantities — platforms do this for content production
- **Boss as essential coordinator**: platforms are not background IT — they are the central organizers of creative production
- **Putting-out system mechanics**: merchant-manufacturers who shifted capital costs and production risk onto workers

## *Revolution in Time* (1983) and the Design-Climbs-the-Stack Thesis

Sathe's [[Not My Lever — The Last Container]] (2026) cites Landes's *Revolution in Time: Clocks and the Making of the Modern World* (1983) as the source for the moment **design became the asset and skill became the commodity**. The case study is the eighteenth-century watch trade in Geneva and London.

- **Pierre Jaquet-Droz** in La Chaux-de-Fonds, 1750s, drew movements on paper and sent the drawings to specialised hands: wheel-cutters, jewellers, pinion-makers, springers. The complete watch came from no single hand.
- Before this separation, the design lived in the carpenter's hand and eye, inseparable from the making. The watch trade is the cleanest case study because the artefact is small, complex, and irreducibly composite — a setting in which the separation between design and skill is *forced* rather than chosen.
- Sathe extends Landes's argument across three centuries — mass production, software, AI auteuring, and now lever discipline at the level of intent. See [[design-climbs-the-stack]].
