# Anne E. Carpenter

American computational biologist; Senior Director of the Imaging Platform at the Broad Institute of MIT and Harvard.

## Trajectory

- **2003.** Began developing CellProfiler as a postdoctoral researcher. Split her time between the Sabatini lab at the Whitehead Institute (cell biology) and the Golland lab at MIT CSAIL (computer vision); the collaboration was the interdisciplinary move that made automated cellular morphological analysis feasible.
- **2006.** First CellProfiler paper in *Genome Biology* (Carpenter et al., "CellProfiler: image analysis software for identifying and quantifying cell phenotypes"). Open-source from inception.
- **2007.** Joined the Broad Institute as faculty; founded the Imaging Platform.
- **2016.** Cell Painting protocol published in *Nature Protocols* (Bray, Singh, Han, Davis, Borgeson et al.). A systematic method for extracting ~1,500 morphological features from cells treated with any perturbation, using multiplexed fluorescent dyes staining six cellular compartments.
- **2023–2024.** JUMP Cell Painting Consortium dataset released: ~136,000 distinct chemical and genetic perturbations, more than five million images, ~115 TB of image data, contributed by ten pharmaceutical companies and deposited under permissive licence. The accompanying paper appeared in *Nature Methods* (2024).

## Significance

Carpenter held a direction the drug discovery field did not initially value. Target-based discovery — identify a protein, develop a binder, push through clinical trials — was the dominant paradigm when she started CellProfiler; image-based phenotypic profiling was considered secondary. The field caught up over two decades, not through her argumentation but through the accumulated weight of the artifacts: a tool in every image-analysis pipeline, a protocol reproduced across institutions, a dataset now used as the training corpus for foundation models of cellular response. Recursion Pharmaceuticals, a multi-billion-dollar public company, builds on top of the techniques her group iterated openly.

## In the Wiki

Carpenter is the macro-scale case of [[wayfinding-as-practice]] in the [[viscous-frontier-and-cost-of-intent]] essay. Each release of CellProfiler was a non-retractable deposit; each Cell Painting iteration was an accumulating artifact; the JUMP dataset is the long-range landing. The arc illustrates the [[artifact-requirement]] at a career scale: twenty years of open, versioned, reproducible deposits that outlasted the fashion cycle of drug discovery and became the field's substrate.

Her work is also a standing counter-example to the claim that open science cannot support institution-scale ambition. The JUMP consortium was assembled with ten pharmaceutical competitors contributing data to a shared, openly licensed pool — the [[artifact-requirement]] (contribute or you do not get access to the pooled dataset) made the coordination robust against the usual freeriding failures of pre-competitive collaboration.
