# Giambattista Vico

**Born**: Naples, 1668
**Died**: Naples, 1744
**Key work**: *Scienza Nuova* (New Science), 1725, 1730, 1744

## Intellectual Profile

Vico was largely ignored in his lifetime and only recovered — by Michelet, Croce, and eventually Berlin — as a foundational figure in the philosophy of history and hermeneutics. His central claim: there is no "view from nowhere" from which to judge all human civilizations. Each must be understood on its own terms, from within.

## Key Ideas

### *Verum Ipsum Factum*
"The true is the made" — we can fully understand only what we have made. This is why mathematics is perfectly knowable (it is constructed) but nature is not fully knowable (God made it). Crucially: *human history* is made by humans, and therefore *fully intelligible* to them — but only if they approach it through imaginative reconstruction (*fantasia*), not abstract reason.

### *Fantasia* (Imaginative Reconstruction)
The method for understanding other historical periods and cultures: imaginative projection into the mental world of the people who lived them. You cannot understand Homer through Cartesian analysis; you must *become* a Greek warrior in imagination. This is the ancestor of [[Johann Gottfried Herder|Herder's]] *Einfühlung* and Dilthey's hermeneutics.

### The Course of Nations (*Corsi e Ricorsi*)
History moves in cycles: the age of gods → the age of heroes → the age of men → decline → renewal. This is not progress toward a rational endpoint; it is recurrence with variation. No moment in the cycle is more "advanced" than another — they are incommensurable.

### Anti-Cartesianism
Vico's entire project is a critique of Descartes's method. Reason cannot provide foundations for historical and human understanding; imagination, memory, and will are equally essential cognitive faculties that Cartesian rationalism suppresses.

## Significance for Berlin

[[Isaiah Berlin]] saw Vico as the first genuine pluralist in the Western tradition — the first to claim, on principled grounds, that different cultures embody different values that cannot be ranked on a universal scale. Vico is the fountainhead of the [[Counter-Enlightenment]] tradition Berlin excavates in *Against the Current*.

## Berlin's Direct Quotation (pp. 6–7)

"Vico's unsystematic works dealt with many other matters, but his importance in the history of the Enlightenment consists in his insistence on the plurality of cultures and on the consequently fallacious character of the idea that there is one and only one structure of reality which the enlightened philosopher can see as it truly is, and which he can (at least in principle) describe in logically perfect language — a vision that has obsessed thinkers from Plato to Leibniz, Condillac, Russell and his more faithful followers."

## Vico in Harold Bloom's Western Canon

Bloom uses Vico's historical schema to organize *The Western Canon* (1994). His three ages — Aristocratic, Democratic, and Chaotic (Bloom's own elaboration of Vico's "Theocratic → Heroic → Human" cycle) — structure the canon's progression from Dante through the moderns. The *Corsi e Ricorsi* pattern maps onto literary history: each age has characteristic burdens and characteristic forms of agon with the tradition.

This is not a faithful application of Vico — Bloom reads him as a precursor for his own theory of literary struggle and belatedness. Bloom's use of Vico is itself an instance of [[Creative Misreading]].
