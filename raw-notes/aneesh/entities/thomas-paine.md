# Thomas Paine

**Thomas Paine** (1737–1809) was an English-born American political activist, philosopher, and pamphleteer. His writing — particularly *Common Sense* (1776) — provided crucial intellectual framing for the American Revolution. He is cited in the [[Cloister Web]] essay as one of the two **intellectual** figures (alongside [[Thomas Jefferson]]) in the American case of the [[Intellectual vs. Revolutionary]] typology.

## Role in the Intellectual/Revolutionary Typology

Paine's *Common Sense* (1776) was a mass-distributed pamphlet making the philosophical case for independence from Britain in language accessible to ordinary colonists. It is one of the most widely read political documents in early American history. His *Rights of Man* (1791) and *The Age of Reason* (1794–1807) extended his political and religious philosophy.

In the typology, Paine represents the **intellectual** who conceptualizes and articulates the framework; [[Samuel Adams]] and [[Patrick Henry]] represent the **revolutionaries** who galvanized public action around it.

The distinction is slightly complicated for Paine: he was himself an activist, not merely a scholar. But his primary contribution was rhetorical-philosophical (making the case) rather than organizational-mobilizing (building the movement).

## Historical Risk

The intellectual in the typology risks censorship and isolation; Paine bore this out — his later works (*Rights of Man*, *Age of Reason*) made him deeply unpopular, he was tried for seditious libel in Britain, and he died in poverty and disrepute.
