# Francisco Varela

**1946–2001.** Chilean biologist, neuroscientist, and philosopher. With Humberto Maturana: *Autopoiesis and Cognition* (1980), *The Tree of Knowledge* (1987). With Evan Thompson and Eleanor Rosch: *The Embodied Mind* (1991). Solo/late: *Ethical Know-How* (1999), neurophenomenology papers.

## Core Contributions

**Autopoiesis** (with Maturana): See [[autopoiesis]], [[humberto-maturana]]. Varela's subsequent work extended and in some ways revised the autopoiesis concept, particularly in relation to embodied cognition and phenomenology.

**Enactivism** (*The Embodied Mind*, with Thompson and Rosch): Cognition is not representation of a pre-given world but *enaction* — the world and the cognitive agent co-emerge through their interaction. Knowledge is enacted, not passively received. This is the "embodied, embedded, enacted, extended" cluster of cognitive science.

**Neurophenomenology**: Varela's late project: integrating first-person phenomenological methods (Husserlian) with third-person neuroscience. Rather than reducing phenomenology to neuroscience, neurophenomenology treats first-person experience as a constraint on neural models and vice versa.

**Structural coupling**: See [[structural-coupling]]. Varela extended this concept into an account of how nervous systems couple to their environments and bodies, producing coordinated behavior without centralized representation.

## Divergence from Maturana

Varela eventually distanced himself from some of Maturana's more radical observer-dependence claims, seeking a less relativist account of the organism-environment relation. His enactivism moves toward a middle path: the world is neither independently given (realism) nor merely constituted by the observer (constructivism), but co-produced through the history of structural coupling.

## Relation to Bryant

Varela's enactivism reinforces Bryant's non-representationalist machine ontology. Machines are not systems that represent the world but systems that *enact* openings to the world through their structural couplings. Bryant's account of how machines select their environments (operational closure + selective structural openness) is consistent with Varela's account of how nervous systems enact their sensorimotor niches.
