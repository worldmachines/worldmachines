# Geoffrey West

## Overview

Geoffrey West (b. 1940, Taunton, England) is a British-American theoretical physicist and a distinguished professor at the **Santa Fe Institute**, where he served as president from 2005 to 2009. He spent the first half of his career in high-energy particle physics — at Stanford University and as leader of the high-energy physics group at Los Alamos National Laboratory — before turning, in the late 1990s, to the physics of biological scaling. He is best known for two bodies of work: the metabolic scaling laws of organisms (with James Brown and Brian Enquist) and the scaling laws of cities (with Luis Bettencourt and colleagues). His 2017 book *Scale* is the trade-press synthesis.

## Scaling Laws in Biology

West, Brown, and Enquist's 1997 *Science* paper proposed a derivation of **Kleiber's law** — the empirical observation that an organism's metabolic rate scales as the 3/4 power of its body mass — from first principles. Their argument: organisms are constrained by space-filling, fractal-like distribution networks (vasculature, tracheae) that minimise transport costs. The geometry of those networks forces the 3/4 exponent. The same framework predicts a family of related quarter-power exponents across lifespan, heart rate, and other physiological variables.

The biology work is contested in detail (alternative derivations, exceptions, debates about whether 2/3 or 3/4 fits the data better at various scales) but established scaling as a serious quantitative physics-of-life programme.

## Urban Scaling

In a 2007 *PNAS* paper, Bettencourt, Lobo, Helbing, Kühnert, and West showed that urban variables scale with population in two opposite directions:

- **Superlinear scaling (~1.15)** for socio-economic outputs: wages, patents, GDP, R&D employment, AIDS cases, violent crime. Double a city's population and these grow by roughly 2^1.15 ≈ 2.22 — more than doubling.
- **Sublinear scaling (~0.85)** for infrastructure: road surface, electrical cable length, gas stations, water mains. Double the population and these grow by roughly 2^0.85 ≈ 1.80 — less than doubling.
- **Linear scaling (~1.0)** for individual human needs: housing, household water and electricity consumption.

The structural claim is that cities are physically efficient (sublinear infrastructure) and socially intensifying (superlinear interaction-driven outputs) at the same time. Larger cities are better amplifiers of individual action than smaller ones. This is the empirical foundation for the "system becomes a better amplifier the larger it gets" claim in section III of ["The Lightening of Intent"](https://aneeshsathe.com/).

## Influence

West's urban-scaling exponents have become standard in complexity science, urban economics, and cross-disciplinary policy work, even as the empirical robustness of the exact exponents across cultures and time periods remains under debate. *Scale* (2017) brought the framework to a wider audience and connected biological, urban, and corporate scaling under a single physics-derived banner. He has been a central figure in establishing the Santa Fe Institute as the home of complex-systems research.
