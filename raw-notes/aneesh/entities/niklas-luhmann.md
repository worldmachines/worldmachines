# Niklas Luhmann

## Biographical

Niklas Luhmann (1927–1998), German sociologist. Worked primarily at the University of Bielefeld. Produced an enormously ambitious theory of society as a self-referential, autopoietic social system. Major works include *Social Systems* (1984/1995), *The Reality of Mass Media* (1995/2000), *Theory of Society* (1997/2012), and numerous domain-specific studies of law, economy, science, art, religion, and politics.

## Core Position: Society as Communication

Luhmann's central thesis: society is not composed of human beings but of *communications*. Human beings (psychic systems) are part of the environment of social systems, not their elements. Social systems reproduce themselves autopoietically through recursive communication operations — each communication selects from previous communications and enables further communications. The organizing concept is not action, meaning, or structure but *operation*: what a system does to reproduce itself.

Social systems are operationally closed (they can only operate with their own elements — communications — not with elements from the environment) and cognitively open (they can be irritated by environmental events, which they process in their own terms). This is the Maturana-and-Varela concept of autopoiesis transposed from biological systems to social ones.

## Luhmann and Worlds

Luhmann's account of "world" is related to the Husserlian concept: world is the ultimate horizon of unquestioned givenness within which all system-environment distinctions are drawn. World for Luhmann is not a social system but the unmarked side of every distinction — a kind of transcendental operator. Social systems produce themselves *within* world but cannot encompass it.

This is precisely what Bryant criticizes. For Bryant, Luhmann's "transcendental" describes how a social machine encounters a world, not the world itself: "this transcendental can in no way be equated with a world. Rather, the transcendental is a sieve or series of operations through which a social machine encounters a world" (Bryant 2014: 113).

## Bryant's Critique

In *Onto-Cartography* Ch. 5, Bryant makes two criticisms of Luhmann:

**1. The communications-only thesis is too narrow.** Luhmann's claim that societies are composed *solely* of communications (Luhmann 2002c) neglects the role of material mediators. "Technological media, the layout of roads, rivers, and power lines, tornadoes and hurricanes, resources, and so on, all play as significant a role in the form that social relations take as communications. Communications are only one element among others in social assemblages." (Bryant 2014: 122)

**2. Conflation of access to world with world itself.** Luhmann's account of how social systems encounter their environment cannot tell us what a world is in its ontological being — only how a particular type of machine (social system) operationally processes its environment.

## What Bryant Takes from Luhmann

Despite the critique, Bryant's debt to Luhmann is substantial:

- The concepts of **operational closure** and **structural coupling** — a machine is closed to its environment while remaining open to flows from other machines through coupling — are adapted directly from Maturana/Varela via Luhmann.
- The idea that each system/machine operates according to its **own codes** (Luhmann's binary codes: legal/illegal, true/false, payment/non-payment) resonates with Bryant's account of how machines selectively respond to flows.
- **Second-order observation** — the observation of observation — is a Luhmannian concept Bryant invokes to describe how onto-cartography maps machine interactions.
