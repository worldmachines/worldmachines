# Erving Goffman (1922–1982)

Canadian-American sociologist; professor at Berkeley and Penn. One of the most influential social theorists of the twentieth century. His work established the "interaction order" as a domain of sociological inquiry in its own right — irreducible to larger social structures or individual psychology.

## Key Works

- *The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life* (1959) — dramaturgical model; front/back regions.
- *Stigma* (1963) — management of spoiled identity.
- *Behavior in Public Places* (1963) — rules of co-presence in public settings.
- *Interaction Ritual* (1967) — face-work, deference, demeanor.
- *Relations in Public* (1971) — territories of the self in public space.

## Core Concepts

### Front and Back Regions

In *Presentation of Self*, Goffman distinguishes front regions (where a performance is given for an audience, governed by norms of propriety and impression management) from back regions (where performers relax, rehearse, and violate the front-stage norms). The distinction is spatial and social simultaneously.

This maps directly onto [[Yi-Fu Tuan]]'s analysis in Chapter 4 of *Space and Place*: buildings, rooms, and cities have front and back sides that encode status differences. Maintenance men enter through service doors at the back; executives through the lobby at the front. Tuan explicitly notes (p. 41) how a middle-class residence "presents an attractive front to impress and welcome social adults, and an unprepossessing rear for the use of people of low status." See [[body-as-spatial-axis]].

### Civil Inattention

In public spaces, strangers manage proximity by performing controlled non-engagement — the brief eye-contact followed by immediate withdrawal of gaze that signals "I see you are there and I pose no threat." Civil inattention is the micro-ritual that makes urban co-presence tolerable despite its proxemic density.

This is the mechanism behind the Eskimo strategy in [[spaciousness-and-crowding]]: when forced to share close quarters in dark winter huts, Eskimos "screen the stimulus of other people by turning them into shadows and objects." This is civil inattention raised to a survival technique.

### The Interaction Order

Goffman argued that face-to-face interaction has its own structural properties that cannot be reduced to the macro-sociological concepts of class, institution, or ideology. The interaction order is a moral order — violations produce shame, embarrassment, or anger. It is also a spatial order: territories of the self (personal space, possessional territory, conversational space) are defended by tacit norms.

## Relation to Tuan

Goffman and Tuan are complementary rather than directly in dialogue. Tuan provides the phenomenological account of how spatial values are derived from the body and elaborated by culture; Goffman provides the micro-sociological account of how those values are enacted and policed in face-to-face encounters. Together they explain both why front/back spatial distinctions exist (body schema, [[body-as-spatial-axis]]) and how they are maintained in practice (interaction order, face-work).
