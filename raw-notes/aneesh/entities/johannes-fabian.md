# Johannes Fabian (b. 1937)

German-American anthropologist, long associated with the University of Amsterdam. His major theoretical work is *Time and the Other: How Anthropology Makes Its Object* (Columbia, 1983).

## Core argument of *Time and the Other*

Fabian argues that twentieth-century anthropology systematically placed its objects of study — the "other" societies it observed — in a **different time** from the time of the observer. The anthropologist returned from fieldwork and wrote up the "ethnographic present" of an "other" who was, in the writing, always already part of the West's past.

> "Anthropology . . . by placing 'those who are observed' in a different time from 'the Time of the observer' . . . sanctioned an ideological process by which relations between the West and its Other, between anthropology and its object, were conceived not only as difference, but as distance in space and Time." (Fabian 1983: 147, quoted Massey 2005: 69)

The move was a "cosmological myth" of long persistence:

> "The all-pervading denial of coevalness . . . ultimately is expressive of a cosmological myth of frightening magnitude and persistency." (Fabian 1983: 35, quoted 70)

## Coevalness as antidote

Fabian's positive concept is [[coevalness]] — the recognition that the world's societies are radical contemporaries, none assignable to an earlier or later stage. Coevalness is a *project*, not a fact already obtaining: it must be actively undertaken against the default reflexes of inherited political-cosmological language.

## On the political/physical analogy

Fabian's most cutting passage (quoted Massey 2005: 73) reads the "occupation of space" rule of classical mechanics — two bodies cannot occupy the same space at the same time — as a transposable principle of colonial politics. When Western bodies came to occupy the autochthonous body's space, three solutions were tried: removal (North America, Australia), partition (apartheid South Africa), or **temporalisation** ("assign to the conquered populations a different Time"). The third has been the dominant strategy.

## How Massey uses Fabian

[[doreen-massey]]'s Part Three of *For Space* (2005) draws heavily on Fabian for:

- The diagnosis of [[spatialising-modernity]] (Ch. 6).
- The naming and elaboration of [[coevalness]] as the political-ontological remedy.
- The argument that [[aspatial-globalisation]] (Ch. 8) repeats the modernist denial of coevalness in contemporary dress.
- The "physical Time" / political-cosmology critique that connects natural science models to political imagination.

Massey extends Fabian beyond anthropology into political geography and the general philosophy of space — but the conceptual debt is explicit and substantial.
