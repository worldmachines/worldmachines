# Ming Dynasty Grand Secretaries

The **Grand Secretaries** (*Daxueshi*) of the Ming dynasty represent one of history's cleanest examples of [[Antimeme|antimemetic]] administrative power: formal insignificance masking decisive real-world authority.

## Origin

The **Hongwu Emperor** (r. 1368–1398) abolished the Central Secretariat to consolidate personal control over all government decisions. His intent: no intermediary between emperor and governance.

The consequence: the volume of correspondence and decisions was overwhelming. The emperor had to handle thousands of memorials personally.

**Solution**: appoint a few "grand secretaries" who held low formal rank and officially "merely recorded imperial decisions." They were initially personal assistants to the emperor, not powerful officials.

## The Word "Merely"

Sathe notes: "If merely were a boxer he would be a heavyweight."

In practice, grand secretaries:
- Drafted responses and decisions for the emperor's approval
- Shaped which matters reached the emperor's attention
- Interpreted imperial will when instructions were ambiguous
- Accumulated enormous personal influence through proximity to power

Over time (especially under weaker emperors), grand secretaries effectively governed the empire while maintaining the formal fiction that they "only recorded."

## Classic Antimemetic Pattern

The Ming grand secretary system illustrates the standard [[Antimeme|antimemetic]] mechanism:
- Formal title obscures actual power
- Deniability is structural ("just following orders" / "just recording decisions")
- Real authority is exercised through framing, drafting, and gatekeeping
- The emperor (memetically visible) absorbs all accountability
