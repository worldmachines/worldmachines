# Susanne Langer (1895–1985)

**Field**: Philosophy of mind, aesthetics, philosophy of symbolism
**Nationality**: American
**Key works**: *Philosophy in a New Key* (1942); *Feeling and Form* (1953); *Mind: An Essay on Human Feeling* (3 vols., 1967–82)

## Role in Tuan's *Space and Place*

Tuan cites Langer twice in Ch. 2, both times to anchor the epistemological premise that reality is a construction of experience rather than a direct recording of it.

**First citation** (Tuan 2002, p. 9): "The world of physics is essentially the real world construed by mathematical abstractions, and the world of sense is the real world construed by the abstractions which the sense organs immediately furnish." This passage establishes that all knowing — including scientific knowing — is a form of construction; the experiential perspective is not therefore inferior to the physical sciences but differently constructed.

**Second citation** (p. 11): Langer's observation that odors of decay are *memento mori* to grown people but carry no such meaning to animals and small children — illustrating that sensory meaning is culturally and developmentally acquired, not biologically fixed.

## Langer's Broader Contribution

Langer argued that **symbolization** is the fundamental human mental act — not rational inference, not sensation, but the construction and use of symbols. This frames her philosophy of art (art as "the creation of forms symbolic of human feeling") and her philosophy of mind (feeling as the subjective dimension of biological process). For Langer, the symbol bridges sensation and meaning; it is what allows the fragmentary data of the senses to constitute a "world."

This is directly relevant to Tuan's claim about [[symbolization-and-place]]: that humans can be attached to places too large to experience directly because symbols — names, maps, flags, stories — constitute those places as experiential realities.

## Connection to Tuan's Project

Langer gives Tuan's experiential approach its philosophical backbone: if reality is always already a construct of experience (sensory or symbolic), then the experiential perspective is not a soft supplement to hard geography but the foundational inquiry. The geographer's map and the longtime resident's muscular memory are both constructions; neither is the "real" city unmediated.
