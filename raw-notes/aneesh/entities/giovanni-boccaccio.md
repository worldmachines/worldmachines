# Giovanni Boccaccio

**Giovanni Boccaccio** (1313–1375) was an Italian author, poet, and early humanist, best known for [[the-decameron]] (c. 1349–1353), the hundred-tale frame narrative set during and after the 1348 Florentine plague. With Dante and Petrarch he is counted among the *tre corone* — the "three crowns" of fourteenth-century Florentine letters — and he is a central figure in the early development of European vernacular prose.

## The Decameron

Boccaccio's principal work and the source of his standing in this wiki. *The Decameron* frames a hundred stories inside a single fiction: seven young women and three young men (the **brigata**) flee plague-struck Florence to a country estate, govern themselves as a rotating monarchy, and tell ten stories on each of ten days. The frame stages storytelling as consolation and as a form of world-maintenance carried out from inside a curated refuge — the reading developed in [[the-brigata-as-thicket]].

Several features of Boccaccio's authorial stance recur across the work and are worth recording:

- **Dedication to women readers.** In the Proem he dedicates the book explicitly to lovesick ladies, arguing they need its consolation most because, unlike men, they cannot ride, hunt, trade, or travel to discharge their melancholy and "abide most time enmewed in the narrow compass of their chambers." The dedication doubles as an argument about the gendered asymmetry of relief.
- **Storytelling as medicine and moral instruction.** Stories are framed as both pleasurable solace and "useful counsel" — teaching what is to be eschewed and what pursued — with the two goals held co-equal rather than ranked.
- **Defence of secular, vernacular literature.** In the prologue to Day Four (the "green geese" half-tale) he defends writing about women and desire as natural, literary, and consistent with serving the Muses: "The Muses are women." The Author's Conclusion extends the defence, locating moral responsibility in the reader's disposition rather than the text — "Corrupt mind never understood word healthily."
- **Anticlericalism.** A sustained satirical thread runs through the tales (the friar-stories, Tedaldo's long sermon in III.7); in the Conclusion, accused of being "venomous" about the friars, Boccaccio is the one charge he refuses to retract, converting his defence into a renewed attack.

## Place in the World Machines framework

In this wiki's framework, the *Decameron* is read as one of the **early catalogs of proliferating voices** that mark the emergence of the [[modernity-machine]] — alongside Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales* — works in which "social life becomes polyphonic" as feudal and religious monopolies loosen. Boccaccio's frame is also the supplying instance for the synthesis [[the-brigata-as-thicket]]: he supplies an unusually precise pre-modern depiction of a [[The Thicket]] constructed inside a [[gramsci-gap]], complete with its formation, its rules, and its deliberate dissolution.
