# Murray S. Davis

Murray S. Davis was an American sociologist at Northern Illinois University, best known for his 1971 paper "That's Interesting! Towards a Phenomenology of Sociology and a Sociology of Phenomenology" in *Philosophy of the Social Sciences*.

## Contribution

Davis argued that what makes a theory great is not truth but *interestingness* — its capacity to deny a specific aspect of the audience's taken-for-granted assumptions. He proposed:

1. **The Index of the Interesting** — a 12-category taxonomy of the logical moves by which propositions can deny assumptions ([[Index of the Interesting]])
2. **The assumption-ground** — the phenomenological concept of the baseline belief web against which propositions operate ([[Assumption-Ground]])
3. **The Sociology of the Interesting** — a proposed field studying the dynamic mechanisms of belief-system transformation ([[Sociology of the Interesting]])

## Intellectual Context

Davis drew on Garfinkel's ethnomethodology and Schutz's phenomenology for the concept of the assumption-ground, and on the sociology of knowledge tradition (Mannheim, Merton) for the claim that intellectual reception is socially structured. His innovation was to shift attention from truth-conditions to attention-conditions: not "Is this theory true?" but "Why does anyone care?"

## Relevance

The paper has become a classic in sociology of science and organizational theory, influencing how scholars think about theory construction, academic rhetoric, and the social dynamics of knowledge production. Karl Weick cited Davis's work in his own arguments about theorizing as process.
