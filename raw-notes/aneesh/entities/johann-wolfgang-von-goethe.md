# Johann Wolfgang von Goethe (1749–1832)

## Biographical Context

- Poet, novelist, scientist, statesman
- Dabbled in gardening (vegetable and botanical gardens at his Weimar residence)
- Key texts for [[Kulturkritik]]: *Elective Affinities* (1809), *Faust I & II*, "On Dilettantism" (*SW18*), the poem "Der Spaziergang" (echoing Schiller's similar poem)
- Correspondence with his sister Cornelia (1767): "Don't laugh about this seemingly foolish philosophy, the seemingly paradoxical pronouncements, they are the most splendid truths"

## Goethe's Relationship to Rousseau

Goethe engages Rousseau's culture-critical paradox through **Wiederholte Spiegelungen** ("repeated mirrorings") — a *Steigerung* (heightening, intensification) rather than a dialectical synthesis. His rhetorical mode creates "a pulsating rather than a synthetic process of sublation."

Unlike Rousseau and Schiller, Goethe does not believe in the utopian potential of the English garden. For him it is *dystopia*, not utopia.

## Rejection of the English Garden

Goethe's objections to the English garden are twofold and fundamental:

1. **Subjective/aesthetic**: It "foregoes objectivity for the sake of subjectivity" — it "perpetuates the current bad habit of wanting to be unrestrained and lawless, and of fantasizing capriciously in aesthetic matters for it can, unlike the other arts, neither be corrected nor disciplined" (*SW18*, pp. 760–62)

2. **Ontological**: It "destroys both art and nature. It makes do with semblance because it turns reality into fantasia," and "diminishes the sublime of nature and nullifies it by simulating it" (*SW18*, p. 760)

Goethe calls its mischievous design "naturspäßig" — as if nature were playing a practical joke. For Hegel (sharing Goethe's view), its picturesque variety precludes "eine in sich seiende Seele" — peace of mind.

## *Elective Affinities* as Laboratory

*Elective Affinities* (1809) is Goethe's test of the English garden ideal. Its main English garden project culminates in the deaths of all parties:
- Nature dies: Ottilie, Eduard, their child Otto
- Culture dies: Charlotte and the Captain (their "Entsagung"/renunciation)

"Sentimentalizing the garden meets with scorn; an attempt at turning three fish-breeding ponds back into a mountain lake is doomed" (*EA*, p. 135). The English garden, whether as cultivation (Schiller's remedy) or naturalization (Rousseau's remedy), results in death.

Goethe's diagnosis: "You cannot walk beneath palm trees with impunity" (*EA*, p. 212). "Free" becomes associated with "libidinal abandon and renunciation"; "wholesome" with "pathological insatiability and anorexia."

## Faust and the *Cura* Myth

Faust's English garden vision (the famous "Tarry a while" moment of damnation, lines 1699–1701) is spoken "in anticipation" and is the product of magic — an illusion. But Goethe introduces a deeper figure: **Sorge** (Care/Cura), drawn from Hyginus's *Fabulae*, where Cura shapes homo from humus — the original gardener and cultivator. When Faust rejects Care, she blinds him, forcing reliance on his "radiant light ... in my inner being" (line 11,500). The blindness reveals that genuine care — a life of concern for something other than oneself — is what a gardener requires. Faust becomes an English gardener only in blindness.

## *Kunstwahrheit* vs. *Naturwirklichkeit*

Goethe's aesthetic model is Claude Lorrain, whose landscape paintings have "the highest truth, but no trace of actuality." This distinction — *Kunstwahrheit* (artistic truth) vs. *Naturwirklichkeit* (natural verisimilitude) — is central:

Lorrain's landscapes "appear very natural to us, but we vainly seek in the actual world" their entirety — "it is the fruit of his own mind; ... of the aspiration of a fructifying divine breath." The ideal is available only in art, not in the English garden's simulation of nature.

Goethe: in Lorrain "nature proclaims herself to be eternal" (*SW22*, p. 534). "Semblance" becomes "symbol," and *wahrhaft* (authentic) is analogous to *Wahrheit* (truth).

## The *Steigerung* Principle

For Goethe, nature and culture are "interlocked in a dynamic upward movement of irreducible polar opposites" — they never blend. They are not synthesized but *intensified* through dynamic relationship. This is why Faust ends not with resolution but with "hinan" (upward/onward) — the play is "quite incommensurable" (Goethe to Eckermann). The final word is directional, not conclusive.

The English garden's error is precisely that it pretends to be a synthesis. Goethe sees this as aesthetically and metaphysically dishonest.
