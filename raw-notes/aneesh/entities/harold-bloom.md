# Harold Bloom

## Biographical Sketch

Harold Bloom (1930–2019) was Sterling Professor of Humanities at Yale University. Born in the Bronx to Yiddish-speaking immigrant parents; grew up speaking Yiddish before English. Famous for a prodigious memory (reportedly able to recite most of what he had read) and an extraordinary reading speed.

Formative experiences: early reading of William Blake and Hart Crane, which shaped his lifelong investments in Romantic poetry and visionary literature. In later life, close friendship with novelist Joshua Cohen, whose stories of Bloom became the basis of Cohen's Pulitzer-winning novel *The Netanyahus*.

## Intellectual Trajectory

### Early Career: Romantic Revival
1950s–60s: distinguished scholarly work reviving the Romantic poets — Shelley, Blake, Keats — at a time when T.S. Eliot's critical authority had somewhat suppressed the Romantics in favor of metaphysical poetry.

### The Anxiety of Influence (1973)
Bloom's decisive self-invention as an original theorist. Introduced the theory that poetic influence is driven by an agonistic struggle against precursors — sublimated competition for literary immortality. The six revisionary ratios (clinamen, tessera, kenosis, daemonization, askesis, apophrades) describe the moves strong poets make against their predecessors. See [[Anxiety of Influence]].

### "Divorce" from Yale English Department (1970s)
Bloom's growing alienation from what he called the "School of Resentment" — the various strands of critical theory and historicism (deconstruction, New Historicism, feminist theory, postcolonial theory, New Multiculturalism) that came to dominate academic literary study. He positioned himself as defending aesthetic value against political or ideological reduction of literary criticism.

### The Western Canon (1994)
Bloom's public manifesto: a defense of aesthetic criteria for canonical selection, an attack on the "School of Resentment," and an extended celebration of 26 canonical writers organized by Vico's historical ages (Aristocratic, Democratic, Chaotic). This made him the figurehead of the "culture wars" in academia and the wider culture. See [[Western Canon]].

### Late Popular Phase
*Shakespeare: The Invention of the Human* (1998), *Genius* (2002), *The Best Poems of the English Language* (2004) — didactic, popular works for general audiences. Widely mocked by academics; deeply loved by many private readers.

## Intellectual Character

The source essay characterizes Bloom as fundamentally a **mystical thinker** — a claim that cuts against both his academic reputation and his popular image:
- His criteria for aesthetic value are essentially religious: what *lasts*, what continues to nourish human souls across centuries
- His framework draws on Kabbalah (interpretation as creative distortion of sacred text)
- His late work reads like an evangelist's sermon — "a beautiful, sad dinosaur" whose arguments are "the hortatory orations of a Gothic cathedral in ruins"

## Academic Reception

Largely negative, for reasons that are as much sociological as intellectual:
- Academics resented his public audience
- His polemics against theory were seen as politically retrograde
- His claims about Shakespeare as "inventor of the human" were easily satirized
- His canonical judgments were treated as arrogant self-evidence

But the source essay observes: academics' rejection of Bloom mirrors their need to limit Shakespeare's preeminence — both are symptoms of the anxiety Bloom himself described.

## What's Missing in the Standard Bloom Critique

- His critics rarely engage seriously with the psychological mechanism of the [[Anxiety of Influence]] — they attack the politics
- His defense of aesthetic autonomy is not obviously wrong even if his specific canonical judgments are contestable
- The "mystical" dimension of his thought — which is what made him inspiring to non-academic readers — is almost entirely absent from academic treatments
