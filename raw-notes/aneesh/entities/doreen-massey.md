# Doreen Massey

British human geographer (1944–2016), professor at the Open University. Her major work *For Space* (2005) and *Space, Place and Gender* (1994) constitute one of the most rigorous relational accounts of space in contemporary social theory.

## Core Position

Massey argues against two dominant conceptions of space:
1. **Space as container**: the Euclidean/Newtonian view of space as a pre-given, empty vessel within which entities are placed and events occur.
2. **Space as fixed and static**: any view that treats spatial relations as settled and given rather than produced and contested.

Her constructive thesis, summarized in *For Space* (2005: 9):

> "First, ... we recognize space as the product of interrelations; as constituted through interactions, from the immensity of the global to the intimately tiny. Second, ... we understand space as the sphere of the possibility of the existence of multiplicity in the sense of contemporaneous plurality; as the sphere therefore of coexisting heterogeneity. Without space, no multiplicity; without multiplicity, no space. Third, ... we recognize space as always under construction. Precisely because space on this reading is a product of relations-between, relations which are necessarily embedded material practices which have to be carried out, it is always in the process of being made. It is never finished; never closed."

## Relation to Bryant

Bryant explicitly draws on Massey in Ch. 6 of *Onto-Cartography*, citing *For Space* as a near-ally while noting she associates space too closely with narrative and expression rather than machinic-material processes. Her three theses on space align closely with his topological conception:
- Space as product of interrelations = Bryant's "space arising from machine-paths"
- Contemporaneous multiplicity = Bryant's plural spatial fields (no single all-containing space)
- Always under construction = Bryant's topological fields as milieus of becoming, never static

## Feminist Geography

Massey's work on *Space, Place and Gender* (1994) argues that spatial organization and gender inequality are mutually constitutive. "A global sense of place" is her concept of places as always-porous articulations of broader geographical relations rather than bounded, fixed local identities. Places are *processes*, not things.

## Positive Theory of Place (For Space, Part Four)

Part Four of *For Space* (Chs. 11–12, "Slices through space" and "The elusiveness of place") is the constructive heart of the book. Having demolished rival accounts in earlier parts, Massey builds her positive theory of space and place.

### Space as Cross-Section through Trajectories

The dominant Western map presents space as a completed horizontal surface. Massey's alternative: any "slice through time" would be "full of holes, of disconnections, of tentative half-formed first encounters" (2005: 107). Space is the simultaneous multiplicity of ongoing trajectories — "Loose ends and ongoing stories" (2005: 107). See [[slices-through-space]], [[loose-ends-and-missing-links]], [[the-chance-of-space]].

### The Travelling Imaginations Critique

The imagination of travel as "crossing space" — whether colonial conquest, commuting, or going home — depoliticises the encounter by reducing others to features of a surface rather than ongoing histories. Hernan Cortés descending on Tenochtitlán is not crossing space but meeting another imperial story-arc. The migrant who imagines home as frozen in the past robs it of its ongoing independent history. See [[nostalgia-as-spacialization]].

### Places as Events and Throwntogetherness

> "Places not as points or areas on maps, but as integrations of space and time; as spatio-temporal events." (Massey 2005: 130)

> "what is special about place is precisely that throwntogetherness, the unavoidable challenge of negotiating a here-and-now (itself drawing on a history and a geography of thens and theres)." (Massey 2005: 140)

Place is an event: an open, internally multiple, non-coherent conjuncture of trajectories (human and nonhuman) that demands negotiation. See [[places-as-events]], [[throwntogetherness]], [[trajectories-as-stories-so-far]].

### The Geological Extension: Immigrant Rocks

The Lake District meditation extends the event-theory to geology. Skiddaw's rocks were deposited in the southern hemisphere 500 million years ago and have been migrating northward since. "Immigrant rocks: the rocks of Skiddaw are immigrant rocks, just passing through here, like my sister and me only rather more slowly" (Massey 2005: 136). Even "timeless" nature is trajectory-in-process. See [[the-lake-district-as-meeting-place]], [[immigrant-rocks]].

## Part Two of *For Space* — Critical Engagements

Part Two (Chs. 2–5) is Massey's diagnosis of how twentieth-century philosophy has misconceived space. She reads four traditions sympathetically-but-critically:

- **Bergson and process philosophy** — ally on time/becoming; structurally problematic on space (space gets all the properties Bergson rejects). See [[henri-bergson]], [[space-as-representation-critique]].
- **Structuralism (Lévi-Strauss, Althusser)** — the "prison-house of synchrony": space mistakenly equated with a-temporal closure. See [[claude-levi-strauss]], [[louis-althusser]], [[prison-house-of-synchrony]].
- **Laclau and de Certeau** — radical-democracy / everyday-life politics that nonetheless reserves "space" for ideological closure or static structure. See [[ernesto-laclau]], [[michel-de-certeau]], [[coupure-and-causal-closure]].
- **Derrida and deconstruction** — most charitable: spacing as positive force in *différance*; but the textual horizontality and the negative ontology pull back from full positive multiplicity. See [[jacques-derrida]], [[horizontalities-of-deconstruction]], [[positive-versus-negative-multiplicity]].

Part Two ends with the constructive thesis: space as **the dimension of multiplicity** — co-implicated with time, integral to the production of becoming. See [[space-as-the-dimension-of-multiplicity]], [[dynamic-simultaneity]], [[the-co-implication-of-space-and-time]], [[massey-for-space-part-2-unpromising-associations]].

## Part Three — The Political Geography of Modernity and Globalisation

Part Three (Chs. 6–10) is the most explicitly political section before Part Five. Massey diagnoses **modernity** and **neoliberal globalisation** as repeating a single structural move: the conversion of coexisting spatial difference into a single temporal queue. Her key terms here:

- [[cosmopolitan-time-queue]] — the figure that ranks societies as "ahead" or "behind" on one developmental line.
- [[coevalness]] — the antidote, drawn from Fabian (1983): radical contemporaneity. See [[johannes-fabian]].
- [[spatialising-modernity]] — the diagnostic move (Ch. 6).
- [[aspatial-globalisation]] — the neoliberal narrative that pretends to transcend space (Ch. 8).
- [[power-geometry]] — Massey's signature concept for the differential capacity to negotiate flows.
- [[space-cannot-be-annihilated-by-time]] — the riposte to the "death of distance" trope (Ch. 9).
- [[happenstance-juxtaposition]] — the productive friction of material co-presence that gated communities and digital tribes try to escape.

See [[massey-for-space-part-3-living-in-spatial-times]].

## Part Five — A Relational Politics of the Spatial

Part Five (Chs. 13–15) is the political climax: politics is constitutively spatial. Key contributions:

- [[the-politics-of-the-event-of-place]] (Ch. 13) — place as the unchosen meeting of trajectories, demanding negotiation. Anchored by the [[hamburgs-oldest-immigrant]] vignette.
- [[no-rules-of-space-and-place]] (Ch. 14) — no general topographic principle yields the right political answer; the specific [[power-geometry]] of each situation must be assessed. Coined the term [[spatial-fetishism]] for the contrary error.
- [[making-and-contesting-time-spaces]] (Ch. 15) — every time-space (lab, home, city) is selectively open and closed; the political question is *what* is admitted/excluded and on *what* terms.
- [[politics-of-connectivity]] — the outward face: local struggles linked through *constructed equivalences* (Laclau & Mouffe), exemplified by Via Campesina.

See [[massey-for-space-part-5-relational-politics]].

## Sathe's Use in "We Need Homes in the Delta Quadrant" (2025)

In §II, Sathe invokes Massey to correct any reading of space as static or neutral: "Doreen Massey reminds us that space is not inert. It is relational, always in flux, co-constituted by those who traverse it." This serves to establish that space is not a passive background awaiting human place-making action but is itself already relational and dynamic — which makes the act of interruption (building a home, making a place) all the more significant. Space is not blank canvas; it is charged with existing relations. Place-making is therefore always a negotiation with existing conditions, not creation ex nihilo.
