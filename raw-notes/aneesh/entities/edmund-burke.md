# Edmund Burke (1729–1797)

**Born**: Dublin, 1729
**Died**: Beaconsfield, England, 1797
**Key works**: *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (1790); *A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful* (1757)

## Intellectual Profile

Burke is the English-tradition counterpart to the German counter-Enlightenment figures — Hamann, Herder, Möser. Though the influence was not direct (Berlin notes the connection was more structural than genealogical), Burke operates on the same intuitions: that society is not a rational contract between individuals but a living organism of immemorial custom, sentiment, and tradition that rational analysis can destroy but not create.

## Key Ideas

### Against the Trading Company Model of Society

Burke's *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (1790) founded its attack on the French revolutionaries on an appeal to "the myriad strands that bind human beings into a historically hallowed whole, contrasted with the utilitarian model of society as a trading-company held together solely by contractual obligations, the world of 'sophisters, oeconomists, and calculators'" — who are "blind and deaf to the unanalysable relationships that make a family, a tribe, a nation, a movement, any association of human beings held together by something more than a quest for mutual advantage."

### The Wisdom of Tradition

Traditions and institutions embody accumulated wisdom that exceeds what any individual rational plan can capture. "What men call superstition and prejudice are but the crust of custom which by sheer survival has shown itself proof against the ravages and vicissitudes of its long life" (Berlin paraphrasing Maistre, but the point applies equally to Burke). Reformers who discard this accumulated wisdom lose the shield that protects a people's national existence, their spirit, habits, memories, faith.

### Non-Rational Social Bonds

Burke stresses the value of "the individual, the peculiar (*das Eigentümliche*), the impalpable, and appeals to ancient historical roots and immemorial custom, to the wisdom of simple, sturdy peasants uncorrupted by the sophistries of subtle 'reasoners'." This emphasis has "strongly conservative and, indeed, reactionary implications" — Berlin acknowledges this without endorsement.

## Relation to the Counter-Enlightenment

Berlin places Burke in the broader counter-Enlightenment constellation alongside Hamann, Herder, and Möser — all defending "organic forms of social life" against rational reorganization. Hamann influenced Herder who influenced the *Sturm und Drang*; Burke operated independently in England but arrived at structurally similar positions.

Unlike Maistre, Burke did not advocate brutality or authoritarian suppression; he was respectful of Church and State and the authority of aristocracies "sanctified by history." His is a conservative rather than a fascist counter-Enlightenment.

## What Berlin Does Not Say

Burke's actual record on Ireland and on colonial subjects is conspicuously absent from Berlin's discussion. Burke opposed the oppression of Ireland and criticized the East India Company's abuses — positions that complicate the simple picture of him as an unreflective traditionalist. Berlin focuses on the *Reflections* without engaging these tensions.
