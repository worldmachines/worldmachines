# William Baumol

**Born**: New York City, 1922
**Died**: 2017
**Affiliation**: Princeton University; NYU Stern School of Business
**Key works**: *Performing Arts: The Economic Dilemma* (with William Bowen, 1966); *The Free-Market Innovation Machine* (2002)

## Intellectual Profile

Baumol was an unusually versatile economist — prolific in industrial organization, innovation theory, and the economics of the arts. He is best known for two contributions:

1. **[[Baumol's Cost Disease]]** — developed with William Bowen in their 1966 study of the performing arts for the Kennedy administration. The study investigated why theaters and orchestras were perpetually financially precarious despite rising ticket prices and donor subsidies. Their answer reframed the problem: it's not mismanagement but an inescapable economic law. Labor-intensive arts cannot achieve productivity gains, yet must compete for labor in an economy where productivity gains elsewhere raise wages. Perpetual relative price inflation follows.

2. **Innovation theory** — Baumol argued that productive entrepreneurship requires favorable institutions; otherwise innovation energy flows into rent-seeking or destructive activities (military conquest, litigation). Less commonly cited but important for understanding why productivity gains cluster in some sectors.

## Origin of Cost Disease Research

Baumol developed cost disease empirically: "poking around backstages, sending questionnaires to theatergoers, and interviewing Broadway producers" — a reminder that foundational economic insights often begin with close observational work rather than abstract theorizing.

## Legacy

Cost disease has become a standard framework for understanding the economics of healthcare, education, and legal services — all sectors where "you can't automate the human interaction" logic applies. [[Alex Mayyasi]]'s 2026 essay extends the framework to explain structural drivers of [[Social Isolation]] in America.
