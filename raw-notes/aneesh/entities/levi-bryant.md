# Levi R. Bryant

## Biographical

American philosopher, associate professor (Collin College, Texas). Born 1969. Active blogger at *Larval Subjects* (larvalsubjects.wordpress.com) — an unusually prolific public-philosophy venue where Bryant worked out many of the ideas that became his books in real time, in dialogue with interlocutors such as Graham Harman, Timothy Morton, Ian Bogost, and others.

## Intellectual Lineage

Bryant's trajectory is one of deliberate apostasy. He began as a committed post-structuralist and Lacanian — trained in Žižek, Derrida, Adorno, and the structuralist tradition. His conversion, narrated in the introduction to *Onto-Cartography*, began around 2006 through encounters with Speculative Realism and Object-Oriented Ontology, and was crystallized — he reports, self-deprecatingly — by playing *SimCity 4*.

Key sources and influences:

- **Gilles Deleuze**: The concept of the machine, the assemblage, the virtual/actual distinction, and the anti-hylomorphic account of production. Bryant's ontology is substantially Deleuzian in vocabulary, though filtered through Anglo-American OOO concerns.
- **Bruno Latour**: Actor-network theory and the insistence that non-humans are genuine actors in social assemblages. Bryant shares Latour's flat ontology and his resistance to the nature/culture distinction.
- **Jacques Lacan**: Bryant's first book (*Difference and Givenness*, 2008) was a Deleuzian reading of Kant; he remained a Lacanian for years before the OOO turn. Elements of Lacanian structure-theory survive in Bryant's account of incorporeal machines and the plane of expression.
- **Graham Harman**: A close interlocutor within Speculative Realism; Harman's "dormant objects" concept is explicitly adopted in *Onto-Cartography*. Bryant and Harman disagree on withdrawal — Harman holds objects withdraw from all relation; Bryant's machines are fundamentally relational entities.
- **Manuel DeLanda**: On emergence without design, and on the geological / biological time-scales of machine formation.
- **Martin Heidegger** ([[martin-heidegger]]): Bryant is in dialogue with Heidegger's tool-analysis and his critique of the Cartesian subject/object divide, though he does not follow Heidegger's phenomenological anthropocentrism. The move from "object" to "machine" is partly a move away from Heideggerian Dasein-centrism.
- **Jane Bennett**: *Vibrant Matter* (2010) is cited approvingly as a fellow traveler in the recovery of thing-agency.
- **Maturana and Varela**: The biological concept of autopoiesis and structural coupling is foundational to Bryant's account of how machines relate.
- **Marshall McLuhan**: Bryant's post-human media ecology is built on an extended and corrected McLuhan — media as anything that modifies the becoming of another entity, not merely extensions of human sense-organs.

## Major Works

- *Difference and Givenness: Deleuze's Transcendental Empiricism and the Ontology of Immanence* (Northwestern, 2008) — early work; Deleuzian reading of Kant, written before the OOO turn.
- *The Democracy of Objects* (Open Humanities Press, 2011; open access) — Bryant's first systematic OOO work. Argues for a flat ontology in which all objects have equal ontological status; introduces the distinction between virtual proper being and local manifestations. The machine concept appears here in embryonic form.
- *Onto-Cartography: An Ontology of Machines and Media* (Edinburgh University Press, 2014) — the mature system. Replaces "object" with "machine" throughout; develops the post-human media ecology framework; addresses social and political consequences.

## Position in Speculative Realism

Bryant is a central figure in the Speculative Realism constellation that emerged from the 2007 Goldsmiths workshop (organized by Ray Brassier). The other main figures are:

- **Quentin Meillassoux** ([[quentin-meillassoux]]): argues for the contingency of necessity; Bryant shares the anti-correlationist commitment but not Meillassoux's mathematicism.
- **Graham Harman** ([[graham-harman]]): Object-Oriented Philosophy; closest collaborator.
- **Timothy Morton** ([[timothy-morton]]): ecological OOO; Bryant shares the ecological orientation.
- **Ian Bogost** ([[ian-bogost]]): "alien phenomenology"; cited in *Onto-Cartography* for his account of how machines observe the world.

Bryant is sometimes credited with coining or popularizing the acronym "OOO" (Object-Oriented Ontology) around 2009, distinguishing it from Graham Harman's slightly earlier "Object-Oriented Philosophy."

## Why Bryant Matters for This Wiki

Bryant's "machine" concept provides a distinct lens on the wiki's existing cluster of machine-concepts — [[world-machines]], [[liveness-machine]], [[divergence-machine]], [[legibility-as-machine-core]], [[dawn-day-dusk-machine-typology]]. That cluster, largely shaped by Venkatesh Rao's framework, uses "machine" as a historical-civilizational metaphor: a world machine is a contraption embodying the logic of an entire civilizational period.

Bryant's use is ontological rather than historical: *every entity without exception* is a machine, including rocks, novels, and constitutions. This creates both a resonance (both frameworks resist reducing the world to human discourse or human design) and a productive tension (Rao's machines are large-scale historical patterns; Bryant's machines are the granular ontological units from which any such patterns would have to be assembled). See [[synthesis/bryant-vs-rao-machine-concepts]] for extended comparison.

## The Larval Subjects Blog

The blog is philosophically significant as an unusual venue for working through technical ontological problems in public, with immediate feedback from other professional philosophers. Many arguments that appear in Bryant's books were first tested there. It exemplifies his conviction that philosophy should intervene in political and social questions rather than remaining academically isolated.

## Onto-Cartography (2014) — Full Argument

*Onto-Cartography: An Ontology of Machines and Media* (Edinburgh University Press, 2014) is the mature statement of Bryant's machine ontology. The book's argument unfolds across eight chapters with a polemical introduction and a programmatic conclusion.

**The polemic ("For a Renewal of Materialism," Intro)**: Bryant opens as a convert describing his apostasy. The "materialist turn" in critical theory paradoxically *evaporated* matter, leaving a *terme d'art* that meant only that phenomena were "historical, socially constructed, involves cultural practices, and is contingent" (p. 4). Bryant's central charge: discursivism could explain how fashion exercises symbolic power but could not theorize how turnstiles, mountain ranges, ocean currents, and atmospheric albedo organize social relations. Most urgently, discursivism made climate change *theoretically unthinkable*. The book's project is to recover the efficacy of non-discursive physical agencies without sacrificing the genuine insights of discursive critique.

**Machine ontology (Ch. 1–2)**: Every entity is a machine — Bryant's universal ontological category. A machine is "a system of operations that perform transformations on inputs thereby producing outputs" (p. 38). The shift is from substance to operation, from "what is it?" to "what does it do?" Bryant clears three prejudices: machines are not all rigid (caterpillars, novels, organizations are plastic), not all designed (most emerged from other machines, following DeLanda), and not all purposive (rocks, neutrinos, capybaras are *pluripotent* — they take on purposes when structurally coupled). The corporeal/incorporeal distinction (rocks vs. recipes), the rigid/plastic spectrum (Pythagorean theorems vs. great works of art), and the inanimate/animate/cognitive subdivision frame the machine taxonomy. *Virtual proper being* (a machine's powers) and *local manifestations* (its actual outputs in context) are split — we discover what a machine can do only by acting upon it.

**Alien phenomenology (Ch. 3)**: Every machine is *selectively open* to particular flows and *operationally closed* around its own operations. Mantis shrimp see eleven primary colors; bureaucracies see only forms; insurance companies see only profit/loss. "What Kant says here about cognitive-machines holds for all machines" (pp. 57–58). All machines relate behind firewalls. The political stakes: addressing a corporation through ethical persuasion is "like trying to explain calculus to a cat" (p. 73). *Thermodynamic politics* engages a machine in terms of its actual operational openness (boycotts, strikes), not its supposed openness to moral discourse.

**Machinic assemblages and entropy (Ch. 4)**: An assemblage is a new machine when its endo-relations produce emergent powers no part possesses individually (p. 77). The man-horse-stirrup-lance assemblage. Andy Clark's extended mind. Every machine is jerry-rigged; "no machine ever manages to totalize or master its parts" (p. 79). Entropy is the central problem: most machines are improbabilities that require constant negentropic operations to persist. Cities are paradigmatic: Hurricane Katrina shows what happens when negentropic flows are interrupted.

**The structure of worlds (Ch. 5)**: A world is "an ecology of loosely coupled machines linked by machines without any of these machines totalizing world" (p. 114). Worlds are flat (no transcendent ground), plural (machines external to relations; some are dark), dynamic, and historical. Worlds differ from machines because their elements are externally related — relations can be severed and reconfigured, which is why political revolution and climate catastrophe are both possible. Within worlds, two semi-autonomous planes: content (corporeal machines) and expression (incorporeal). Bryant's typology of objects within worlds — bright, dim, dark, rogue — is the basis for the political analysis to come. Most importantly: *worlding* names the active, ongoing process by which an ecology of machines constitutes its own field of relevance, independent of any subject's experience.

**Topologies of space and time (Ch. 6)**: Space is not Newtonian container but *topological field* arising from machine relations — Atlantic City casinos topologically remote from low-income neighborhoods despite metric proximity (p. 144). Time is plural: "the rate at which a machine can engage in operations" (p. 158). The political consequence: temporal mismatch between machines (climate at geological rates, governance at electoral rates, news media at cycle rates) is the structural impediment to climate response. Memory introduces topological folding of time (Serres's crumpled handkerchief). *Pace is political*; fatigue is ontological; "creating time" is central to emancipation. Overdetermination closes the chapter — every local manifestation arises from a conjunction of causes, never one.

**Gravity (Ch. 7)**: Bryant's most important political concept. Replacing "power" with *gravity* — borrowing the Einsteinian move that bodies travel paths produced by mass, not forces. Gravity is the structural way one machine bends the field of action for others. The six object-types: dark (withdrawn, no manifestations), bright (overcoding the field — oil, the Sun, company scrip), satellite (captured in orbit), dim (Rancière's "part of no part"), black hole (no exit), rogue (reconfiguring assemblages). The brightest object today: oil. Heavy machines (infrastructure, energy, supply chains) exercise gravity that does not require belief; light machines (ideologies, narratives) exercise gravity through signification. Strategic implication: ideology critique alone cannot dislodge company scrip; "the question therefore is one of *building* alternatives" (p. 205). Quasi-objects (Serres's soccer ball) reframe the subject as transitory functional role. Events are catalytic becomings — supernovas of assemblage transformation.

**Geophilosophy (Ch. 8)**: The political payoff. Three practices: *cartography* (four maps — topographical, genetic, vector, modal), *deconstruction* (severing relations, both expressive and corporeal), and *terraformation* (building alternatives — Bryant's example: LETS mutual-credit systems, "flight in place"). The Earth is the non-transcendent horizon: "there is only nature; culture is itself a formation of nature" (p. 252). The first principle of onto-cartography: "avoid abstractions!" (p. 257). "Capitalism," "patriarchy," "racism" are shorthand for assemblages, not explanatory agencies. Critical theory's failure: it stops at deconstruction without terraforming. The framework synthesizes the linguistic turn, Marxist analysis, Foucault, McLuhan, Latour, Diamond, Braudel, the new materialists, and the developmental systems theorists into a single program for the climate-collapse era.

The synthesis claim of the book: *social ecologies are nothing more than how different types of machines are bound together in an assemblage* (p. 190) — and adequate political analysis therefore requires mapping which machines are coupled, what flows circulate between them, and where alternative assemblages might be terraformed.
