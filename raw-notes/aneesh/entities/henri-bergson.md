# Henri Bergson (1859–1941)

French philosopher, winner of the 1927 Nobel Prize in Literature. His philosophy centres on *durée* (duration) — the lived, continuous flow of time that resists discretization — against the "spatialized" time of science and measurement.

## Core Position

Bergson argued that the intellect distorts reality by spatialising it: cutting the continuous flow of experience into discrete, countable units. Genuine time — duration — is qualitative, continuous, and irreversible. Science and measurement impose a *spatial* grid on time, producing the illusion of a succession of identical instants.

Key texts engaged by the wiki: *Time and Free Will* (1910), *Matter and Memory* (1911), *Creative Evolution* (1911/1975).

## Massey's Engagement

[[doreen-massey]] treats Bergson in Part Two of *For Space* (2005) as an exemplar of a recurrent problem: thinkers who are allies on the importance of process and openness but who inadvertently characterise space as the enemy of those goods.

### What She Approves

Bergson's insistence on genuine becoming, real novelty, and the openness of the future resonates with Massey's Proposition 3 (space as always under construction). His attack on "empty time" — discrete instants that produce only succession, not genuine change — is a move Massey wants to make about space too. His famous "time prevents everything from being given at once" (1959: 1331) has, in Massey's dual, a spatial counterpart: space provides the condition for the relations that generate time.

### What She Critiques

Bergson's strategy required characterising space as static and discrete — the very thing that threatens time's dynamism:

> "The rejected time of instantaneous time-slices attracts the label 'spatial', as in: what is at stake for Bergson-Deleuze is 'the primacy of the heterogeneous time of [temporal] difference over the spatialized time of metrication.'" (Massey 2005: 22, citing Boundas 1996)

The consequence: space gets loaded with all the properties Bergson wants to escape (discreteness, stasis, fixity, reversibility). It becomes the permanent negative foil for time's positivity. "Everything is stacked on the side of duration." (Massey 2005: 24)

### The Inadvertent Opening

In *Creative Evolution*, Bergson recognised duration in external things — the world, not just consciousness, has duration. Massey reads this as an inadvertent opening toward her own position: if external things have duration, then the spatial configurations they produce also have it. Space can be "a simultaneity of stories-so-far" — a dimension of multiple durations, not a static grid.

> "That recognition of the duration in external things and thus the interpenetration, though not the equivalence, of space and time is an important aspect of the argument in this book." (Massey 2005: 24)

## Bergson and Classical Physics

Chapter 2 of *For Space* traces Bergson's polemic with classical mechanics (the time-reversible Newtonian physics of his day). Bergson needed time to be open and irreversible; the physics insisted on reversibility; his strategic move was to consign everything that physics did to time — pin it down, hold it still, write it as a path — to "space." Massey, drawing on Prigogine and Stengers (1984), notes that the physics Bergson was fighting has since changed (nonequilibrium thermodynamics, complexity, indeterminism). The polemical motive for the move has dissolved. See [[space-as-representation-critique]].

## Deleuze-Bergson

Massey frequently runs Bergson together with Deleuze (following Boundas 1996): both are committed to the primacy of continuous over discrete multiplicity. Gilles Deleuze intensifies Bergson's temporal commitments but does not fully resolve the space problem.

## Relation to Wiki

Massey's Bergson critique illuminates a general pattern: when thinkers build their argument against stasis and closure primarily on the side of *time*, they tend to dump all closure onto space. Laclau does this (space = ideological closure), de Certeau does this (space = scientific writing's blank slate), and Bergson does this (space = the static discrete multiplicity that threatens duration). The wiki's [[space-as-always-under-construction]] page is the direct counter to this pattern.
