# Humberto Maturana

**1928–2021.** Chilean biologist and cognitive scientist. With Francisco Varela: *Autopoiesis and Cognition* (1980), *The Tree of Knowledge* (1987). Solo: *Biology of Language* and numerous papers on cognition and epistemology.

## Core Contributions

**Autopoiesis**: The concept that living systems are self-producing networks of processes that continuously generate and regenerate their own components and boundaries. See [[autopoiesis]]. Maturana coined the term with Varela in the early 1970s to capture what distinguishes living from non-living organization.

**Biology of cognition**: For Maturana, cognition is not representation of an external world but a biological process of autopoietic self-maintenance. An organism "knows" its environment insofar as it maintains its organization in interaction with it. This is a radically non-representationalist account of mind — the observer does not passively receive information but actively constitutes what counts as relevant input.

**Structural coupling**: See [[structural-coupling]]. The mechanism by which autopoietic systems interact with their environments: operationally closed (internal operations refer to internal states) but structurally open (perturbations from the environment shape internal organization without determining it).

**Observer-dependence**: Maturana's most radical epistemological claim — all cognition is observer-relative. We cannot step outside the perspective of an organism to compare its representations with a mind-independent world. This is sometimes called "constitutive constructivism."

## Relation to Bryant

Bryant does not extensively cite Maturana in Chapter 4, but the concepts of autopoiesis and structural coupling are operative throughout. Bryant's account of how organic and social machines maintain themselves against entropy is essentially a description of autopoietic operation extended to the social domain. Bryant's "operational closure" and "structural openness" vocabulary are Maturanean.

The political extension — that mind-body assemblages coupled to different media possess genuinely different powers — follows from the structural coupling thesis: different couplings constitute different cognitive systems, not just differently equipped versions of the same cognitive system.
