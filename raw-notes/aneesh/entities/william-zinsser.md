# William Zinsser

**William Knowlton Zinsser** (1922–2015) was an American writer, editor, literary critic, and teacher. He taught writing at Yale and the New School. He is best known for *On Writing Well* (1976), one of the most widely used style guides for non-fiction prose.

## Key Works (as referenced)

### On Writing Well (1976, many editions)
The foundational text for non-fiction writing. Emphasizes clarity, simplicity, and the elimination of clutter. Zinsser's core argument: writing is thinking, and bad writing is unclear thinking made visible.

Referenced in both [[Divine Documentation]] and [[A Good Dictionary]]: Zinsser credits the **dictionary** as one of his key writing tools — the prompt that led to the [[1913 Webster's Dictionary]] thread.

### Writing to Learn (1988)
Argues that writing is not just a way to *communicate* ideas but to *discover* them. Writing as a tool of cognition, not just expression. Referenced in [[Divine Documentation]] as the first step in the staged reading sequence.

## Intellectual Position

Zinsser is in the plain-prose Anglo-American tradition: [[Strunk and White]] → Zinsser → modern clarity advocates. He is suspicious of jargon, passive voice, and abstraction. His approach is deeply empathetic — always asking what the reader needs, not what the writer wants to say.

This aligns with [[Documentation as Empathy]]: Zinsser's writing pedagogy is a form of documentation practice.
