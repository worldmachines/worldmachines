# Paul Carter

**Field**: Cultural history, spatial history, postcolonial theory
**Key work**: *The Road to Botany Bay: An Exploration of Landscape and History* (1988)
**Location**: Australia

## Relevance to Language and Place

Paul Carter is Tuan's most important scholarly reference for the argument that naming is constitutive of exploration — that place does not precede the journey but is created by it. *The Road to Botany Bay* is a spatial history of Australian colonization that focuses on the act of naming as the primary way in which explorers converted undifferentiated space into navigable territory.

Tuan draws on Carter's argument that explorers needed to name features in order to proceed: "to call this feature a 'mount' and that feature a 'river'" (Tuan 1991, p. 688; Carter 1988, 47–55). The names used were often inappropriate to Australian reality — English topographical terms that fit poorly with Australian landscape — but their functional inadequacy did not diminish their constitutive power. For the explorer, what mattered was not accuracy but navigability: a named feature becomes a waypoint; a waypoint enables a destination; a destination enables forward movement.

## *The Road to Botany Bay*

Carter's central thesis is that the history of Australian colonization cannot be understood through what he calls "imperial history" — the story of events and their consequences — but only through "spatial history": the history of how space was produced through language, movement, and naming. The book reads explorers' journals, naming practices, and cartographic conventions as primary acts of cultural production.

For Carter, the explorers did not discover a pre-existing Australia; they *produced* it through the spatial language of naming, mapping, and journaling. The route to Botany Bay was not a pre-existing path that Cook found — it was constituted by the naming and recording of waypoints along it. The map is not a representation of the territory; it is one of the acts through which the territory becomes humanly real.

This is a strong version of Tuan's thesis: not just that language *helps* constitute place but that place in the colonial context *is* the product of linguistic acts, with material transformation coming later as a secondary consequence.

## Postcolonial Implications

Carter's account has implications for understanding the violence of colonial naming: to name a feature with a European term was to incorporate it into a European spatial imagination while displacing whatever indigenous names and meanings the feature already carried. The constitutive power of naming was asymmetric — the explorer's name tended to persist and the indigenous name to disappear, not because the explorer's name was more accurate but because it was connected to the more powerful documentary infrastructure (journals, maps, official records).
