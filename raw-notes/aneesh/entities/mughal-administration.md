# Mughal Administration

The **Mughal Empire** (1526–1857) developed a sophisticated administrative structure that exemplifies [[Bureaucratic Diffusion of Responsibility]] and [[Antimeme|antimemetic]] governance design.

## Structure

The empire was divided into provinces (subas), each headed by a **subahdar** (governor). However, key provincial officials reported directly to the central Mughal court rather than to the subahdar:

- **Bakhshi** (military paymaster) — reported centrally
- **Sadr as-Sudr** (religious/charitable endowments) — reported centrally
- **Diwan** (finance minister) — reported centrally

This matrix structure served several functions:
1. Central control: the emperor maintained direct sight lines into provincial finances, military, and religious affairs
2. Divided loyalty: no provincial official owed full allegiance to the subahdar
3. Diffused accountability: when things went wrong, responsibility was distributed across multiple officials with competing loyalties

## Antimemetic Design

The complexity of Mughal administration made the locus of decision-making opaque even to other officials. External observers (and even the peasantry) could not easily identify who was responsible for specific policies or failures. This made the administrative machinery antimemetic: high-impact but hard to blame.

When popular anger arose, it was directed at visible figures — the emperor or the provincial governor — not at the distributed machinery of bakhshis, sadrs, and diwans doing the actual work of governance.

See [[Memetic vs Antimemetic Structures]] and [[royalty-administration-and-antimemetics|Royalty, Administration, and Antimemetics]].

## Historical Context

The Mughal system borrowed from and adapted earlier Islamic administrative traditions (Sultanate Delhi, Timurid Persia). The structure evolved across emperors: Akbar's administrative reforms (Ain-i-Akbari system) are the most documented version.
