# Michel Serres

French philosopher (1930–2019), professor at Stanford and the Académie française. Serres worked at the intersection of philosophy, science, and literature, developing accounts of communication, noise, disorder, and the genesis of objects that anticipate many themes in contemporary new materialism.

## Core Ideas Relevant to Bryant

### Vortexes and Dynamic Stability

Serres argues that all things are vortexes — dynamic stabilities arising from flow rather than fixed substances. A child's spinning top is paradigmatic: it is stable *because* it is in motion, not despite it.

> "Vortex[es] ... [are] none other than the primitive form of the construction of things, of nature in general." (Serres 2000: 6, quoted in Bryant p. 142)

Stability is the improbable thing requiring explanation; motion is ontologically primitive.

### The Crumpled Handkerchief: Topological Time

Serres's most influential metaphor for time (developed with Bruno Latour in *Conversations on Science, Culture, and Time*, 1995):

> "If you take a handkerchief and spread it out in order to iron it, you can see in it certain fixed distances and proximities... Then take the same handkerchief and crumple it, by putting it in your pocket. Two distant points suddenly are close, even superimposed. If, further, you tear it in certain places, two points that were close can become very distant. This science of nearness and rifts is called topology, while the science of stable and well-defined distances is called metrical geometry." (Serres and Latour 1995: 60, quoted in Bryant p. 162)

Linear, metric time assumes the past simply recedes from the present at uniform rate. Topological time — the crumpled handkerchief — allows remote past events to be immediately present while recent events are topologically distant.

### Noise and Order

In *The Parasite* (2007) and elsewhere, Serres argues that noise and disequilibrium are not merely disruptions of order but generative conditions for new order. This is why Bryant insists positive and negative feedback should be treated descriptively, not normatively — noise can produce organization.

### Flows Circulating on Paths

Space, for Serres, is structured by constraint. All movement is related to stability; flows circulate on paths; objects are entanglements in the void that hinder or redirect flows. Bryant draws on this directly: "flows circulate on paths" (Serres, ibid.: 51, quoted in Bryant p. 143). Space is not empty but populated by varying densities of machines.

## Serres and Latour

Serres's collaborative book with Bruno Latour (*Conversations on Science, Culture, and Time*, 1995) is particularly important for Bryant's temporal topology. It bridges Serres's philosophy of science with Latour's actor-network theory.
