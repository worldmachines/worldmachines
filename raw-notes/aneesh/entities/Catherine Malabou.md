# Catherine Malabou

**Born**: 1959, Algiers
**Field**: Continental philosophy; philosophy of neuroscience
**Institutional affiliation**: Kingston University (London); previously UCI
**Key work in this wiki**: *What Should We Do with Our Brain?* (2008, French 2004)

## Core Contribution

Malabou develops a philosophical account of **neuroplasticity** — the brain's capacity not merely to adapt to stimuli (flexibility) but to give and take form actively (plasticity). She distinguishes these rigorously:

- **Flexibility**: receiving and adapting to external forms; the brain as responsive but ultimately passive
- **Plasticity**: actively shaping *and* being shaped; the giving of form as well as the taking

The distinction is more than descriptive — it is normative. Malabou argues that dominant ideologies (managerial capitalism) demand *flexibility* while suppressing *plasticity*. Flexibility is plasticity "without its genius."

## Intellectual Background

Malabou trained with Derrida and has an extensive engagement with Hegel — she reads Hegel's dialectic as a proto-theory of brain plasticity. She brings neuroscience into dialogue with European continental philosophy rather than treating them as separate domains.

## Sathe's Use

In [[The End of Identity: AI, Plasticity, and the Divergence Machine]], Sathe deploys the flexibility/plasticity distinction as the key to navigating the [[divergence-machine]]:

- The modernity machine required flexibility — bending to centralized systems
- The divergence machine strips those systems — leaving agents with raw plasticity and no feedback loop
- The response cannot be better flexibility; it must be genuine plasticity: actively giving new forms without a centralized system defining what they mean
