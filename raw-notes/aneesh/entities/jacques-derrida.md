# Jacques Derrida (1930–2004)

French philosopher of Algerian-Jewish origin; founder of deconstruction. The standard image — Derrida as the philosopher of disrupted texts, undecidable meanings, the trace — is what the wiki engages with primarily through [[doreen-massey]]'s critique in Part Two of *For Space*.

## Core Position (for Massey's purposes)

Derrida's *différance* names both **deferral** (the temporal postponement of full meaning) and **differentiation** (the spatial differentiation among signs that produces meaning). Spacing (*espacement*) is the operation that opens this gap — productive, positive, generative:

> "Spacing is a concept which also, but not exclusively, carries the meaning of a productive, positive, generative force . . . not only the interval, the space constituted between two things . . . but also spacing, the operation . . . . This movement is inseparable from temporization — temporalization and from *différance*." (Derrida 1972/1987, fn 42 to the Houdebine/Scarpetta interview, p. 106)

Unlike [[ernesto-laclau]] and [[michel-de-certeau]], Derrida does **not** use "space" as a mere residual category for what is left over after temporality is subtracted. He gives spacing an explicit, positive, ontological role.

## Massey's Critique

Two residual problems, despite this advance. See [[horizontalities-of-deconstruction]].

### 1. The textual imagination is horizontal

Deconstruction grew out of work on texts, and texts are two-dimensional. As deconstruction generalised — "the world is like a text" — the imagery of a flat surface to be deconstructed came along. Massey wants to invert: a text is like the rest of the world, not the other way round. The world is not a horizontal surface but a thick simultaneity of trajectories.

### 2. Spacing-as-expulsion is negative

Derrida defines spacing as "the movement of setting aside . . . what is set aside from itself, what interrupts every self-identity" (1972/1987: 107). Massey reads this as a *negative* ontology: difference is produced by disrupting a presumed One. She prefers the Spinoza–Bergson–Deleuze line that begins with positive plurality. The Houdebine exchange (in the *Positions* interview) shows Derrida himself slowly coming to recognise the alternative — what Houdebine calls the "positivity of this 'nothing' designated by spacing" (1972/1987: 92).

## What Massey Keeps from Derrida

- The principle that spacing names a productive operation, not just a static layout.
- The integration of spatial and temporal in *différance*.
- The general anti-essentialism that refuses self-presence and integrity.

## What She Modifies

The mode of seeing must shift from **looking at** a text to **recognising one's place within** continuous and multiple processes of emergence. Deconstruction's reflex is to gaze on; Massey's project asks for participation in the ongoing making.
