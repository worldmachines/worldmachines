# Ron Wakkary

**Nationality**: Canadian
**Field**: Design research, interaction design, philosophy of design
**Position**: Simon Fraser University; Eindhoven University of Technology
**Key work**: *Things We Could Design: For More Than Human-Centered Worlds* (MIT Press, 2021)

## Core Contribution

Wakkary argues against the dominance of **human-centered design** — the paradigm in which designed artifacts are understood as tools created by humans for human purposes. In its place, he proposes **more-than-human design**: an approach that takes seriously the agency, needs, and "perspectives" of non-human entities (materials, ecosystems, animals, artifacts themselves) in the design process.

His key claim: designed things exceed their designers' intentions. The artifact is not a completed expression of design intent; it is an ongoing participant in a relationship that extends beyond the moment of creation. Design that ignores this is systematically impoverished — it misses the generativity that emerges when things are allowed to become what they are through use.

## *Things We Could Design* (2021)

The book develops three interrelated arguments:

1. **Things as participants**: Artifacts are not passive objects but active participants in human life — they create habits, organize attention, generate needs, and resist certain uses while affording others. To design a thing is to introduce a participant, not to deploy a tool.

2. **Unselfconscious design**: Wakkary extends Alexander's insight: the best-loved and most enduring designed things often acquired their character through informal, unplanned use rather than through the specification process. The fridge-as-bulletin-board, the courtyard-as-marketplace — these emerge from the gap between designed intent and lived use.

3. **Design as initiation**: Design should be understood as initiating a relationship rather than completing an object. This requires humility about what the artifact will become and attentiveness to the ways users, contexts, and other non-human agents transform it.

## Relation to Alexander

Wakkary extends Christopher Alexander's critique of professional design culture. Where Alexander focused on the loss of the feedback loop between use and form — the consequence of separating the designer from the user — Wakkary extends this to the non-human: not only users but materials, ecosystems, and technological others must be participants in the design conversation.

## Sathe's Use in "We Need Homes in the Delta Quadrant" (2025)

Sathe pairs Wakkary with Alexander in §III to support the concept of [[custodianship-as-design-ethos]] and [[unselfconscious-design]]. Together they argue that good places emerge from the slow dance between structure and improvisation — and that the designer's responsibility extends beyond launch to the ongoing relationship between artifact and inhabitant. Wakkary's "things we could design" are precisely Sathe's launchpads: open structures that enable rather than foreclose the development of place.
