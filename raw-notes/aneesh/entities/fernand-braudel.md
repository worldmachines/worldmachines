# Fernand Braudel

French historian (1902–1985), central figure of the Annales school. His major works — *The Mediterranean and the Mediterranean World in the Age of Philip II* (1949) and *The Structures of Everyday Life* (1981, vol. 1 of *Civilization and Capitalism*) — develop a multi-temporal historiography that distinguishes the short term (events), medium term (conjunctures), and long term (*longue durée*: geological, climatic, and structural time).

## The Longue Durée

Braudel's most influential concept: history operates at multiple timescales simultaneously. The history of events (battles, reigns, crises) unfolds on the short-term scale of years and decades. But beneath it are structural temporalities — climate, geography, agricultural rhythms, demographic patterns — that change across centuries and millennia, and which shape the possibilities within which short-term events occur.

This is the empirical, historical correlate of Bryant's temporal topology: not all historical machines operate on the same timescale, and the mismatches between them are crucial to understanding historical outcomes.

## Grain Temporalities and Social Organization

Bryant's most extended use of Braudel in Ch. 6 of *Onto-Cartography* concerns the temporal rhythms of wheat and rice (*Structures of Everyday Life*, 1981: 108–57):

**Wheat** (one harvest per year, disease-vulnerable):
- Structures European societies around intense planting/harvest periods with long fallow intervals
- Necessitates food preservation technologies (granaries, salting, fermentation)
- Enables family-farm agriculture because large labor forces are not required
- Produces a seasonal rhythm of intense labor followed by relative leisure
- Leaves time for individual crafts and cottage industries

**Rice** (two to three harvests per year, labor-intensive):
- Requires large collective labor forces for planting and harvest
- Pushes societies toward collective farming organization
- Generates stratification: a priestly or noble class that tracks rice reserves and distributes them
- Leaves farmers with little time for non-agricultural activities
- Produces surplus that enables denser social stratification

The temporal rhythm of a grain is a machine that structures social organization — not metaphorically but materially. Grain temporality is a slow machine to which human social machines become structurally coupled.

## Braudel and Temporal Mismatch

The Braudel example illustrates Bryant's point about temporal mismatch: human institutions and practices (family farming, class structure, religious calendars, seasonal festivals) are shaped by the temporal machines of agricultural ecology, climate, and geography. Understanding why European and Asian societies organized differently requires attending to these non-human temporal machines rather than seeking explanations solely at the level of culture or ideology.
