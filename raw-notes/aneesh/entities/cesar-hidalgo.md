# Cesar Hidalgo

**Cesar A. Hidalgo** (b. 1979) is a Venezuelan-American physicist, information scientist, and complexity researcher. He directs the Center for Collective Learning at the University of Toulouse and formerly led the Macro Connections group at MIT Media Lab.

## Central Thesis

Hidalgo's central contribution is the reframing of **economic development as the growth of information embodied in products**. In *Why Information Grows: The Evolution of Order, from Atoms to Economies* (2015), he argues that products are not merely useful objects but **crystallized imagination** — physical instantiations of accumulated knowledge and know-how. Economic growth is therefore not primarily about capital accumulation or institutional quality but about the capacity of social networks to hold, recombine, and physically instantiate knowledge.

The limiting factor on this capacity is what Hidalgo calls the **personbyte** — the amount of productive knowledge one individual can effectively hold. Complex products require more personbytes than any individual possesses, necessitating networks of complementary specialists. The quality and density of these networks determine an economy's complexity ceiling.

## Key Works

- *The Atlas of Economic Complexity* (2011, with Ricardo Hausmann): Develops the **Economic Complexity Index (ECI)** — a measure of the diversity and sophistication of a country's export basket. The key finding: ECI is the best predictor of future economic growth, outperforming standard development indicators.
- *Why Information Grows* (2015): The popular-science articulation of the crystallized imagination thesis, extending it from physics (the growth of order) through biology (the accumulation of information in genomes) to economics (the accumulation of knowledge in products and networks).

## Intellectual Position

Hidalgo occupies a distinctive niche: a physicist who takes economic questions seriously without reducing them to equilibrium models. His approach is fundamentally **information-theoretic** rather than neoclassical — growth is about information, not utility maximization. This places him in productive tension with both mainstream economics and the institutional-quality school (Acemoglu, Robinson) by offering a more mechanistic, measurable account of why some economies produce complex goods and others do not.
