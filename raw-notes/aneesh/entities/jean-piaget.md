# Jean Piaget (1896–1980)

**Field**: Developmental psychology, genetic epistemology
**Nationality**: Swiss
**Key works**: *The Child's Conception of Space* (with Inhelder, 1948/1956); *The Origins of Intelligence in Children* (1936/1952); *The Construction of Reality in the Child* (1937/1954)

## Role in Tuan's *Space and Place*

Piaget is the dominant empirical authority in Ch. 3 ("Space, Place, and the Child"). Tuan draws on Piaget and his colleagues to establish the developmental sequence of spatial cognition from birth through school age.

The key findings Tuan uses:
- **Sensorimotor intelligence precedes conceptual grasp, sometimes by several years**. A six-month-old can distinguish a square from a triangle perceptually; the concept of "square" appears only at age four. The child can push a truck in a straight line long before grasping "straight line" as a geometric concept (Tuan 2002, p. 25)
- **Object permanence is gradual**: a young infant may fail to recognize a milk bottle when the wrong end is offered; he learns to turn it around at about eight months (p. 22)
- **Egocentric spatial frame**: young children's drawings reveal a body-centered reference frame; their spatial knowledge is topological (connectivity, proximity) before it is projective or Euclidean

## Piaget's Core Framework

Piaget's developmental epistemology holds that intelligence is not given but constructed through the child's active interaction with the environment. He identifies four stages: sensorimotor (0–2), preoperational (2–7), concrete operational (7–11), formal operational (11+). For spatial cognition specifically, the relevant transition is from sensorimotor spatial intelligence (implicit, action-based, egocentric) to concrete operational spatial cognition (explicit, representational, allocentric).

Piaget's insight that **practice precedes concept** — that the child can do before she can think-about — has wide implications beyond developmental psychology.

## Relevance to Tuan's Project

Tuan uses Piaget to ground the claim that bodily, sensorimotor experience is the developmental and epistemological foundation of all spatial knowledge. The child's route-learning, the infant's mouth-exploration, the toddler's mapping of near vs. far — these are not primitive approximations of adult spatial cognition. They are the **base layer** on which abstract spatial reasoning is built.

This supports Tuan's broader argument in Ch. 2 that sensorimotor and tactile experience lie at the root of Euclidean geometry — abstract space is an elaboration, not a replacement, of bodily space.
