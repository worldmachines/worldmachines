# Mircea Eliade

**Dates**: 1907–1986
**Field**: History of religions, comparative mythology
**Positions**: University of Chicago (Divinity School)
**Key works**: *The Sacred and the Profane* (1959); *Cosmos and History* (1954); *Patterns in Comparative Religion* (1958)

## Relevance to Language and Place

Tuan cites Eliade to document the religious-baptismal significance of European explorer rituals of possession. When Spanish and Portuguese conquistadores raised the cross over new territories:

> "They consecrated them in the name of Jesus Christ and believed that, by doing so, they enabled the territories to undergo a 'new birth.' For through Christ 'old things are passed away; behold, all things are become new' (II Corinthians, 5, 17). The newly discovered country was 'recreated' by the cross — reinstated into God's cosmos — as though it had no prior existence, or that its prior state was one of unredeemed wildness." (Eliade 1959, 32, as cited in Tuan 1991, p. 688)

This is constitutive language at civilizational scale: the verbal and ceremonial act of consecration does not merely acknowledge a new place — it *creates* it by incorporating it into a pre-existing divine order.

## The Sacred and the Profane

Eliade's foundational distinction between sacred space (consecrated, oriented, ordered — a *cosmized* place) and profane space (formless, homogeneous, undifferentiated) maps directly onto Tuan's space/place distinction. For Eliade, sacred space is created through *hierophany* — the manifestation of the sacred, which renders a previously ordinary location extraordinary and bounded. The ritual act is always verbal and ceremonial as well as physical.

This provides historical depth for Tuan's claim: the linguistic constitution of place is not a modern or secular phenomenon. It is continuous with ancient religious practices of world-consecration. Every time a European explorer "raised the cross" and proclaimed a territory in the name of the Crown and of God, he was performing an act structurally identical to what Eliade documents in archaic religious cultures worldwide.
