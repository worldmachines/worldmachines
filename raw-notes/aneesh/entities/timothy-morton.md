# Timothy Morton (b. 1968)

## Position

Morton is one of the most provocative thinkers associated with Object-Oriented Ontology (OOO) and ecological philosophy. His central claim: the concept of "Nature" — as a place outside of or beyond culture, the pristine wild to which we retreat on weekends — is itself an obstacle to genuine ecological thinking. Retaining the nature/culture divide, even in ostensibly ecological discourse, reproduces the very separation that enables ecological destruction. *Ecology Without Nature* (2007) argues that ecological art and thought must abandon nature as a category to become truly ecological.

## Core Concepts

- **Hyperobjects**: entities so massively distributed in time and space that they transcend localization — global warming, the biosphere, the totality of nuclear materials. Hyperobjects are real but cannot be directly encountered; we only experience their local manifestations (rain, mutation). Coined in *Hyperobjects: Philosophy and Ecology after the End of the World* (2013).
- **Dark ecology**: ecological thought that confronts the uncanny, messy, non-ideal reality of nature rather than retreating to pastoral ideals. Nature is strange, dark, recursive, and horrifying — not beautiful and harmonious.
- **The mesh**: Morton's term for the interconnectedness of all living and non-living things — a de-centered, non-hierarchical network without edges or center.

## Bryant's Engagement

Bryant engages Morton as both ally and critical foil in chapter 8. Agreement: Morton is right that nature should not be conceived as "something *outside* of, *beyond*, and independent of culture" — a place we visit on weekends, untouched by humans (p. 255). This framing makes it impossible to see how our social assemblages are materially continuous with and dependent on the natural world.

Disagreement: Bryant resists the move to simply abandon the concept of nature. "While I share Morton's rejection of the notion that nature is a place outside of culture to which we might go on weekends, geophilosophy sees abandonment of the concept of nature as inadvisable" (p. 256). Morton's move "risks leading to the view that culture is all there is, that there is only society, and therefore is in danger of reinforcing vertical ontologies premised on human exceptionalism or anthropocentrism" (p. 256).

Bryant's counter-proposal: not ecology without nature, but an ecology *of* nature in which culture is understood as "itself a formation of nature" — nature thought historically, contingently, creatively, without teleology or essentialism (p. 256).

## Later Engagement

Bryant also invokes Morton's concern about the nature/culture divide in the eco-political conclusion: "Contrary to Timothy Morton's 'ecology without nature,' what we need more than ever is social and political thought as a thought of nature" (p. 281).

## Key Works Referenced

- *Ecology Without Nature* (2007) — cited pp. 255–256, 281
- *The Ecological Thought* (2010) — the mesh, dark ecology
- *Hyperobjects* (2013) — published same year as Bryant's book; not explicitly cited in Ch 8

## Intellectual Lineage

- Influenced by: OOO (Graham Harman), Derrida, Shelley, Keats, Darwin
- Shares orbit with: Bryant, Harman, Ian Bogost, Jane Bennett
- Contested by: Bryant (on abandoning nature), some ecocritics (hyperobjects as too abstract for political mobilization)
