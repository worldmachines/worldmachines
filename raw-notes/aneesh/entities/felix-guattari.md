# Félix Guattari

**1930–1992.** French psychoanalyst, philosopher, and political activist. Co-authored with Gilles Deleuze: *Anti-Oedipus* (1972), *A Thousand Plateaus* (1980), *What is Philosophy?* (1991). Solo works: *The Three Ecologies* (1989), *Chaosmosis* (1992).

## Core Positions

**Anti-psychiatry and schizoanalysis**: Guattari trained under Lacan and worked at the La Borde clinic. With Deleuze, he developed schizoanalysis as an alternative to psychoanalysis: instead of interpreting desire through the family triangle (Oedipus), schizoanalysis maps the machines of desire — what desires connect to, what it produces, what assemblages it forms.

**Machinic desire**: Desire is not a lack (Freud, Lacan) but a productive force that makes connections and assemblages. The unconscious is a factory, not a theater.

**Assemblage theory**: See [[gilles-deleuze]]. Guattari's contribution emphasizes the *enunciative* and *collective* dimensions of assemblages — how groups, institutions, and media produce subjectivity.

**Ecosophy** (*The Three Ecologies*): Guattari's late work develops ecosophy as the analysis of three interlocking ecologies — environmental, social, and mental. This prefigures what Bryant calls [[post-human-media-ecology]]: mind, society, and environment are not separate domains but mutually constituting assemblages.

**Chaosmosis**: Guattari's late aesthetic-political theory: how subjectivity is produced at the intersection of biological, social, and semiotic machines. Media and technology are constitutive of subjectivity, not merely tools of its expression.

## Relation to Bryant

Guattari's assemblage vocabulary (*agencement*) is the primary source for Bryant's machinic assemblage concept. Guattari's insistence on the productive, non-representationalist character of desire maps onto Bryant's account of machines as defined by powers rather than qualities. Guattari's ecosophy anticipates Bryant's mapping of machine networks (worlds) as ecological systems.
