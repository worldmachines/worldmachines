# Justus Möser (1720–1794)

**Born**: Osnabrück, 1720
**Died**: Osnabrück, 1794
**Key works**: *Osnabrückische Geschichte* (1768); *Patriotische Phantasien* (1774–78)
**Role**: Statesman, jurist, and local historian of the Osnabrück region

## Intellectual Profile

Berlin identifies Möser as "the first historical sociologist" — a figure who anticipated both Herder's cultural pluralism and Burke's defense of tradition, but from the standpoint of a practicing Hanoverian conservative statesman rather than a philosopher or poet.

## Key Ideas

### *Zeitstil* and *Volksstil*

Every age has its own style (*Zeitstil*) and every people has its own style (*Volksstil*). Every war has its own particular tone, the affairs of State a specific colouring, dress and manners inner connections with religion and the sciences. There is a local reason for this or that institution that "is not and cannot be universal."

### Holistic Understanding

Societies and persons can be understood only by means of a "total impression" — not by isolating elements in the manner of analytical chemists. This is what Voltaire had not grasped when he mocked the fact that a law which applied in one German village was contradicted by another in a neighboring one. For Möser, it is precisely by such rich variety, founded upon ancient, unbroken tradition, that the tyrannies of uniform systems (such as those of Louis XIV or Frederick the Great) were avoided — "it is thus that freedoms were preserved."

### Tradition as Freedom

This is Möser's paradox and his most politically interesting contribution: local variety, customary irregularity, and irrational tradition are not obstacles to freedom — they are its precondition. Uniform rational systems are the enemy of liberty, because uniformity enables centralized control. The village that has its own law is harder to tyrannize than the one subsumed under a general code.

## Relation to the Counter-Enlightenment

Möser is the moderate, practically-minded wing of the counter-Enlightenment — not a mystic like Hamann or a catastrophist like Maistre, but a sober statesman who observed that societies working well have a kind of organic complexity that rational reformers never achieve. Berlin places him in the lineage that flows through Burke and into 19th-century conservative thought.
