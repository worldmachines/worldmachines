# Bruce Chatwin

**Dates**: 1940–1989
**Field**: Travel writing, fiction
**Key work**: *The Songlines* (1988); also *In Patagonia* (1977), *On the Black Hill* (1982)

## Relevance to Language and Place

Chatwin is Tuan's key reference for the Australian Aboriginal songline tradition. In *The Songlines*, Chatwin documented how Aboriginal Australians maintain a continental-scale geography through oral song: the Dreamtime ancestral tracks are "songlines," and "the ancestors have sung the world into existence" (Tuan 1991, p. 687, citing Chatwin 1988).

Chatwin's account makes vivid what is otherwise difficult to grasp: that for Aboriginal Australians, geographical knowledge and narrative knowledge are not two separate domains. To know a country is to know its songs; to sing the songs is to re-perform the ancestral creation events that constitute the land as humanized place. The songline is simultaneously a navigational route and a literary text.

## The Songlines

*The Songlines* is a hybrid work — part travel memoir, part ethnography, part philosophical notebook — centered on Chatwin's encounters with Aboriginal Australia and his attempt to understand the songline system. The book combines field observation with extensive quotation from Chatwin's notebooks, in which he speculates on the relationship between nomadism, language, and human nature.

Chatwin's central intuition (disputed by some anthropologists as romanticized) is that humans are constitutionally nomadic and that the songline is the purest expression of the human animal's need to move and name. For Tuan's purposes, what matters is the mechanism: ancestors stopped to perform actions (cooking a meal, instituting a rite), and the places where they stopped became nodes in a linguistic network. "Telling is perhaps not quite the word, for frequently the myths are delivered through songs" (Tuan 1991, p. 687).
