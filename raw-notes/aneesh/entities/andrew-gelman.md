# Andrew Gelman

**Position**: Professor of Statistics and Political Science, Columbia University
**Blog**: [Statistical Modeling, Causal Inference, and Social Science](https://statmodeling.stat.columbia.edu/)
**Key works**: *Bayesian Data Analysis* (with Carlin, Stern, Dunson, Vehtari, Rubin); *Red State, Blue State, Rich State, Poor State*; *The Stan probabilistic programming language* (lead developer)

## Significance in Statistics

Gelman is among the most influential contemporary Bayesian statisticians. He is also unusually reflexive about scientific practice — his blog regularly discusses methodological failures, replication crises, and the sociology of how science actually works (as opposed to how it is supposed to work).

## In This Wiki

Referenced in [[The Tortured Artist Is So Yesterday]] as someone who — reflecting on Samuel Lipman's 1984 essay about the lone struggling artist — notes that scientists feel versions of this same pressure but that the scientific burden "rarely falls on one person." Instead it is "distributed across collaborative projects that outlive any single contributor."

This makes Gelman a key witness for the argument that the solitary-genius model is an artifact of individualist cultural ideology rather than a feature of serious intellectual work. Science, the paradigm case of serious intellectual work, has always been distributed.

## Connection to Collaborative Creativity

Gelman's observation supports [[Collaborative Creativity]] and [[Distributed Creative Risk]] as the natural form of ambitious intellectual work — not the tortured solo creator, but teams with distributed stakes in a shared project. See [[The Tortured Artist Is So Yesterday]] for the full argument.
