# Achille Mbembe (b. 1957)

**Nationality**: Cameroonian
**Field**: Political philosophy, postcolonial theory, African studies
**Positions**: University of the Witwatersrand (South Africa); senior researcher, WISER
**Key works**: *On the Postcolony* (2001); *Necropolitics* (essay 2003; book 2019); *Out of the Dark Night* (2021); *Brutalisme* (2020)

## Core Contribution

Mbembe's most widely cited concept is **necropolitics**: the analysis of power as the exercise of authority over death. Where Foucault's biopolitics identified a modern form of power concerned with the management of life — the optimization of populations, their fertility, productivity, and health — Mbembe argues that in the colonial and postcolonial context, the fundamental expression of sovereignty is the authority to determine *who may be killed, who may be let die, and who may be exposed to lethal conditions without political consequence*.

Necropolitics is not a replacement for biopolitics but an extension of it into zones of exception — colonies, occupied territories, refugee camps, racial enclaves — where the liberal biopolitical promise of life-management gives way to organized exposure to death.

## Key Concepts

**The colony as the paradigm case**: The colony is the site where European sovereignty first organized itself around death rather than life — where "civilized" life was produced by the systematic exposure of colonized populations to violence, displacement, and death. The plantation, the colony, and the apartheid regime are Mbembe's three paradigm cases of necropolitical spatial organization.

**Placelessness as necropolitical exposure**: Following Mbembe's spatial logic: a person denied a stable, legally recognized, culturally acknowledged place is stripped of the buffers that place normally provides. Placelessness — legal, economic, cultural — becomes a form of exposure. This is the claim Sathe deploys in *We Need Homes in the Delta Quadrant*: placelessness equals systemic vulnerability.

**Contemporary necropolitics**: Mbembe extends the framework beyond colonial history to contemporary formations — the siege warfare of Gaza, the global refugee crisis, the racialized carceral system of the United States, the "world of enmity" produced by neoliberal capitalism's retreat from social protection.

## Relation to Foucault

Mbembe is in dialogue with Foucault's *Society Must Be Defended* (1976), where Foucault introduced the idea of "letting die" as a distinctive modern power. Mbembe's critique: Foucault's account remains Eurocentric — it treats biopower as a distinctly modern European development, missing the colonial practices that preceded and enabled it. Slavery and colonial extermination are not aberrations of European biopolitics; they are its conditions of possibility.

## Relation to Agamben

Agamben's concept of "bare life" (*homo sacer*) — life stripped of political protection, killable without crime — is a parallel but distinct framework. Where Agamben develops a quasi-transcendental analysis (the state of exception as sovereign structure), Mbembe insists on the historical specificity of race, colonialism, and capital as the actual mechanisms through which necropolitical power operates.

## Sathe's Use in "We Need Homes in the Delta Quadrant" (2025)

Mbembe appears in §III to extend the critique of exploration's violence into its most extreme form. Sathe uses necropolitics to make the spatial-justice argument: place is not an amenity but a protective layer; its denial is not inconvenience but exposure to systemic violence. The necropolitical analysis raises the stakes of place-making as a political practice — homebuilding is not merely epistemic but protective, and its denial is a form of organized abandonment.
