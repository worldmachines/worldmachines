# Bayo Akomolafe

Bayo Akomolafe is a Nigerian-born philosopher, writer, and speaker whose work joins Yoruba cosmology, Black studies, trickster thought, posthumanism, and decolonial critique. In [[bayo-akomolafe-the-untimely]], he develops [[untimely]] as a name for the surplus produced inside modern temporality's own mechanisms of discipline.

## Position in This Wiki

Akomolafe is useful as a theorist of **anti-programmatic transformation**. Like Fred Moten and other Black studies thinkers he cites, he resists the liberal-modern demand that politics become a legible plan, project, or alternative institution. The politically charged site is instead the crack, the syncopation, the failure, the excess that modernity cannot metabolize.

## Key Ideas

- [[untimely]] — not another clock, but what time cannot count.
- [[idowu-function]] — the third that comes after the binary but is older than it; a holder of charge rather than resolver of opposition.
- [[clock-as-incomplete-discipline]] — the plantation bell and slave-ship dance discipline bodies, but their very counts produce intervals and upbeats.
- [[accommodation-of-modernity]] — modernity as a field that grants legibility by making bodies archivable, surveillable, and governable.
- [[parapolitics-of-the-untimely]] — politics in excess of recognition-politics; sanctuary in cracks rather than a program for capture.
- [[awkward-grace]] — grace as monstrous interruption, not smooth blessing.

## Intellectual Lineage

In the talk Akomolafe explicitly invokes Frantz Fanon, C. L. R. James, Fred Moten, Chinua Achebe, Wole Soyinka, Sylvia Wynter, Hortense Spillers, Du Bois, Nahum Chandler, Saidiya Hartman, Édouard Glissant, Dostoevsky, Walter Benjamin, and Yoruba figures such as Eshu. The lineage matters because his critique of time is not merely phenomenological; it is routed through slavery, coloniality, Black fugitivity, and cosmological trickster traditions.
