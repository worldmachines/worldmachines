# Ernesto Laclau (1935–2014)

Argentine political theorist, long based at the University of Essex. With Chantal Mouffe, he developed one of the most influential post-Marxist theories of hegemony and radical democracy.

## Core Position

Laclau's key move is to insist on the "constitutive dislocation" at the heart of any social order: social structures are always traversed by an excess that cannot be sutured, a remainder that escapes representation. Hegemony is the operation by which particular contents are elevated to represent the universal (the "empty signifier"), temporarily suturing the social, but always incompletely. Genuine politics is only possible on the basis of this constitutive openness.

Key works: *New Reflections on the Revolution of Our Time* (1990), *Hegemony and Socialist Strategy* (with Mouffe, 1985/2001), *On Populist Reason* (2005).

## Massey's Complex Engagement

### Ally on Openness

[[doreen-massey]] cites Laclau (1990) and Laclau & Mouffe (2001) as fellow-travellers on Proposition 3 (space as always under construction):

> "Only if we conceive of the future as open can we seriously accept or engage in any genuine notion of politics. Only if the future is open is there any ground for a politics which can make a difference." (Massey 2005: 11, paraphrasing Laclau)

Laclau's insistence on genuine political openness aligns directly with Massey's argument that space must also be open.

### Target on Space

At the same time, Massey explicitly critiques Laclau's equation of *space* with the opposite of that openness. For Laclau, "spatialisation is equivalent to hegemonisation: the production of an ideological closure, a picture of the essentially dislocated world as somehow coherent." (Massey 2005: 24, citing Laclau 1990)

> "Any representation of a dislocation involves its spatialization. The way to overcome the temporal, traumatic and unrepresentable nature of dislocation is to construct it as a moment in permanent structural relation with other moments, in which case the pure temporality of the 'event' is eliminated... this spatial domesticization of time." (Laclau 1990: 72, quoted in Massey 2005: 25)

Laclau ends up treating space as inherently reactionary — the medium of ideological closure. This is the same move Bergson makes: space gets loaded with all the properties (fixity, closure, representation) that must be escaped in the name of genuine politics/time. See [[coupure-and-causal-closure]].

Massey's response: this is an inadmissible slide from *spatialisation* (the activity of fixing and representing) to *space* (the dimension). Space, properly conceived, is precisely the open dimension within which political futures remain undecided.

### The Self-Dislocation

Massey also notes — at Laclau (1990: 84) — a moment where Laclau himself writes that "space itself becomes an event." She reads this as Laclau's framework opening the door it had spent the rest of the book closing. See [[space-as-event]].

## Significance for the Wiki

Laclau's double role — ally on openness, adversary on space — is itself philosophically instructive. It shows the systemic pattern Massey is diagnosing: thinkers who are deeply committed to openness, politics, and becoming tend to dump all closure onto space because they have not thought space through. The remedy is not to reject their politics but to extend their insights about time into a parallel account of space.
