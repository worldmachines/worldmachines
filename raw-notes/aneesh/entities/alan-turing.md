# Alan Turing

## Biographical

Alan Turing (1912–1954) was a British mathematician, logician, and computer scientist. Foundational contributions include the theoretical framework of computation (Turing machines), the breaking of Enigma codes during WWII, and the beginnings of artificial intelligence research.

## Key Work Referenced

### "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" (1950)

Turing opens with the question "Can machines think?" and immediately deems it too vague — instead proposing the **Imitation Game** (Turing Test): can a machine produce text responses indistinguishable from a human's?

The move is explicit: convert a philosophically intractable question into an operationalizable behavioral test.

## Sathe's Reading

In [[Chatbots, Bats & Broken Oracles]], Turing's move is read as a canonical instance of [[Problems vs. Questions|converting a question into a problem]]:

> "He turned the question of what is it to think into the problem of how do you detect thought."

**Consequence**: LLMs pass the Turing Test. We remain unconvinced. This is evidence that the test — as a problem-conversion — failed to capture what the original question was actually asking about.

The "hard problem" of consciousness (cf. [[Thomas Nagel]]) is what Turing's operationalization bypassed. Passing the behavioral test says nothing about first-person phenomenal experience.

## Broader Significance

Turing stands at the origin of the entire tradition of using computation to address questions about mind. Sathe's essay series is in dialogue with this tradition: it accepts that LLMs represent a new computational capability while questioning whether that capability addresses the deep questions Turing's test was meant to settle.

## Intellectual Position in the Series

Turing's reductionism (question → problem) is the historical starting point that the essay series pushes against. The claim is that the most valuable human-computer interactions will be those that *preserve* the question-character of inquiry rather than immediately converting questions to problems.
