# Bruno Latour (1947–2022)

## Position

Latour is one of the central predecessors of new materialism and OOO. His core claim: modernity is built on the "purification" of nature and culture into two separate domains, while simultaneously *proliferating hybrids* — entities that are simultaneously natural and social (AIDS, the ozone hole, a nuclear power plant). The more rigorously modernity insists on the nature/culture divide, the more hybrids it produces that it cannot classify. *We Have Never Been Modern* (1993) is the canonical statement.

## Influence on Bryant

Bryant cites Latour's account of modernity's nature/culture partition as the key obstacle that geophilosophy must overcome: "As Latour has argued, modernity has been premised on a strong distinction and partition between the realm of nature and the realm of culture" (p. 248). This partition makes it structurally impossible to understand why social assemblages take the form they do — because it excludes non-human agencies from the analysis.

Bryant also invokes Latour as an exemplary *cartographer* — specifically the Latour of *The Pasteurization of France* (Latour 1988), which maps how Pasteur's microbes transformed French society through a meticulous Actor-Network analysis (p. 259). This is genetic and topographical cartography of the highest order: attending to the non-human actors (bacteria, vaccines, laboratories) that reorganize social assemblages.

## Key Works Referenced

- *We Have Never Been Modern* (1993/1991) — modernity's nature/culture partition
- *The Pasteurization of France* (1988) — exemplary cartographical analysis; microbes as political actors

## Intellectual Lineage

- Influenced by: Michel Serres, Gabriel Tarde, Whitehead
- Influence on: Bryant, Jane Bennett, Timothy Morton, Graham Harman, Karen Barad
- Contested by: Harman (for Latour's relationism, which Harman sees as eliminating objects)

## Key Concepts

- **Actor-Network Theory (ANT)**: social and natural phenomena are effects of heterogeneous networks of human and non-human actants; agency is distributed across the network
- **Purification vs. translation**: modernity's explicit practice of separating nature from culture, versus the actual practice of constructing hybrids
- **Quasi-objects**: entities that are neither purely natural nor purely social — the paradigm case of what modernity cannot classify
