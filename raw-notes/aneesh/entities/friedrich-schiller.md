# Friedrich Schiller (1759–1805)

## Biographical Context

- Son of a gardener
- Playwright, poet, philosopher — contemporary of Goethe and Kant
- Key texts for [[Kulturkritik]]: *Letters on the Aesthetic Education of Man* (1794), "On Naïve and Sentimental Poetry" (1795), "Concerning the Sublime" (1801)
- Strong interest in horticulture: reviewed garden calendar for 1795; identified Hohenheim Park near Stuttgart as his horticultural ideal

## Schiller's Kulturkritik (per Kempf)

Schiller positions his work as "an answer to the French Revolution gone awry." Where [[Jean-Jacques Rousseau]] blamed cultural overrefinement in general, Schiller identifies **overspecialization** as the specific culprit of self-alienation. Like Rousseau, he sees the Enlightenment as both cause and cure.

His norm, however, is not original nature (which he sees as a wretched "troglodyte" state) but **the Greeks of antiquity**, whose "wholeness of being is irretrievably lost" ("AE," p. 102).

## The Drive Theory

Schiller's *Aesthetic Education* proposes that modern humans are split between two drives:
- **Sensuous drive** (Sinntrieb): the material, sensory, particular
- **Formal drive** (Formtrieb): the rational, universal, abstract

The **play drive** (Spieltrieb) sublates both, producing "living form" (lebende Gestalt) — the incarnation of beauty. Key claim: "It is only through beauty that man makes his way to freedom" ("AE," p. 90).

This is Schiller's reformulation of the [[Nature-Culture Dialectic]]: the synthesis is achieved not through nature (Rousseau) nor through pure reason, but through *art*.

## The Garden as Integrative Ideal

Schiller's horticultural ideal is a "middle way between the rigidity of the French style of garden and the anarchy of the so-called English garden" — explicitly attributed to Hirschfeld's *Theory of Garden Design* (1779). The Hohenheim Park "combines such disparate elements into a whole" — it is "soulfully inspired and artistically exalted nature, which satisfies not just the untutored person but even the person spoiled by culture."

This garden is an "integrative ideal" — it appeals to both intellect and feeling. But Schiller is "disregarding actuality for the sake of appearance and effect": the ideal is a postulate, not a description.

## The Aesthetic Semblance Problem

In the "Twenty-Second Letter," Schiller writes that the truly successful work of art requires an artist "who can make his form consume the material" — the beautiful semblance of art is a "calculated effect in which culture keeps nature in check."

Schiller's own admission: his ideal is "aesthetic semblance" — a beauty that is only an illusion, a freedom that exists only in fiction. This makes his Kulturkritik self-consciously a move from "renaturalization" (Rousseau) to "refinement" — an "*éducation esthétique*" rather than an "*éducation naturelle*."

## The Sublime Paradox

"Concerning the Sublime" (1801) reveals the deepest contradiction in Schiller's position. He prefers the "inspiring disorder of a natural landscape" to the "insipid regularity of a French garden." But to *use* this nature as a source of the sublime, it must be "brought under the control of culture." In the sublime, man becomes "conscious of his independence from nature" — and what the sublime generates *is* culture, not nature.

Kempf's summary: "Schiller criticizes the sedative culture of the French garden and, more broadly, harmony and beauty, in favor of an experience of nature that delivers ... more culture."

## Key Distinction from Rousseau

| Rousseau | Schiller |
|----------|----------|
| Man in natural state = innocent, wholesome | Man in natural state = "troglodyte" (miserable) |
| Reflection is "contrary to nature" | Reflection *begins* humanity |
| Thinking human being = "depraved animal" | Taking "delight in semblance ... ornamentation and play" |
| Cure: return to nature or nascent society | Cure: aesthetic education through art |
| Model: the Swiss peasant | Model: the Greeks |
