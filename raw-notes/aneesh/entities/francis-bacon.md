# Francis Bacon

**Francis Bacon** (1561–1626) was an English philosopher, statesman, and essayist. He is often credited with developing the empirical scientific method and is a foundational figure in the history of science and modern philosophy.

## Relevance to This Wiki

Bacon is cited in [[Universal Library in the River of Noise]] as an exemplar of the [[Universal Library]] aspiration. His *Instauratio Magna* ("The Great Instauration") proposed an exhaustive reorganization of all the sciences — a comprehensive reclassification of human knowledge that would replace the inherited Aristotelian taxonomy.

This is the Enlightenment form of the universal library impulse: not just to gather all books, but to organize all knowledge systematically so that it could be used productively rather than merely stored.

## Key Works

- ***Novum Organum*** (1620, part of *Instauratio Magna*) — proposes inductive method as the basis for all knowledge
- ***The Advancement of Learning*** (1605) — survey and classification of existing knowledge and its gaps
- ***New Atlantis*** (unfinished, 1627) — utopian vision including "Solomon's House," a proto-research institution for systematic knowledge production

## The Universal Library Connection

Bacon's ambition was structurally similar to later knowledge-organization projects:
- [[Conrad Gessner]]'s *Bibliotheca Universalis* — catalog all books
- The French *Encyclopédie* — systematize all knowledge
- Modern [[Domain Ontologies]] — formally represent knowledge within a domain

Each attempts to impose structure on the totality of human knowledge. Each discovers the same tensions: the more comprehensive, the more unwieldy; the more organized, the more exclusionary.

## Sathe's Use in "We Need Homes in the Delta Quadrant" (2025)

In §I, Sathe cites Bacon specifically on memory and direction: "Francis Bacon wrote that memory without direction is indistinguishable from wandering. We do not remember freely; we remember *through* structures." This is not the Bacon of systematic empiricism or universal taxonomy (the use in the Universal Library entry) but the Bacon of orientation — memory as requiring scaffolding, structure, place. Sathe uses this to motivate the concept of [[prenotions]]: the early constraints that allow orientation are the personal and cultural equivalents of Bacon's memory-structures. The essay's central argument (that exploration requires homebuilding first) is in some sense a spatial translation of Bacon's point about memory.
