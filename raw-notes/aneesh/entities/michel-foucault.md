# Michel Foucault (1926–1984)

## Biographical

French philosopher and historian of ideas. Professor at the Collège de France (1970–1984), chair in "History of Systems of Thought." His work spans epistemology, psychiatry, penology, sexuality, and political rationality.

## Core Concepts

**Power/Knowledge**: Power and knowledge are not independent — regimes of truth (what counts as knowledge) are produced within and serve to sustain specific power arrangements. There is no knowledge outside power, and power always operates through knowledge-claims.

**Genealogy**: Foucault's method for tracing how present institutions and practices emerged through historically contingent struggles, rather than through teleological development or rational progress. Genealogy reveals contingency where necessity is assumed.

**Discipline**: *Discipline and Punish* (1975) traces the emergence of disciplinary power — a form of power that operates not through public spectacle and punishment but through the continuous surveillance and normalization of individuals. Paradigm institution: the Panopticon prison (Bentham). Operates through spatial organization, timetables, examinations, and hierarchical surveillance.

**Biopolitics**: From the late 1970s lectures at the Collège de France (*Society Must Be Defended*; *The Birth of Biopolitics*), Foucault analyzed the emergence of power directed not at individual bodies but at populations — the management of birth rates, life expectancy, health, and productivity at the level of the species.

**The Dispositif**: The "apparatus" or "device" — the heterogeneous ensemble of discourses, institutions, architectural arrangements, regulations, administrative measures, and scientific statements that form a network of power/knowledge at a given historical moment.

## Relation to Bryant's Gravity Framework

Bryant cites Foucault as a representative of the discursive analytics of power that his gravity concept extends. In *Onto-Cartography* Ch. 7, Bryant writes:

> "The concept of gravity proposed here includes all of the discursivist and practice-based elements of concepts of power proposed by theorists such as Foucault and Bourdieu, while also helping us to recognize the power that things themselves exercise — by virtue of what they *are*, not by virtue of how we symbolize or represent them." (p. 194)

Bryant does not reject Foucault. His critique is one of *scope*: Foucauldian power analytics are excellent at capturing how discourse, norm, and subject-formation operate, but they systematically underweight the plane of content — the material, non-discursive, non-human agencies that organize social arrangements regardless of representation.

Significantly, Foucault's later "material turn" (especially the architectural analysis of the Panopticon, the spatial geography of the clinic, and the analysis of specific techniques like the examination) already gestures toward what Bryant theorizes more systematically. The gap is between a *primarily* discursive analytics and a flat ontology in which material and discursive agencies are equally first-class.

## Sathe's Use in "We Need Homes in the Delta Quadrant" (2025)

In §III, Sathe deploys Foucault's concept of [[heterotopias]] — places that operate under different logic than the dominant spatial order — to argue that counter-spatial practice is both possible and historically persistent. Heterotopias function as Sathe's answer to the question raised by Harvey's and Mbembe's critique: if place-making is always imbricated in power, what does resistant or ethical place-making look like? Foucault's heterotopias show that spaces outside the dominant map have always existed, and that their existence reveals the contingency of the dominant order. For Sathe, this motivates the turn to [[custodianship-as-design-ethos]]: the designer who creates heterotopic conditions — places with different affordances, different logics — is doing something genuinely political.
