# Johann Gottfried Herder

**Born**: Mohrungen, Prussia, 1744
**Died**: Weimar, 1803
**Key works**: *Ideas on the Philosophy of the History of Humanity* (1784–91); *Yet Another Philosophy of History* (1774); *Fragments on Recent German Literature* (1767–68)

## Intellectual Profile

Herder was a student of Immanuel Kant who turned sharply away from his teacher's universalism. Where Kant sought the universal conditions of rational cognition, Herder insisted on the irreducible particularity of cultures, languages, and historical moments.

## Key Ideas

### Cultural Pluralism
Each culture (*Volk*) has its own center of gravity — its own constellation of values, practices, and forms of excellence. These are not steps on a single progressive ladder toward a universal ideal; they are genuinely different and incommensurable forms of human life. To judge one culture by another's standards is a category error.

### Language and Thought
For Herder, language does not merely express thought — it shapes it. Each language embodies a different way of carving up experience. This is why translation is always approximate and why cultural understanding requires *Einfühlung* — imaginative empathy, entering the other's perspective from within.

*Einfühlung* (empathy) as a method of cultural understanding: Herder coined or popularized the term as the hermeneutic key to understanding other cultures and historical periods. This directly anticipates [[Giambattista Vico]]'s *fantasia* as the key to historical understanding.

### Against Enlightenment Universalism
Herder's critique: the Enlightenment's "march of Reason" is actually the imposition of 18th-century European bourgeois values onto all of humanity, past and present. Every historical period and every culture has its own *Humanität* — its own version of human flourishing — and none is more "advanced" than any other.

## Significance for Berlin

[[Isaiah Berlin]] regards Herder as one of the most important counter-Enlightenment thinkers — a genuine pluralist who intuited what Berlin would later theorize as [[Value Pluralism]]. Berlin writes that Herder's "belief in the multiplicity of cultures" is the anticipation of his own view that ultimate values are incommensurable.

Herder is also a warning: his cultural pluralism fed into 19th-century German nationalism in ways he did not intend. The insight that each Volk has its own spirit easily became the claim that each Volk should have its own state — a short step to ethnonationalism.

## Key Quotations (from Berlin's text, pp. 15–16)

- On cosmopolitanism: "Cosmopolitanism is the shedding of all that makes one most human, most oneself."
- On cultural belonging: "To belong to a given community, to be connected with its members by indissoluble and impalpable ties of common language, historical memory, habit, tradition and feeling, is a basic human need no less natural than that for food or drink or security or procreation."
- On cultural homogenization: "Uniformity is death. The tree of (science-dominated) knowledge kills the tree of life."
- On cultural diversity: "I am always frightened when I hear a whole nation or period characterised in a few short words; for what a vast multitude of differences is embraced by the word 'nation', or 'the Middle Ages', or 'ancient and modern times'."
