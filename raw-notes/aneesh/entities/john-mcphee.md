# John McPhee

**John Angus McPhee** (b. 1931) is an American writer and longtime staff writer at *The New Yorker*. He has written more than 30 books of creative non-fiction, covering geology, wilderness, sports, physics, and more. He taught writing at Princeton for decades.

He is widely considered one of the masters of American long-form non-fiction — notable for his ability to make specialized, technical subjects deeply readable without condescending to their complexity.

## Key Work (as referenced)

### Draft No. 4 (2017)
A collection of essays on the craft of writing. The essay "Draft No. 4" describes McPhee's revision process; another essay demonstrates his method of dictionary use.

Referenced in [[A Good Dictionary]] via [[James Somers]]: McPhee is used to demonstrate *how the dictionary should be used* — as a generative, exploratory tool, not just a disambiguation device. His practice exemplifies [[Lexicographic Richness]] in action.

## Intellectual Position

McPhee represents the tradition of deep reporting combined with structural craft. His work requires extreme research immersion followed by careful architectural shaping. He is arguably a **hedgehog** in the [[Fox-Hedgehog Distinction]] sense: committed to the long form over decades, mastering one way of working deeply.

This creates an interesting tension: he's cited in an essay about hacky, foxy problem-solving, but his own practice is disciplined and depth-oriented.
