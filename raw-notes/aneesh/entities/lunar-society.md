# Lunar Society of Birmingham

## Overview

The Lunar Society was an informal learned society based in Birmingham, England, active approximately 1765–1813. Members met monthly on the Monday nearest the full moon (hence "Lunar") — the full moon providing enough light for travel home after meetings.

## Key Members

- [[Josiah Wedgwood]] — potter, industrialist
- Erasmus Darwin — physician, naturalist, grandfather of Charles Darwin
- James Watt — steam engine inventor
- Matthew Boulton — manufacturer, Watt's business partner
- Joseph Priestley — chemist, discoverer of oxygen
- Richard Lovell Edgeworth — inventor, educator
- William Small — physician, philosopher

## Character of the Society

Per Lovell Edgeworth (cited by Tristram Hunt via Velitchkov): members gathered to share "the first hints of discoveries, the current observations, and the mutual collision of ideas." A **dynamic pluralist discourse** used to make sense of the expanding scope of experience.

This is precisely the [[divergence-machine]]'s epistemic mode: exploratory, multi-disciplinary, operating in ontological openness rather than commitment to a single framework. Compare to the MM's legibility drive — the Lunar Society was not trying to make knowledge legible and controllable but to generate new knowledge through collision.

## Role in the Divergence Machine

[[Ivo Velitchkov]] cites Wedgwood's Lunar Society participation as evidence that Wedgwood was not just an engineer of the [[Modernity Machine]] but also an early contributor to the [[divergence-machine]]. The Society's mode — heterogeneous expertise, informal structure, shared curiosity — is structurally DM rather than MM.

This is notable: the Industrial Revolution is typically framed as the MM's triumph (legibility, division of labor, standardization). But the knowledge-culture that generated it was already operating in DM mode — pluralist, experimental, non-hierarchical.

## The Illuminati Parallel

The Lunar Society and the Bavarian Illuminati (1776) are near-contemporaries, and both represent early DM institutions: informal networks of intellectuals/practitioners operating outside official church/state structures. The difference: the Illuminati explicitly sought to change the world; the Lunar Society was more genuinely exploratory.
