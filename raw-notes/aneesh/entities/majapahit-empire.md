# Majapahit Empire

## Overview

Majapahit (c. 1293–1527 CE) was a Hindu-Buddhist maritime empire centered on the island of Java, with influence across Sumatra, Bali, Borneo, and the Malay Peninsula. At its height in the 14th century, it was one of the most powerful and cosmopolitan polities in Southeast Asia.

## Key Features

### Cosmopolitanism and Religious Pluralism
The Chinese ambassador Ma Huan, visiting the port of Canggu at Majapahit's peak, observed:
- Hindus with shaved heads
- Buddhist recluses with matted hair
- Widowed women dressed in white
- Chinese and Arab traders
- Small groups of apparent animists with "wild-eyed, mildly deranged look"
- Weeklong pan-religious festivals

Religious tolerance was structural, not merely incidental.

### Naval Technology
Majapahit's "jong" ships were nine-decked vessels potentially carrying up to 1,000 people — larger than Portuguese ships of the 15th century. No shipwrecks have been found; their exact size remains unknown.

### Cartographic Knowledge
Javanese cartography was advanced enough to be threatening. The Portuguese, by Royal Decree of November 13, 1504, ordered the destruction of all maps, charts, and logbooks connected with navigation south of the equator. Not a single indigenous Javanese cartographic specimen survives, explaining why pre-Portuguese Indonesian cartography remains unstudied.

This is one of the most striking examples of [[Deliberate Archival Erasure]] in pre-modern history.

### Administrative Innovation
Gajah Mada — a commoner who rose to effectively run the state as Prime Minister — is the archetypal "low-born consul viciously running state affairs." His rise against aristocratic opposition illustrates that Majapahit had channels for ability-based advancement outside hereditary class.

## Collapse and Afterlife

### Collapse (~1400-1527)
Majapahit disappeared quickly in the 15th century despite its sophistication — an instance of the [[History Machine]] thesis: even highly developed systems can collapse when their components fall out of alignment.

### Afterlife
1. **Structural leftovers** became material for Dutch colonialism — infrastructure, administrative patterns
2. **Royal legitimacy** seeded subsequent Javanese and Indonesian kingdoms
3. **Cultural transplant**: The royal class fled to Bali, taking the royal library, ceremonial costumes, drums, and bronze bells. "Bali became the reincarnation of Majapahit."
4. **National mythology**: Majapahit is now the primary source of national identity for Indonesia — a country of 270+ million people, the 4th largest population in the world and the largest Muslim nation. A Hindu empire thus legitimizes a Muslim state.

## The Chronicler

An anonymous "oddball court official" documented daily life in Majapahit. His observations were considered irrelevant at publication; his book is now the primary historical source. This exemplifies [[Lohse]]'s "notes in the margins" figure: the future is already present, barely recognized.

## As History Machine Instance

Majapahit illustrates how history machines work:
- Many sophisticated "components" developed over centuries independently
- The empire collapsed suddenly but its components persisted and reassembled in new configurations
- The significance of those components was only legible retrospectively
