# Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778)

## Biographical Context

- Born Geneva; closest European approximation of his own "noble savage" ideal was the Swiss peasant
- Wrote *Discourse on the Sciences and Arts* (First Discourse, 1750) and *Discourse on the Origin of Inequality* (Second Discourse, 1755) — the founding texts of Kulturkritik
- *Julie, or the New Heloise* (1761): novel featuring the Elysium garden (Clarens)
- *Emile* (1762): novel of education — Emile turned through artifice into a "savage made to inhabit cities"
- *Reveries of a Solitary Walker* (posthumous): the St. Pierre Island episode as English garden experience
- *Social Contract* (1762): political culmination

## Intellectual Position (per Kempf)

Rousseau's [[Kulturkritik]] begins with a rhetorical modification: he rephrases the Academy of Dijon's question to include "or corrupt," turning a binary into a confrontation. This sets up the oppositional structure his work will inhabit.

**Nature** ("l'homme sauvage" = the *bête*) vs. **Culture** ("l'homme civil" = the *bourgeois*). The key antithetical claim: "The Savage lives within himself; the sociable man, always outside of himself" (*CW3*, p. 66).

**What he actually means by "noble savage"**: not the solitary primitive beast, but the inhabitant of *nascent society* — "the happiest and most durable epoch" (*CW3*, p. 48) — "at equal distances from the stupidity of the brutes and the fatal enlightenment of Civil man." The closest existing approximation: the Swiss peasant.

## The Rhetorical Structure

Rousseau maintains two mutually exclusive points of view simultaneously — like "standing between two mirrors." This enables:
1. **Self-projection**: the *bête* as utopia, the *bourgeois* as dystopia
2. **Self-reflection**: foreignizing culture (wound) and familiarizing nature (noble) — the reciprocal mirroring that makes critical self-reflection possible

The *bête* is explicitly admitted to be a construct: "the brute no longer exists, ... perhaps never existed [and] probably never will exist" (*CW3*, p. 13). Its function is purely rhetorical — to maximize the contrast needed for comprehensive critique of the *bourgeois*.

## The Homeopathic Paradox

"Healing us from reason through reason." Rousseau's own English garden experiences use the tools of culture (magnifying glass, Linnaeus's *Systema naturae*, the botanizer's trained eye) to return to a nature that was supposedly prior to culture. His solutions:
- The St. Pierre Island experience (*Reveries*): natural happiness experienced by a man equipped with scientific tools and cultural sensitivity
- The Elysium garden (*Julie*): Julie admits she created only "the illusion of natural wilderness" — "Nature did it all, but under my direction"
- The *citoyen*: Rousseau's urban ideal — achieves natural inner harmony through *unnatural* means
- Emile: turned into "a savage made to inhabit cities" through elaborate artifice

## Influence and Lineage

- **Precursor**: The frontispiece of the *Discourse on Inequality* (the "noble savage" returning to nature) was drawn from a travel account, showing the "imperial eyes" problem built into the concept from the start
- **Schiller**: Acknowledges Rousseau's Kulturkritik initiated processes culminating in the French Revolution; positions his own work as "an answer to the French Revolution gone awry"
- **Goethe**: Writing to his sister in 1767: "Don't laugh about this seemingly foolish philosophy, the seemingly paradoxical pronouncements, they are the most splendid truths"
- **Kempf's reassessment**: Contrary to scholarship that reads Kulturkritik as straightforward binary opposition, Rousseau's rhetorical strategy posits a *reciprocity* of nature and culture rather than a simple binary — what looks like opposition is actually a site of convergence and divergence simultaneously
