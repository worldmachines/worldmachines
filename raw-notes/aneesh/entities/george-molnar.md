# George Molnar

Hungarian-Australian philosopher (1934–1999). Worked at the University of Sydney. His major work *Powers: A Study in Metaphysics* (2003) was published posthumously, edited by Stephen Mumford. Molnar develops a realist account of natural powers — the dispositional properties of physical objects that explain their causal behavior.

## The Account of Powers

Molnar argues against the Humean view that properties are merely categorical (non-dispositional) and that causation is an external, contingent regularity. Instead, powers are intrinsic, directional features of objects that partially constitute what those objects are. His five-feature account of powers (cited by Bryant, *Onto-Cartography* p. 40–41):

1. **Directedness**: powers are directed toward a specific type of manifestation. A plant has the power to produce oxygen; that power is directed toward oxygen-production. "Powers are like mathematical functions" — directedness makes them analogous to f(x) = x² − 3, which is directed toward a specific output for any given input.

2. **Independence**: powers are independent from their manifestations. A power can exist unmanifested. Skunks have the power to produce pungent odors even when they don't spray; matches have the power to produce flame even in a vacuum.

3. **Actuality**: the independence of powers from manifestations does not mean powers are merely potential. Powers are *actual* features of objects. Bryant: "actuality does not refer to the *exercise* of a power in producing a manifestation, but rather refers to features possessed by a machine regardless of whether or not it exercises that power" (p. 41).

4. **Intrinsicness**: powers are intrinsic to the objects that possess them, not dependent on relations to other objects or on being observed. A match's power to produce flame is its own power even if it never comes into contact with oxygen.

5. **Objectivity**: powers are objective — features of objects regardless of whether anyone knows them or has observed them.

## Bryant's Adoption

[[levi-bryant]] adopts Molnar's five-feature account to characterize the *virtual proper being* of machines — the system of powers a machine possesses regardless of whether and how they are currently exercised (pp. 40–42). Bryant reserves "power" for the capacity and "operation" for the actual exercise of that power in producing a manifestation (p. 42).

The Molnar framework allows Bryant to distinguish between:
- What a machine *is* (its virtual proper being, its powers)
- What a machine *does* in a particular context (its local manifestation)

This distinction is foundational for the concepts of [[rigid-plastic-machines]] (how stable the powers are), [[pluripotency-and-exaptation]] (the range of possible manifestations from those powers), and [[operations-not-substance]] (powers as what defines being, not properties).
