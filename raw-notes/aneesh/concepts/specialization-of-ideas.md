# Specialization of Ideas

[[Aneesh Sathe]]'s term for the platform-era parallel to industrial task specialization. Where 18th-century factories divided physical production into discrete steps that could be performed by less-skilled workers, platforms exploit an analogous fragmentation of intellectual and creative production: many creators exploring the same conceptual or genre space from different angles.

## The Factory Analogy

Industrial specialization allowed the factory owner to "purchase exactly that precise quantity of which is necessary for each process" — hiring skilled labor only for steps that required it. Platforms apply the same logic to creative production: a viral star commands high reward, a niche creator commands little, and the platform calibrates payout to market impact with algorithmic precision.

## Implications

- **Platforms don't need to predict success** — they fund everything and let audience selection do the work. The diversity of approaches guarantees that *something* sticks.
- **Quality-agnosticism**: the platform's incentive is engagement, not quality or truth. The specialization logic makes whatever sticks lucrative, regardless of what it is.
- **Niches become viable but precarious**: specialization enables hyper-niche content (there is an audience for anything), but niche creators occupy the low end of the payout curve

## Connection to Economic History

The phrase invokes [[David S. Landes]]'s analysis of the "advantage of disaggregating a productive process" from his 1986 essay — Sathe is showing the structural isomorphism rather than asserting direct causality.
