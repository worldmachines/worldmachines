# Assumption-Ground

The assumption-ground is [[Murray S Davis]]'s term (drawing on Garfinkel's ethnomethodology and Schutz's phenomenology) for the web of taken-for-granted beliefs that constitutes an audience's baseline reality. It is the surface against which interesting propositions operate.

## How It Works

Interesting propositions target a *specific strand* of the assumption-ground:
- If the proposition leaves the entire web intact → **"That's obvious"**
- If the proposition denies one specific assumption → **"That's interesting!"**
- If the proposition threatens the entire web → **"That's absurd"**

A proposition that is "interesting" to one audience may be "obvious" to another (who already hold the counter-assumption) or "absurd" to a third (whose assumption-ground is too distant).

## Relation to Frames

The assumption-ground is structurally equivalent to Klein's "frames" in [[data-frame-theory-of-sensemaking]]: both are pre-theoretical structures that filter reality, organize perception, and resist disruption. Davis's contribution is the logical taxonomy of *how* disruption operates (the [[Index of the Interesting]]); Klein's contribution is the cognitive dynamics of *why* disruption succeeds or fails (anchoring, elaboration, reframing).

## Relevance to AI Homogenization

If LLMs compress cognitive diversity toward a statistical mean, they homogenize assumption-grounds across populations. This narrows the space of what can be found *interesting* — because interestingness depends on the gap between a proposition and its audience's assumptions. When everyone shares the same assumption-ground, fewer propositions can achieve the targeted denial that produces intellectual surprise.
