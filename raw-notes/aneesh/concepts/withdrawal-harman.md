# Withdrawal (Harman / OOO)

## The Harman Thesis

For [[graham-harman]], every object withdraws from all relations. An object is never fully present to another object or to any system of relations — it always holds something in reserve, exceeding any particular encounter. This is the core claim of Object-Oriented Ontology (OOO): objects have a depth or interiority that no relation exhausts.

The withdrawal thesis is derived from Heidegger's tool-analysis: the hammer, in use, withdraws into the background; only when it breaks does it become conspicuous (present-at-hand). Harman generalizes this: withdrawal is not limited to broken tools or conscious observation — it is a feature of *all* relations between objects. A flame burning cotton does not encounter the cotton's color, softness, or smell — only its flammability. The flame encounters only a small slice of what cotton *is*.

Source: Harman (2002); Bryant also references Harman (2005: 95–9) for the "firewalls" discussion.

## Bryant's Adoption and Modification

[[levi-bryant]] credits Harman for the thesis that "all machines encounter one another behind 'firewalls'" (*Onto-Cartography* p. 58). All machines relate to each other not directly but mediated by their own operations — the operations of each machine constitute the firewall through which flows must pass.

Bryant uses this to support the claim that every machine transforms the flows it receives: the sonar returns data about size, shape, and velocity, not the full being of the shark. The insurance company processes the claim through its profit-maximization operations, not through the patient's suffering.

However, Bryant modifies the Harman thesis: "contra Harman, I do hold that entities can directly affect one another" (p. 58). Bryant's concern is with causal efficacy — machines *do* genuinely affect each other, even if they never encounter each other's full being. He wants to preserve real causality against what he sees as OOO's tendency toward a too-absolute withdrawal. For Bryant, the firewall is the machine's *operations* (what the machine brings to bear on flows), not an absolute barrier to causal influence.

## The Withdrawal-Operations Distinction

The difference between Harman's withdrawal and Bryant's operational closure:
- **Harman**: objects withdraw from *all* relations; their real being is never touched by any encounter. Real objects relate only through "vicarious causation."
- **Bryant**: machines encounter each other behind operational firewalls — they never encounter flows *as they are*, but this does not mean they fail to causally affect each other. The operational transformation is a real causal process. The shark's motion really affects the sonar; the sonar's ping really bounces off the shark.

Both agree on the non-transparency of relations. Where Harman tends toward a metaphysics of inaccessibility, Bryant emphasizes a metaphysics of operational transformation — always a partial, selective, active reshaping of flows.

## Relevance to Machine-Being

The withdrawal thesis, in Bryant's modified form, supports several claims:
1. No machine is ever *fully* present in any particular coupling. Its virtual proper being always exceeds its local manifestation. See [[operations-not-substance]], [[rigid-plastic-machines]].
2. The world of each machine (its [[for-itself-machine]]) is constituted by what its operations can reach — its openness — not by the full reality of what surrounds it.
3. Machines are therefore never fully knowable from outside; [[alien-phenomenology]] is always approximate, inferential.
