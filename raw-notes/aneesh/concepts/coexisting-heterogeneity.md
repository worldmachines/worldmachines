# Coexisting Heterogeneity

Massey's second proposition in *For Space* (2005): "that we understand space as the sphere of the possibility of the existence of multiplicity in the sense of contemporaneous plurality; as the sphere in which distinct trajectories coexist; as the sphere therefore of coexisting heterogeneity. Without space, no multiplicity; without multiplicity, no space." (Massey 2005: 9)

## The Claim

Space is what makes it *ontologically possible* for distinct trajectories to coexist without collapsing into one. Remove space, and you remove the condition of possibility for multiplicity: you are left with a single timeline, a single story. Multiplicity and space are mutually constitutive.

The difference Massey intends here is **positive**, not negative:

> "All I mean at this point is the contemporaneous existence of a plurality of trajectories; a simultaneity of stories-so-far... It is simply the principle of coexisting heterogeneity. It is not the particular nature of heterogeneities but the fact of them that is intrinsic to space." (Massey 2005: 12)

This is distinct from:
- Deconstructive "difference" (which operates within discourse to expose constitutive exclusions) — useful but not the same move.
- "Difference" in the old political sense (identity politics of already-constituted groups) — also not what Massey means.

The minimum condition: being positioned differently from something else is already to be in a spatial relation of coexistence. "The minimum difference occasioned by being positioned raises already the fact of uniqueness." (Massey 2005: 12)

## Political Corollary

The neoliberal globalisation narrative reduces space to time: Mozambique is not a contemporary other with its own trajectory, just an earlier point in the universal historical queue. This sleight-of-hand obliterates contemporaneity. Massey's counter: "a genuine, thorough, spatialisation of social theory and political thinking can force into the imagination a fuller recognition of the simultaneous coexistence of others with their own trajectories and their own stories to tell." (Massey 2005: 11)

The political demand is to recognise others as *coeval* — neither behind nor ahead, but alongside, simultaneous, with their own histories and potential futures.

## Relation to Massey's Diagonal Move

Massey distinguishes her multiplicity from Bergson's and Deleuze's use of the concept. For Bergson-Deleuze, there are two multiplicities: discrete (spatial, quantitative, bad) and continuous (temporal, qualitative, good). Massey refuses this division: she wants a space that already *has* duration — "Space as the dimension of a multiplicity of durations." (Massey 2005: 24) The coexisting heterogeneity of her Proposition 2 is not a static array of frozen differences but a simultaneous co-presence of ongoing trajectories.
