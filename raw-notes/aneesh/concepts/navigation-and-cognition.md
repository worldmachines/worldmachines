# Navigation and Cognition

## The Core Claim

The cognitive mechanisms underlying [[Space and Place in Cognition|place-making]] are not metaphorical — they are extensions of phylogenetically ancient navigation systems. There is a continuous ladder from:

```
Bacteria (chemotaxis) → Animals (landmark navigation) → Humans (internal cognitive maps)
```

At each level, the organism uses environmental signals to construct a navigable representation of the world.

## From Chemotaxis to Landmarks

- **Bacteria**: At microscopic scale, *territory is the map* — the chemical gradient field is directly navigated. No internal representation needed; the organism *is* in the map.
- **Animals**: As organisms grow larger and environments more complex, external cues are externalized as **landmarks** — stable signals that anchor navigation.
- **Humans**: We mirror the landmark process internally. Cognitive landmarks function like spatial ones: stable reference points that orient movement through conceptual space.

> "When you're microscopic, the territory is the map; at human scale, we externalise those cues as landmarks — then mirror the process inside our heads."

## Neural Architecture

Humans are biologically predisposed to seek navigational markers:
- This tendency emerges early in development.
- It is shared across many animal species.
- It is rooted in neural architecture (the paper cites Chan et al. 2012 on visual landmarks; Freas & Cheng 2022 on cross-species navigation).

The hippocampus (and its role in spatial and episodic memory) is the obvious locus, though the essays do not discuss it directly.

## Applied to Cognition

The internal equivalent of external landmarks is the [[Insta-Places|insta-place]]:
- Conjured rapidly from experience.
- Treated as solid.
- Oriented toward action.

Disorientation (absence of landmarks) produces discomfort — a survival signal. This is the biological substrate of [[Information Hunger and Orientation]].

## Applied to Social Media

People use phones and the internet as a genuine part of their physical-virtual environment. Without sufficient places for orientation, users experience the same evolutionary discomfort as an animal without landmarks: "glassy-eyed, lost."

The social internet's failure is the failure to provide genuine landmarks — it provides only signal that *gestures toward* information without delivering it.
