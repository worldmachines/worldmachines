# Limits of Mapping (Bryant)

## Overview

Bryant is explicit that onto-cartography is not a totalizing framework. Chapter 8 contains several distinct self-criticisms that together constitute a rigorous acknowledgment of cartographic limits. These are not reluctant concessions but structurally necessary features of any honest geophilosophy.

## 1. The Map Is Not the Territory

The most fundamental limit: "The paradoxical danger of cartographical analyses is that they can blind us to the new forms social ecologies are taking. We forget that the map is not the territory and that, in any event, territories change" (p. 285).

Exemplary form of this problem: Marx and Foucault produced maps of *specific* social ecologies under *specific* historical conditions. Their value lies in their techniques, not their findings. "It is not just their findings that we should take from Marx and Foucault, but also their *techniques*" (p. 285). To apply their findings directly to new ecologies is to mistake the map for the territory — to let inherited cartography blind you to the actual configuration of machines in the present.

This is a constitutive epistemological limit: representation always selects; selection always excludes. No map captures a territory fully. The more complex the territory, the more partial any representation of it.

## 2. Surprising Central Actors

"We have to take care not to overly simplify the assemblages we wish to map because often the central actors of these assemblages will be surprising" (p. 259). Bryant's example: a crude topographical map of contemporary empire focuses on governments, laws, corporations, militaries, money — and misses that what *makes* contemporary empire possible is its nervous system of fiber optic cables, satellites, radio waves, cell phone towers, clocks, container ships, binary computer codes (p. 259–260). The non-human agencies are invisible precisely because they are ubiquitous.

Practical limit: the cartographer's prior concepts and attentional habits will cause them to over-represent some machines (especially human and semiotic ones) and under-represent others (especially technological and material ones). Anthropocentric bias is a systematic mapping error.

## 3. Specificity and Non-Transferability

"Just as you can't transfer the findings of an ecological map of the Amazonian rain forests to the great sequoia forests of California, you can't transfer Foucault's analysis of power in *Discipline & Punish* to just any geographical location or point in history" (p. 285). Maps are specific; assemblages are historically singular. The transfer of a map from one ecology to another is always a distortion unless accompanied by independent cartographical work on the new ecology.

## 4. Unintended Consequences of Deconstruction

Material deconstruction — severing relations between machines to open new paths of movement — can cascade through assemblages in unpredictable ways. Bryant's example: cane toads introduced into Northern Australia in 1932 to prey on insects ravaging sugar cane were wildly successful in eliminating the insects and equally devastating to the broader ecosystem, having no natural predators (p. 273).

"In devising a modal map to be produced through deconstruction, it is important to take contingencies into account as much as desired outcomes. This requires a knowledge of the topography of the system into which we're intervening, the nature of the machines that populate that topography, and how they're likely to react to these deconstructions" (p. 273). The better the topographical map, the better one can anticipate second-order effects — but never perfectly.

## 5. Human Exceptionalism in Available Cartographies

Bryant acknowledges that even the best available cartographical traditions — Marxist and Foucaultian — remain "too mired in human exceptionalism" (p. 285). Their maps are good maps of human-centered assemblages; they systematically underweight the roles of technology, geography, climate, and non-human agencies. The limitation is not incidental but structural — it follows from philosophical assumptions (the linguistic turn, humanist ethics) that the maps inherited.

## The Cartographic Paradox

These limits together generate a paradox: cartography is both necessary and constitutively inadequate. Without maps, effective intervention is impossible. With maps, you risk freezing your view of the territory at the moment of mapping. The resolution is not to map less but to map with explicit awareness of partiality, to iterate maps as territories change, and to hold findings tentatively while holding techniques firmly.
