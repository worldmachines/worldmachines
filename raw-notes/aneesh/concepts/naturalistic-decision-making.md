# Naturalistic Decision Making (NDM)

## Definition

Naturalistic Decision Making is a branch of applied cognitive psychology that studies how skilled practitioners make decisions in real-world conditions: under time pressure, with incomplete information, high stakes, and dynamic environments. Founded in the 1980s–90s primarily by [[Gary Klein]] and colleagues, initially funded by the US military.

## Methodological Core

NDM uses **field study methods** — ethnographic observation, critical decision method interviews, think-aloud protocols with actual experts doing actual work. The unit of analysis is the expert practitioner in naturalistic context, not the undergraduate in a psychology lab.

This is the key methodological contrast with the [[Heuristics and Biases]] tradition (Kahneman, Tversky, Thaler): H&B administers toy problems to unskilled subjects in controlled lab settings, labels departures from normative models as "biases."

## Core Findings

1. **Experts do not deliberate between options in the way classical decision theory predicts.** They pattern-match to past experience, construct a first-plausible-action, mentally simulate it, and execute — or adjust and try the next option. (Recognition-Primed Decision model).
2. **The "cognitive biases" identified in lab settings are, in naturalistic settings, often the source of expert performance** — not error. The same cognitive processes that produce biases on toy problems enable rapid, accurate performance on real problems.
3. **What distinguishes expert from novice is primarily the richness of the pattern library and mental models**, not reasoning process differences.

## The NDM vs. H&B Dispute

This is one of the most important unsettled disputes in cognitive psychology. The stakes:

| H&B (Kahneman et al) | NDM (Klein et al) |
|---|---|
| Lab experiments, novices, toy problems | Field studies, experts, real tasks |
| "Biases" are system-wide cognitive limitations | "Biases" are domain-specific; expertise corrects them |
| Error reduction via debiasing training | Error reduction via expertise development |
| "Keep an open mind" strategies | "Commit early, monitor vigilantly" strategies |
| Gary Kahneman, Amos Tversky, Phil Tetlock | Gary Klein, Karl Weick, Mica Endsley |

Klein et al's claim: **debiasing interventions consistently underperform expertise development in naturalistic settings.** The 2021 Weiss & Shanteau paper *The Futility of Decision Making Research* represents the frustration of the H&B tradition itself with its practical failures.

## Key NDM Frameworks

- **Recognition-Primed Decision (RPD) model** — how experts make fast, good decisions via pattern recognition and mental simulation
- **[[Data-Frame Theory of Sensemaking]]** — how experts construct meaning from ambiguous data
- **Critical Decision Method** — interview protocol for extracting tacit expert knowledge (related to [[Cognitive Task Analysis]])

## Military Origins and Applications

NDM grew directly out of US military research needs: how do commanders make good decisions under the fog of war? How do Navy operators decide whether a radar blip is enemy or friendly? This context explains the emphasis on time pressure, incomplete information, and high stakes — all features absent from lab decision studies.

Applied results include: better training programs for the armed forces, better UI for military and industrial control systems, [[Accelerated Expertise]] training programs.

## Expert-Novice Differences in Sensemaking (Klein et al. 2007)

Klein et al.'s study of information operations specialists found that experts and novices used the **same reasoning strategies** — both abductive, both connection-seeking, both speculative. What differed:
- Experts had richer, more differentiated frames (more specific expectancies)
- Experts were more context-sensitive and their inferences more plausible
- Experts oriented to functional action (what to do) while novices stayed at the level of abstraction
- Experts could ignore noise more reliably (recognized contraband cigarettes as irrelevant; novices investigated further)
- Experts were ~3x more likely to generate action recommendations than novices

The implication for training: don't teach experts to "think like novices think" (there's nothing to import from novice reasoning). Instead, give novices access to richer frames through experience-building and causal relationship expansion. See [[Just-in-Time Mental Models]].
