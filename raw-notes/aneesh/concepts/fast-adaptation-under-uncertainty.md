# Fast Adaptation Under Uncertainty

## Core Idea

Effective businesspeople do not outperform competitors by *predicting the future more accurately*. Forecasting is too expensive and too unreliable. Instead, the superior competitive strategy is **faster adaptation** — setting up structures and habits that allow faster response to new information and changing conditions.

This is Cedric Chin's (Commoncog) distillation of patterns across business biographies and technological history. It implies:
- **Forecasters want to be right; businesspeople want to win** — different objective functions lead to different epistemic strategies
- Advantage accrues to whoever can sensemake faster and act in response to present conditions
- "You don't have to predict the future if you can see the present moment clearly."

## Required Conditions

For fast adaptation to work as a strategy, the operator needs:
1. **Superior sensemaking**: more accurate and faster construction of the current situation's frame
2. **Organizational agility**: structures that can change direction quickly when new information arrives
3. **Information sharing infrastructure**: mechanisms for collective sensemaking (shared channels, curated knowledge feeds)
4. **Experimentation orientation**: willingness to treat new capability claims as hypotheses to test, not conclusions to adopt or reject

## The Superforecasting Comparison

Chin's claim is implicitly in tension with Phil Tetlock's [[Superforecasting]] work, which suggests that calibrated probabilistic forecasting can be meaningfully improved. Chin's response: forecasting skill is irrelevant for operators whose competitive position depends on action, not accuracy of prior beliefs. The businessperson's question is "what should I do?" not "what will happen?"

## Technological Disruption Context

During technological revolutions, fast adaptation is especially valuable:
- Impacts are uneven and context-specific — general forecasts are useless for specific operators
- The technology develops faster than prediction cycles can track
- First-mover advantages tend to be transient; sustained advantage comes from organizational coupling (see: Walmart and PCs)

The Walmart case (from [[How Experts Sensemake]]) illustrates: the winning move was not predicting that PCs would matter, but using the technology to construct a new organizational form (decentralized logistics, cross-docking) that competitors couldn't easily copy.

## Relationship to Data-Frame Theory

Fast adaptation is the operational implication of the [[Data-Frame Theory of Sensemaking]] at the competitive strategy level:
- Superior sensemaking = better frame construction = seeing more of the present moment
- Organizational agility = structural capacity to act on reframings
- Experimentation = the elaboration cycle enacted at scale
