# The Old Internet

A recurring concept in contemporary media criticism: the era before social media platforms dominated publishing, when the web was characterized by:

- **Personal homepages and early blogs**: written for self-expression or small communities, not for audiences, virality, or monetization.
- **Niche forums**: organized around shared interest rather than identity performance.
- **No algorithmic curation**: you followed links, not feeds optimized for engagement.
- **Asymmetric audience expectations**: most writers expected few readers; they wrote anyway.

## As Counter-Example to Storyselling

In [[Looking Forward to Montaigne]], the old internet is invoked as the most recent example of a pre-storyselling mode of writing. [[Michel de Montaigne]]'s frank meandering is said to "remind of the old internet" — a high compliment meaning: authentic, non-optimized, genuinely exploratory.

The old internet had no conversion funnels, no engagement optimization, no brand to maintain. This made it structurally resistant to [[Storyselling vs. Storytelling|storyselling]].

## The Lost Quality: Liveness

The essay argues that what's been stolen is a sense of *liveness* — the feeling that real people are living real lives and occasionally publishing glimpses. Social media performs liveness (updates, stories, reels) while systematically draining it through curation and optimization.

Small, granular personal stories — "I thwarted the bugs in my balcony garden" — restore liveness in a way that polished influencer content cannot.

## Relation to Platform Economics

The shift from old internet to social media is partly structural: platforms discovered that engagement optimization increases time-on-site and ad revenue. The old internet's distributed, non-monetized character was economically "inefficient" from a platform perspective. Its loss is a product of economic incentives, not cultural decline per se.

## Open Questions

- Can the old internet's qualities be reproduced on current infrastructure (newsletters, independent blogs)?
- Is the nostalgic framing accurate, or does it romanticize what was also a less accessible, more homogeneous space?
