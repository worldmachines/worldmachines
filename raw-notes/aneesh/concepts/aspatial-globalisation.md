# Aspatial Globalisation

The target of Chapter 8 of [[doreen-massey]]'s *For Space* (2005). The dominant late-twentieth and early-twenty-first century narrative of globalisation presents the world as becoming a single, frictionless, market-integrated space. Massey reads this as **a-spatial** in two senses: it pretends space doesn't matter (everything's connected, distance is irrelevant), and it denies the actual spatial reality of how globalisation operates.

## What the narrative claims

1. Globalisation is **inevitable** — like gravity (Clinton 1998; cited 5).
2. Globalisation is **uniform** — every region, every economy will be integrated.
3. Globalisation **transcends space** — geography no longer matters; "the end of geography."
4. Resistance is **futile and irrational** — those "behind" must catch up.

## What's wrong with it

### 1. It is a temporal convening of space all over again

The "ahead/behind" rhetoric is identical to the modernity move ([[spatialising-modernity]]). Different places are recoded as different stages in a single inevitable trajectory. See [[cosmopolitan-time-queue]].

### 2. It is a normative project, not a description

"This vision of global space, then, is not so much a description of how the world is, as an image in which the world is being made." (Massey 2005: 83)

The discourse is dominantly produced in the global North, has its institutions (IMF, World Bank, WTO), and is enforced through structural adjustment programmes. It justifies and naturalises the very practices it claims merely to describe.

### 3. It is internally inconsistent

The same actors simultaneously deploy *two* contradictory spatial imaginations:
- **Free trade rhetoric**: space is barrier-less, mobility is virtue, borders are obstacles.
- **Anti-migration rhetoric**: borders are sacred, "local people" have rights to "local places," asylum-seekers are bogus.

> "In one breath such spokespeople assume that 'free trade' is akin to some moral virtue; in the next they pour out venom against asylum-seekers . . . and 'economic migrants' ('economics', it seems, is not a good enough reason to want to migrate — what was that they were saying about capital?)." (85)

The doubleness is **the operating principle**. Capital and the rich enjoy the first imagination; the poor and the unskilled get the second. The contradiction does the political work.

### 4. It denies its own [[power-geometry]]

Globalisation does not erase spatial relations; it produces **new geometries of power** — new directions of flow, new asymmetries, new exclusions. Pretending these are absent is part of how they get reproduced.

> "What are at issue are the constantly-being-produced new geometries of power, the shifting geographies of power-relations." (85)

### 5. It denies coevalness

Like the modernity narrative, globalisation operates through the **denial of coevalness** ([[coevalness]]): "Africa" is not different, it is "developing." The future is the West's present.

## What Massey wants instead

A **spatialised** account of globalisation that:

- Recognises **multiplicity**: not one process but many simultaneous, partially-overlapping ones.
- Maps **power-geometries** rather than smoothed surfaces.
- Recognises **discontinuities, ruptures, exclusions** as constitutive features, not unfortunate side effects.
- Holds open the possibility of **alternative globalisations** rather than naturalising one.

She quotes Spivak's point that the West's recent "discovery" of globalisation is in fact a discovery of *being globalised* — the experience of inward investment, immigration into Western cities, the so-called "arrival of the margins at the centre." But, Massey adds, most of the margins have *not* arrived; they have been strictly excluded.
