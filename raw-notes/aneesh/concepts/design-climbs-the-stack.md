# Design Climbs the Stack

**Articulated by**: [[Aneesh Sathe]], [[Not My Lever — The Last Container]] (Section V), 2026, building directly on [[david-s-landes|Landes]]'s *Revolution in Time* (1983) and extending it through the AI-auteuring and lever-discipline rungs.

## The Thesis

Design has been climbing the stack for three centuries. At each rung, the design discipline separates from the layer below it. The layer below, once design has separated, becomes cheap — *commoditised by the act of separation itself*. The pattern has a direction, and the direction has now reached **intent**.

## The Five Rungs

| Rung | When | What separated | What was commoditised |
|--|--|--|--|
| 1. **Skill → design** | Geneva / La Chaux-de-Fonds, c. 1750s | Design separated from craft (Jaquet-Droz; [[david-s-landes|Landes]]) | Skill |
| 2. **Craft → mass production** | 19th century | Design separated from craft entirely | The bespoke artefact |
| 3. **Machining → software** | 20th century | Design compiled into copies for the cost of writing once | Manufacturing per-unit cost |
| 4. **Generation → AI auteuring** | 2020s | Design separated from generation; model produces, [[auteuring|auteur]] picks | Generation |
| 5. **Intent → lever discipline** | 2020s+ (Liveness era) | Design separated from intent itself; substrate offers, lever-disciplined person picks | A worldview held over a lifetime |

> "Each climb commoditizes the layer it left behind. Skill became cheap once design separated from it. Generation has become cheap because auteuring has separated from it. The pattern has a direction." — Sathe, 2026.

## Rung 1: Pierre Jaquet-Droz and the Geneva Workshop

Before the climb began, the carpenter knew how to make a chair because he had made many chairs; the design lived in his hand and his eye, inseparable from the making. To buy a chair was to buy the carpenter's accumulated practice. The chair could not exist outside the workshop because the design did not exist outside the carpenter.

In Geneva and London in the eighteenth century, watchmakers separated the two. **Pierre Jaquet-Droz**, working in **La Chaux-de-Fonds in the 1750s**, drew movements on paper and sent the drawings to specialised hands: wheel-cutters, jewellers, pinion-makers, springers, each executing the parts they were paid to execute. The complete watch came from no single hand. The design lived in the drawing and the drawing lived in the workshop; the parts were made by people who knew their own piece and only their own piece.

> "David Landes, in *Revolution in Time*, called this the moment design became the asset and skill became the commodity." — Sathe, 2026.

The Geneva watch trade is the cleanest case study because the artefact (the movement) is small, complex, and irreducibly composite — a setting in which the separation between design and skill is *forced* rather than chosen. The pattern, once established, propagates.

## Rungs 2-4: The Industrial Climb

The same separation, repeated at higher layers:

- **Mass production** took the next step: design separated from craft entirely, and the manufactured object became cheaper than the bespoke one. The Ford Model T is the canonical case.
- **Software** took another: design separated from machining; *a specification compiled into a million identical copies for the cost of writing it once*. The marginal cost per unit fell to zero.
- **AI auteuring** took another: design separated from generation. The model produces a thousand candidates; the auteur picks the one. The candidates are commodity; the pick is the asset.

At each rung the same logic holds: the act of designing *above* the layer makes the layer cheap. Whatever was once expensive in that layer (the skilled craftsman's wage, the manufactured object's per-unit cost, the line of code, the generated candidate) collapses toward zero.

## Rung 5: Intent — the Liveness-Era Climb

The Liveness era is the latest rung. **Design separates from intent itself.** The substrate offers a thousand intents one could hold — climate, supply chain, discourse, the war in the feed, the algorithm, the next century — and the [[not-my-lever|lever-disciplined]] person picks the few they will hold and *designs the mindset that will hold them*, for the duration of one project.

The mindset itself is the asset; the unbounded availability of intent-objects is the commodity. The [[designed-mindset|designed mindset]] (Stoic indifference for the finishing phase, Romantic immersion for the seeing-it-fresh phase) is what replaces the perma-worldview that earlier eras carried over a lifetime.

The commodity at this rung is striking: *a worldview held over a lifetime* is what becomes cheap. There is so much worldview available, so well-articulated, so frictionlessly accessible, that holding one of them is no longer the costly act it was for Marcus Aurelius. What is costly is the *picking* — which intents to hold, which modules to wire in, for which duration.

## Kannan's Formulation: Intelligence as Information Directing Energy

The essay quotes (second-hand, via [[venkatesh-rao|Rao]]) **Sreeram Kannan** of EigenLabs:

> "Intelligence is a unit of information directing a unit of energy."

The formulation collapses the climb into a single relation. The "unit of information" *is* design — what design becomes when it stops being skill. The "unit of energy" is whatever the design directs at each rung: a piece of metal in the watch, a kilowatt-hour in the factory, a CPU cycle in software, a generated candidate in AI, an intent in the Liveness era.

At each rung, intelligence increases in resolution: it directs ever finer units of energy at ever higher layers of the stack. Lever discipline is intelligence operating at the layer of intent — a unit of information (an audit) directing a unit of energy (the few levers actually pulled).

## Why the Pattern Has a Direction

Three reasons the climb is monotonic:

1. **Commoditisation is irreversible.** Once skill becomes cheap, it does not become expensive again. The Geneva watchmaker's apprenticeship cannot recover its 1750 price.
2. **Each new rung consumes the surplus the previous one created.** Cheap skill made mass production economically viable; cheap mass production made software economically distinguished; cheap software made AI generation possible at scale; cheap generation makes auteuring the binding skill; cheap auteuring makes lever discipline the binding skill.
3. **Design eats upward by definition.** The discipline of design is the discipline of selecting *which thing to make*. As the layer being made becomes cheap, the design discipline necessarily focuses on the *next layer up* — there is no other useful place to apply it.

## What This Means for the Working Person

A craftsperson in 1700 made by skill; a designer in 1900 specified for craftspeople to make; a software engineer in 2000 wrote specifications that compiled themselves; an auteur in 2025 picks among candidates a model produced; a lever-disciplined worker in 2026 picks which intents to hold and designs the mindset that holds them. Each is the *same operational position* — the asset-bearer at the top of the stack — at a different rung.

The implication is not that earlier rungs disappear. The watchmaker still exists; mass production still exists; software still exists; AI generation still exists. The implication is that the *binding skill* has moved up the stack, and the people who command the binding skill at each rung capture the surplus the rung produces.
