# Contingent vs. Self-Maintaining Assemblage

## The Distinction

Not all assemblages that happen to be organized at a given moment are equally durable. Bryant implicitly distinguishes two types:

**Contingent assemblages** are low-entropy systems at a given moment — their elements happen to be organized in a particular, improbable way — but they have no active mechanisms for maintaining that organization against entropic drift. They are merely "lowly entropic" rather than *negentropic*. Their organization will naturally drift toward high entropy absent some active intervention, and nothing internal to the assemblage prevents this.

**Self-maintaining assemblages** (negentropic systems) actively engage in ongoing operations to reproduce their organization across time. Their persistence is not passive but *operational*: the assemblage is doing work — using energy — to maintain the improbability of its structure.

Bryant frames it: "A low entropic system is one that merely maintains a particular organization at a particular temporal moment. The destiny of such systems is often to evolve to a high entropic system. By contrast, a negentropic system is a system that engages in *operations* that stave off — at least for a time — transition to a high entropic state." (p. 95)

## Examples

| Assemblage | Type | Basis of Organization |
|---|---|---|
| Gas introduced into a bottle (T₁) | Contingent low-entropy | Gas is initially localized but no operation maintains this |
| Rock | Contingent (force-maintained) | Physical forces maintain structure without active operations |
| Crystal | Contingent (force-maintained) | Molecular bonding; no regrowth if chipped |
| Bacterial colony | Self-maintaining | Autopoietic operations reproduce the colony |
| Organism | Self-maintaining | Metabolism, cell reproduction, wound healing |
| Conversation | Contingent-to-self-maintaining | Exists only during active co-production; ceases when operations stop |
| City | Self-maintaining (complex) | Infrastructure maintenance, energy flows, social reproduction |
| Mathematical equation | Rigid incorporeal (force-based) | Not threatened by entropy; requires no work |
| Political movement | Self-maintaining (fragile) | Requires ongoing operations of solidarity, belief-maintenance, organization |

## The Temporal Dimension

The contingent/self-maintaining distinction is fundamentally temporal. Time is "the duration required for a machine to produce the elements that compose it" (p. 96). Self-maintaining assemblages produce time as the rhythm of their reproductive operations. Contingent assemblages have no such temporal depth — they exist at an instant but have no internal mechanism for producing the next instant.

This is why Bryant says machines are processes: the self-maintaining assemblage is not a static structure but a process of continuous reproduction. "Reproduction" here is not copying but "the continuation of either a low entropic or negentropic organization across time." (p. 107)

## Why the Distinction Matters for Political Analysis

The distinction has direct political stakes:

1. **Durability**: Understanding whether a machine is contingent or self-maintaining determines how it can be disrupted. Contingent assemblages dissolve when their circumstances change; self-maintaining assemblages must have their *operations* disrupted — their energy supplies cut, their feedback mechanisms jammed, their reproductive cycles interrupted.

2. **New Orleans after Katrina**: The city revealed itself as a self-maintaining assemblage when the material flows sustaining its operations were cut — the negentropic structure collapsed. This shows that even apparently stable social machines are contingent on ongoing material flows; they are *operationally* self-maintaining but *materially* dependent on an exterior.

3. **Revolutionary strategy**: "Revolutionary machines must develop knowledge of the negentropic machines that allow these reactionary machines to persist across time, structuring lives, cognition, associations between people, etc." (p. 108) To dismantle an oppressive machine, you must understand its self-maintaining operations.

## The Bricolage Problem

All self-maintaining assemblages are also *bricolages* — they are never perfectly organized because their component parts retain semi-autonomous trajectories. This means self-maintaining assemblages are perpetually engaged in *two* kinds of work: (1) maintaining the pattern of their own organization against entropy, and (2) managing the machinic problems introduced by their component machines' semi-autonomy. No machine ever fully resolves either problem; "every machine is jerry-rigged by the anonymous Macgyvers of nature and culture." (p. 80)
