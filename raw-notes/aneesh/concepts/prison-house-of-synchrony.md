# The Prison-House of Synchrony

A phrase from Chapter 3 of [[doreen-massey]]'s *For Space* (2005). The target is the philosophical translation of **synchrony** into **space**, with the consequence that "the spatial" becomes the realm of stasis, closure, and political inertness.

## The structuralist move

[[claude-levi-strauss]] and the broader structuralist tradition arose in part as an *anti-narrative* project: a refusal to convene other societies into Europe's developmental past (see [[spatialising-modernity]]). Each society was to be analysed as a coherent **structure** in its own right rather than as a stage in a single story. Structure was contrasted with process; synchrony with diachrony.

So far, so good. The trouble was the next step: synchrony was equated with **space**, on the mere assumption that whatever is not-temporal must be spatial:

> "If these structures weren't temporal, they must be spatial. Structure and process were read as space and time. Space was conceived . . . to be the absolute negation of time." (Massey 2005: 37)

The result is a **negative** definition: space is whatever is left over when temporality is subtracted. As Osborne (1995: 27, cited 37) puts it, "synchrony is not con-temporality, but a-temporality." The structuralist's "space" is not a slice through real coexistence; it is a conceptual schema purged of time.

## The double closure

Massey identifies two compounded closures in this picture:

1. **A-temporality** — the structure is robbed of internal dynamism.
2. **Causal closure** — "all the elements of the whole . . . are in an immediate relationship with one another" (Althusser 1970: 94, quoted 40). Every part expresses every other; nothing arrives from outside; no real change is possible.

The combination is what makes the prison-house. Space-as-synchrony cannot be the site of politics because politics requires dislocation, and dislocation cannot enter a closed system.

## The post-structuralist legacy

The framing survives intact in writers who otherwise reject structuralism:

- [[louis-althusser]] critiqued Hegel's "essential section" and the structuralist synchrony — but his constructive theory of overdetermination still treats "space" largely as the term for closure.
- [[ernesto-laclau]] (*New Reflections*, 1990) reserves "spatial" for **causal closure** ("any repetition that is governed by a structural law of successions is space," Laclau 1990: 41, quoted 43). Massey argues this is "a lack of (a particular definition of) temporality" rather than a description of any positive space. See [[coupure-and-causal-closure]].
- [[michel-de-certeau]]'s strategy/tactic distinction maps the same binary onto urban resistance: strategy = space (the proper, the static); tactic = time (the mobile, the on-the-wing). The dichotomy traps both terms.

In each case the **liberation of temporality is bought at the price of leaving space as the prison-house**.

## Why this matters

The implication of the diagnosis: every effort to open up politics by opening up time has tacitly endorsed a picture of space as the realm where nothing happens — and has therefore *underwritten* the very closure it was trying to escape. If space is the closed system, then change happens only in time, and "history" is conceived as one homogeneous story flowing through a series of static cross-sections. This image grounds the singular universal, the one-history, the one voice that speaks for everyone.

> "It is lacking in the contingency which is the condition for that openness which, in turn, is the precondition for politics." (41)

## Massey's exit

Drop the equation synchrony=space. **Spaces are not cross-sections of a closed system.** A spatial moment is a constellation of trajectories — each carrying its own temporality, none reducible to a single narrative line. The synchronic "now" is not contemporaneity-as-stasis; it is **dynamic simultaneity** — see [[dynamic-simultaneity]], [[simultaneity-of-multiple-trajectories]].
