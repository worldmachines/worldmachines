# Frame Fixation

## Definition

Frame fixation is the pathological form of the **preservation cycle** in the [[Data-Frame Theory of Sensemaking]]: the practitioner encounters disconfirming data but refuses to abandon their current explanatory frame, even when that frame is demonstrably inadequate. The result is systematic blindness to alternative interpretations of events.

## Why It Happens

The structural cause: **anchor lock-in**. Data points used to anchor the current frame become cognitively difficult to redeploy in constructing an alternative frame. Early anchors are especially sticky — the earlier a flawed anchor is introduced, the harder it is to escape.

Contributing factors:
- Sunk cognitive investment in the current frame
- Each new data point is interpreted in light of the current frame, which reinforces it
- Constructing alternative frames requires fragment availability — novices with thin fragment libraries have fewer alternatives to try

## Expert-Novice Differences

Experts are *faster* to recover from frame fixation, not immune to it. They recover faster because:
1. Richer expectancies → earlier anomaly detection
2. Larger frame repertoire → cheaper to propose alternatives
3. Painful prior experience of being burnt by fixation → vigilance habit

Klein et al: experts in many domains report wariness of frame fixation as a career-long learned instinct.

## Domain-Specific Coping Strategies

**Medicine (stable domain)**:
"Logical competitor set" — a practitioner groups diseases with identical symptom presentations together, not by medical category but by diagnostic ambiguity. This forces systematic anchor reuse: the same symptoms that anchor a diagnosis of condition A must be tested as anchors for B and C. The human body changes slowly enough that this set stays useful for an entire career.

**Business/Investing/War (dynamic domains)**:
The logical competitor set fails because the domain itself changes — new technology can invalidate an entire set of frames. Expert strategy here:
- Commit early to a frame (enables specific expectancies)
- Remain vigilantly attentive to anomalies
- Maintain large fragment libraries so alternative frame construction is fast
- Prefer constructing a new frame and seeking confirming evidence over searching for disconfirming evidence against the current one (exploits the brain's positive evidence bias)

## Asymmetry: Positive vs. Negative Evidence

> "It is easier to construct an alternative frame and seek confirming evidence for it than to search for disconfirming evidence for a current frame, because human brains find it easier to reason about positive evidence than negative evidence."

This asymmetry means that the effective strategy for escaping fixation is *not* Popperian falsificationism (seek disconfirmation of current hypothesis) but rather **alternative frame construction** (build a competing hypothesis and look for confirming data).

## Relevance to Technology Adoption

When facing a revolutionary new technology (e.g., AI), the primary risk for experienced practitioners is frame fixation: interpreting new technological capabilities through the lens of current domain mental models. This produces data blindness — new outcomes that only make sense in a new frame are simply not noticed.

Chin's practical implication: actively elaborate alternative frames in parallel with your current operating frame, even before you've committed to them.
