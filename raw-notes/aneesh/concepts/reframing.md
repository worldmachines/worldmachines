# Reframing

## Definition

**Reframing** is the process of replacing the current explanatory frame with a new one. It is one of the seven forms of sensemaking identified by Klein et al. (2007) and represents the most significant cognitive transition in the data-frame model: a change not in data or details but in the fundamental structure through which the situation is understood.

Reframing is not simply accumulating inconsistencies. It requires that the replacement frame and the new data emerge simultaneously — the replacement frame guides the search for data that will anchor it, and the new data suggest the replacement frame.

## The Mechanism

Weick (1995): "During reframing people consider data elements that had previously been discarded, so that formerly discrepant cues now fit."

This is the signature of genuine reframing: data that were previously explained away, discarded as noise, or categorized as irrelevant become central to the new understanding. The NICU nurse example (Example 5) illustrates this: the nurse maintained two frames simultaneously — "making a good recovery" and "developing sepsis" — and eventually the accumulating anomalies (limpness, skin color, bradys, residual) made the sepsis frame dominant, reinterpreting all the previous normal readings.

## The Anchor Replacement Problem

Reframing is difficult because anchors that have been used to build one frame are difficult to use in a competing frame. This "rival use" of anchors creates conceptual strain. Experts develop "logical competitor sets" (LCS) — memory structures that activate competing diagnoses simultaneously — specifically to manage this problem. LCS lets an expert decouple anchors from their initial frame, allowing them to serve double duty. See [[Anchoring in Sensemaking]].

## Reframing vs. Elaboration

Elaborating a frame adds detail without changing its structure. Reframing changes the structure — the causal story, the script, the map — that organizes all the data. A partial indicator: after elaboration, the previously perceived data remain in place; after reframing, they may be reinterpreted entirely.

The shading between elaboration and reframing is continuous. The helicopter accident (Example 3) shows sequential reframing through anchor replacement — each new anchor suggesting a better frame, with the previous frame fading away. Whether each step was "elaboration" or "reframing" is partly a matter of degree.

## Reframing and Insight

Duncker (1945) introduced the concept of insight as requiring reframing. His "radiation problem" — how to use radiation to destroy a tumor without damaging surrounding tissue — was unsolvable as long as subjects held the initial frame ("use radiation to treat a tumor without destroying healthy tissue"). Subjects who could reframe it ("how to minimize radiation intensity except at the site of the tumor") discovered the solution of converging weak beams. The solution was not obscure; it was invisible within the original frame.

This connects reframing to the broader literature on creative problem-solving and insight: the Aha moment is often a frame replacement, not a new piece of information.

## Reframing vs. Diagnostic Vagabonding

The pathological excess of reframing is **diagnostic vagabonding** (Dörner, 1996; Rudolph, 2003): continual reframing without sufficient commitment to any frame to test it properly. Rudolph found vagabonding to be nearly as dysfunctional as [[Fixation Error]]. The optimal is:
- Sufficient commitment to generate specific expectancies (which anomalies become detectable against)
- Sufficient openness to recognize when expectancies are systematically violated
- A competing frame available when questioning begins

## Piaget Parallel

Reframing maps onto Piaget's *accommodation*: restructuring the cognitive schema to fit data that cannot be assimilated. Elaborating a frame maps onto *assimilation*: fitting data into existing schema. The data-frame cycle is Piagetian development compressed into situational timescales.

## Functional Reframing

Klein et al. emphasize that reframing is not just an epistemic achievement — it changes what a practitioner does. Functional sensemaking means knowing what to do in a situation. Reframing changes the action repertoire: Captain L. in the laundry chute fire reframes from "basement fire" to "heavily involved building" and immediately shifts strategy from attack to search-and-rescue, radios for a "triple two" alarm, and dispatches his truck crew to the fourth floor. The reframing was not abstract; it was action-generating.
