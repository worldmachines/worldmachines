# Custodianship as Design Ethos

**Source**: [[aneesh-sathe]], *We Need Homes in the Delta Quadrant* (2025), §III — Who Gets to Make Place?

## The Concept

Sathe argues that the asymmetry of power between place-creators and place-users demands a particular ethical posture — one of custodianship rather than sovereignty:

> "This asymmetry between creator and user has led some to argue that ethical design requires more than usability — it requires an ethos of custodianship. The act of place-making must be informed not only by technical possibility, but by moral imagination. A well-designed place is not simply functional — it is inhabited, sustained, and responsive to the people who live within it." (§III)

> "To make a place, then, is not to finalize it. It is to initiate a relationship. The designer, the founder, the engineer — each acts as a temporary steward rather than a sovereign. The real test of their creation is not how complete it feels on launch day, but how it adapts to the people who enter it and make it their own." (§III)

## Steward vs. Sovereign

The distinction is sharp:
- **The sovereign** makes the place once, claims ownership, and resists change. Success is measured by fidelity to original vision.
- **The steward/custodian** initiates the place, then releases it. Success is measured by how well it enables those who inhabit it to make it their own.

The custodian's temporal orientation is forward: what will this place become? The sovereign's temporal orientation is backward: what did I intend?

## The Unselfconscious Model

Sathe grounds custodianship in Alexander and Wakkary's account of [[unselfconscious-design]]: good places emerge from "a slow dance between structure and improvisation" rather than top-down specification. The fridge-as-bulletin-board, the courtyard-as-marketplace — these show that fit between structure and inhabitant takes time, use, and adaptation. A custodian designs for this adaptation rather than foreclosing it.

## Platform and Tool Designers as Unseen Cartographers

Sathe extends the argument to contemporary technology: "those who create the tools through which places are made — architects, technologists, platform designers — wield a power that is both formative and silent." A navigation app, a job platform, a software protocol — each pre-maps possibilities before any individual user encounters them. This is cartographic power exercised silently at scale.

Custodianship demands that these designers acknowledge this power and design with an awareness of who is included and excluded by their choices — not only at launch, but as conditions change and user populations shift.

## Relation to Casey and Bryant

Edward Casey's claim that place is a *generator* of identity implies that whoever designs the place designs, in part, the possibilities of becoming available to its inhabitants. This is not a neutral technical act. Bryant's [[bryant-gravity-as-power]] supplies the mechanism: the designer creates a gravitational field that constrains and enables all future movement within it. Custodianship requires attending to what gravitational field one is creating and whether it enables or traps.
