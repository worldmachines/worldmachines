# Entropy and Assemblage Maintenance

## The Problem

Machines are improbable. In Bryant's information-theoretic usage (following Peirce, Luhmann, and systems biology), entropy measures the *probability distribution* of elements within a system. A highly entropic system is one where any element has an equal probability of appearing anywhere — maximum disorder, no structure from which inferences can be made. A machine, as a differentiated, organized, low-entropy system, is precisely an *improbability*: a state of affairs whose maintenance requires ongoing work against the drift toward high entropy.

"The vast majority of machines are in a constant state of disintegration and are also threatened from without by dissolution. Far from being static lumps that just sit there, machines must instead engage in constant operations to continue their existence from moment to moment. Where those operations cease, the machine also ceases." (p. 107)

## Three Entropy Concepts

Bryant disambiguates three senses of entropy that are often conflated:

1. **Thermodynamic entropy** (heat death): closed systems lose available energy for work; this is the backdrop but not Bryant's primary usage.
2. **Information-theoretic entropy** (Shannon/Peirce): measures probability distribution among elements. High entropy = elements equi-probably distributed = no structure = no inference. This is Bryant's operative concept.
3. **Biological/systems-theoretic negentropy** (Schrödinger, Luhmann, autopoiesis theory): living and social systems actively engage in operations to *maintain* a state of low entropy — to resist the natural drift toward disorder.

## Negentropic Systems: The Key Category

A *negentropic* system is one that actively works to maintain low entropy rather than merely happening to be in a low-entropy state at a given moment. The distinction matters:

- A *low entropy system* may simply happen to be organized at T₁ but have no mechanisms preventing drift to high entropy by T₂.
- A *negentropic system* has ongoing operations — what Luhmann calls *operations* — that continuously reproduce the system's organizational pattern.

"A negentropic system is a system that engages in *operations* that stave off — at least for a time — transition to a high entropic state. A negentropic system engages in operations to reduce and exclude noise, thereby maintaining the improbability of its organization." (p. 95)

Luhmann's formulation, quoted by Bryant: "reproduction is a continuous problem for systems with temporalized complexity... not with static but with dynamic stability. All elements pass away. They cannot endure as elements in time, and thus they must constantly be produced on the basis of whatever constellation of elements is actual at any given moment... [W]e will call the reproduction of eventlike elements *operations*." (Luhmann 1995: 49, quoted p. 96)

## Two Strategies of Entropy Resistance

Bryant identifies two broad modes by which machines resist entropy:

### 1. Force-Based (Inorganic Machines)
Most inorganic corporeal machines — rocks, crystals, stars — maintain their organization through the agency of *forces*: molecular bonding, electrostatic attraction, gravitational cohesion. These forces hold the machine together without requiring the machine to engage in active operations. A rock that has a piece chipped off does not regrow it. Inorganic machines maintain low entropy passively, through the physics and chemistry of their composition.

### 2. Operations-Based (Organic, Cognitive, Social Machines)
Organic bodies, minds, and social systems maintain organization through active *operations* that continuously reproduce both the parts and the relations between the parts:
- **Organic**: cells die and are replaced; wounds heal; the body reproduces not copies of dead cells but cells that restore the organizational pattern of the organism
- **Cognitive**: memory, habit, belief-maintenance — some externalized via media (Clark's extended mind)
- **Social**: education, law, norm-enforcement, infrastructure maintenance, economic reproduction

This is why time is constitutive for these machines: "time is, in part, the duration required for a machine to produce the elements that compose it." (p. 96) Machines are not in time; they produce time as the rhythm of their reproductive operations.

## The City as Paradigm Case

Bryant's extended example of a social machine facing entropy is the city. A city is a low entropy system: its stratification into classes, occupations, ethnic/religious groups is an improbable distribution of persons. This improbability is continuously maintained by:

**Incorporeal machines** (norm-producing and regulatory):
- Educational institutions (teaching trades, differentiating occupations)
- Legal systems (sanctioning deviation)
- Media (television, radio, newspapers forming shared public opinion)
- Churches and cultural institutions (instilling norms)
- Informal neighborhood norms (Spike Lee's *Summer of Sam* as example of regulatory feedback)

**Corporeal machines** (material infrastructure):
- Roads, transit, fiber optic cables (channeling and segregating populations)
- Building stock (enabling differentiated functions)
- Energy infrastructure (powering operations)

Above all, cities require constant **flows of energy and matter from outside**:
> "Cities only exist in and through the operations that perpetuate them. In this respect, and as is the case with all machines, a city is more a verb than a noun." (p. 103)

## Katrina as Thought Experiment

Hurricane Katrina cutting New Orleans off from energy and communication flows serves as Bryant's starkest illustration. Deprived of inputs, the city's negentropic operations ceased. The population rapidly shifted from a low-entropy, differentiated social machine to something like a "gaseous cloud in Brownian motion" — high entropy, where inferences from one element to another became impossible, and familiar social distinctions dissolved. The organization of New Orleans evaporated not because people stopped believing in norms but because the material flows sustaining the operations of norm-reproduction ceased.

## Entropy, Plasticity, and Political Stakes

Bryant introduces entropy as the basis for a non-normative but politically charged analysis:

**Entropy is not evil**: "entropy, low entropy, and negentropy, are phenomena of being, not moral preferences." (p. 103) A world of absolute order (zero entropy) would be oppressive and fatal.

**Plasticity** is a degree of entropy within a machine that allows it to develop new forms of openness and new operations. Rigid machines (very low entropy, fixed selective openness) cannot adapt to novel inputs; they will be destroyed by environmental changes they cannot register. Social machines that cannot register climate change because their operational patterns were formed under different climatic conditions exemplify this.

**Political emancipation** often requires introducing entropy: disrupting the negentropic operations that maintain oppressive social machines. Foucault's and Althusser's analyses of power are, on Bryant's reading, investigations of negentropic operations that normalize bodies and maintain existing social stratifications. Revolutionary politics must understand these operations to dismantle them.

"One of the central tasks of onto-cartography is thus the investigation of those operations by which machines stave off entropy and forestall dissolution. Sometimes this investigation will be for the sake of improving a machine. For example, machines like revolutionary political movements might be particularly interested in the operations that allow them to persist." (p. 107)
