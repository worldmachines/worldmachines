# Small Web

## The Idea

From [[A Good Dictionary]]:
> "I'd love to get back to a world where the internet was used in its raw form. If you are reading my posts, please do comment, share your site/blog and your posts."

The "small web" or "indie web" is the aesthetic and political stance that the original hyperlinked web — of personal sites, blogrolls, direct links between individuals, and comment threads — was superior to the platform-mediated attention economy that replaced it.

## What Was Lost

- Direct person-to-person linking (replaced by platform retweets/shares)
- Persistent personal domains (replaced by profiles on rented platforms)
- Comment sections as conversation (replaced by replies that exist only within platform graphs)
- Serendipitous discovery via browsing (replaced by algorithmic feeds)

[[James Somers]]' essays are cited as examples of what the small web produces: careful, long-form pieces that surface underappreciated tools and ideas.

## The Documentation Connection

The small web longing connects to [[Documentation as Empathy]]: personal sites and blogs are a form of documentation of the self — a gift to future readers who might find the work. Platform content is ephemeral and platform-owned; personal sites persist and compound.

## The Language Log as Small Web

[[Language Log]] (mentioned in [[A Good Dictionary]]) is presented as an example of the old web at its best: a site that goes extremely deep on niche topics (how much can you write about spinach?) because it exists for the topic's own sake, not for engagement optimization.
