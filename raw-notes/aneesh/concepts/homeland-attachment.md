# Homeland Attachment

## Core Claim

**Homeland attachment** is Yi-Fu Tuan's term for the affective bond between people and a region — city or countryside — large enough to support a people's livelihood. It is [[topophilia]] at the medium scale: more than the intimate attachment to a chair or a corner, less abstract than love of a nation-state. Its character is shaped by ancestral memory, religious sanction, and communal identity; its intensity varies across cultures and historical periods but its presence is nearly universal.

Tuan's thesis: "Attachment to the homeland is a common human emotion. Its strength varies among different cultures and historical periods. The more ties there are, the stronger is the emotional bond" (*Space and Place*, p. 158).

## The Cosmic Center

Human groups nearly everywhere tend to regard their homeland as the center of the world. Home is the focal point of a cosmic structure — a vertical axis linking heaven and earth passes through it; the stars are perceived to revolve around one's abode. "Such a conception of place ought to give it supreme value; to abandon it would be hard to imagine" (p. 149).

But Tuan notes that this cosmic centrality is anthropocentric rather than place-centric: it can travel with the people. What generates genuine attachment to a *specific* locality is not cosmological structure but the presence of **gods and ancestors**.

## Gods and Ancestors as Anchors

Ancient cities derived their sacred status from being home to particular divinities. To conquer a city was to displace its gods — to make the people cosmically homeless. The Sumerians listed "the destruction of cities" as one of the divine institutions upon which civilization rests (p. 151). A citizen of Carthage, facing Roman destruction, pleaded:

> "Spare the city's hearth, spare our forum, spare the goddess who presides over our council, and all else that is dear and precious to the living. . . . Spare the city which has done you no harm, but, if you please, kill us, whom you have ordered to move away." (p. 151)

Fustel de Coulanges showed how Roman and Greek family life was anchored by the sacred hearth fire — which "had nothing in common with the fire of a neighboring family, which was another providence" (p. 153). The family was "as much fixed to the soil as the altar itself." Exile was the worst punishment because it stripped a man not just of land and livelihood but of religious protection and ancestral continuity.

## Cross-Cultural Range

Tuan surveys homeland attachment across radically different cultural and economic formations:

- **Greek autochthony**: Athenians prided themselves on having sprung from their own soil; Pericles: "Our ancestors deserve praise, for they dwelt in the country without break in the succession from generation to generation" (p. 154).
- **Maori**: "Mine is the land, the land of my ancestors" — a prisoner about to be killed asks to be taken first to the border of his tribal territory to look upon it once more (p. 155).
- **Native American**: "Every part of this country is sacred to my people. Every hillside, every valley, every plain and grove has been hallowed by some fond memory or some sad experience of my tribe" (an Indian chief, p. 155).
- **German *Heimat***: "Heimat is first of all the mother earth who has given birth to our folk and race. . . . Heimat is landscape. That means one that has been fought over, menaced, filled with the history of families, towns, and villages" (South Tyrolean almanac, 1953, p. 156).
- **Lakota Plains Indians**: Migratory, yet worship the earth as mother; the Black Hills are "a reclining female from whose breasts issue life-giving forces" (p. 157).
- **Australian Aboriginals**: No land ownership, no fixed territory, yet intense emotional attachment to their ancestral "estate" — the dreaming place fixed in myth and landscape.

The pattern is consistent: whether sedentary or nomadic, literate or nonliterate, people form deep bonds to the lands their ancestors inhabited and are buried in.

## Quiet vs. Rhetorical Attachment

Tuan distinguishes two modes of homeland attachment:

1. **Rhetorical**: expressed in speeches, poetry, patriotic literature; the register of Pericles and Isocrates; associated with heroic or defensive occasions.
2. **Quiet and subconscious**: "Contentment is a warm positive feeling, but it is most easily described as incuriosity toward the outside world and as absence of desire for a change of scene" (p. 159). The Tasaday of Mindanao — isolated for generations — reply when asked why they do not leave the forest: "We love to stay in our forest. We like it here. It is a quiet place to sleep. It is warm. Not loud." (p. 160). The American farm family in Daviess County, Illinois, has lived and died there for six generations without any desire to travel.

The quiet mode is harder to articulate but no less real. "Neither the rhetoric of an Isocrates nor the effusive prose of a German *Volkskalender* seems appropriate" for expressing it (p. 159).

## Homeland Attachment and Nationalism

The crucial modern development: homeland attachment at the village or city scale must be transferred to the nation-state. The emotion itself is genuine and old; the object it is directed at is constructed. See [[nationalism]] and [[visibility-and-the-creation-of-place]] for the mechanisms: history books, maps, civic shrines, ceremonial altars to *la Patrie*.

Tuan implies that nationalism is a parasitic form — it borrows the emotional charge of genuine homeland attachment and redirects it toward an abstraction that cannot be directly experienced. The bond is real; the object is manufactured.

## Synthesis Seeds

- **Homeland attachment in the age of platforms**: Can a platform community constitute a homeland? Tuan's criteria — ancestral memory, shared sacrifice, gods and graves — suggest the answer is no. Platforms lack ancestors, lack burials, and replace organic topophilia with designed engagement loops. This is the inverse of [[homeland-attachment]].
- **Statelessness and ontological homelessness**: Tuan's account implies that the worst condition is not poverty but rootlessness — losing the place that gives one's life its cosmic frame. This resonates with contemporary refugee and diaspora literature.
- **The Ilbalintja case**: the Aranda people of Australia were taught to love their own country and "not to lust after the lands belonging to other men." The specificity of homeland attachment — its embeddedness in mythic history rather than abstract ownership — suggests a very different ontology of land from that of modern property law.
