# Topological Distance

## Definition

Topological distance measures proximity between machines not by Euclidean or metric units but by the number and character of *paths and intervening nodes* that must be traversed to connect them. It is relational, not intrinsic to position.

From [[onto-cartography]]: "In a topological conception of space, matters are very different... notions of proximity and distance become different under a topological conception of space." (Bryant, p. 144)

## The Core Inversion

In Newtonian space, Austin, TX is necessarily closer to any nearby US city than to Cairo, Egypt, because a constant metric governs all distance. In topological space:
- A colleague in another country, reached by a single email, is topologically *nearer* than a neighbor one never interacts with
- A node metrically distant may be structurally adjacent (one hop), while a node metrically proximate may require many hops through resistant or absent paths

Node 9 may be topologically closer to node 4 (one hop away) than to node 30 (no direct path, requiring four-node traversal), even if nodes 9 and 30 are metrically adjacent in Euclidean space (Bryant, p. 144–145).

## Distance as Absence of Path

A key clarification from Bryant: topological distance is not merely the length of a path but the *presence or absence of a possible relation*. "A discontinuity between worlds or spaces consists in the absence of a possible relation or interaction between two machines" (p. 146).

This means:
- Radio waves passing through a person who has no radio create no topological proximity
- Physical co-presence does not entail topological nearness if no structural coupling is possible
- Two metrically adjacent communities can be topologically on different planets if the paths connecting them are absent, indirect, or blocked

## Mediating Nodes and Non-Human Paths

Radio transmission illustrates mediated topological proximity. For a song transmitted by radio to reach a person:
1. It must pass through the radio tower node
2. It must be received by a tuned radio receiver node
3. The receiver must be structurally open to it (not deaf, volume adequate)

Each intermediate node is a condition of topological proximity. Remove any node and topological distance becomes infinite — the song cannot reach the person at all regardless of how close the transmitter is physically.

## Political Topology of Distance

Expressive and semiotic machines create topological distance between metrically proximate machines:
- **Passports and national borders**: immigrants metrically inside a country may be topologically excluded from its labor and service networks
- **Class topologies**: people in poverty live metrically close to wealthy institutions but face long topological paths to access them — both attractive feedback (family obligations pulling back) and repulsive feedback (cultural codes of wealthy institutions closing doors)
- **Racial and gender segregation** (including apartheid, Atlantic City casino design): structural mechanisms create topological remoteness within shared metric space

The Atlantic City case (Bryant, pp. 148–149): casinos were built assuming metric proximity to low-income neighborhoods would generate economic connection. Topologically, casinos were self-enclosed: visitors arrived directly, spent money internally, and left via direct return paths. The surrounding city was as topologically remote as if it were on another continent.

## Memory and Temporal Topological Distance

Serres's crumpled handkerchief metaphor extends topological distance into time: points metrically distant in chronological sequence can become topologically proximate through memory. A revolutionary in 1789 may be in closer topological contact with Roman Republicans (via texts, shared framework) than with her contemporaries. A tenured philosopher thinking in 1950s existentialist terms is temporally distant from colleagues despite physical co-presence. See [[temporal-topology-of-machines]].
