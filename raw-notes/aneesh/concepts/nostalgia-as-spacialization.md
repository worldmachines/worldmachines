# Nostalgia as Spatialisation

**Source**: [[doreen-massey]], *For Space*, Ch. 11 (2005: 121–125)

## The Argument

Massey uses the figure of the northerner who has moved south — a figure she embodies — to develop an argument about nostalgia as a spatial-temporal confusion with political consequences.

The dominant imagination of "going home" treats it as travel through *both* space and time: going north means going back in time. Home is held in the amber of the moment one left it. The "Angry Young Men" of 1950s/60s Britain are Massey's iconic case: writers who moved south to make their careers and then held their northern places "in aspic" — frozen at the point of their departure. "The spatial surface, from London to the north, sloped backwards in time" (Massey 2005: 123).

## The Chocolate Cake Vignette

Massey's own moment of recognition: returning to south Lancashire, sitting in the front room having tea with her parents. The treat was the chocolate cake — "heavy and with some kind of mixture of butter, syrup and cocoa powder in the middle. A wartime recipe I think, invented out of necessity, and a triumph." But on this visit, the mother came back with a completely different cake — "All light-textured and fluffy, and a paler brown." A new recipe she'd found.

"But with one voice my sister and I sent up a wail of complaint — 'Oh Mum ... but we like the old chocolate cake.'"

Massey reflects: "part of the point of going home was to do things as we'd always done them. Going home, in the way I was carrying it at that moment, did not mean joining up with ongoing Mancunian lives. Certainly it was time travel as well as space travel, but I lived it in that moment as a journey to the past. But places change; they go on without you. Mother invents new recipes. A nostalgia which denies that, is certainly in need of re-working." (Massey 2005: 124)

## The Political Consequence

The error of nostalgia-as-temporal-travel is not merely personal:

> "when nostalgia articulates space and time in such a way that it robs others of their histories (their stories), then indeed we need to rework nostalgia." (Massey 2005: 125)

This connects to Massey's broader argument about the political cosmology that treats space as static:

- Western travellers imagining they are going to "find the past" when they visit non-Western societies
- Globalisaton discourse that imagines the global "arrives" from somewhere else (not produced locally)
- Migration nostalgia that freezes the home-place at the moment of departure

In all cases, the spatial imagination immobilises others — holds them still for the purposes of the returning/imagining subject — while the subject does the moving. This is a version of the power-geometry of movement: the northern woman in her pinny at the back drain, frozen forever in the train passenger's sight — she is given no ongoing history by the imagination that holds her there.

## Raymond Williams and the Train

Massey invokes a famous Raymond Williams passage (probably from his fiction or essays — "I think from Raymond Williams"): Williams is on a train and catches sight of a woman in her pinny bending over a drain with a stick. From the train, "She will forever be doing this." She is held in that instant, almost immobilised.

But perhaps she is clearing the drain before leaving to visit a sister half the world away. The train's passenger is incapable of granting her an ongoing life. "Thinking space as the sphere of a multiplicity of trajectories, imagining a train journey (for example) as a speeding across on-going stories, means bringing the woman in the pinny to life, acknowledging her as another on-going life" (Massey 2005: 118–119).

## Wendy Wheeler's Objection

Massey engages sympathetically with Wendy Wheeler (1994), who argues that nostalgic longing for place and home is a legitimate affective response to the losses of modernity — a "return of the repressed" of Enlightenment rationalism. Wheeler's title: "Nostalgia isn't nasty."

Massey agrees that the affective needs are real and that a progressive nostalgia is possible. But she argues that nostalgia is in need of reworking when it "robs others of their ongoing independent stories" (Massey 2005: 125). The question is not whether nostalgia is nasty *in itself*, but whether its spatial-temporal imagination is adequate to the ongoing life of places and people.

## The Corrective

"You can never simply 'go back', to home or to anywhere else. When you get 'there' the place will have moved on just as you yourself will have changed." (Massey 2005: 124)

What you can do is "meet up with others, catch up with where another's history has got to 'now'" (Massey 2005: 125). This is a very different orientation: not going back to a preserved past, but joining up with an ongoing process.
