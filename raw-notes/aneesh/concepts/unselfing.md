# Unselfing

## Source

The term is used by [[Maria Popova]] in her essay on [[Bertrand Russell]]'s *The Conquest of Happiness*, quoted in [[Aneesh Sathe]]'s [[Better Company Than Caesar]]:

> "In my darkest hours, what has saved me again and again is some action of unselfing — some instinctive wakefulness to an aspect of the world other than myself: a helping hand extended to someone else's struggle, the dazzling galaxy just discovered millions of lightyears away, the cardinal trembling in the tree outside my window."

## The Mechanism

Unselfing is not self-denial or self-abnegation. It is a *turning of attention outward* that temporarily dissolves the boundary between self and world. The ego, which is the locus of suffering (anxiety, rumination, status-seeking), is bypassed when attention is fully absorbed by something external.

The examples in Popova's passage span three scales:
- **Interpersonal**: extending a hand to someone else's struggle
- **Cosmic**: a newly discovered galaxy millions of light-years away
- **Intimate**: a cardinal trembling in a tree outside the window

The scale doesn't matter; what matters is the quality of absorbed attention — what Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi called *flow* (unacknowledged here).

## Russell's Formula

Russell's prescription in *The Conquest of Happiness*:
> "The secret of happiness is this: let your interests be as wide as possible, and let your reactions to the things and persons that interest you be as far as possible friendly rather than hostile."

Width of interest = multiple available objects for unselfing. Friendliness = openness to absorption rather than defensiveness. The formula is structural: build a life with many entry points for ego dissolution.
