# Maximal Interestingness

From [[On Protocols, Wagons, and Associated Acrobatics]]:

> "Pointing in the direction of maximal interestingness."

This is offered as a navigational heuristic for when destination is uncertain — which, in creative and intellectual life, is almost always.

## The Heuristic

Rather than optimizing for a specific goal (become a great writer, build a successful company), the maximal interestingness heuristic says: at each decision point, take the path that seems most interesting. Trust that interest is a reliable signal of where useful attention should go.

This is related to, but distinct from, [[Ludic Feedback Loops]]: ludic frequency is about the *cadence* of reward; maximal interestingness is about the *direction* of exploration.

## Theoretical Foundation

[[Murray S Davis]] provides the formal theory behind this heuristic. In "That's Interesting!" (1971), he argues that interestingness is not subjective preference but a structural relationship: a proposition is interesting when it denies a specific strand of the audience's [[Assumption-Ground]] (taken-for-granted beliefs). The [[Index of the Interesting]] catalogs 12 logical categories of such denial. Maximal interestingness, then, is navigating toward the propositions that most productively disrupt your own assumption-ground — and the capacity to do this depends on maintaining an idiosyncratic, richly developed assumption-ground rather than a homogenized one.

## Intellectual Lineage

- [[Murray S Davis]] — the formal theory of interestingness as assumption-denial
- Implicit connection to [[Tyler Cowen]]'s advice on intellectual curiosity and following your interests aggressively
- Related to [[Richard Feynman]]'s reported practice of pursuing problems that delighted him, without worrying about their importance
- Echoes [[serendipity engineering]] — structuring your environment to maximize unexpected productive encounters

## The Wagon Problem

The heuristic also addresses the wagon problem (see [[Habit and Identity]], [[Infinite Game]]): if your direction is "maximal interestingness," then falling off the specific wagon doesn't mean losing your direction — you're still pointing toward interesting things, just temporarily not writing about them.

## Criticism

- **Dilettantism risk**: maximal interestingness may produce broad exposure without depth. At some point, mastery requires persisting through the uninteresting.
- **Interest vs. importance**: what's interesting to you may not be important. The heuristic is best suited to intellectual/creative life where the two often align.
- **Local maxima**: you can be locally interested and miss globally more interesting territories you haven't yet encountered.
