# Just-in-Time Mental Models

## Definition

A **just-in-time (JIT) mental model** is a causal understanding of a system assembled in context from fragmentary knowledge, rather than retrieved from a pre-built comprehensive representation. Klein et al. (2007) define mental models as "our causal understanding of the way things work" — models of brakes, computers, project teams. The JIT concept distinguishes *how* such models are actually constructed from how they are theoretically supposed to work.

**Comprehensive mental model**: Captures the essential relationships of a system. An automobile mechanic has one for braking systems; an IT specialist for operating systems. Rare and domain-specific.

**JIT mental model**: A construction, using fragmentary knowledge from long-term memory to build explanations in context. "Just as our memories are partial constructions, using fragments of recall together with beliefs, rules of inference, we are claiming that most of our mental models are constructed as the situation warrants."

## The Empirical Claim

In most of the incidents Klein et al. examined, decision makers did not have a full mental model of the situation or phenomenon they needed to understand. The cholera outbreak example is illustrative: none of the information operations specialists (including experts) automatically connected sewage system failure + refugee movement + cholera outbreak into a causal chain — because they lacked even partial models of cholera transmission. A few experts managed to construct the connection when given pointed clues, using fragmentary disease knowledge to build a causal chain on the fly.

Klein et al.: "We suggest that people primarily rely on JIT mental models — building on the local cause-effect connections they know about, instead of having comprehensive mental models of the workings of an entire system."

## When Comprehensive Models Are Possible

Klein et al. acknowledge that in some domains, genuine comprehensive mental models are plausible:
- **Closed systems** (medicine, engineering): The human body can be treated as a roughly closed system. Highly experienced pediatric cardiologists develop sophisticated, comprehensive models of specific cardiac functions. But even these specialists fail at unusual presentations.
- **Open systems** (military, social, organizational): A comprehensive mental model is essentially unrealistic. A soldier developing frames for peacekeeping operations in Iraq cannot have a comprehensive mental model; they rely on accumulated fragments.

## Training Implication

If people primarily use JIT models, training strategies should change:
- **Expand causal relationship repertoire** rather than teaching fully worked-out domain models
- Fragmentary knowledge generalizes across domains more easily than comprehensive models
- "We may be better advised to train people by helping them expand their repertoire and depth of causal relationships, rather than trying to teach fully worked out mental models within a domain."

This is counterintuitive: it suggests that broad, fragmentary causal knowledge is more useful than deep, comprehensive models in most naturalistic settings.

## JIT Models and Frames

JIT mental models are one of the structural forms that frames can take. A frame does not have to be a complete causal model — it is sufficient if it:
1. Identifies the relevant causal factors
2. Provides expectations about what should be present
3. Suggests what additional data to seek

The JIT model supplies the causal backbone; the frame supplies the relational structure that makes data visible.

## The Memory Analogy

The JIT account of mental models parallels constructivist accounts of memory. We don't retrieve stored memories; we reconstruct them from fragments using inference and belief. JIT mental models are similarly reconstructive, not retrievative. This means they are:
- Flexible and adaptable across contexts
- Subject to distortion by current frames
- More accurate about familiar situations than unfamiliar ones
- Improvable by adding causal fragments, not by building complete models

## Connection to Expertise

Expert-novice differences in JIT model quality:
- Experts have a larger repertoire of causal relationships
- Their causal knowledge is more accurate (better calibrated to reality)
- They can chain inferences further ("A" → "B" → "C" → "D") before reaching the edge of their knowledge
- Novices reach the edge of their knowledge sooner and cannot extend the chain

The result is that the same JIT construction process produces better models from experts — not because experts think differently, but because they have more to build from.
