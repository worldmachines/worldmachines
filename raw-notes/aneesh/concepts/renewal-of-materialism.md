# Renewal of Materialism (Bryant)

## The Problem

Bryant's polemic opens *Onto-Cartography*: the "materialist turn" in critical theory produced a paradox. The term "materialism" became so empty that Žižek could write that "[m]aterialism means that the reality I see is never 'whole' — not because a large part of it eludes me, but because it contains a stain, a blind spot, which indicates my inclusion in it" (Žižek 2006: 17, cited at Bryant 2014, p. 2). Materialism had come to mean simply that something is historical, socially constructed, involves cultural practices, and is contingent. "It has nothing to do with processes that take place in the heart of stars, suffering from cancer, or transforming fossil fuels into greenhouse gases." (Bryant 2014, p. 2)

The analytic consequence: physical agencies were rendered *invisible*. The discursivist tradition taught how fashion exercises power through symbolic capital, how ideology distorts consciousness, how language structures social relations. What it could not explain was how turnstiles in subways, mountain ranges, and ocean currents also organize social relations — sometimes more durably and implacably than any text.

The political consequence: "The sole strategy for producing change became first revealing how we had discursively constructed some phenomenon, then revealing how it was contingent, and then showing why it was untenable." Removing actual turnstiles as a mode of emancipation "wasn't even on the radar." (Bryant 2014, p. 4)

## Genealogy of the Collapse

Bryant traces the slide to a *misreading* of Marx. Marx was right that commodity fetishism conceals the social relations behind products (Marx 1990: 165). The social relations are real. But his successors drew the wrong conclusion: things are *only* crystallizations of the social, with no powers of their own. "To speak of the powers of *things* themselves, to speak of them as producing effects beyond their status as vehicles for social relations, became the height of naïveté." (Bryant 2014, p. 3)

The result was a "curious anti-dialectical gesture that somehow failed to simultaneously recognize the way in which non-human, non-signifying agencies, structure social relations as much as the discursive." (Bryant 2014, p. 4)

Climate change made the stakes undeniable. A discursivist theory had only culture as a category. There was "no longer a place for thinking the real physical efficacy of fossil fuels, pollutants, automobiles, sunlight interacting with the albedo of the earth, and so on." (Bryant 2014, p. 4)

## What Gets Recovered

A renewal of materialism, for Bryant, attends to:
- **Fossil fuels** and their role in shaping industrial organization and climate
- **Bees** and the agricultural systems that depend on their pollination
- **Sunlight on the earth's albedo** — the non-cultural physics of climate
- **Automobiles** and the road infrastructure that redesigns settlement patterns
- **Turnstiles** as literal physical barriers structuring movement and access
- **Clocks** and timepieces that restructured all of social life independently of intent
- **Writing** as a medium that gave law permanence and territorial reach

These are not metaphors for social relations; they are machines with their own operations, imperatives, and gravitational pull on other machines.

## What Does Not Get Discarded

Bryant is explicit that the renewal of materialism does not refute discursive theory — it contextualizes it. Drawing on Whitehead: "the chief error in philosophy is overstatement." Discursive theories are not false; they are overstated. The linguistic turn correctly identified that signifiers, ideologies, and cultural practices shape reality. The error was treating this as the complete account.

The goal is a framework that can accommodate both: "robust enough to integrate the discoveries of the critical theories, while also making room for a non-reductive account of the role played by physical media in the structuration of social relations." (Bryant 2014, p. 8)

## Relation to New Materialism

Bryant's project overlaps significantly with what is sometimes called "new materialism" (associated with Jane Bennett, Karen Barad, Donna Haraway, Rosi Braidotti). Bryant cites Bennett (*Vibrant Matter*, 2010) approvingly: "things have their power as well." What distinguishes Bryant's approach is its explicit grounding in a systematic ontology of machines and in the OOO lineage — giving it a more explicitly ontological armature than some other new materialisms.

## Relation to This Wiki

The renewal-of-materialism argument resonates with the wiki's existing line of thinking about non-discursive forces — particularly [[solar-as-flow-vs-fossil-as-stock]], [[infrastructure-inheritance]], [[constructal-law]], and the Rao-influenced world-machines framework. Rao's world machines are precisely the kind of thing Bryant wants to theorize: large-scale contraptions with their own imperatives that shape human possibility irrespective of cultural intent.

The critique of discursive materialism is also a useful foil for the wiki's engagement with AI and knowledge: LLMs are, in Bryant's vocabulary, incorporeal machines that nonetheless require massive corporeal machine-ecologies (server farms, energy grids, mineral extraction) to exist. A purely discursivist account of AI would miss these physical dependencies.
