# Superlinear Scaling

**Superlinear scaling** refers to the empirical finding, most associated with Geoffrey West and the Santa Fe Institute, that certain outputs of cities scale faster than linearly with population. When a city's population doubles, its economic output, patent production, wages, and number of creative professionals increase by approximately **115%** (exponent ~1.15), while its infrastructure needs — road surface, electrical cable length, number of gas stations — increase by only approximately **85%** (exponent ~0.85).

## The Two Exponents

West's research reveals two distinct scaling regimes operating simultaneously in cities:

- **Infrastructure** scales **sublinearly** with an exponent of approximately **0.85**. Doubling the population requires only 85% more road, pipe, and cable. This is an economy of scale — larger cities are more materially efficient per capita.

- **Social outputs** scale **superlinearly** with an exponent of approximately **1.15**. Doubling the population yields 115% more patents, GDP, creative output, and — notably — also 115% more crime, disease, and pollution. The superlinear exponent applies to all social interaction products, not just desirable ones.

The gap between these two exponents creates a systematic **efficiency dividend**: larger aggregations of people produce disproportionately more output per unit of infrastructure. This is not a planning outcome — it is an emergent property of social network density.

## Cities as Social Reactors

West's explanation is that cities function as **social reactors**. The superlinear scaling of innovation arises from the density and frequency of social interactions. More people in proximity means more encounters, more idea exchange, more combinatorial possibilities. The social metabolic rate — the pace at which ideas circulate and recombine — accelerates with city size.

This makes the city a thermodynamic engine: it processes energy and information at increasing rates as it grows, producing more complex outputs per unit of input. The infrastructure savings (sublinear scaling) subsidize the innovation surplus (superlinear scaling).

## Implications

The superlinear scaling result has a striking implication for the relationship between scale and agency: **aggregation into larger energy-processing systems makes individual productive action cheaper while accelerating the pace of innovation**. The per-capita cost of maintaining infrastructure falls, while the per-capita rate of creative and economic output rises. This is a systematic structural subsidy for individual agency at larger scales.

However, the same scaling law that produces innovation also produces accelerating pace. West shows that cities must innovate at an ever-faster rate to sustain growth — each successive innovation cycle must arrive sooner than the last. This creates a **finite-time singularity** dynamic: without continual acceleration of innovation, the system collapses.
