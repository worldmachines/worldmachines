# Normative Self-Blindness

## Definition

Coined by [[Agnes Callard]] in *Open Socrates*.

> "Normative self-blindness is a trick to gloss over details and keep moving."

The built-in refusal to saw off the branch we stand on — the cognitive mechanism that treats current beliefs as unexaminable foundations, because examining them would undermine the very stance from which examination proceeds.

## Mechanism

We can accept that a belief-place was:
- **Loosely formed in the past** — an error we have since corrected.
- **Not yet defined in the future** — an unknown we will eventually fill in.

What we cannot accept *in the present moment*:
- That a currently operational belief-place might be false *right now*.

This is not irrationality but functional necessity: acting requires standing somewhere. Insta-places must be treated as solid even when they are semi-solid smoke.

## Relation to [[Insta-Places]]

Insta-places are the cognitive content; normative self-blindness is the *protection* mechanism that prevents them from being revised mid-operation. Together they explain why pre-answered questions remain pre-answered.

## Why It Matters for Socratic Inquiry

Socratic thinking begins by admitting that everyone's guiding landmarks may be made of [[Insta-Places|semi-solid smoke]]. This is psychologically threatening precisely because normative self-blindness is not a defect to be corrected but a feature of cognition.

The practical implication: Socratic dialogue must be structured to make belief-revision feel like *advancement* (finding a better place) rather than *loss* (having a place taken away).

## Connection to the Turing Test

[[Alan Turing]]'s move — converting "What is thinking?" into "Can we detect thought?" — can be read as normative self-blindness at the level of scientific inquiry: the original question is pre-answered (we already assume we know what "detecting" thinking would mean) and converted into a tractable problem.
