# Luminosity of Machines (Bryant)

## The Metaphor

In Chapter 5 of *Onto-Cartography* (2014), Bryant borrows vocabulary from astrophysics to describe the differential influence machines exert within a world. Just as astronomical objects vary in how much light they emit — from blazing stars to invisible dark matter — machines within a world vary in how much causal-gravitational influence they radiate to other machines.

Luminosity, in this sense, is a measure of *relational salience*: how much a machine's outputs, flows, and structural couplings shape the operations and local manifestations of other machines in its world. It is not an intrinsic property of the machine in isolation but a function of its actual couplings within a particular ecology.

## Four Degrees of Luminosity

| Category | Description |
|---|---|
| **Bright** | Highly active; disproportionate organizing influence; other machines orient around it. |
| **Dim** | Active but local; significant to nearby machines but not field-organizing. |
| **Dark** | Present but causally inert relative to the world's flows; no current coupling transmits its influence. |
| **Rogue** | Enters from outside or shifts behavior suddenly; disrupts existing couplings; introduces entropy. |

See [[bright-dim-dark-rogue-objects]] for full definitions and examples.

## Why a Luminosity Metaphor?

The metaphor is apt for several reasons:

**1. Distance-dependence.** In astrophysics, luminosity appears to decrease with distance (inverse square law). Similarly, a machine's influence in Bryant's framework is mediated — it passes through chains of other machines, each adding friction or transformation. A machine that is bright in its local neighborhood may appear dim at a world-scale remove.

**2. Variable detection.** Not all wavelengths are visible to all instruments. A machine may be dark relative to one world (no flow links it) while being bright relative to another. Luminosity is observer-relative not in the subjective sense but in the sense that it depends on what kinds of couplings (instruments, media) a given world contains.

**3. Relational, not intrinsic.** Dark matter in astrophysics is dark because it does not interact via electromagnetism — not because it lacks mass or causal efficacy altogether. Bryant's dark objects are similarly not ontologically deficient; they are operationally unavailable to the world in question. This is a relational fact, reversible if new mediators emerge.

**4. Temporal variability.** Stars change luminosity over their life cycles. Machines similarly shift categories: a technology newly adopted becomes bright; an institution that loses its flows dims; a catastrophe transforms a previously peripheral machine into a rogue.

## Connection to Gravity as Power

In Ch. 7, Bryant develops the gravitational analogy into a full theory of *power*. Bright objects exert "gravity" — they constrain and shape the movements, developments, and becomings of less luminous machines. This is Bryant's materialist account of power: power is not fundamentally discursive or ideological but gravitational, arising from the structural position of certain machines within a world's ecology.

The luminosity framework thus provides the phenomenology of that gravity: from a given machine's perspective, the world is structured by luminosity gradients — brighter machines exert more pull, darker machines are invisible, rogues arrive as perturbations to the existing gravitational field.

## Onto-Cartographic Practice

The practical task of onto-cartography is, in part, the mapping of luminosity distributions within a world. Which machines are bright and why? Which machines are currently dark but contain latent potential for brightness (e.g., unexploited organizing resources)? Which rogues are available for strategic deployment?

This gives the typology a political edge: understanding the luminosity structure of a social world is a prerequisite for effective intervention. Purely expressive critique — ideology-analysis, discourse deconstruction — misses the material bright objects that actually organize the field. Bryant's recommendation: map the full luminosity distribution, identify the material bright objects, and design interventions at that level.
