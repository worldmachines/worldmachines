# Bright, Dim, Dark, and Rogue Objects (Bryant)

## Overview

In *Onto-Cartography* (2014), Levi Bryant introduces a typology of machine salience to describe the internal topology of a world. Not all machines in a world exercise equal influence. Bryant uses a luminosity metaphor — borrowed from astrophysics — to chart how much "light" (causal-gravitational influence) a given machine radiates within its ecology.

This typology is the internal structural complement to [[bryant-pluralism-of-worlds]]: if worlds are plural and flat (no ontological hierarchy), they are nonetheless unequal in their internal distributions of influence. "Flat" does not mean "homogeneous." See [[luminosity-of-machines]] for the metaphor itself.

## The Four Categories

### Bright Objects
Machines that are highly active and exercise disproportionate organizational influence over the field. Bright objects structure the relations of other machines around them — they determine which flows circulate, what kinds of couplings are possible, and how other machines develop. A city, a legal system, a large corporation, a dominant technology platform, the sun relative to the solar system — these are bright objects in their respective worlds.

Bright objects function as gravity-wells in the topology of a world: other machines orient around them, are drawn into interaction with them, and have their local manifestations shaped by proximity to them. Bryant develops this gravitational metaphor more fully in Ch. 7 under the heading of *gravity as power*.

### Dim Objects
Machines that are active participants in a world but whose influence extends only to a narrower or more peripheral zone. They interact, they produce outputs, they couple with other machines — but they do not organize the field the way bright objects do. Most machines in any world are dim: they matter to their immediate neighbors but do not radiate far.

### Dark Objects
Machines that are present in a world (in the sense of occupying it spatio-temporally) but are currently *not* affecting any other machine in that world — no flow reaches them, or they are selectively closed in ways that make them operationally invisible to other machines. Bryant links this to the possibility that some machines may be entirely unrelated to any other machine in a given world — a theoretical limit that grounds his [[bryant-pluralism-of-worlds|pluralism]].

The neutrino is a recurring example: it is a material entity, it passes through the world, yet it cannot be affected by most of the matter that makes up the furniture of our daily life. For practical purposes it is dark with respect to ordinary physical worlds.

Dark objects are not concealed in the phenomenological sense (they are not "withdrawn" as Harman's objects are). They are dark because no interaction currently links them to the world's flows. This is a relational fact, not an intrinsic one: a machine can become bright — a new technology can make a previously inert material causally potent (uranium before the discovery of nuclear fission).

### Rogue Objects
Machines that enter a world from outside — or that are already in a world but change their behavior so radically — that they disrupt the existing ecology. Rogues are not simply dim objects becoming brighter; they are *disruptors*, introducing entropy into established couplings and forcing reorganization of the field.

Examples Bryant gestures toward: invasive species entering an ecosystem; new technologies that introduce entropy into older worlds (writing undermining oral cultures; the printing press fragmenting the medieval church's control of text); natural catastrophes like hurricanes that reconfigure social machines. The Arab Spring and Occupy movements are analyzed in Ch. 5 in terms of how new communication technologies (social media, smartphones) acted as rogue objects in relation to older party-politics assemblages.

## The Luminosity Metaphor

Bryant's metaphor treats objects as if they emitted light — as sources of gravitational and causal influence radiating outward through the ecology of a world. The metaphor is apt in several ways:

1. **Luminosity is distance-relative**: a dim object may be bright in its local neighborhood while appearing dim from a world-level perspective.
2. **Luminosity changes over time**: objects can move between categories as flows are established or broken — a newly discovered oil field shifts from dark to bright; a collapsing institution dims.
3. **Luminosity is not intrinsic**: it is a function of the machine's *actual couplings* within a world, not a property of the machine considered in isolation.

See [[luminosity-of-machines]] for the full treatment of the metaphor.

## Political Implications

The typology is not merely descriptive — it is a tool for onto-cartographic *practice*. Bryant's project in Ch. 8 ("Terraformation") is to use the map of bright/dim/dark/rogue distributions to identify leverage points: which bright objects must be dismantled or reconfigured to restructure oppressive social assemblages? Which dark objects — existing but unactivated resources — could be awakened into brightness? Which rogue objects are available as tools of disruption?

This connects to his critique of purely discursive political theory: focusing only on the plane of expression (ideology, discourse) while ignoring the bright material machines that organize a world is a systematic blind spot in continental social theory.

## Relation to Ch. 7's "Gravity"

Bryant previews in Ch. 5 a more developed account in Ch. 7, where he explicitly theorizes the *gravity* (power) that machines exercise on one another. Bright objects are gravitationally massive; dark objects are gravitationally inert within a given world. The Ch. 7 framework extends this into a full political theory.
