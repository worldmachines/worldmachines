# Abductive Reasoning

## Definition

Abductive reasoning is **inference to the best explanation**. The structure:
1. You observe something surprising or anomalous
2. You ask: what hypothesis, if true, would best explain this observation?
3. You adopt that hypothesis tentatively

Contrasted with:
- **Deduction**: all As are Bs; X is an A; therefore X is a B (truth-preserving, no new knowledge)
- **Induction**: observed N cases of pattern P; generalize P to all cases (ampliative but risky)
- **Abduction**: observe Q (surprising); H would explain Q; therefore H is tentatively true

## Origins

Associated with Charles Sanders Peirce, who introduced the term in the late 19th century. Peirce saw abduction as the logic of discovery — the only form of reasoning that can generate genuinely new hypotheses.

## Role in Sensemaking

In the [[Data-Frame Theory of Sensemaking]], abductive reasoning is the dominant mode during frame construction. Klein et al:

> "When constructing a frame, the type of thinking used is most accurately described as abductive reasoning... you observe something surprising, then settle on what you think is the most likely cause."

The process looks like: propose a candidate frame → test it against available anchors → discard if it doesn't fit → propose another. Captain B. in the helicopter accident example cycled through multiple abductive inferences as new data points arrived.

Experts also use **deduction** during sensemaking — inferring what *should* be present if a frame is correct, then looking for it. The brigadier general's inference: "tanks tend not to operate alone; therefore there are more tanks; let me find them" is deductive within his current frame, but the initial recognition that he's dealing with a larger maneuver was abductive.

## Abduction and Expertise

Expert advantage in abductive reasoning is indirect: experts don't reason more *efficiently* per step. They:
- Have more candidate hypotheses available (larger frame repertoire)
- Can generate better-fitting initial guesses (richer mental models)
- Know what confirming/disconfirming evidence to look for faster (more specific expectancies)

This means expert abductive reasoning produces better frames not because the reasoning process is different but because the starting material (fragments, models) is richer.

## Relationship to Confirmation

A common misreading: abduction followed by confirmatory evidence-seeking looks like [[Confirmation Bias]]. The Data-Frame theory reframes this: seeking confirming evidence for a new abductive hypothesis is the *correct* epistemic strategy when you lack the cognitive resources to search for disconfirming evidence simultaneously. The alternative (holding all possibilities open simultaneously) is practically impossible under real-world conditions.

## Philosophical Context

- **Peirce** saw abduction as logically distinct from Bayesian updating — you can't assign prior probabilities to hypotheses you haven't yet conceived; abduction generates the hypothesis space
- **Sherlock Holmes** is the cultural archetype of abductive reasoning (though Holmes erroneously calls it "deduction")
- **Inference to the best explanation** is the contemporary philosophical label; W.H. Harman coined the phrase in 1965

## Klein et al.'s Empirical Support

Klein et al. (2007) studied information operations specialists and found that both experts and novices:
- Were more likely to speculate about causes given effects than deduce effects from causes
- Would infer causal relationships from simple correlations ("if one event preceded another, they speculated the first caused the second")
- Were "explanation machines" — unable to stop finding connections between messages

The formal structure they cite from Josephson & Josephson (1994):
> D is a collection of data. H explains D. No other hypothesis explains D as well as H. Therefore, H is probably true.

This is technically the fallacy of affirming the consequent in deductive logic — but is the appropriate strategy for hypothesis generation and evaluation under uncertainty. The NICU nurse who reasons "I'm seeing these symptoms, which in my experience connect with sepsis; therefore perhaps this baby has sepsis" is not committing a logical error; she is doing naturalistic sensemaking.

**Overexplaining risk**: The abductive disposition is always-on. Doherty (1993) showed this leads to pseudocorrelation — finding connections in data that aren't there. The benefit of abductive reasoning is in generating candidate frames; the cost is noise.

**Plausibility vs. accuracy**: Weick (1995) and Klein et al. argue that effective sensemaking prioritizes plausibility, coherence, and reasonableness over strict accuracy. A sufficiently good frame enables action; waiting for certainty is itself a pathology.
