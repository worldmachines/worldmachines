# Making and Contesting Time-Spaces

The closing constructive chapter (Ch. 15) of [[doreen-massey]]'s *For Space* (2005). Two arguments combined:

1. **Every time-space is selectively open and closed.** There is no purely-open or purely-closed place; what matters is **what** is allowed in, **what** is kept out, and **why**.
2. **A relational politics of place extends to a politics of connectivity** — local struggles are linked through *constructed equivalences*, not through the accumulation of bounded particularities.

## The lab and the home

Massey's empirical case study (developed from research in the Cambridge area) compares two time-spaces:

### High-tech R&D labs

Initial impression: **open**. Globally connected (conference calls with other continents, frequent international travel, integration into global research networks). Modern stylish buildings, science parks, the iconography of the "new economy."

Closer look: **selectively closed**. Single-minded spaces devoted to one activity (thinking, research). No creches; in one lab, children kept out by security guards. No supermarket bags, no non-work reading matter. "Defensive, quite tightly sealed against 'non-conforming' invasions from other worlds." Closure produced both materially (security guards) and imaginatively (the symbolism of exclusivity, the elite spatial cachet).

### The scientists' homes

Initial impression: **closed**. The English country cottage, the village, the bounded domestic enclosure. Apparent "return from globalised days to bounded local security."

Closer look: **selectively open**. Littered with the variety of family life; "his" work pours in (journals on the settee, notebooks by the bed, work-thinking during play with the kids). The home is porous to commodity flows, to communications, to ideas, to consumer culture. Inside the home there may be a "study" — a place-within-a-place that mimics the lab's closure.

### The political question

> "The real socio-political question concerns less, perhaps, the degree of openness/closure . . . than the terms on which that openness/closure is established. Against what are boundaries erected? What are the relations within which the attempt to deny (and admit) entry is carried out? What are the power-geometries here; and do they demand a political response?" (Massey 2005: 179)

Every time-space negotiates terms of openness and closure. The politics is in the **terms**, not in the abstract openness or closure.

## The Aldo van Eyck provocation

> "A house must be like a small city if it's to be a real home; a city like a large house if it's to be a real home." (Aldo van Eyck, cited 179)

Massey uses this to focus the question: a home like a city would be a place of chance encounter; a city like a home would be hospitable. Each, in its way, would be more open to the multiple becomings of space.

## Politics of connectivity

The second half of the chapter extends the relational thesis to **scale**. A "global sense of place" implies that local struggles are not internally-given — they are themselves relational achievements, drawing from beyond the local. The political question of how to link local struggles is therefore not the question of "adding up particularities" (an additive politics that risks dilution or contradiction). It is the question of **building constructed equivalences**:

> "Equivalence 'does not simply establish an alliance between given interests, but modifies the very identity of the forces engaging in that alliance.'" (Laclau and Mouffe 2001: 184, quoted 182)

The Confederation paysanne's network (Via Campesina) is Massey's example: farmer movements in Mali, Brazil, France, and the US connect through "constructed equivalences" rather than through a single unified identity. The negotiation of the connection is itself transformative.

## What this does to "local versus global"

The chapter undermines the local/global counterposition that runs through much political theory and through everyday political rhetoric. Three moves:

1. **The local is already global.** Local struggles are relationally produced; their identity comes partly from outside.
2. **The global is always local.** Global processes have to be enacted, somewhere, by someone.
3. **The political question is the form of relation**, not its scale. What kinds of connections, on what terms, with what power-geometries?
