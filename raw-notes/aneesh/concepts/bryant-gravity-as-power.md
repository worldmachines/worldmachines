# Bryant's Gravity as Power

## The Concept

In *Onto-Cartography* (2014), Levi Bryant introduces "gravity" as his systematic replacement for the concepts of "force" and "power" in social and political philosophy. The substitution is not merely terminological — it carries a full ontological reorientation.

Bryant's move follows Einstein's correction of Newton. For Newton, gravity was a force transmitted across distance between massive bodies — which posed the materialist problem of action at a distance. Einstein showed gravity is not a force at all, but a **curvature of space-time** produced by mass: bodies don't attract one another, they travel the paths that mass curves in space-time. The Moon orbits the Earth not because it is attracted, but because the Earth's mass curves space-time into a path the Moon follows.

Bryant applies this structure to social ontology:

> "What I wish to capture with the onto-cartographical concept of gravity is the way in which one machine influences the movement and becoming of other machines, as well as the interactions possible between machines. Thus, with Einstein, the onto-cartographic concept of gravity conceives gravity as the manner in which one machine bends the space-time movement and becoming of another entity." (p. 188)

Machines generate **gravitational fields** — structured fields of possible action and becoming through which other machines move. This is why worlds are organized as they are: not because of a special substance called "power" or "social force" that sits behind phenomena and explains them, but because machines structure the space-time paths along which other machines move and become.

## Why Not "Power"?

Bryant gives two reasons for rejecting "power" (pp. 187–88):

**1. Tautology.** Following Hegel's *Science of Logic*, Bryant notes that social theorists explain phenomena by appealing to "social forces" or "power" — but these explanations are tautological. Explaining unemployment concentration in a region by "social forces" merely repeats what needs explaining. Power, like Newtonian force, is an "occult" explanation — it gestures at a mechanism without providing one.

**2. Anthropocentrism.** "Power" draws analytical attention to narratives, signs, discourses, and human institutions. It belongs to the plane of expression. But social assemblages are also organized by the plane of content: rivers, ocean currents, disease epidemiology, altitudes, mineral distributions, weather. The concept of power cannot account for how mountains channel migration or how fossil fuel reserves organize geopolitics, because it does not extend to non-human entities.

Gravity is preferable because it is **all-purpose**:

> "The rhetorical advantage of the concept of gravity over terms like power and force is that it gives an all-purpose term capable of straddling both humans and non-humans, the social and the natural, so that we avoid fixating on the cultural. Gravity is as operative in the world of a deep ocean volcanic vent as it is in the social dynamics of an institution like a government agency." (p. 188)

## What Gravity Includes

Bryant's gravity is *broader* than Foucault's or Bourdieu's power — it encompasses discursive power (Foucault's genealogy of norms, disciplinary institutions, the dispositif) and practice-based power (Bourdieu's habitus and field theory) *plus* the power that things exercise by virtue of what they are rather than how they are represented:

> "The concept of gravity proposed here includes all of the discursivist and practice-based elements of concepts of power proposed by theorists such as Foucault and Bourdieu, while also helping us to recognize the power that things themselves exercise — by virtue of what they *are*, not by virtue of how we symbolize or represent them." (p. 194)

The gravity of a highway, a food distribution network, a fossil fuel deposit, or a company-scrip system operates regardless of whether anyone believes in it, represents it positively, or is conscious of it. This is the essential asymmetry Bryant is after: material gravity precedes and exceeds ideological representation.

## Symmetrical and Asymmetrical Gravity

Gravitational relations between machines can be symmetrical or asymmetrical (pp. 196–97):

- **Symmetrical**: both machines are mutually dependent; neither could exist without the other. The Brazil nut tree and the agouti rodent approach this (though the agouti is somewhat less dependent).
- **Asymmetrical symmetrical**: both depend on each other but in unequal degrees. Owner and worker: both need each other, but workers are more needed by owners (who cannot produce surplus value without them) than owners by workers (who need only the capital the owners control, which is replaceable).
- **Absolutely asymmetrical**: one machine is entirely dependent on the other; the other is not dependent at all.

The political significance: asymmetrically gravitational relations are those in which one party can be analyzed as a satellite of a bright object. Understanding the *degree* of asymmetry tells us how much capacity for escape the satellite has.

## The Strategic Implication

If gravity names the mechanism, the strategic question becomes: how do you change the gravitational field? Bryant's answer is infrastructure. Satellites are trapped not primarily because they have false beliefs about their situation but because alternative gravitational fields do not exist:

> "The question therefore is one of *building* alternatives that might generate lines of flight or paths of escape from these orbits." (p. 205)

Changing ideology without changing infrastructure leaves the gravitational topology intact. This is Bryant's most pointed political claim: **the heavy gravity of infrastructure is more determinative than the light gravity of ideology**, and political theory has systematically attended to the latter while neglecting the former.
