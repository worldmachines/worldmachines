# Putting-Out System

*(Also: outwork, cottage industry, domestic system)*

A mode of production common in 16th–19th century Europe in which a merchant-manufacturer ("putter-outer") owned the raw materials and controlled distribution, but contracted production to home workers who used their own equipment and space. The merchant paid per piece and bore no fixed labor costs; the worker bore the cost of space, tools, and idle time.

## Key Economic Logic

- **Capital cost shift**: the worker provides plant and equipment; the merchant provides materials and market access
- **Risk shift**: if demand falls, the worker's labor is simply not commissioned; the merchant has no fixed obligation
- **Upside extraction**: the merchant captures margin on every successful piece while insulated from failure

## The Platform Analogy

[[Aneesh Sathe]] draws a direct structural parallel: digital platforms are the putting-out system for creative labor.

| Putting-Out System | Platform Economy |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Merchant owns raw material | Platform owns distribution and audience |
| Worker owns equipment | Creator owns computer, camera, studio |
| Payment per successful piece | Payment per successful post/monetized view |
| Merchant captures margin | Platform takes cut of revenue |
| Worker absorbs failure | Creator absorbs failed content |

The critical move is that platforms deploy [[Creator-as-Hero Rhetoric]] to obscure this structure — framing the risk arrangement as empowerment.
