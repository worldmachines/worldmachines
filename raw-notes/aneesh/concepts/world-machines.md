# World Machines

> **Canonical public reference**: [[worldmachines-org-theory]] (worldmachines.org/theory). The public-facing project page is the source of canonical phrasing.
>
> **Distinct from [[world-machines-framework]]**, which covers the meta-level apparatus: epistemological status, canonical table with Dawn/Day/Dusk naming, genealogy, and open questions. This page covers the concept itself — what world machines *are*, how they behave, and what the current machine landscape looks like.

## Definition

A **world machine** (concept by [[venkatesh-rao]]) is a contraption that "embodies the logic of how the entire world works for a period of time." Not a metaphor for society or technology in isolation, but for the complete gestalt of civilizational organization — including its phenomenology, its logic of agency, and how different parts of the world relate to each other.

Key feature: world machines are *machinic* (not organic or humanistic) constructs. The framework centers "machinic phenomenology" over natural or humanistic framings.

## Lifecycle

Each world machine follows an approximate pattern:
- ~400 years to **construct** (seeds planted, logic worked out)
- ~400 years to **operate** at full capacity (plateau of stable civilizational deployment)
- ~100–200 years of **decline** (disassembly, often rapid once started)

Total lifespan: ~900–1000 years. But world-machine lifespans appear to be *falling* over longue durée history:
- Paleolithic world machine: ~100,000+ years
- Neolithic: ~15,000 years
- Bronze Age: ~4,000 years
- Medieval machine: ~600–800 years (plateau 1200–1600)
- Modernity machine: ~800 years total
- AI world machine (unnamed, nascent): possibly only ~300 years total

The *acceleration of history* is, in part, the shortening of world machine lifespans.

## Simultaneity and the Three-Machine Problem

At any given time:
- One machine is in **operation** (dominant)
- One is under **construction** (emergent)
- One may be **declining** (zombie/ghost/necromantic)

To understand how the world works, you must hold the logics of two (possibly three) machines simultaneously. This creates irresolvable interpretive complexity — which is why "the future always looks more chaotic from perspectives rooted in the past."

## Historiographic Hygiene

Rao is explicit that these numbers and models are **mnemonic devices and intuition pumps**, not rigorous theories of history. The point is to approach texts with a particular sensibility — paying attention to machinic phenomenology, asking which machine's logic a given phenomenon expresses.

World machine temporalities are also increasingly complex: later machines feature multi-temporalities, "bigger balls of wibbly wobbly, timey wimey stuff." This is why historical time "can and does dilate or contract" and why periodizing into world-machine epochs is a useful caricature, not a precise description.

## The Gramsci Gap

The meta-logic of world machine transitions derives from [[Gramsci Gap|Gramsci's interregnum]]: "The old is dying and the new cannot yet be born; in this interregnum there arise a great diversity of morbid symptoms."

In the world machine framework: what Gramsci described as morbid symptoms are the [[Late Modernity as Energetic Zombie|energetic zombies]] and [[Postmodernity as Intelligent Ghost|intelligent ghosts]] of the declining machine. The "new world being born" is actually two things: a completed new world entering full production mode, and the seeds of a further future world being planted.

## Relationship to Adjacent Concepts

- **Modernity machine**: convergent canonicity, homogenization, European centerline
- **Divergence machine**: variety generation, mutual retreat, non-canonicity as feature space
- **Postmodernity**: *not* a world machine — falls short; it's a set of responses to the modernity machine rather than a new logic
- **Metamodernity**: also not a world machine — a set of repair attempts on modernity's ruins
