# Algorithmic Curation as Place-Making

## The Core Argument

Algorithmic curation is a form of [[Space and Place in Cognition|place-making]]. When a platform's algorithm suggests content, it:
- Interrupts pure possibility-space (all possible content).
- Creates a curated *place* defined by the user's behavioral history.
- Imposes a way of looking, being, and acting within that space.

The mechanism is identical to any other place-making act. The difference is: *who* creates the place and *why*.

## The Netflix Example

Netflix's suggestion algorithm takes the "pure possibility space of the search bar" and converts it into a particular place shaped by watch history. The platform interprets your history as a probe to ensure you are always watching "good" shows — good as defined by the algorithm's optimization target.

Problem: the optimization target (engagement, time-on-platform) is not the same as the user's actual values. The place is curated for platform benefit, not user understanding.

## Bad Place-Making

Not all place-making is equally good. Algorithmic curation creates *bad places* when:
1. The optimization target diverges from genuine orientation.
2. Signal is provided without information (gesture toward relevance, no actual meaning-making).
3. The place is curated to maximize engagement (anxiety, outrage) rather than understanding.

This is the mechanism behind [[Information Hunger and Orientation]]: users receive signal that *feels* like information but delivers no genuine orientation — leaving them hungry.

## Contrast: Socratic Place-Making

[[Socratic Thinking]] is good place-making because:
- Both parties are oriented toward knowledge (shared goal).
- Places are created by examination and agreement, not behavioral prediction.
- The place-maker is accountable to the person being placed.

## The Printing Press Parallel

Early modern Europe faced analogous bad place-making: mystical texts created "places" that felt meaningful but lacked empirical grounding. [[Giordano Bruno]] is the extreme case — placed by click-bait mysticism rather than observation. Culture corrected by developing critical tools for evaluating sources.
