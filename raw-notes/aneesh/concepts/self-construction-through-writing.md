# Self-Construction Through Writing

The claim: writing is not the expression of a pre-existing self but a mechanism through which the self is continuously built.

## The Protocol (after Robert Peake)

As articulated in [[Robert Peake]]'s Summer of Protocols talk and summarized in [[On Protocols, Wagons, and Associated Acrobatics]]:

1. **Observation**: even when not writing, the writer notices — inner environment, outer environment, what they've read.
2. **Synthesis**: when writing begins, accumulated observation transforms into text.
3. **Feedback loop**: the writing itself becomes part of the self, changing future observation.
4. **Iteration**: the sum of all cycles = the life's work = the constructed self.

This is a recursive model: writing changes the observer who does the observing that feeds the next writing.

## Montaigne as Founding Instance

[[Michel de Montaigne]] did not write essays *about* himself because he had a fixed, stable self to report. He wrote to discover what he thought. The *Essais* are the record of a self constructing itself through writing. He explicitly said he and his book were one: "I have no more made my book than my book has made me."

This makes Montaigne the originating case for this concept — see [[Looking Forward to Montaigne]].

## Contrast: Writing as Expression vs. Construction

- **Expression model**: self precedes writing; writing is output of an interior that already exists.
- **Construction model**: self and writing co-evolve; there is no finished self to express, only a process of becoming.

The construction model has implications for how we evaluate creative output: a "bad" piece is not a failure to express the true self — it is a step in the construction. This connects to [[Infinite Game]] thinking.

## Relation to Ludic Feedback Loops

[[Ludic Feedback Loops]] operates similarly but at a different register: where self-construction emphasizes the developmental arc over time, ludic feedback loops focus on the moment-to-moment reward cadence that keeps one in the game.
