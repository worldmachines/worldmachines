# Geophilosophy: Three Dimensions

## Overview

Geophilosophy names the practice-oriented wing of Bryant's project. Where chapters 1–7 establish the ontology, chapter 8 establishes what one does with it. Geophilosophy "is composed of three interrelated practices: cartography, deconstruction, and terraformation. These practices are undertaken not simply for the sake of understanding worlds, but more fundamentally for the sake of producing more just, equitable, and sustainable worlds" (pp. 256–257).

The three dimensions are not temporally sequential — they are "intertwined and take place simultaneously" (p. 257). Cartography and deconstruction often occur together; terraformation typically involves deconstruction as a prerequisite.

## Cartography (Mapping)

**Practice**: Mapping assemblages — identifying the machines that compose an ecology, the hierarchical relations between them, the bright objects and their satellites, the flows of energy and matter, the material outputs and waste.

**Aim**: Understanding the gravitational organization of worlds.

**Products**: Four map types:
- *Topographical maps*: present-snapshot of an assemblage
- *Genetic maps*: historical genesis; reveals contingency
- *Vector maps*: trajectory of the assemblage given current tendencies
- *Modal maps*: possible futures conditional on specific interventions

**First principle**: "avoid abstractions" — master-terms like "capitalism" and "patriarchy" are assemblages to be mapped, not sovereign agencies that explain (p. 257).

See [[onto-cartography-as-practice]] and [[strategic-cartography]] for extended treatment.

## Deconstruction (Severing Relations)

**Practice**: Severing relations between machines — both expressive/semiotic (challenging signifying assemblages) and corporeal/material (severing physical dependencies).

**Aim**: Toppling gravity to free movement, becoming, and local manifestations; creating "lines of flight."

**Products**: Destruction of machines at the level of expression and content; undermining existing ecologies.

Two modes:
- *Expressive deconstruction*: challenges the legitimacy and function of signifying machines. Examples: Judith Butler's performance theory of gender (*Gender Trouble*); Althusser's ideology critique of institutional state apparatuses (p. 267–268).
- *Material (corporeal) deconstruction*: severs physical dependencies. Examples: building a tunnel through a mountain that isolated a community; rolling jubilee's elimination of debt gravity (pp. 270–273).

**Key limit**: Material deconstruction can cascade unpredictably (the cane toad problem). Modal maps must account for contingency as much as desired outcomes (p. 273).

## Terraformation (Building Worlds)

**Practice**: Adding new machines to existing worlds to create new paths of movement and becoming.

**Aim**: Building new assemblages that are more satisfying, just, and sustainable; creating new fields of life, materiality, and affectivity.

**Products**: Construction of machines and assemblages at the level of expression and content; building new ecologies.

Two modes:
- *Expressive terraformation*: constructing new signifying universes. Example: OWS introducing the discourse of income inequality and the 1%/99% framing into mainstream US politics — prior to OWS, income inequality was largely absent from national political discourse (p. 275).
- *Material terraformation*: adding corporeal machines to open new paths. Example: eyeglasses for the nearsighted child that open an entirely new world of learning (p. 275); LETS as an alternative economic ecology that lets people meet needs outside capitalist wage-labor (pp. 276–278).

**Critical theory's failure**: Critical theory tends to stop at deconstruction, leaving Phase 2 blank:
> Phase 1: Radical Critique / Phase 2: ? / Phase 3: Revolutionary Society! (p. 274)

Absent any terraformation — any picture of how to build alternatives — people remain trapped in the gravitational structure of the assemblages they recognize as unjust (p. 275).
