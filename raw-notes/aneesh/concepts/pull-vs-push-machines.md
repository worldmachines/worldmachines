# Pull vs. Push Machines

## The Distinction

A key mechanical metaphor within the [[world-machines-framework]], introduced by [[venkatesh-rao]]:

- **Pull Machine**: Driven by shared attractor forces — a telos that draws all participants toward the same destination. Convergent because the *same small set of pull forces act on everybody*.
- **Push Machine**: Driven by individual or small-scale forces pushing from behind. Divergent because each push acts independently, sending things in random directions.

## The Analogy

Rao's driving metaphor: think of **front-wheel drive** (pull) vs. **rear-wheel drive with no steering** (push). The latter will go off in random directions. The divergence is structural, not incidental.

## Modernity Machine as Pull

The [[Modernity Machine]] was a pull machine, "pulled along by a *telos* of Progress™." All participants — regardless of geography or class — were oriented toward the same endpoints: legibility, rationalization, material abundance, scientific knowledge, nation-state formation. This is why MM history is *convergent*: the same playbook applies across contexts.

## Divergence Machine as Push

The [[divergence-machine]] is a push machine, driven by "individual or small-scale push forces." This is structurally *why* it diverges. Divergent history is a much stronger function of **grounding conditions** — local context, initial state, particular contingencies — than convergent history.

Implication: You cannot write a universal account of the DM era the way you can write a universal account of the MM era. History under the DM is inherently more local, more path-dependent, more sensitive to initial conditions.

## Historical Case: Landes's Horology (Pull Built the Clock; Push Built the Industry)

David Landes's *Revolution in Time* (1983) supplies the longest empirical case of the distinction in the wiki, and it cuts in a useful direction: the *invention* was pull-driven, but the *industry* was repeatedly won by push.

**The clock as a pull-machine invention — the China/Europe divergence.** Landes's central thesis is demand-pull: "the clock did not create an interest in time measurement; the interest in time measurement led to the invention of the clock" ([[rit-05-are-you-sleeping-brother-john]]). The pull was a shared attractor — salvation through punctual, *simultaneous* collective prayer — drawing the mechanical escapement into existence from a monastic regime that needed to wake reliably for matins. The medieval city then multiplied and secularized that attractor: equal-hour public time as a single readable standard everyone converged on ([[rit-06-the-greatest-necessity]]). This is textbook pull, and it fits the MM's character. The contrast case is China, where the same attractor was absent: Su Sung's water clock was technically superior but demand-starved and politically monopolized — "his time was China's time," a sovereign-captured calendar with no distributed wanting ([[rit-03-a-magnificent-dead-end]]) — and even when European clocks arrived with the Jesuits, they found no socket and were metabolized as toys ([[rit-04-why-are-the-memorials-late]]). The divergence between the two civilizations is therefore the presence or absence of a *socially organized pull*, not a difference in talent: capability without a distributed attractor produces a magnificent dead end.

**The industry repeatedly won by push.** Once the clock existed, the production history inverts the moral, and this is where Landes pressures the typology's "what does the pushing?" gap. Three episodes:

- **The Swiss swarm beats Britain's canonical form.** Britain organized around one canonical attractor — the high-quality fusee'd full-plate watch, "chronometer = detent escapement" — a pull toward a single legible standard. The Swiss won as a *push-structured swarm*: many independent local makers, emulative and competitive, proliferating variant escapements and winding schemes, with no shared canonical form. Landes explicitly rejects individual-talent explanations and demands "a model of collective effort" for "the swarm of people and shops" ([[rit-20-ah-but-he-could]]). Push beat pull-toward-the-traditional-attractor.
- **State transplants (pull) die; the organic *fabrique* (push) wins.** Geneva's deliberate, top-down transplants — Waterford's "New Geneva," Constance (1785–87, dead by 1800), Revolutionary Besançon (1793–94) — all failed, because the conditions (refugee networks, credit, a skills ecology) were non-portable. What worked was the Jura's unplanned, organic rural *fabrique* ([[rit-17-multum-in-parvo]]). A clean illustration that a pull-style designed convergence loses to a push-style emergent proliferation when grounding conditions are decisive.
- **The dispersed Swiss push beats the integrated American pull.** At the top of the S-curve, the Swiss "push-structured swarm" (in 1901, ~38 employees per firm vs. ~529 per US enterprise; "the worst and the best" side by side) beat the integrated single-purpose American pull-model, because once *product proliferation* rather than process decided the market, dispersed versatility was the better insurer against missing the next technique ([[rit-22-who-killed-cock-robin]]).

**What this contributes to the open question.** The page above flags that no mechanism is specified for what does the pushing in a push machine, and that the pull-to-push transition is unexplained. Landes offers a candidate answer for both: the pushing is done by *many local actors under non-portable grounding conditions (terroir, refugee skill flows, family-bench apprenticeship)*, and the transition happens when a market shifts from rewarding convergence on one optimized form (process competition, a single legible standard) to rewarding proliferation and variety (product competition). The pull machine wins while there is one right answer to converge on; the push machine wins once the surplus has fragmented the question. Note the symmetric caution Landes also supplies: the Swiss swarm's *own* drive toward normalization (standard ébauches, observatory metrics) shows push feeding a new convergence — divergence and convergence are phases of one cycle, not fixed natures.

## Epistemological Consequences

This distinction matters for how WM histories are written:
- MM histories can be written with universal categories (legibility, class restructuring, Progress)
- DM histories must track local grounding conditions; universal categories will miss the point

The [[Liveness Machine]] raises the question: what is the motor character of the third machine? Push and pull are both exogenous metaphors. Liveness suggests something more like *emergence* — self-organization from feedback — which is neither pushed nor pulled but recursively self-generating.

## What's Missing

Rao flags this as a "random thought I'm trying to chase down." The push/pull distinction is evocative but:
- No mechanism specified for *what* does the pushing in the DM
- The transition from pull to push is not explained — why does the DM emerge with a push character?
- The cybernetics/autopoiesis tradition (which Rao is "mildly hostile to") would have a ready answer in feedback loop dynamics
