# Books as Dwellings

## The Core Metaphor

From [[Aneesh Sathe]]'s [[Books Are Dwellings]]: "Books are dwellings. Books are places."

The spatial metaphor is doing real philosophical work. It inverts the usual framing of reading as a *temporal* activity (reading *takes* time, books are *long*) in favor of a *spatial* one: books are places you inhabit, rooms you walk through. The experience of reading is not consumption but residence.

This has several implications:
1. **Devotion, not duration** — A dwelling is built for someone to live in; it demands care from both builder and inhabitant. "A post demands little effort; a book demands devotion."
2. **Memory and return** — You can return to a room. You can live in a book again.
3. **Shared inhabitation** — When you discuss a book with others, "each book becomes a room I walk through with others — and when we meet to talk, it feels like discovering a doorway between those rooms."

## Against "Reading Differently"

Sathe explicitly recants a prior position: he used to believe that digital reading (scrolling blogs, threads, chats) was merely *different* reading. He now rejects this. Digital text is "letting highway billboards flash through my mind" — the billboard cannot be a dwelling because it demands nothing and offers no interior.

The criterion for dwelling-ness appears to be **devotion at the point of creation**: because a book demands enormous effort from the author, it creates a space substantial enough to inhabit. A tweet or post demands little, and cannot therefore become a dwelling.

## Resonances

- **Gaston Bachelard**, *The Poetics of Space* (1958): "The house is our first universe, a real cosmos in every sense of the word." Bachelard's intimate spaces — nests, corners, drawers — anticipate Sathe's dwelling-books. Neither author is cited in the other, but the structural parallel is strong.
- **The Cloister Web** ([[The Cloister Web]]): Sathe's concept of semi-private creative rooms resonates with the dwelling metaphor. The Cloister Web is where ideas are housed before being carried into public.
- **[[RSS as Protocol Theology]]**: the River of Posts is also a spatial metaphor — a stream flowing through everyone's house at once. Both RSS and books organize information architecturally rather than algorithmically.

## What's *Not* a Dwelling

The essay implicitly condemns:
- Social media posts and threads
- Forums and chat
- Email (implicitly)

What's unaddressed: long-form digital essays, Substack, serialized fiction. Sathe himself writes long essays online — are these billboards or dwellings?
