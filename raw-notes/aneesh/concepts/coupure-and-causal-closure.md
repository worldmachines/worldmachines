# Coupure and Causal Closure

The deepest variant of the [[prison-house-of-synchrony]] move. In [[ernesto-laclau]]'s *New Reflections on the Revolution of Our Time* (1990) the term "spatial" is reserved for **causal closure**: any structure in which the relations among its elements are fully internal and no genuine novelty can arise.

> "Spatiality means coexistence within a structure that establishes the positive nature of all its terms." (Laclau 1990: 69, quoted Massey 2005: 43)

> "Any repetition that is governed by a structural law of successions is space." (Laclau 1990: 41, quoted 43)

A *coupure* (cut) is the moment Laclau associates with this closure: a structure cut out of the flow, its terms in fully determined relations to each other. "Politics and space are antinomic terms. Politics only exist insofar as the spatial eludes us." (Laclau 1990: 68, quoted 44)

## Massey's two-pronged response

### 1. This is not space at all

[[doreen-massey]] grants Laclau the diagnostic move: yes, there exists a kind of conceptual schema in which everything is causally interlocked and nothing changes. But why call that **space**? The term has been transferred from any positive description of the world to a label for "a lack of (a particular definition of) temporality" (43–44). Even Laclau's own gesture toward "physical space" treats *that* as temporal — exposing that what he means by "space" is not space at all but **closure**.

### 2. The framework dislocates itself

Laclau himself writes: "insofar as any 'transcendentality' is itself vulnerable, any effort to spatialize time ultimately fails and **space itself becomes an event**" (Laclau 1990: 84, quoted 45; emphasis added by Massey). Massey reads this as Laclau opening the door he has spent the rest of the book trying to close. If space itself becomes an event, then the equation space=closure is broken, and the radical-democracy project gains the resource it needs: a positive concept of the spatial as **event**, as the dimension where dislocation actually happens. See [[space-as-event]].

## The cost of not making the fix

Three downstream consequences in Laclau's framework:

1. Space is "deprived of any potential for politics" (44). Because it is defined as closure, it cannot be the site of intervention.
2. The "realm of the spatial itself (physical, social space, the space of the human geographers) is rarely directly addressed" (44).
3. A whole field of potential sources of dislocation — actual encounters of trajectories, the throwntogether co-presence of different stories — is left unexplored.

## The broader lesson

Massey uses the Laclau case to make a general point: many of the most generative twentieth-century theorists of politics worked very hard to open up temporality while leaving space conceptually inert. The result is that **time inherited all the political vocabulary** (event, novelty, dislocation, becoming) while space inherited the negatives. Reclaiming space requires reclaiming this vocabulary.
