# Bryant's Machine Concept

**Note**: This page covers Bryant's specific ontological use of "machine" as introduced in *Onto-Cartography* (2014), Introduction and Ch. 1. This is distinct from the Rao-derived "machine" vocabulary in [[world-machines]], [[liveness-machine]], [[divergence-machine]], and [[dawn-day-dusk-machine-typology]], which use "machine" as a civilizational-historical metaphor. Both senses are active in this wiki; see the synthesis note at the bottom.

## The Definition

"I call these entities 'machines' to emphasize the manner in which entities dynamically *operate* on inputs producing outputs." (Bryant 2014, p. 6)

A machine, in Bryant's sense, is any entity whatsoever: material or immaterial, corporeal or incorporeal, natural or artificial, designed or emergent, human or non-human. "Entity," "object," "existent," "substance," "body," "thing" are all synonyms of "machine" (Bryant 2014, p. 15). The term is chosen for two reasons:

1. **Operational emphasis**: it captures that entities are fundamentally *operational* — beings that do, function, operate, act. To be is to operate.
2. **Escape from subject/object**: "machine" sidesteps four hundred years of philosophical obsession with the subject/object relation. A machine operates independently, without requiring a subject to posit or experience it. As the OED puts it: "a body regarded as functioning as an independent body."

## Why Not "Object"

The term "object" carries two problems. First, it implies a subject that grasps or posits the object — re-inscribing the correlationst framework Bryant is trying to escape. Second, it encourages thinking in terms of predicates and properties (what qualities does this object have?) rather than operations (what does this entity do?).

## Three Prejudices Cleared

### 1. Not Only Rigid Machines

The stereotype of a machine — automobile, cell phone, lamp — is a *rigid* machine: fixed material parts, routinized functioning, incapable of learning, growth, or development. Bryant argues rigid machines are only a sub-species. Butterflies, caterpillars, living planets, children, otters, organizations, ecosystems are all machines capable of growth, development, and learning. Even linguistic entities — constitutions, recipes, novels — are machines, operating on inputs (readers, situations) to produce outputs (norms, meals, affects), without being material assemblages of fixed parts (Bryant 2014, pp. 15–16).

### 2. Not Designed

The hylomorphic model (Aristotle through the early modern period): an artisan first conceives a form in mind, then imposes it on passive matter. Bryant rejects this as the paradigm. Production is always a *negotiation* between human intentions and the imperatives of matter. The steam engine imposed imperatives on its designers (requiring large industrial plants). The clock restructured all of social life without anyone intending it. Most machines — trees, copper atoms, living planets — were designed by no one; they *emerged* from other machines (following DeLanda). "The designer of the train is no less designed by the train than she designs the train." (Bryant 2014, p. 20)

The better concept is biological *exaptation*: a trait or feature taking on a function different from the one it originally served. The history of technology is a history of exaptations, not designs: black powder migrated from firearms to mining; lungs may have originally been flotation devices.

### 3. Not Purposive

Entities as diverse as neutrinos, black holes, seeds, capybaras, and ballpoint pens do not have intrinsic purposes. They are *pluripotent*: they possess a range of possible becomings and can be *put to* many uses without any of those uses being their "true" purpose. A rock can become a paperweight, a weapon, a doorstop, a boiling stone. Machines "take on" a purpose only when structurally coupled to another machine (Bryant 2014, pp. 23–24).

## Taxonomy: Two Primary Divisions

### Corporeal vs. Incorporeal

**Corporeal machines**: made of matter, occupying a discrete time and place for a duration. Subatomic particles, rocks, grass, human bodies, institutions, refrigerators.

**Incorporeal machines**: defined by *iterability*, potential eternity, and the capacity to manifest in multiple spatial and temporal locations while retaining identity. Recipes, musical scores, equations, scientific theories, novels, cultural identities, constitutions. They require a corporeal body to exist in the world — numbers must occur in brains, graphite, data banks — but they are not reducible to any particular corporeal body. Virginia Woolf's *The Waves* is the same novel whether on paper, in memory, or in a data bank (Bryant 2014, pp. 26–27).

Crucially, incorporeal machines complicate causality. With corporeal machines, effects are caused by immediately preceding events (E₁ → E₂ → E₃). But incorporeal machines can cause effects across time: the Bible continues to structure contemporary life; a person can organize their ethical life according to Epictetus; DNA structures the development of organisms in the present. The remote past can directly influence the immediate present via preserved incorporeal machines.

"Dormant" incorporeal machines: those that still exist through their corporeal inscriptions but are no longer active or remembered — the *Dead Sea Scrolls*, Lucretius's *De Rerum Natura* before its fifteenth-century rediscovery. Like the tick in Deleuze and Guattari awaiting a warm-blooded entity, dormant incorporeals exist in suspended animation, capable of activating and transforming the present if encountered (Bryant 2014, pp. 27–28).

### Within Corporeal: Inanimate, Animate, Cognitive

- **Inanimate**: change only through external causes or internal processes; no self-preserving operations; ultimate fate is entropy. Rocks, stars, dead planets.
- **Animate**: engage in operations to preserve their organization. A cat's wound heals; a plant grows toward light. Unlike inanimate machines, animate machines resist entropy through active self-maintenance.
- **Cognitive**: capable of directing their own action toward goals. Cats, birds, dolphins, octopuses, humans, institutions, some technologies. Differ from simple animate machines in being intentionally goal-directed.

Overlaps are common: dolphins are both cognitive and animate; governments combine incorporeal machines (laws, procedures), animate machines (personnel replenishment), and cognitive machines (learning, goal-direction).

### Within Both: Rigid vs. Plastic

- **Rigid**: operations fixed, not modifiable by flows. Mathematical equations, bureaucratic rules, certain moral codes.
- **Plastic**: modifiable by flows passing through them. Organisms, novels, cultural identities, political theories.

## Structural Coupling

Machines relate through *structural coupling* (from Maturana and Varela): interactive relations in which machines perturb one another and develop in relation to one another. Couplings can be:
- **Unidirectional**: machine A triggers responses in machine B without reciprocal triggering (the sun and a flower).
- **Bidirectional**: mutual perturbation and co-development (predator-prey evolutionary arms races).

Structural coupling is how machines become media for one another — see [[post-human-media-ecology]].

## Relation to the Wiki's Machine Concepts

Bryant's "machine" and the Rao-inflected "machine" vocabulary in this wiki ([[world-machines]], [[liveness-machine]]) are genuinely distinct:

| Dimension | Bryant's machine | Rao's world-machine |
|---|---|---|
| Scope | Every entity without exception | Large-scale civilizational contraptions |
| Register | Ontological (what is) | Historical-phenomenological (how things feel) |
| Agency | Distributed among all entities | Concentrated in the world machine as gestalt |
| Design | Anti-design, emergence-first | Quasi-designed by historical processes |
| Scale | From neutrinos to planets | From centuries to millennia |

The resonance: both resist reducing social reality to human discourse or human intention. Both insist on the causal efficacy of non-human entities. Both are interested in how entities shape the field of possibility for other entities.

The tension: Rao's world machines are legible historical shapes; Bryant's machines are the granular ontological substrate from which any such shape would have to be assembled. A world machine, in Bryant's vocabulary, would itself be a massive assemblage of structurally coupled machines — corporeal and incorporeal, rigid and plastic.
