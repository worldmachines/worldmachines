# Infinite Game

The infinite game, as developed by philosopher [[James Carse]] and popularized by [[Simon Sinek]], distinguishes between:

- **Finite games**: fixed rules, fixed players, clear winners and losers. You play to end the game.
- **Infinite games**: evolving rules, open player pool, no final winner. You play to keep playing.

## Application to Creative Practice

[[Robert Peake]], in his Summer of Protocols talk "The Infinite Game of Poetry," applies this to writing and creative life. The poet does not write to *finish* writing or to *win* at poetry. The point is to remain a player — to keep noticing, synthesizing, and writing. Falling off the wagon is not losing; it's a temporary exit from the game. Getting back on is re-entering.

This reframes "consistency" from a moral obligation to a strategic necessity: you can't play if you're not playing.

## Protocol Implications

Peake derives a protocol from this:
1. Even when not actively producing, the infinite game player is *observing* — inner environment, outer environment, reading.
2. Observation feeds synthesis; synthesis feeds writing.
3. Writing changes the self, which changes future observation.
4. The sum of all iterations is the life's work.

See: [[Self-Construction Through Writing]]

## Relation to Habit and Identity

The infinite game framing is gentler on the self than the "streak" or "habit" framing. A broken streak ends a finite game; in the infinite game, you simply re-enter. This has practical implications for creative productivity — it reduces the all-or-nothing dynamics that make people quit permanently after a single miss.

See also: [[Habit and Identity]], [[On Protocols, Wagons, and Associated Acrobatics]]

## Contradictions / Open Questions

- Does the infinite game framing risk becoming an excuse for inconsistency? The finite game's pressure is partly what produces results.
- Carse's original framework is descriptive, not prescriptive — Sinek and Peake extend it normatively.
