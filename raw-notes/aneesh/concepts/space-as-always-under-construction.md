# Space as Always Under Construction

Massey's third proposition in *For Space* (2005): "that we recognise space as always under construction. Precisely because space on this reading is a product of relations-between, relations which are necessarily embedded material practices which have to be carried out, it is always in the process of being made. It is never finished; never closed. Perhaps we could imagine space as a simultaneity of stories-so-far." (Massey 2005: 9)

## The Claim

Space is a process, not a product. Because it is produced through material practices — which are never exhausted, never fully settled — any spatial configuration is irreducibly provisional. Space is not a completed simultaneity (all interconnections established, all relations in place) but an ongoing process of relating:

> "In this open interactional space there are always connections yet to be made, juxtapositions yet to flower into interaction (or not, for not all potential connections have to be established), relations which may or may not be accomplished." (Massey 2005: 11)

The consequence: space can never be a closed system. "A space, then, which is neither a container for always-already constituted identities nor a completed closure of holism. This is a space of loose ends and missing links." (Massey 2005: 11–12)

## The Formula: Simultaneity of Stories-So-Far

Massey's most evocative phrasing — "a simultaneity of stories-so-far" — captures the irreducible openness. Each trajectory is mid-sentence; there is no final chapter. The spatial arrangement at any moment is not an endpoint but a cross-section through ongoing becomings. See [[simultaneity-of-stories-so-far]].

## Political Corollary

Only an open space can sustain an open future. The grand narratives of modernity — Progress, Development, Modernisation, the Marxist succession of modes — all presupposed that the direction of history was known. Massey allies with:
- Laclau (1990; Laclau & Mouffe 2001): radical democracy requires the genuine openness of the future; politics is only real where outcomes are not predetermined.
- Deleuze & Guattari (1988): active experimentation rather than following a pre-given script.
- Queer theory (Haver 1997): refusal of the developmental narrative.

> "Only if the future is open is there any ground for a politics which can make a difference." (Massey 2005: 11)

And for the future to be open, space must be open too.

## The Bergson Tension

Massey credits Bergson's insistence on genuine becoming (the real production of novelty) while noting that his strategy led to an impasse: he characterised space as static precisely to rescue time from stasis. Massey's move is to claim that space too can have the quality of duration — multiple durations in fact:

> "A dynamic simultaneity would be a conception quite different from a frozen instant." (Massey 2005: 23, citing Massey 1992a)

Space-as-always-under-construction is what results when you refuse the Bergsonian equation of space with the frozen instant.

## Contradictions / Conflicting Data

Massey explicitly refuses Laclau's own use of "space": Laclau equates space with ideological closure and representation. Massey's Proposition 3 argues the opposite — space, properly conceived, is the *ground* of openness, not its enemy. This is a direct, named disagreement. See [[coupure-and-causal-closure]].
