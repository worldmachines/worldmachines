# Correlationism

## Source

Quentin Meillassoux, *After Finitude: An Essay on the Necessity of Contingency* (Continuum, 2008). Adopted by Levi Bryant in *Onto-Cartography* (2014) and throughout OOO discourse.

## The Concept

Correlationism is Meillassoux's term for the dominant post-Kantian philosophical position: we can only ever think the *correlation* between thinking and being, or between subject and object — never either term in isolation. We cannot know "the great outdoors," being as it is independently of human access.

Meillassoux identifies this as the fundamental move shared by otherwise radically opposed modern philosophies: Kantian transcendental idealism, phenomenology (Husserl, Heidegger), post-structuralism (Derrida), and even pragmatism. What varies is what the correlation is *between* — subject/object, Dasein/world, language/reality, experience/nature — but the structural commitment to the primacy of the correlation is constant.

The correlationist can be either a "weak" correlationist (we don't know being in itself, but we can posit it) or a "strong" correlationist / absolute idealist (being in itself is not even thinkable without contradiction).

## Meillassoux's Argument Against It

The "ancestral" problem: what about events prior to any possible human experience — the formation of the earth 4.5 billion years ago, the accretion of the solar system? Science produces statements about these events, and we take them as true. But correlationism cannot account for their truth: there was no correlation (no subject to experience them) when they occurred. The correlationist is forced to "de-world" the ancestral past, retroactively projecting a human correlate onto a time before humans existed.

Meillassoux's alternative: *speculative materialism* — the claim that contingency itself is absolute and necessary, while all else is contingent. The one thing we can think that is not merely for-us is the contingency of everything.

## Bryant's Use

Bryant adopts "correlationism" as a diagnostic label for the deeper philosophical error underlying [[discursive-materialism-critique]]. The slide from Marx to discursivism is enabled by the correlationist assumption: matter's powers cannot be affirmed without immediately translating them into human experience, discourse, or signification.

Bryant's [[renewal-of-materialism]] is a correlationism-breaking project: an ontology in which entities (machines) have powers and engage in operations that do not require human experience to be real. The machine operates; the operation is real; the human is optional.

In this sense, Bryant's project is allied with Meillassoux's, though Bryant pursues the correlationism-break through a Deleuzian ontology of machines and structural couplings rather than through Meillassoux's mathematical speculative materialism.

## In Speculative Realism Generally

All four main figures from the 2007 Goldsmiths workshop — Meillassoux, Harman, Brassier, Grant — share the anti-correlationist commitment. Their solutions differ:
- Meillassoux: mathematical necessity of contingency
- Harman: objects withdraw from all relations including cognition
- Brassier: eliminative naturalism
- Grant: Schellingian naturphilosophie

Bryant's position is adjacent: machines have real operations independently of human access, but Bryant insists machines are fundamentally relational (contra Harman's withdrawal), forming ecologies of structural coupling.

## Relation to This Wiki

The wiki's [[spatial-epistemology-the-master-thread]] is largely epistemological — concerned with how humans make places and navigate knowledge. Bryant's anti-correlationism asks: what are machines doing when no human is navigating?
