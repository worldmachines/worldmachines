# Immigrant Rocks

**Source**: [[doreen-massey]], *For Space*, Ch. 12 (2005: 136)

## The Phrase

> "Immigrant rocks: the rocks of Skiddaw are immigrant rocks, just passing through here, like my sister and me only rather more slowly, and changing all the while. Places as heterogeneous associations." (Massey 2005: 136)

The phrase is Massey's compressed result from a night spent reading local geology during a winter stay in Keswick, Lake District. See [[the-lake-district-as-meeting-place]] for the full geological argument.

## What It Does

"Immigrant rocks" does several things at once:

1. **Extends process-ontology to the geological**: the argument that everything is trajectory-in-process, not settled thing, has typically been made for human social phenomena. Immigrant rocks apply it to geology — to the apparently most immovable, foundational dimension of place.

2. **Undermines the nature-as-fixity argument**: the appeal to mountains, wilderness, and "natural landscape" as the enduring ground of place is a commonplace. If even mountains are immigrants — if Skiddaw's rocks have been moving northward for 500 million years and are still moving — then nature cannot serve as the stable foundation for place identity.

3. **Creates cross-scale symmetry**: the sister visiting for a weekend; Massey visiting for a long weekend; the swifts migrating annually between Kilburn and Southern Africa; the rocks moving northward across 500 million years. All are trajectories, all are "just passing through," all are "changing all the while." The phrase achieves this symmetry playfully but with philosophical precision.

4. **The word "immigrant"**: loaded. The immigrant is figured culturally as the disruptor of a stable local identity. But if the rocks are immigrants — if the supposedly most permanent features of the place are also in transit — then the "local" has no prior, stable identity to be disrupted.

## The Geological Facts

Skiddaw's rocks (the "Skiddaw slates" or "Skiddaw Group") were deposited in the Iapetus Ocean approximately 500 million years ago, when that ocean lay in the southern hemisphere, roughly a third of the way from the equator toward the south pole. Subsequent volcanic activity (Ordovician period), followed by repeated phases of:

- Folding and erosion
- Intrusion of plutonic igneous rocks from below
- Overlay and folding of subsequent strata (Silurian, Carboniferous, Permian, Triassic)
- Gentle uplift producing the elongated dome of the present Lake District
- Glaciation (multiple ice ages, last retreating ~10,000 years ago)
- Ongoing erosion (including by tourists)

The rocks only emerged above sea level approximately 10 million years ago. Continental drift continues at a few centimetres per year. The present landscape is a temporary articulation of all these processes — not a terminus.

## The Argument Against Dirlik

Arif Dirlik (2001) argues that relational accounts of place (like Massey's) are "overly zealous in disassociating place from fixed location" and that ecological conceptions of place, which emphasise the meeting of social and natural, can restore a necessary fixity. Massey accepts the ecological extension (the nonhuman must be brought into place-theory) but rejects the fixity conclusion. Immigrant rocks are the rejoinder: bringing nature in does not restore stability — it reveals that nature too is in motion. "To assume there is [stable ground] is to fall into those imaginations criticised in Chapter 9 for celebrating a mobile culture while holding (or trying to hold) nature still" (Massey 2005: 137).

## Resonances

**Sarah Whatmore** on the "mobile lives" of animals and plants: swifts, migrating whales, seeds in the bellies of animals. The Lake District has been repopulated since the last ice age by movements of animals, plants, and humans. "So what is indigenous here?" (Massey 2005: 138).

**Latour**: "History is no longer simply the history of people, it becomes the history of natural things as well" (Latour 1993: 82, cited Massey 2005: 138).

**Barbara Bender** (personal communication, cited Massey 2005: 138): "Landscapes refuse to be disciplined. They make a mockery of the oppositions that we create between time (History) and space (Geography), or between nature (Science) and culture (Social Anthropology)."
