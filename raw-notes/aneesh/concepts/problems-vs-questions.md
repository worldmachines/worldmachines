# Problems vs. Questions

## The Distinction

Drawn from [[Agnes Callard]]'s *Open Socrates*.

| | Problems | Questions |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | Barriers blocking a goal | Demands for new understanding |
| **Relationship to answer** | Dissolve when solved | Open into quests; new understanding remains |
| **Method** | Established; technology applicable | No established method in current state |
| **Example** | "How do I get a congee recipe?" | "What is it to eat well?" |
| **Stance** | Satisfied by solution | Perpetual dissatisfaction drives science |

## Why the Distinction Matters

- Technology (including LLMs in their typical use) solves problems. Problems don't require shared purpose — only methods satisfactory to the asker.
- Science's characteristic posture is "perpetual dissatisfaction with existing answers" — this is the *question* orientation toward the world.
- Most people rush to *convert* questions into problems: ask Google, get recipe, done. This forecloses the quest.

## Alan Turing's Reduction

[[Alan Turing]]'s formulation of the Turing Test is an instance of converting a question ("What is thinking?") into a problem ("Can we detect thought?"). The fact that LLMs pass the test but don't convince us they think shows that the conversion lost something essential.

See: [[Turing Test]]

## Pre-Answered Questions

A special case: questions that arrive *already answered*. We hold them as settled without examination. They require Socratic pressure to reopen.

Example: "What is it to be a good scientist?" A scientist gives rote answers (statistical rigor, publishing, experimental care) without these answering the root question.

See: [[Normative Self-Blindness]] — the mechanism that seals pre-answers shut.

## Aneesh Sathe's Spatial Extension

Sathe argues that the Q&P distinction maps precisely onto the [[Space and Place Philosophy]] framework:
- Problems = **places**: bounded, ordered, tractable; you can stand here and say "do this"
- Questions = **spaces**: pure potential, defined by the places that surround them; the horizon you constantly seek

He prefers the spatial framing because it extends beyond the cognitive domain to physical, scientific, and experiential territory. Questions *contain* problems: sub-problems are waypoints en route to an answer, but solving all the sub-problems never closes the question — it opens a larger horizon.

See: [[problems-are-places-questions-are-spaces|Problems are Places, Questions are Spaces]] (Sathe, 2025)
