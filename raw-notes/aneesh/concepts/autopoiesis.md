# Autopoiesis

## Core Definition

Autopoiesis (from Greek *autos* = self, *poiesis* = making) is the property of a system that continuously produces and reproduces the components of its own organization through its own operations. Coined by Chilean biologists [[humberto-maturana]] and [[francisco-varela]] in the early 1970s, it captures what is distinctive about living systems: they are self-producing rather than merely self-organizing.

An autopoietic system:
1. Has a boundary that distinguishes it from its environment
2. Produces the components of that boundary and of its own internal organization through its internal network of processes
3. Maintains this organization across time through ongoing operations — the system does not simply *be* organized but actively *produces* its organization moment to moment

The canonical example is the living cell: its metabolism produces the molecular components (membranes, enzymes, proteins) that constitute the cell's organization, and this production network is itself constituted by those components. The cell is thus self-referentially productive.

## Why It Matters: Distinction from Mere Low Entropy

Autopoiesis specifies what kind of low-entropy system a living organism is. Any crystal or rock is also a low-entropy system (organized, differentiated), but rocks do not produce their own components. An autopoietic system does something more: it uses its operations to *regenerate* the very machinery that performs those operations. This is Luhmann's point, developed from Maturana and Varela, that living systems have *dynamic stability* rather than static stability — they persist not by staying the same but by continuously reproducing sameness through change.

## Bryant's Usage in Onto-Cartography

In [[onto-cartography]], autopoiesis underlies Bryant's account of how organic machines resist [[entropy-and-assemblage-maintenance]]. Organic bodies are negentropic systems precisely because they are autopoietic: they reproduce their cells, and crucially, they reproduce not merely the cells themselves but the *organizational relations between cells*, thereby maintaining the body's overall pattern.

"The activities through which an organic body produces its cells (in development), reproduces its cells, and reproduces relations between those cells, are the operations through which it resists entropy. When machines operate in this way, they are negentropic." (p. 97)

Bryant extends the autopoietic logic beyond biology to social systems (following Luhmann's sociological extension): cities, institutions, and collectives must reproduce the conditions of their own operation to persist. This is social autopoiesis — the reproduction of the relations and practices that constitute the social machine. Althusser's claim that "every social formation must reproduce the conditions of its production" (Althusser 2001: 86, quoted p. 98) is Bryant's formulation of social autopoiesis.

## The Tension: Autopoiesis and Machinic Problems

Autopoiesis at the level of the whole organism is complicated by the fact that the organism is a [[machinic-assemblage]] of other autopoietic systems (cells, organs, organ systems) that have their own operational closure and structural openness. These component autopoietic systems do not simply subordinate themselves to the whole. Cancer is a paradigm case: cells begin autopoietically reproducing on their own terms, refusing the regulatory operations of the organism's negentropic management.

This is Bryant's "machinic problems" point generalized: no machine ever fully autopoietically totalizes its parts, because the parts are themselves (semi-)autonomous machines.

## Luhmann's Sociological Extension

Niklas Luhmann extended autopoiesis from biology to social systems and to consciousness. Social systems, for Luhmann, are autopoietic systems whose basic elements are *communications* (not persons): each communication is produced by the system from prior communications, and the system thereby reproduces itself. Persons are part of the *environment* of social systems in Luhmann's framework — a more radical formulation than Bryant's, who retains persons as component machines.

Bryant's use of Luhmann's entropy/negentropy concepts is consistent with but not fully committed to Luhmann's social autopoiesis framework.
