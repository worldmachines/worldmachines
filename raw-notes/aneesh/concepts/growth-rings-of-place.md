# Growth Rings of Place

## The Figure

In Ch. 13 of *Space and Place* (1977/2002), Tuan introduces the image of a city's growth rings as a way to visualize the mutual constitution of place and time. The "annual rings of an aged tree" (p. 191) serve as the explicit analogy: just as a tree's cross-section reveals its temporal history in concentric layers, a European city reveals its history in successive walls, architectural styles, and material strata.

**The canonical example is Paris.** Tuan reproduces a diagram (Fig. 22, p. 192) showing the concentric growth rings of the city's successive walls:
- Wall of Philip Augustus (thirteenth century)
- Wall at the time of Louis XIV (seventeenth century)
- Wall of 1840

Landmarks — the Louvre (L), the Eiffel Tower (E), Place de la Bastille (B), Place de l'Etoile (PE) — are readable as temporal coordinates within this spatial structure. "Time is made visible in the concentric growth rings of a city" (Fig. 22 caption, p. 192).

## The Argument

The growth-rings figure is doing more than providing a picturesque urban metaphor. It supports a specific philosophical claim: **temporal depth is not merely *known about* a place; it is *experienced* in a place** — if the built environment preserves the layering.

"History has depth, and time bestows value" (p. 191). This depth is, Tuan argues, more likely to develop in people surrounded by artifacts they know to have taken a long time to make. A great cathedral measured several human generations against the steady rise of its construction. "The edifice is a public timepiece. The city in which it is located also has temporal depth objectified in the city's successive walls that accrue like the annual rings of an aged tree" (p. 191).

The city as public timepiece is not merely symbolic. It enables a particular mode of experience — the inhabitation of historical depth rather than the mere contemplation of it. One moves *through* time in moving through Paris; the walls are not labeled exhibits but environmental conditions.

## The Chinese Contrast

Tuan's comparison with Chinese urbanism sharpens the concept. Chinese civilization is ancient, but Chinese urban landscapes are not:

> "Very little that can be seen dates back more than a few centuries. The Great Wall itself, or what can be seen of it, is largely the work of Ming dynasty (A.D. 1366–1644)." (p. 190)

Chinese cities were built quickly, without the stone-permanence ethic that produces European growth rings. Chang'an (Han capital) was completed in under three years; the Sui capital Lo-yang in less than a year; Kublai Khan's Cambaluc in a few years (pp. 191). The Chinese landscape "evinces no clear story line; relics that point to stages of the past are not evident" (p. 191). It is not that China lacks history, but that China's built environment does not *spatialize* history in the same way.

The consequence is that the European sense of history as "a long chronicle with plots that do not repeat themselves" (p. 191) depends partly on the built environment that makes such plots legible. The growth-rings city produces — and then sustains — the historical consciousness of its inhabitants.

## The Concept's Limits

The growth-rings figure has a paradox embedded in it. Once Tuan introduces the distinction between **being rooted** and **cultivating a sense of place**, the growth-rings city turns out to be associated with the latter more than the former. The museum — the institutional form of growth-ring consciousness — "reflects a habit of mind opposed to one that perceives place to be rooted, sacred, and inviolable" (p. 194). The museum consists "wholly of displaced objects. Treasures and oddities are torn from their cultural matrices in different parts of the world and put on pedestals in an alien environment" (p. 194).

So: growth rings make time visible, and visible time supports historical consciousness and preservation movements. But the very deliberateness of this cultivation signals a distance from the unselfconscious rootedness that constitutes living place. The growth-rings city is a place for people who know they have a past, not for people who simply *are* their past.
