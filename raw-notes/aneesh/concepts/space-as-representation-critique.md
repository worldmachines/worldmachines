# Space-as-Representation Critique

The target of Chapter 2 of [[doreen-massey]]'s *For Space* (2005). Massey identifies a hegemonic but rarely-examined chain of equations running through much twentieth-century philosophy and social theory:

> space = representation = a slowing-down of life = a flattening-out = a-temporality = death

The chain shows up in [[henri-bergson]] ("you substitute the path for the journey"), in [[michel-de-certeau]] ("the 'proper' is a victory of space over time"), in [[ernesto-laclau]] ("dislocation is the very form of temporality . . . the 'spatialization' of an event consists of eliminating its temporality"). Each of these writers treats representation as that which fixes the flow of life, and then — without further argument — calls what is fixed "space."

## Massey's two-step counter

Massey separates two distinct claims that the tradition has run together:

1. **Representation fixes life.** Partially defensible, though not in the strong form Bergson once held. Modern process-philosophical and post-representational positions (Deleuze and Guattari; Latour; Ingold; Thrift) have softened it: representation is itself an activity, an embedded engagement, not a mirror.
2. **What representation produces is *space*.** This Massey rejects outright. "There is no case at all for the second proposition" (Massey 2005: 27).

The point: the tracing is not the territory. A representation may *be* spatial (it's laid out side-by-side on a page) but the world it represents is **time-space** — both temporal and spatial — and is no more amenable to capture by representation than time alone.

## Why this matters

Conflating space with representation has three costly effects:

1. It makes space "self-evidently" a surface (flat horizontality of the page) — see [[horizontalities-of-deconstruction]].
2. It robs space of the "characteristics of freedom (Bergson), dislocation (Laclau) and surprise (de Certeau) which are essential to open it up to the political" (28).
3. It produces the dismal pyrrhic-victory image in which space "conquers time" only by being reduced to stasis: "in the very moment of its conquering triumph 'space' is reduced to stasis. The very life, and certainly the politics, are taken out of it" (30).

## Historical origin of the conflation

Massey traces it (via Prigogine and Stengers 1984) to [[henri-bergson]]'s polemic with classical physics. Bergson needed time to be open, irreversible, creative — against a science of time-reversible determinism. He fought by claiming that whatever science did to time (the pinning, the writing-down, the holding-still) was its *opposite* and that opposite he called space. The polemical move worked. But the science Bergson was fighting has since changed (nonequilibrium thermodynamics, complexity, indeterminism), so the polemical motive for the move has dissolved. The conflation survives only as a sediment.

## Massey's positive alternative

Drop the equation. Space is not what representation produces; space is the **dimension of coexisting multiplicity** — see [[space-as-the-dimension-of-multiplicity]], [[dynamic-simultaneity]]. Representations capture neither time nor space adequately; what they always partially capture is **time-space**.
