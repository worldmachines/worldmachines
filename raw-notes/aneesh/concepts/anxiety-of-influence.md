# Anxiety of Influence

## Origin

Harold Bloom's 1973 book *The Anxiety of Influence: A Theory of Poetry* introduced this framework. It remains his most intellectually rigorous work — "the work that best sustains rereading" by his own criteria.

## Core Theory

Every serious poet (and by extension, any strong creative writer) is shaped by **precursors** — earlier writers who first inspired the work. But this relationship is not benevolent transmission. It is sublimated **agon**: unconscious conflict and competition for literary immortality.

The mechanism:
1. Young writer encounters precursor, is inspired and enabled
2. Precursor's achievement threatens the new writer's sense of originality
3. Writer must produce a strong enough **misreading** (creative revision) of the precursor to justify their own existence
4. This misreading, if successful, becomes the new writer's distinctive voice

> "To become a serious author of any real enduring merit, one must necessarily take on certain literary precursors... but for Bloom this is no simple craft tradition, passed down benevolently. What begins as literary inspiration ends up as sublimated agon."

## The Six Revisionary Ratios

Bloom describes six moves by which strong poets manage the anxiety of influence:
1. **Clinamen** — a swerve away from the precursor; the poem reads as though the precursor went wrong precisely where it did
2. **Tessera** — completion and antithesis; the ephebe retains the precursor's terms but means something different
3. **Kenosis** — a discontinuation of the self; the ephebe seems to empty themselves of poetic godhood
4. **Daemonization** — a Counter-Sublime against the precursor's Sublime, opening to a power in the parent-poem
5. **Askesis** — a movement of self-purgation, solitary stance against the precursor
6. **Apophrades** — the return of the dead; the mature poet holds open house for the precursors

## Belatedness

A crucial corollary: every writer after the earliest (Homer, the Bible) faces **belatedness** — the crushing awareness of arriving too late, when everything has already been said. The accumulation of literary history increases this burden over time. By the Romantic era it was acute; by the 20th century it was almost unbearable.

This is why late-career poets (Milton, Keats, Stevens) often feel the weight of the tradition most acutely — and why the strongest late-career work tends to be a direct confrontation with it.

## What Bloom's Theory Excludes

- **Joy of borrowing**: classical concepts of imitation (mimesis), apprenticeship, and craft transmission are dismissed
- **Collective or folk traditions**: the theory is almost entirely about individual strong poets
- **Collaborative creation**: the Bloomian cosmos has no room for it
- The essay source notes: "Where is the ecstasy of influence? Where is the joy of borrowing? Where is the classical ideal... of the artist as artificer?"

## The Paralysis Problem

Taken as a final statement rather than a provocation, the theory can be debilitating: behind every apparent inspiration lurks only repression; behind every apparent originality lurks strategic evasion. This transfers anxiety from the texts to the reader/writer.

The essay source's resolution: anxiety must be metabolized — moved through, not escaped. The writer must allegorize their own anxiety and make it generative material.

## Relationship to the Western Canon

The anxiety of influence provides the *mechanism* that Bloom's [[Western Canon]] runs on. Works remain in the canon because writers keep returning to them as precursors — keep misreading them. If no one were made anxious by Shakespeare, Shakespeare would exit the canon.
