# Happenstance Juxtaposition

A phrase from Chapter 9 of [[doreen-massey]]'s *For Space* (2005). One of the productive characteristics of material space is that we **don't choose our neighbours**. We turn corners and meet alterity. We share blocks of flats with people who arrived here by other routes than ours. We negotiate the cohabitation that none of us planned.

> "The productiveness of spatiality which may enable 'something new' to happen . . . the happenstance juxtaposition of previously unrelated trajectories, that business of walking round a corner and bumping into alterity, of having (somehow, and well or badly) to get on with neighbours who have got 'here' . . . by different routes from you; your being here together is, in that sense, quite uncoordinated." (Massey 2005: 94)

## Why it matters

Three claims bundled together:

1. **Spatial co-presence is mostly unchosen.** Even when we choose where to live, we don't choose who else does. The "neighbours" are a fact, not a curation.
2. **Unchosen co-presence is productive.** It generates friction, surprise, negotiation. New things become possible because of the encounters that nobody planned.
3. **Politics happens here.** "Some form of social negotiation" is necessary because the others *are* there — they aren't going anywhere, you aren't going anywhere, and the situation has to be lived.

## What it resists

Massey reads various forms of contemporary spatial purification as attempts to escape happenstance juxtaposition:

- **Gated communities of the rich.** Choose your neighbours by exclusion.
- **Science parks and high-tech enclaves.** "Purified quite rigorously . . . of 'non-conforming' uses" (96).
- **Digital tribalism / cyberspace.** "A kind of disembedding into non-contiguous communities of people-like-us which evade all those challenges thrown up by what material spatiality always presents you with — the accidental, unchosen (different) neighbour" (95).
- **Anti-immigration politics.** The "right to one's own place" purified of the unfamiliar.

The pattern: contemporary technologies and politics offer increasingly powerful ways to **select your simultaneity** — to live with only those you've chosen. Massey thinks this is corrosive, because the encounter with the unchosen is one of the conditions of genuine politics and of novelty.

## The political reading

The argument is not "we should all live together regardless of preference." Massey is explicit that the marginalised may need their own spaces, that purification is sometimes defensive rather than oppressive. The point is structural: **the doubleness of contemporary spatial regimes** — free trade for capital, border discipline for migrants; networked communities for the privileged, exclusion for the rest — represents an unequal distribution of the ability to *escape* happenstance juxtaposition. The wealthy escape; the poor are left to negotiate. The political stake is to redistribute this capacity, not to impose forced co-presence.
