# Enlightenment Monism

## The Doctrine

[[Isaiah Berlin]] identifies what he calls "the central dogma of the entire Enlightenment": that the laws governing human life are real, can be known (with certainty or at least probability), and are universal — valid for all men, in all places, at all times. This is Berlin's target concept, the thing the [[Counter-Enlightenment]] thinkers were opposing.

The doctrine had several interlocking components (pp. 1–4 of "The Counter-Enlightenment"):

1. **Human nature is fundamentally the same** in all times and places; local and historical variations are unimportant compared to the constant core
2. **Universal human goals exist**: all men ultimately seek food, shelter, security, peace, happiness, justice, truth, and *humanitas* — however different the means they use
3. **Newtonian method applies to ethics and politics**: just as physics discovered laws of inanimate nature, so rational inquiry can discover laws of human nature and build a science of society
4. **Progress is achievable**: once the right laws are known, irrational and oppressive institutions can be replaced by rational arrangements

## The *Philosophes*

The major Enlightenment figures Berlin invokes:
- **Voltaire, d'Alembert, Condorcet**: believed the development of the arts and sciences was the most powerful weapon in attaining universal human ends, the sharpest weapon against ignorance, superstition, fanaticism, and oppression
- **Rousseau and Mably** (in partial dissent): institutions of civilization corrupt men, alienating them from their natural simplicity — but even they shared the belief in a universal set of natural human ends and a universal natural law
- **Helvétius, Holbach**: materialist versions of the same monism — pleasure and pain as universal levers, virtue and vice as functions of social arrangement

The *philosophes* "might differ about what these laws were, or how to discover them, or who were qualified to expound them; that these laws were real" was never questioned.

## The Sceptical Tradition's Failure to Break It

Berlin acknowledges that a tradition of scepticism ran back to Greek sophists (Protagoras, Antiphon, Critias) — "beliefs involving value-judgements rest not on discoveries of objective and unalterable natural facts, but on human opinion, which was variable." This was reinforced by Montaigne, Agrippa, Charron, and most powerfully by Hume.

But the sceptics, however much they doubted the knowability of universal laws, "retained a common core of conviction that the ultimate ends of all men at all times were, in effect, identical." Hume the sceptic still believed men sought pleasure and avoided pain. The counter-Enlightenment thinkers deny even this residual universalism.

## The Three Interlocking Pillars

Berlin summarizes Enlightenment monism as three mutually reinforcing doctrines:

1. **Monism** (in value theory): one correct set of answers to all genuine questions about how to live
2. **Universalism**: reason is the same in all rational beings; therefore any properly reasoning person, anywhere, at any time, can in principle arrive at the same answers
3. **Progressivism**: history is a story of improvement; obstacles to progress are ignorance or corruption, not deep human nature or irresolvable value conflict

## Why Berlin Thinks the Counter-Enlightenment Was Right (Partially)

The counter-Enlightenment's attack on these three pillars was, Berlin argues, partially correct:
- Cultures are not simply variations on a universal theme — they embody genuinely different and incommensurable values ([[Value Pluralism]])
- Historical understanding requires imaginative participation, not just rational analysis ([[einfuhlung|Einfühlung]], *fantasia*)
- Progress is not a single trajectory — there is no "view from nowhere"

Where the counter-Enlightenment was wrong was in the political conclusions it drew: that the limits of reason justify authoritarianism, that organic traditions should be immune from rational criticism, that violence in service of the organic whole is self-realization.
