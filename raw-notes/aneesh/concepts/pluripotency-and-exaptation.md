# Pluripotency and Exaptation

## Pluripotency in Bryant

[[levi-bryant]] introduces *pluripotency* to describe the highest form of machine plasticity — the capacity to produce genuinely different outputs across radically different contexts of coupling. A pluripotent machine is not one that does the same thing everywhere, but one that *resonates* differently depending on what it encounters.

"A great work of art is plastic in the sense that it is *pluripotent*. It is a machine that is capable of resonating in a variety of ways given the historical and cultural milieus that it encounters." (*Onto-Cartography*, p. 52)

The paradigm case is Kafka's *The Trial* and *The Castle*: these novels can be read psychoanalytically, politically (as critiques of fascism), theologically, as bureaucracy critiques, as handbooks for resistance, as analyses of alienation (p. 52). Each reading is not a misreading — it is a genuine local manifestation produced by the coupling of the text-machine with a particular cultural-historical milieu. The milieu actualizes the work in a particular way; but the work also organizes the milieu, leading readers to attend to certain phenomena as significant (p. 53).

## Pluripotency vs. Infinite Plasticity

Pluripotency is not the claim that machines can do *anything*. "Clearly a rock cannot become a butterfly, nor a butterfly an automobile. Rather each machine has a plasticity proper to the sort of machine that it is." (p. 46) Pluripotency is the breadth of possible manifestations *within* a machine's range, not unlimited transformability. A work of art can produce many readings; it cannot become a chemical reaction.

## Spinoza's Formulation

The pluripotency concept draws on Spinoza's challenge: "we do not know what a machine can do" (Spinoza 2002: 280–1, cited p. 46). The full range of a machine's powers is not deducible from inspection — it must be discovered through varying its relations and couplings. This makes the study of machines empirical and experimental, not a priori.

## Exaptation: The Evolutionary Parallel

*Exaptation* (Gould and Vrba's term, though Bryant does not explicitly use it in these chapters) is the evolutionary process by which a trait that evolved for one function is co-opted for a different function. Bird feathers, which may have evolved for thermoregulation, were exapted for flight. The eye's lens, originally a heat-shock protein, was exapted for vision.

Pluripotency is the synchronic, ontological version of exaptation: a machine's powers are not exhausted by the function for which it was designed or originally deployed. The steam engine was designed as a pump for coal mines; it was exapted into the general-purpose power source of industrial capitalism. Writing was designed as an administrative accounting tool; it was exapted for literature, law, theology, and philosophy.

In Bryant's terms: the *virtual proper being* of a machine always exceeds any particular *local manifestation*. The machine is always more than what it currently does. This excess — the surplus of powers over any particular exercise — is the ontological basis for exaptation.

## Political and Design Implications

1. **Against design intent as definition**: a machine should not be understood through what it was built for. Its operational profile — what it actually can do — is the measure of what it is.

2. **Technology and teleology**: Kevin Kelly's *What Technology Wants* (cited in Ch 3) argues that technologies have "tendencies" irreducible to the purposes of their designers (p. 68–69). The steam engine's exigencies (labor-intensive, favoring large-scale production) shaped industrial capitalism regardless of anyone's intentions. This is pluripotency operating at the civilizational level.

3. **Alien phenomenology** demands that we ask what a machine *can do* from its own operational perspective, not just what humans use it for. The corporation is not just a legal instrument for shareholders; it is a machine with its own structural openness and operational closure that may systematically block certain inputs (moral appeals) while being highly sensitive to others (profit signals).
