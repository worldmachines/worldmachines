# Staged Learning

## Core Idea

From [[Divine Documentation]]: learning is not just about selecting good resources but about sequencing them correctly. Reading an advanced text before you have the prerequisites doesn't just waste time — it can actively discourage you by demonstrating the gap without giving you the tools to close it.

The author's reading sequence for prose writing:
1. *Writing to Learn* ([[William Zinsser]]) — *why* to write
2. *On Writing Well* ([[William Zinsser]]) — deeper craft
3. [[Strunk and White]] — the *how* (mechanics)
4. *Artful Sentences* ([[Virginia Tufte]]) — sentence mechanics (deferred to anti-library)

## The Anti-Library as a Stage

[[Virginia Tufte]]'s *Artful Sentences* is placed in the **anti-library** — books owned but not yet readable. The anti-library is not a failure; it is a staging area. A book in the anti-library is waiting for the reader to become the reader who can use it.

This is [[Nassim Taleb]]'s concept: the books you haven't read are more important than the books you have, because they mark the boundary of what you don't yet know.

## Documentation as a Learning Stage

The [[Documentation as Empathy]] concept applies here too: good documentation enables staged learning by meeting readers where they are. Bad documentation assumes prerequisites the reader doesn't have — it fails at stage-awareness.

## Connection to [[Lexicographic Richness]]

The dictionary sequence matters too: before reaching for [[1913 Webster's Dictionary]], the writer needs to be at the stage where they're looking for *words they don't know they want* rather than disambiguation. Stage-awareness determines which tool is useful.
