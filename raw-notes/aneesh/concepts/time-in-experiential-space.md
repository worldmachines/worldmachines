# Time in Experiential Space

**Primary source**: Yi-Fu Tuan, *Space and Place* (1977/2002), Chapter 9, pp. 118–135
**See also**: [[tuan-2002-space-and-place-ch7-9]], [[mythical-space]], [[pilgrimage-and-journey-as-place-practice]], [[cosmogonic-architecture]]

---

## The Fundamental Entanglement

Space and time are not separable dimensions of human experience; they are aspects of a single experiential orientation that is artificially divided in analytical thought. The body itself is the locus of their fusion:

> "The experience of space and time is largely subconscious. We have a sense of space because we can move and of time because, as biological beings, we undergo recurrent phases of tension and ease. The movement that gives us a sense of space is itself the resolution of tension." (p. 118)

Language reveals this fusion: architectural space is "frozen music" — spatialized time. The passage of time is described as "length." Time is "volume" — to have a "big time" is psychologically more accurate than to have an "exciting" time (p. 118). The body's rhythms of effort and ease, tension and relaxation, are the primordial clock and the primordial measure of distance simultaneously.

## Environment and Spatio-Temporal Consciousness

How an environment is structured determines how richly or shallowly its inhabitants elaborate space and time together. Tuan gives two contrasting cases:

### Congo Pygmies (Forest)
The dense forest occludes the horizon, conceals landmarks, makes the sky rarely visible. Without landmarks to translate apparent size into distance, the Pygmy "tends to see a distant buffalo as a very small animal" (p. 119) — distance, which implies time, is not a developed spatial concept. Space is "a dense net of places with no overall structure" (p. 119–120). Correlatively, time is shallow: "neither the genealogical past nor the future holds much interest" (p. 120).

### Hopi Indians (Semi-Arid Plateau)
The Hopi live on a plateau of panoramic views and sharply etched landmarks — the antithesis of the forest. They elaborate a complex dual cosmology:
- **Manifested (objective) reality**: historical, physical, accessible to the senses — the horizontal plane oriented to the four cardinal directions
- **Manifesting (subjective) reality**: the mental, the timeless, the anticipated — represented by the vertical axis pointing to zenith and underworld

Distance in Hopi thought shades from objective into subjective as it increases. The remote spatial horizon is the temporal mythic past: "the greater the distance the greater the lapse of time" (p. 121). This is not an error in spatial cognition; it is a cosmological structure in which far space and deep time are co-constituted.

## Remote Space as Ancient Time: A Universal Structure

The equivalence of spatial remoteness and temporal antiquity is nearly universal:

> "'Long ago and far away' are the opening words of many legends and fairy tales. Associating a remote place with a remote past is a way of thinking that the Hopi share with other peoples." (p. 122)

Taoist timeless paradises are "located myriads of miles from any known human settlement" (p. 122). European Isles of the Blest and Utopias are in remote, inaccessible places. Modern vacationers seeking to "go as far away as possible" replicate this structure: remote resorts are "removed from the burdens of time" and lie "at the borderland between objective and subjective realms" (p. 122).

The explorer's journey into Africa or Asia reads as an odyssey into the past rather than the future — because the popular and scientific imagination had characterized these continents as ancient, pre-adamite, a museum of dead cultures. Spatial penetration becomes temporal regression.

## The Time-Structure of Purposive Movement

Ordinary intentional action creates a spatio-temporal structure: "here is now, there is then" (p. 129). This is not a metaphor but an experiential fact.

- **Going to work** is forward movement through space and forward time
- **Returning home** traces steps backward in space but returns to the familiar — "home provides an image of the past. Moreover in an ideal sense home lies at the center of one's life, and center connotes origin and beginning" (p. 128)
- **Plans** project spatial goals into temporal futures — to envisage a destination is to create historical time
- **Habit** dissolves the intentional structure, collapsing space-time into unconscious routine
- **Music and dance** actively cancel directional time and oriented space, allowing inhabitation of what Erwin Straus calls "presentic" unoriented space (p. 129)

Distance is routinely measured in time precisely because time-units convey effort: "village B is two 'sleeps' away" tells you how much energy is needed, not merely how many meters separate you (p. 129–130).

## Space Historical and Ahistorical

> "Space is historical if it has direction or a privileged perspective. Maps are ahistorical, landscape paintings are historical." (p. 122)

The map is God's view — sightlines parallel, extending to infinity, no privileged viewpoint. The landscape painting converges on a focal point and organizes space as a "happening in time — that is, an irreversible sequence of events" (p. 123). This distinction tracks the difference between abstract geometric space and experiential space: the latter is always already oriented, directed, and temporally charged.

The landscape picture, with its perspectival vanishing point, teaches viewers to see "time 'flowing' through space" (p. 124). The vanishing road ahead is the future; a backward glance at the horizon is the past. Photography has extended and popularized this temporalized spatial vision.

## Three Kinds of Mythic Time and Their Spatial Forms

> "Mythic time is of three principal kinds: cosmogonic, astronomic, and human. Cosmogonic time is the story of origins, including the creation of the universe. Human time is the course of human life. Both are linear and one-directional. Astronomic time is experienced as the sun's daily round and the parade of seasons; its nature is repetition." (p. 131)

Each kind of time generates a different spatial form:

| Time type | Character | Spatial correlate |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| Cosmogonic | Linear, one-directional | Asymmetrical, migration-marked space (Pueblo myth of emergence from Shipap) |
| Astronomic | Cyclical, repetitive | Symmetrical sacred space (Ming T'ang — circular, cardinally oriented) |
| Human | Linear, future-biased | Asymmetrical "City of Man" — cosmically ordered in design but distorted by the privileged southward axis from palace to meridian gate |

The key synthesis: "the traditional city, though strongly influenced by cosmic ideas in basic design, is nonetheless... the City of Man. It mirrors the asymmetry of human time and life" (p. 135). Lived time distorts the symmetries of cosmic time — the future pulls harder than the past; the City of Man cannot sustain the perfect symmetry of the sacred cosmos.
