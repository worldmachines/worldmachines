# The English Garden as Epistemic Object

## The Standard Account (and Its Problems)

Conventional wisdom maps the 18th-century shift from French to English garden design onto a political shift from despotism to democracy: the *jardin français* celebrates the omnipotence of reason; the English garden celebrates the freedom of nature. For Shaftesbury, the *jardin français* was "the invention of a princely caprice, while a princely slavery and dependence keep it alive."

But this clean binary immediately complicates itself. The "purported subjugation of nature" in the French garden can also be seen as "an aesthetically pleasing enhancement of nature." And the "purported naturalness" of the English garden is likened to "the horticultural enactment of a painting" — produced through the "Claude glass," a handheld mirror named after the landscape painter Claude Lorrain.

## The Paradox

Both garden types use artifice. Both create semblance rather than reality.

- **French garden**: its openness fosters sociability; celebrated for *eurhythmy* (graceful proportion of the body); straight lines → transparency → transcendence
- **English garden**: serpentine paths force the body into twists and turns; varied and limited perspectives → confusion, obstruction, regression. Goethe calls it "naturspäßig" (as if nature were playing a practical joke); Hegel says it precludes "eine in sich seiende Seele" (peace of mind)

Herder's dictum: "in the English garden, nature and culture act as if each were the other" — pointing to the *paradox* that lies at the heart of Kulturkritik.

## The Illusion Admitted

In [[Jean-Jacques Rousseau]]'s *Julie*, the Elysium garden (Clarens) appears to St. Preux as "verdant, fresh, vigorous" with "nowhere the slightest trace of cultivation." In fact it is "an abandoned orchard turned wilderness, a French garden metamorphosed into an English garden." Julie admits: "Nature did it all, but under my direction, and there is nothing here that I have not designed" (*CW6*, p. 388). She confesses she has done "violence" to nature, "forcing her in a way to come and live with" her. "All this cannot be done without a modicum of illusion" (*CW6*, p. 394).

If Julie can create only the illusion of natural wilderness, then Rousseau's "solutions" can create only the illusion of human wholeness — "and thus only the illusion of human freedom" (David Gauthier).

## The Experiences Compared

| | French Garden | English Garden |
|--|---------------|---------------|
| **Movement** | Eurhythmic, graceful | Forced twists and turns |
| **Perception** | Open vistas, transparency | Limited perspectives, obstruction |
| **Social effect** | Sociability (Goethe, Hegel) | Hampers conviviality |
| **Epistemology** | Omnipotence of reason | Appearance of freedom of nature |
| **Reality** | Artifice admitted | Artifice concealed |
| **Political metaphor** | Ancien régime, despotism | Modernity, democracy |

## Goethe's Rejection

[[Johann Wolfgang von Goethe]] makes the sharpest critique. The English garden:
1. Destroys both art and nature — "makes do with semblance because it turns reality into fantasia"
2. "Diminishes the sublime of nature and nullifies it by simulating it"
3. Is susceptible to "the whims of subjectivity and unrestraint"
4. Its "beautiful semblance is a shiny surface, a *laterna magica*-type illusion, a delusion without essence"

In *Elective Affinities*, Goethe tests the English garden ideal to destruction: the novel's main English garden project coincides with the deaths of all major characters.

## [[Friedrich Schiller]]'s Intermediate Position

Schiller endorses a "middle way between the rigidity of the French style and the anarchy of the so-called English garden" — specifically the Hohenheim Park near Stuttgart. But he is "disregarding actuality for the sake of appearance and effect" — his horticultural ideal is also a postulate, not a description.

## The Meta-Point

As Kempf concludes: "In the sublative English garden, with its inherent, mutually enriching *and* beautiful reciprocity of 'nature' and 'culture,' 'nature' is, in the last analysis, violated, while 'culture' reigns supreme." Both gardens "use artifice, and both are united in the creation of an illusion, a beautiful semblance that masks the instrumentalization of nature *and* culture for the sake of the beautiful effect." We seem to have come full circle — back to the French garden.

## Extended Resonances

- [[Florian Lohse]]'s [[The Thicket]] (as the *opposite* of the English garden): the thicket is not a garden at all — it's explicitly illegible, unkempt, not designed to please or to simulate naturalness. Where the English garden produces the illusion of wilderness, the thicket *is* a wilderness that refuses to be aestheticized
- The English garden as a precursor to theme parks and "hyper-real" theme parks (Kempf suggests Frank Lloyd Wright's Fallingwater or the hyper-real theme park as the English garden's post-horticultural successors)
