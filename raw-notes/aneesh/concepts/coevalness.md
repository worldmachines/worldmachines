# Coevalness

A term Massey adopts from [[johannes-fabian]]'s *Time and the Other* (1983) and makes central to the political argument of *For Space* (2005), Part Three.

## Definition

Coevalness names the **radical contemporaneity** of the world's societies: the fact that they all share *this* time, alongside one another, each with its own trajectory, none assignable to an "earlier" or "later" stage. It is not the trivial fact of clock-simultaneity (everyone is in 2005); it is the **political-ontological** recognition that no society is the past or future of any other.

> "Coevalness aims at recognizing cotemporality as the condition for truly dialectical confrontation." (Fabian 1983: 154, quoted Massey 2005: 69)

> "What are opposed . . . are not the same societies at different stages of development, but different societies facing each other at the same Time." (Fabian 1983: 155, quoted 69)

## What it refuses

Coevalness refuses the **denial of coevalness** — the operation Massey diagnoses in [[spatialising-modernity]] and [[cosmopolitan-time-queue]]. In its anthropological form: the move by which "those who are observed" are placed in a different time from the observer. In its globalisation form: the move by which the "developing" countries are placed in the West's past.

> "The absence of the Other from our Time has been his mode of presence in our discourse — as an object and victim. That is what needs to be overcome; more ethnography of Time will not change the situation." (Fabian 1983: 154, quoted 70)

## What it is not

Coevalness is not:

- **Bland relativism.** Massey is explicit that the concepts of "progress" and "development" can still be used. What's wrong is the *singularity* and the *undemocratic determination* of those concepts.
- **Romanticised radical difference.** It does not require that the Other be exotic, untouched, or wholly other. Coevalness recognises mutual implication — we are entangled with each other in this time, not standing apart.
- **An end to politics.** "Coevalness concerns a stance of recognition and respect in situations of mutual implication" (Fabian 1983, paraphrased 69). It is the *condition* for politics, not its conclusion.

## Why it is a project

Fabian (and Massey after him) call coevalness "a project" rather than a fact:

> "The radical contemporaneity of mankind is a project." (Fabian 1983: xi, quoted 69)

A project because the denial of coevalness is the default reflex of inherited "political cosmologies" (Fabian's term). To recognise coevalness is to actively undo the queue — politically, intellectually, conversationally, methodologically.

## How Massey extends Fabian

Fabian's argument is primarily about anthropology. Massey extends it to:
- The discourse of **modernity** more broadly (Ch. 6).
- The neoliberal discourse of **globalisation** (Ch. 8).
- The general conceptualisation of **space** (the whole book).

The extension is: coevalness is what space itself, properly understood, **already is**. Recognising coevalness is recognising what is in fact the case — the simultaneity of stories-so-far.
