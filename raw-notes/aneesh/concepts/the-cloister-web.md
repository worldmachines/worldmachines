# The Cloister Web

## Definition

Coined by [[Aneesh Sathe]] in [[The Tortured Artist Is So Yesterday]]:

> "In what I've called the 'Cloister Web,' people draft and explore ideas in semi-private creative rooms before carrying only a few into the open. LLMs make experimentation cheap; they also make commitment expensive. The hard part now is choosing which idea you are willing to be accountable for."

The Cloister Web is:
- **Semi-private**: not fully public, not fully closed. A room you can invite people into.
- **Generative**: ideas compound through conversation and collaboration before being tested publicly
- **Pre-commitment space**: the place where you work out *which* ideas are worth the risk of public commitment

## Why Now

Two pressures converge to make the Cloister Web the emergent form of creative practice:

1. **LLMs make experimentation cheap** — generating variations, testing arguments, exploring implications is near-zero cost. This devalues the solitary labor of draft-making.
2. **Public commitment becomes more expensive** — in a world of infinite plausible-sounding ideas, staking your identity on a specific claim is the genuinely scarce act. The Cloister Web is where you incubate before staking.

## Historical Analogue

The medieval cloister was a protected interior space for intellectual labor (copying, commentary, debate) that produced work occasionally released into the public sphere (the sermon, the illuminated manuscript). The Cloister Web recapitulates this structure: protected rooms, internal collaboration, selective public release.

Also resonant: Renaissance salons, Enlightenment correspondence networks, and the pre-print manuscript culture — all were "Cloister Web" structures, in the sense that most intellectual work happened in semi-private circulation before becoming public artifact.

## Contrast with the Open Web

The Open Web (see [[The Open Web]], [[RSS as Protocol Theology]]) assumes that all publishing is public. The Cloister Web complicates this: not everything worth creating is worth broadcasting. The small god of the internet waits for you to carry something to the door; the Cloister Web is where you decide *what* to carry.

## Connections to Books as Dwellings

The [[Books as Dwellings]] essay describes books as rooms; the Cloister Web extends this to *groups of rooms* — a creative community that inhabits ideas together before deciding which ones to make public.
