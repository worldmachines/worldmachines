# Bryant's Six Object-Types (Gravitational Typology)

## Overview

In Chapter 7 of *Onto-Cartography* (2014), Levi Bryant develops a six-type taxonomy of machines based on their gravitational role in a given assemblage. The types are: **dark objects**, **bright objects**, **satellites**, **dim objects**, **black holes**, and **rogue objects**.

Two features of this taxonomy are fundamental:

1. **Relational, not intrinsic.** No machine is intrinsically any of these types. Which type a machine is depends entirely on its relations to other machines in a specific assemblage. "One and the same machine can be one type of object in one assemblage and another type of machine in another assemblage." (p. 198) A virus is a bright object in a person's body, a dim object in the broader social world.

2. **Transitory.** The classification can shift as relations change. A dim object can become a bright object; a satellite can escape its orbit. This is the political significance of the typology: it identifies transformation possibilities, not fixed essences.

## The Six Types

### Dark Objects

A dark object is a machine that either exercises no gravitational effects on the other machines in an assemblage, or whose powers are entirely dormant — generating no local manifestations. An absolute dark object would manifest to no entity anywhere in the pluriverse. Relative dark objects are dark within a particular assemblage while active elsewhere.

Examples: the Dead Sea Scrolls (hidden for centuries, dark to religious scholarship until discovered); ultraviolet light (dark to human visual perception); neutrinos (dark to most matter due to electrical neutrality). The concept connects to Graham Harman's Heideggerian "withdrawal" — every object has depths that exceed any particular local manifestation.

Dark objects remind onto-cartographers that their maps are necessarily incomplete — assemblages are haunted by machines that do not yet appear. The Lucretian *clinamen* (swerve) is the theoretical signal that dark-object eruptions are always possible.

### Bright Objects

A bright object **gravitationally overcodes** the movements, local manifestations, and becomings of other machines in its assemblage. It does not simply influence — it *captures* other machines in its orbit, making them satellites.

Primary examples: the Sun (organizes all planetary motion, all life, all energy ultimately derived from sunlight); oil (organizes transportation, agriculture, economy, geopolitics, climate); rice (organizes agrarian labor, body postures, social relations); the coal company (organizes workers through scrip into total material dependence).

Bright objects are like stars: they define the field of choices available to satellites, not by restricting choice to one option, but by pre-delineating the field within which choice operates. A child in a family can choose among foods — but the foods available are determined by the parental bright object's gravitational field.

Bright objects are not inherently oppressive. The Sun is a condition of possibility for all life. Unions and revolutionary parties can function as bright objects that open lines of flight from other bright objects. Evaluation is always case-by-case.

### Satellites

Machines captured in the orbit of a bright object — their movements and becomings substantially organized by the bright object's gravitational field. The child relative to parents; workers relative to company scrip; consumers relative to oil infrastructure. Satellites exist on a spectrum of capture: some can conceivably escape (at high cost); others are so thoroughly organized by the bright object that exit is practically foreclosed.

The key political question about any satellite relation: is there a viable alternative gravitational field? If not, the political task is to *build* one.

### Dim Objects

Machines that exist in an assemblage but exercise very little gravity of their own — what Rancière calls "the part of no part": "those who have 'no part in anything'" (Rancière 1999: 9, cited p. 205). They are present but nearly voiceless in the assemblage.

Examples from Bryant: slaves, the homeless, the disabled (prior to organizing), women prior to suffrage, illegal immigrants, the proletariat, non-dominant religious minorities. Importantly, Bryant refuses to restrict dim objects to humans: non-human entities (animals, plants, ecosystems, microbes) are also dim objects in most human social assemblages. The 2010 BP oil spill forced entire marine ecosystems into visible dim-object status.

Rancière's definition of **politics** (as distinct from "the police" — the dominant gravitational structure of an assemblage) is: the moment when dim objects rise from faintness to challenge the gravitational configuration in the name of "anyone whatsoever." This is the political act — the becoming-visible of the part of no part.

### Black Holes

An object whose gravitational field is so strong that nothing can escape it. Bryant uses this carefully as a metaphor for situations of near-total capture: terminal illnesses, consuming addictions, imprisonment without recourse, perhaps capitalism as a system. "As Žižek somewhere says, it is easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism." (p. 208) Bryant hopes black holes are rare.

### Rogue Objects

Machines unattached to any particular gravitational field that wander in and out of assemblages, reconfiguring gravitational relations between existing entities when they appear — like rogue planets wandering between solar systems. They arrive from elsewhere (another assemblage) or erupt from within a world as previously dark or dormant potential suddenly activated.

Two essential features: (1) they appear from nowhere — not predictable from the existing gravitational topology; (2) they *reconfigure* gravitational relations throughout the assemblage they enter.

Examples: the bubonic plague (arrived from Asia, restructured European civilization); Hurricane Katrina; the Occupy movement; rock and roll; the Internet. Bryant engages Badiou's theory of the **event** here — accepting the basic structure (events are ruptures that restructure everything) while refusing Badiou's restriction to the human domains of science, love, politics, and art. A new fashion and a scientific revolution are both rogue objects; the scientific revolution is more significant but not categorically different.

Rogue objects are neither inherently positive nor negative: the Internet has enabled human connection and also child exploitation; Occupy has opened discourse about inequality and also been absorbed into spectacle.

## The Typology as Analytical Tool

Bryant's six types provide the grammar for onto-cartographical political analysis (pp. 210–11):

1. Identify the **bright objects** in an assemblage — what structures the dominant gravitational field?
2. Analyze how bright objects influence satellites — what paths do they create, what becomings do they enable and foreclose?
3. Map **asymmetrical flows** — what is the dependency structure between bright objects and satellites?
4. Identify **dim objects** — what machines are present but voiceless?
5. Watch for **rogue objects** — what eruptions might reconfigure the field?
6. Identify **strategic intervention sites** — where can new gravity be built, which machines can be decoupled from existing orbits?

The aim: not just mapping but intervention — "producing more satisfying and just assemblages" (p. 197).
