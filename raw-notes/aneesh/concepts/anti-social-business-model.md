# Anti-Social Business Model

## Definition

An anti-social business model is one that:
1. Generates revenue by replacing communal or in-person experiences with digital substitutes
2. Is scalable — can serve more users without proportional increase in costs
3. Is therefore well-capitalized (attracts investment) and able to outcompete non-scalable social alternatives

The term is [[Alex Mayyasi]]'s, developed in [[Baumol Cost Disease and Toxic Solitude]].

## Examples

| Anti-social product | Social alternative it displaces |
|---|---|
| Netflix streaming | Cinema, theater, communal viewing |
| DoorDash food delivery | Sit-down restaurant dining |
| TikTok/Instagram | Local clubs, neighborhood socializing |
| AI companions | In-person friendship, support groups |
| Online gambling | Casino (social space), card nights |

## The Cost Disease Connection

Anti-social businesses thrive precisely because [[Baumol's Cost Disease]] makes their social alternatives progressively more expensive. The asymmetry is structural:

- Anti-social businesses are *in* the productive, scalable economy — they benefit from technology and can achieve efficiency gains
- Social businesses are *in* the labor-intensive, cost-diseased economy — their costs rise as wages rise, with no offsetting productivity gain

This means capital allocation systematically favors anti-social businesses. Venture capital funds the next food delivery app because it can scale exponentially; it does not fund the neighborhood restaurant because it cannot.

## The Facebook Evolution

Facebook's trajectory is the paradigm case. Starting as a social network (connecting specific people), it evolved into a media platform (algorithmically curated content from anywhere). This evolution was driven by cost disease logic: user-generated social content didn't scale efficiently. To achieve productivity gains, Facebook had to become more like broadcasting — and therefore more anti-social. The "social" in social media became vestigial.

## The Efficiency Trap

When social businesses survive, they often achieve efficiency by becoming anti-social:
- Fast-casual restaurants replace servers with kiosks, optimize seating for turnover rather than comfort
- Concert venues reduce staff, optimize ticketing for volume over experience
- Coffee shops replace baristas with self-service to reduce labor costs

Each efficiency gain is simultaneously a decrease in the social quality of the experience. The surviving social business is less social than the failing one it replaced.

## Policy Implications

If anti-social business models are structurally favored by cost disease, the remedy is structural. See [[Social Infrastructure]] for the policy framework and [[Baumol's Cost Disease]] for the economic rationale.
