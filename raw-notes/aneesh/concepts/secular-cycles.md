# Secular Cycles

Peter Turchin and Sergey Nefedov's *Secular Cycles* (2009) proposes that agrarian societies exhibit recurrent structural-demographic cycles driven by the interaction of demographic, economic, and political variables. The "secular" in the title refers to the long duration of these cycles (roughly 200–300 years), not to secularism.

## The Cycle

A typical secular cycle moves through four phases:

1. **Expansion** — population grows, land is abundant, wages are high, elites are few, the state is fiscally healthy
2. **Stagflation** — population approaches carrying capacity, real wages decline, elite competition intensifies, state revenues stagnate
3. **Crisis** — demographic pressure, elite overproduction, and fiscal collapse produce political instability, civil war, or state failure
4. **Depression/Intercycle** — population crashes, land becomes abundant again, the cycle resets

The key driver is the interaction between population dynamics and elite dynamics. As population grows, labor becomes cheap and land becomes expensive — transferring wealth to elites. But elite numbers also grow, producing "elite overproduction": too many aspirants for too few positions. This intensifies intra-elite competition, which destabilizes the state.

## Cliodynamics

Turchin's broader project, *cliodynamics*, applies mathematical modeling — differential equations, agent-based models, statistical analysis — to historical dynamics. The ambition is to make history a predictive science, or at least a retrodictive one: if you can model past cycles accurately, you can identify the variables that matter for the present.

## Seshat Global History Databank

The Seshat Databank, co-founded by Turchin, encodes quantitative variables (population, territory, military technology, ritual complexity, administrative hierarchy) across hundreds of polities spanning 10,000 years. It enables statistical testing of civilizational hypotheses that were previously argued purely on qualitative grounds — including the [[axial-age]] thesis (Mullins et al. 2018) and the relationship between moralizing gods and social complexity.

## Intellectual Lineage

Turchin draws on Jack Goldstone's demographic-structural theory of revolution and, further back, on Ibn Khaldun's 14th-century theory of *asabiyyah* (group solidarity) and dynastic cycles. Ibn Khaldun observed that nomadic groups with strong *asabiyyah* conquer settled civilizations, become urbanized and decadent, lose their solidarity, and are in turn conquered by new groups with stronger solidarity — a cycle of roughly three to four generations.
