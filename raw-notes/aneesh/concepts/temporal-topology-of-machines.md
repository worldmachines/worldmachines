# Temporal Topology of Machines

## Core Thesis

Just as [[onto-cartography]] rejects the notion of a single homogeneous Newtonian space, it rejects the Kantian notion of a single containing time. Time is plural, heterogeneous, and produced by machines — not a pre-given form of inner sense.

> "The thesis of onto-cartography is that time is the rate at which a machine can engage in operations." (Bryant, p. 158)

Every machine has its own temporal rhythm, and these rhythms are incommensurable. There is no master clock reconciling geological time, institutional time, media time, fly-perception time, and stellar time. Machines operate and unfold within their own temporal fields, and the interactions between machines with different temporalities are sites of friction, misregistration, and political struggle.

## Three Pluralizing Moves Against Kant

Bryant accepts Kant's insight that time is a form of inner sense — not something external — but makes three corrections:

1. **Pluralization**: every machine has its own form of temporality, not just rational subjects. Time is not the form of *our* inner sense but of *each* machine's inner sense.
2. **Non-totalization**: different machine-times do not add up to one time. There is no whole or totality of temporalities.
3. **External duration**: machines also have temporal *duration* in the outer world. Subatomic particles exist for fractions of seconds; galaxies for billions of years. Duration is as machine-specific as rhythm.

## Rate as the Basic Unit

Rate — cycles per second, operations per unit time — is the primitive category. Examples from Bryant:
- Humans perceive at approximately 60 Hz
- Houseflies perceive at ~200 Hz
- Honeybees at ~300 Hz (Mineault 2011)

The fly's faster perceptual rate means it registers human arm motions as information; the human cannot register the fly's movements as information at those speeds. What seems still to us is motion-information to the fly.

This is Bateson's information principle extended across machine temporalities: "information is a difference which makes a difference" (Bateson, p. 159). Information is not intrinsic to a flow — it depends on whether the flow triggers a new state in the receiving machine, which depends on whether the machine can register it at the relevant rate.

## Trivial vs. Non-Trivial Machines and Memory

Heinz von Foerster's distinction between **trivial machines** (fixed input → fixed output) and **non-trivial machines** (previous outputs modify the machine's structure, so the same input produces different output later) is central to temporal topology.

Non-trivial, mnemonic machines fold time topologically: the past does not simply recede but persists as memory-traces that can leap over intervening events to directly shape the present. Serres's **crumpled handkerchief**:

> "If you take a handkerchief and spread it out in order to iron it, you can see in it certain fixed distances and proximities... Then take the same handkerchief and crumple it, by putting it in your pocket. Two distant points suddenly are close, even superimposed." (Serres and Latour 1995: 60, quoted p. 162)

Freud's Rome-as-psychical-entity illustrates the same: if Rome preserved all its historical phases simultaneously, the Temple of Jupiter would coexist with the Coliseum, Nero's Golden House with the present city (Freud, p. 163). This is the temporal structure of memory: earlier events (E₁) can directly influence later events (E₄), leaping over the linear sequence.

## The Politics of Temporal Mismatch

When machines with radically different temporal rates are coupled, systematic misregistration occurs. Bryant's key examples:

**Climate change and governmental machines**: Climate change unfolds at geological rates — imperceptible year-to-year, requiring data accumulation across decades. US governmental machines operate on two-to-four-year electoral cycles. The news media machine selects only deviation from norm as information, favoring the immediately controversial over slow-moving processes. These three machines are temporally mismatched such that climate change *cannot appear as information* to governmental-media assemblages operating at their current rates.

**Braudel on grain temporalities**: Wheat (one harvest/year, disease-vulnerable) structures European societies around intense planting/harvest periods with long fallow intervals — requiring food preservation, enabling family farms, producing boom-bust rhythms tied to harvest. Rice (two-to-three harvests/year, labor-intensive) requires collective farming, generates priestly/noble classes managing reserves, and leaves farmers with little time for non-agricultural activities. The temporal rhythm of a grain *structures social organization*.

**Military command and communication speeds**: Armies organized around horseback-speed messaging respond to the enemy slowly, with orders proliferating through spatially disconnected troops at the horse's rate. Radio and satellite change the temporal field of warfare entirely — new forms of coordination and strike become possible.

**Bureaucracy and human time**: The rate at which a government institution can process information and engage in operations differs significantly from a person's rate. A person who loses a family member abroad lacks a passport; the ability to respond to grief is structured by the temporality of the State Department's paperwork processing (p. 161).

**GPS and relativistic time**: GPS satellites run faster than ground vehicles because they are further from Earth's mass (general relativity). This temporal differential accumulates ~10km error per day. The satellite and car are "present" to each other while literally existing in different temporal frames; clock-compensation is a constructed temporal path bridging them (p. 167–168).

## Temporal Mismatch and Historicism

Mnemonic machines can be "closer" to machines from the remote past than to their contemporaries. A revolutionary drawing on Roman Republican imagery is in tighter topological-temporal contact with Rome than with her present. A tenured philosopher still thinking in 1950s existentialist terms inhabits a different time than his physical contemporaries.

This creates difficulties for **historicism** — the assumption that cultural artifacts are best explained by their contemporary context. Onto-cartography requires mapping *which* past machines a given machine is coupled to, not assuming contemporaneity entails temporal proximity.

## Fatigue and the Energy of Operations

Operations always require energy. Organization never comes for free. **Fatigue** is thus a real ontological dimension of non-trivial machines:

> "Operations never come for free, but always require some sort of energy to take place. Order never comes for free, but always requires work and energy to be produced." (p. 170)

A machine saturated by the temporal demands of survival — work, childcare, debt, basic maintenance — has its remaining operational capacity occupied. Little energy remains for temporal operations of reflection, political organizing, or imagining alternative social relations. Bourdieu's *skholè* (leisure as condition of "pure thought") is the class privilege that emancipatory theorists forget they possess: their temporal freedom to theorize is structurally produced by others' temporal saturation.

> "If time is a central site of the political, then this is because time is the possibility of carrying out operations. A life that is saturated by the exigency of demands ... finds it tremendously difficult to explore the possibility and construction of other operations and social relations. It is a life trapped in a web of existing relations of power." (p. 174)

Creating time — opening time — is therefore a project central to any emancipatory politics. Latour's speed bump: non-human machines can redistribute temporal-energetic constraints as reliably as ideology.
