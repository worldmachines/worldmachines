# Eco-Politics of Machines

## Bryant's Core Political Argument

Standard Continental left politics has been dominated by two related failures: (1) it operates on the plane of expression — signs, ideologies, discourse, norms — treating these as the primary mechanisms of power; (2) it maintains the nature/culture distinction that renders material, non-human agencies invisible as political actors. The result is an emancipatory politics adequate to an older era of analysis but inadequate to the ecological emergency of the twenty-first century.

Bryant's eco-political program addresses both failures simultaneously. It is an attempt to produce a "post-humanist framework that is able to synthesize the findings of the linguistic turn, Marxist thought, Foucaultian thought, media theorists such as McLuhan, Kittler, and Ong, as well as the post-humanist thought of the ecologists, the new materialists, the actor-network theorists" (p. 286).

## The Nature/Culture Divide as Political Obstacle

The nature/culture distinction is not merely a philosophical error — it is a *political* one:

1. When social theory is restricted to the cultural/discursive, it cannot explain "why social formations take the form they do and why they persist as they do even in the face of compelling critiques" (p. 253). Focusing only on signifiers cannot account for the role of geography, disease, climate, technology, fossil fuels in shaping social assemblages.

2. The nature/culture divide encourages us to see non-human processes as external to politics — nature is "the place *to which* we go when hiking or fishing" (Timothy Morton, cited p. 255). This renders invisible how the Big Mac eating I do is implicated in Amazonian deforestation, fossil fuel logistics, and climate change.

Bryant follows Morton in rejecting nature as outside of culture, but refuses to simply abandon the concept. "What we need more than ever is social and political thought as a thought of nature" (p. 281).

## Thermopolitics and Thermoethics

Bryant's most radical political proposal: the development of a "thermopolitics" and "thermoethics" — a political thought oriented around work and energy (p. 281).

Energy — "whether or not it is available, whether or not one has it — is one of the primary mechanisms by which power (in the political sense) or gravity exercises itself within social assemblages" (p. 280). This is a systematic blindspot in the history of philosophy: "the concepts of work and energy are almost entirely absent from the history of philosophy. It's as if, for the philosopher, being consisted entirely of objects, concepts, and minds" (p. 280).

The political consequences are significant: people tolerate oppressive conditions not primarily because they are duped by ideology but because material energy dependencies leave them with no alternative. The company scrip system is the paradigm case — workers are not false-consciousness victims; they are genuinely trapped by energetic relations (p. 262). Material deconstruction of those energy dependencies does more than ideology critique.

## Ecological Collapse as Engineering Problem

Bryant frames the ecological crisis not as a crisis of meaning or value (though it is that too) but as an engineering problem: the machine ecologies humans have built are on a vector toward collapse. Climate change "threatens us with a danger unprecedented in human history" (p. 286). Addressing it requires:

- **Deconstruction**: severing relations of dependence on fossil fuels — not just ideologically but materially (dismantling energy dependencies, offering material alternatives)
- **Terraformation**: building new assemblages — new technologies, new agricultural practices, new forms of energy, new modes of transportation, new conceptions of the good life at the level of expressive machines (p. 278)

This reframes ecological politics from a culture war over values to a project of world-engineering. The question is not "what should we believe?" but "which machines need to be subtracted, which added, to produce a survivable world?"

## Human Emancipation vs. Ecological Politics

Bryant's critique of his own tradition: even Marx and Foucault — the exemplary cartographical politicians — remain too anthropocentric. Marx's ultimate aim is human emancipation; the broader ecology of the world requires political engagement that does not have human flourishing as the only or ultimate terminal value (p. 285). "While John Bellamy Foster has made a compelling case for a deep and rich ecological thought in Marx's work, this way of thinking has not been deeply represented in mainstream and dominant strains of Marxist thought" (p. 285).

The ecological crisis demands an emancipatory program that is also a program for *non-human* machines — ecosystems, organisms, climate systems — not merely for human subjects.

## Key Practical Examples

- **Rolling jubilee**: material deconstruction of debt's gravitational hold — severing the energetic dependency that indentured debtors to creditors (pp. 271–273)
- **LETS**: terraformation of an alternative economic ecology that sidesteps capitalist gravity through mutual credit (pp. 276–278)
- **Climate action**: requires both deconstruction of fossil fuel dependencies and terraformation of new energy assemblages (p. 278)
