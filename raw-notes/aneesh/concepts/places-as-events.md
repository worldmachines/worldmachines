# Places as Events

**Source**: [[doreen-massey]], *For Space*, Ch. 12 (2005: 130–145); also earlier in *Space, Place and Gender* (1994)

## The Core Claim

> "Places not as points or areas on maps, but as integrations of space and time; as spatio-temporal events." (Massey 2005: 130)

Place is an event in multiple senses:

1. **A coming-together of the previously unrelated** — a constellation of processes rather than a thing
2. **A conjuncture** — not a permanent state but a specific here-and-now where trajectories intersect
3. **Open and internally multiple** — "not capturable as a slice through time in the sense of an essential section. Not intrinsically coherent" (Massey 2005: 141)
4. **Necessitating response** — "politics is the (ever-contested) question of our being-together" (Donald 1999, cited Massey 2005: 142)

## What the Event of Place Is NOT

Massey arrives at this positive construction by contrast with three failures:

**A. Place as bounded territory**: the map's presentation of Samarkand as a point, the USA as a circumscribed area. On this view, place has an interior (the coherent local) and an exterior (the global that threatens it). Massey's critique: this makes local places appear as always the victims of globalisation, denies them agency, and makes a politics of "the defence of place" against the global structurally inevitable.

**B. Place as collage of historical periods**: the Berkhamsted Castle view — "eleventh-century castle abutting nineteenth-century railway station." This understands space as a collage of frozen instants from different times, a "theatre of memory." But both castle and railway station continue their histories as we pass through. "The depthless commodified present which Jameson so effectively points to precisely denies all this. But it does so not only, as is usually argued, by commodifying 'the past', but also by refusing to recognise the histories which are ongoing through the present" (Massey 2005: 119).

**C. Place as pre-given community**: the assumption that a place has a prior coherence and collective identity, to be defended against external disruption. This is politically dangerous because it naturalises existing power relations and forecloses the negotiation that throwntogetherness requires.

## The Geological Illustration

The most dramatic illustration is Skiddaw in the Lake District. The mountain appears timeless, immovable — a foundation for the appreciation of place. But geological reading reveals its rocks were deposited in the southern hemisphere 500 million years ago, have since migrated northward, been folded and eroded, and only emerged as something we might call a mountain 10 million years ago. Continental drift continues. The mountain is an event in deep time. "All the essences become events" (Latour 1993: 82, cited Massey 2005: 139).

This allows Massey to say: "Here is no more (and no less) than our encounter, and what is made of it. It is, irretrievably, here and now. It won't be the same 'here' when it is no longer now" (Massey 2005: 139).

## The Political Payload

Reconceptualising place as event puts a different set of political questions on the agenda:

- No assumption of pre-given community or collective identity
- The throwntogetherness of place demands negotiation
- Place "necessitate[s] invention; [it] pose[s] a challenge" (Massey 2005: 141)
- "A world which demands the ethics and the responsibility of facing up to the event; where the situation is unprecedented and the future is open" (Massey 2005: 141)

This is also the grounds for Massey's critique of "the defence of place" discourse: if London is an event — a temporary constellation of trajectories, many of which *produce* the global — then London cannot simply defend itself against globalisation. It is a node through which globalisation is constituted. The politics must be different.

## The Science Parks Case

The boxed section on science parks (Massey 2005: 143–145) shows how even highly controlled, planned places are events in this sense. A science park bundles together: the long history of the mind/body distinction in Western knowledge-production, the gendered exclusions embedded in desert monasticism and the medieval university, the contemporary class dynamics of the knowledge economy, and the economic calculations of regional development policy. These trajectories do not add up to a coherent "place" — they are woven together in ways that contain contradictions and remain open to contestation. "Ironically, these 'places of innovation' seem designed to limit their potential character as places as innovation. And yet of course, in the end, the potential event of place remains. The containment is impossible" (Massey 2005: 145).

## Comparison with Other Accounts

**Tuan's place**: place as pause in movement; the experiential accumulation of value through repeated dwelling. Phenomenologically grounded, human-centred, experiential. Tuan's place *can* become an event if the accumulation is sudden (a traumatic encounter that fixes a place in memory), but normally it is gradual. Massey's event-account is faster in one sense (the conjuncture is always now) but also extends to geological timescales.

**Bryant's topology**: places-as-events resonate strongly with Bryant's account of milieus as constituted by the intersecting paths of machines. Both accounts insist on the processual character of spatial configurations and both resist the container/territory model. Bryant's vocabulary is more formal (phase-space, topological distance); Massey's is more politically inflected (power-geometry, throwntogetherness).

**Heidegger's dwelling**: Heidegger locates place-making in building and dwelling — the house as the paradigmatic place. This is closer to Tuan than to Massey: Heideggerian place has a founding character, a rootedness. Massey's event-place is specifically anti-foundational.
