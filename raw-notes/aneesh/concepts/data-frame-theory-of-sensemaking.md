# Data-Frame Theory of Sensemaking

## Core Definition

> "Sensemaking is the process of fitting data into a frame and fitting a frame around the data." — Klein et al, 2007

**Sensemaking**: the deliberate effort to understand events.

**Frame**: an explanatory structure that defines entities by describing their relationship to other entities. Forms: stories, maps, scripts, plans, causal mental models.

**Data**: not passively received — *constructed* by the frame. Only elements that the active frame makes relevant count as data. This is the theory's sharpest departure from passive information-processing models.

## The Four Cycles

```
[Data] <---> [Frame]
     Basic Cycle

Frame committed, gaps remain → Elaboration Cycle
Anomalous data appears → Preservation OR Reframing
```

### 1. Basic Data-Frame Cycle
- Frames and data are mutually constitutive
- Initial frame constructed from ≤3–4 anchor data points
- Switching back and forth between data examination and frame construction
- Sensemaking stops when data and frame reach congruence

### 2. Elaboration Cycle
- Committed to a frame; actively seek data to fill gaps
- May take action to generate new data
- Key: *you cannot elaborate a frame you haven't committed to* — this is why "keep an open mind" strategies fail

### 3. Preservation Cycle
- Anomalous data encountered
- Practitioner dismisses it or reintegrates it as a gap rather than a threat
- Reinforces current frame
- Can be rational (anomaly is genuinely low-quality data) or pathological (see [[Frame Fixation]])

### 4. Reframing Cycle
- Too much incongruent data → "frame breaking"
- Option A: Construct alternative frame in parallel, compare (experts hold ≤2–3 frames simultaneously; more degrades performance)
- Option B: Drop current frame, restart from new anchors

## Key Properties

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Anchors per frame | ≤3–4 |
| Parallel frames (expert max) | 2–3 |
| Expert-novice difference | Not process, but mental model richness and frame repertoire |
| Dominant reasoning mode | [[Abductive Reasoning]] — inference to best explanation |
| Trigger for sensemaking | Surprise / violated expectancy |

## What the Theory Challenges

- **Confirmation bias as a "bias"**: In naturalistic settings, what looks like confirmation bias is rational frame-guided information seeking. See [[Heuristics and Biases vs. Naturalistic Decision Making]]
- **The data→insight pipeline**: Frame construction, not data volume, is the high-order bit for analysis quality
- **"Keep an open mind" advice**: Practitioners who use this strategy consistently underperform those who commit early to frames and monitor vigilantly for anomalies

## Anchor Lock-In Problem

A data point used as an anchor for one frame is cognitively difficult to redeploy as an anchor for a different frame. This is a structural source of [[Frame Fixation]]. Strategies to counter it:
- Medicine: "logical competitor set" — organizing competing diagnoses by shared symptom presentation
- Dynamic domains: build large fragment libraries so alternative frame construction is cheap

## Origins and Validation

Developed at the Klein Associates research group, funded initially by US Army Research Institute. Draws on naturalistic fieldwork across: military intelligence, NICU nurses, fireground commanders, weather forecasters. Positioned within the [[Naturalistic Decision Making]] tradition.

## Related Frameworks

- [[Karl Weick]]'s sensemaking — similar bidirectional process, but emphasizes retrospective sense-making, organizational context, and narrative identity
- [[Cynefin Framework]] — maps different decision contexts; the Data-Frame theory describes the cognitive process within complex/chaotic domains
- [[Cognitive Task Analysis]] — extracts the tacit frames and mental models that expert performance depends on
