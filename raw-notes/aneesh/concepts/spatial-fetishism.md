# Spatial Fetishism

[[doreen-massey]]'s critical term (Chapter 14 of *For Space*, 2005) for the political error of treating a **topographic category** — openness, closure, the local, the global, hybridity, rootedness — as bearing political value in itself.

## What the fetish does

The spatial fetishist treats the **shape** of a spatial arrangement as the carrier of its political meaning. "Openness is good." "Closure is bad." "The local is authentic." "Globalisation is liberating." Each of these is a topographic universal pressed into service as a political guide.

Massey argues this always fails. Each category cuts both ways:

- **Openness** can be the right of refugees to hospitality *or* the imposition of free trade that destroys local agriculture.
- **Closure** can be Fortress Europe's anti-migrant racism *or* the Deni Indians' demarcation of their land against multinational logging.
- **The local** can be the cradle of authentic community *or* the seat of nationalist exclusion.
- **The global** can be the dimension of solidarity *or* the rhetorical instrument of neoliberal inevitability.

> "The issue is not bounded or unbounded in itself; not a simple opposition between spatial openness and spatial closure. Not spatial fetishism." (Massey 2005: 165)

## How the fetish operates politically

In Massey's diagnosis, the most powerful political actors are usually skilled at **switching** between contradictory spatial fetishes as convenient. The right argues for the free movement of capital (open) and against the free movement of labour (closed). The left sometimes argues against globalisation (closed) and for migration (open). The shift is rhetorically managed; the contradiction is invisible to its operators.

> "The shifting between contradictory geographical imaginations, all of them less stable than they are claimed to be, can be . . . significant manoeuvre." (175)

The instability of meaning is itself the political resource — meaning slips, contradictions are unnoticed, the fetish does its work.

## How to avoid it

Massey's prescription, developed across Ch. 14:

1. **Refuse universal topographies.** "There is no universal politics of topographic categories." (Laclau & Mouffe 2001: 180, quoted 165)
2. **Examine [[power-geometry]].** What are the actual asymmetries; who is positioned how; what is the cost of each arrangement.
3. **Specify.** "We come to each place with the necessity, the responsibility, to examine anew and to invent." (169)
4. **Hold the [[no-rules-of-space-and-place]] thesis.** The political answer is not deducible from the spatial form.

## What this is not

Spatial fetishism is **not** the claim that space is irrelevant to politics. Space is *constitutive* of politics (see [[throwntogetherness]], [[the-politics-of-the-event-of-place]]). What Massey rejects is the deductive shortcut: from a spatial form (open or closed; local or global) to a political verdict. The form does not yield the verdict; the verdict requires the assessment of the specific configuration of trajectories and power.
