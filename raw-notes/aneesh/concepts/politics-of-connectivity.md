# Politics of Connectivity

The outward-facing dimension of [[doreen-massey]]'s relational politics of place, developed in Chapter 15 of *For Space* (2005). Where the [[the-politics-of-the-event-of-place]] is the *inward* face — the negotiation of throwntogetherness within a place — the politics of connectivity is the *outward* face: how do local struggles, places, and identities connect to each other across distance?

## The two bad alternatives

Massey identifies two inadequate ways to think about linking local struggles:

### 1. Denigration of "the merely local"

The left's recurrent tendency to dismiss local struggles as parochial, insufficient, or NIMBY. The assumption: real politics happens at the global/universal scale; the local is a distraction.

### 2. Romanticisation of "the authentic local"

The mirror tendency: local struggles are *especially* valid because of their authenticity, rootedness, or unmediated lived experience. The assumption: the local is pure; building larger movements risks dilution.

Both views, Massey argues, "depend upon a notion of the local as effectively closed, self-constitutive" (Massey 2005: 181). Both treat the local as a given identity to be defended or surpassed.

## The relational alternative

Massey's third option: **the local is itself a relational achievement**. Every local struggle is already constituted through connections beyond the local; its identity is partly produced by what touches it. Even "militant particularisms" (David Featherstone 2001) are relationally produced.

If this is true, the political question is not "how to add up local struggles" but **how to build connections that recognise and transform the relationality already in place**.

## Constructed equivalences

Massey takes the term from Laclau and Mouffe:

> "Equivalence does not simply establish 'an alliance' between given interests, but modifies the very identity of the forces engaging in that alliance." (Laclau and Mouffe 2001: 184, quoted 182)

A constructed equivalence is not an alliance of pre-formed parties. It is a **practice** that:

- Identifies a common antagonist (the WTO, multinational capital, neoliberal globalisation).
- Builds shared ground while preserving the autonomy of constituent struggles.
- Transforms the identities of all parties through the process of connection.

The construction is itself political work. It does not happen automatically; it requires negotiation, communication, the building of trust across difference.

## Via Campesina as exemplar

Massey's running example is the international network of small-farmer movements coordinated through Via Campesina, which includes [[the Confederation paysanne]] in France (Bové, Dufour, et al.). The network spans Mali, Brazil, France, the US, India, and many other countries. The farmers' situations differ radically; their tactics differ; their cultural contexts differ. Yet they construct equivalence around the WTO, around the right to small-scale agriculture, around food sovereignty.

> "We didn't expect one side to convince the other . . . What holds for Santiago or Bamako doesn't necessarily hold for Rome or Paris. The exchange of opinions and experiences makes this a wonderful network for training and debate." (Bové and Dufour 2001: 158, quoted 183)

> "The strength of this global movement is precisely that it differs from place to place, while building confidence between people." (Bové and Dufour 2001: 168, quoted 183)

## Implications

A politics of connectivity reworks what it means to do "global politics":

- **Not a transcendence of the local** — global politics happens through the construction of connections among locals.
- **Not a flattening of differences** — connectivity requires the persistence of difference; what is constructed is *equivalence*, not identity.
- **Not a single antagonist** — the antagonist itself is constructed in the practice of connection.
- **Always provisional** — the equivalence is never settled; it is a continuing negotiation.
