# Internet of Beefs

The **Internet of Beefs** is [[venkatesh-rao]]'s 2020 framing of the social-media commons as a stable low-grade civil war. Rather than read online conflict as a pathology to be cured, Rao reads it as the *equilibrium operating mode* of the platform-era public sphere. The system is not malfunctioning when it produces beef; the production of beef is the function. Identity, tribal belonging, and status get transacted through performative conflict, and the platform metabolises the conflict into engagement, which is the product its business model actually sells.

The original essay (Ribbonfarm, January 2020) is now the canonical reference for the structural diagnosis of platform-era public discourse and the prototype for the AI-mediated successor described under [[intent-collisions]].

## Definition

A **beef** in Rao's sense is not a disagreement that seeks resolution. It is a sustained, ritualised conflict whose function is to produce and maintain the identities of the participants. Beefs are not won; they are *performed*. The IoB is the network of all such ongoing beefs — millions of them, mostly low-stakes, occasionally flaring into pile-ons — running continuously across Twitter, Facebook, Reddit, YouTube, TikTok, and their successors.

The IoB is **stable** in the same sense a chronic infection is stable: it does not resolve, and the host adapts to its presence rather than expelling it. It is **low-grade** in that no individual beef has the intensity of a real war, but the aggregate energy expenditure across the system is enormous. It is a **civil war** in that the parties share the substrate, the same platforms and norms and feeds, even as they treat each other as existential threats.

## The Knight-Mook Architecture

Rao's central structural claim is that the IoB has a two-tier participant economy:

- **Knights** are the visible combatants — pundits, micro-celebrities, blue-checks, posters with reach. They produce the headline beefs, the quotable burns, the screenshot-able takes. Knights perform.
- **Mooks** are the audience — the followers, the retweeters, the lurkers, the comment-section infantry. Mooks consume the performance and signal allegiance through engagement (likes, shares, dunks of their own).

The relationship is not hierarchical in the old patron-client sense. Knights need mooks for status and reach; mooks need knights for identity and entertainment. The mook's tribal belonging is constituted by *which knights they ride for*. The knight's status is constituted by the size and ferocity of the mook army that rallies behind them in any given beef.

Crucially, **knights perform for mooks, not against opposing knights**. The opposing knight is a prop in the performance, not the actual addressee. This is why beefs do not resolve: resolution would require the knights to be talking to each other, and they are not. They are talking past each other, to their respective audiences, who are watching a show.

## Performance vs Production

The IoB's defining feature, in Rao's reading, is that **performance is the product**. Nothing material is produced by the beef itself — no policy, no artefact, no settled question. What is produced is the *ongoing performance of identity*, which the platform converts into ad inventory, and which the participants convert into social capital within their tribe.

This is the diagnostic that distinguishes the IoB from earlier modes of intellectual or political conflict. Pre-platform polemic produced books, pamphlets, treatises — durable artefacts that persisted beyond the moment of conflict and could be re-read, cited, refuted in turn. The IoB produces tweets, threads, screenshots: ephemera designed for the timeline, optimised for the engagement spike, discarded once the algorithmic moment passes. The performance is the entire output.

## Stability of the Beef Regime

Rao's argument for the IoB's stability rests on three reinforcing dynamics:

1. **Identity dependency.** Participants cannot exit the beef without losing the identity the beef constitutes. A culture-war partisan whose tribe-membership depends on opposition to the other tribe cannot stop opposing without becoming someone else.
2. **Platform incentive alignment.** The platforms monetise engagement, and beef generates engagement reliably. Any platform feature that reduces beef reduces revenue.
3. **No exit cost for non-participants.** Mooks who disengage are not punished by anyone in particular; they simply drift, and the system absorbs their absence. So the system is not actively defended — it is self-sustaining because the active participants cannot leave and the passive ones do not care to.

This is why the IoB has not been broken by any individual platform reform, deplatforming campaign, or content-moderation regime. The substrate is bigger than any of its parts, and the underlying logic — performative conflict as identity-production — predates the internet by millennia. Twitter is not the cause; Twitter is one venue.

## AI as Industrial Successor

The Lightening of Intent (essay, 2026) reads the IoB as the **manual prototype** for the AI-mediated regime now arriving. Where the IoB produced ephemera through human labour, the AI-mediated successor produces *artefacts* — full-length books, papers, films, products — through machine labour acting on human intent. The underlying logic (intent-vs-intent, identity through opposition, no resolution sought) carries forward; the medium of expression industrialises.

Specifically:

- The IoB's performance becomes the AI era's **production**. Disagreement no longer expresses itself by yelling; it expresses itself by commissioning the counter-book, in five days, indistinguishable from a publisher's edition.
- The knight-mook architecture flattens. With AI agents, every mook can become a knight at minimal marginal cost. The asymmetry that made the IoB stable (few producers, many consumers) erodes.
- The beef no longer needs an audience to function. Two intents can collide in artefact-production without any third party watching, because the artefacts themselves enter the substrate and compete for downstream attention regardless of whether the originators ever interact.

This is what Rao's framing predicts and what the Lightening essay calls [[intent-collisions]]: the IoB scaled up, industrialised, and shifted from performance to production. The beef regime does not end; it gets faster, denser, and harder to ignore.
