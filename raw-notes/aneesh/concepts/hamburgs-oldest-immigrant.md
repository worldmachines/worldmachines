# Hamburg's Oldest Immigrant

The opening vignette of Part Five of [[doreen-massey]]'s *For Space* (2005). Like the [[the-cortes-vignette]] of Part One, this is a politically-loaded micro-narrative that anchors the conceptual work that follows.

## The vignette

In autumn 1999, workers labouring on the bed of the river Elbe near Hamburg came upon a massive boulder. It became newsworthy. Citizens visited it. The rock proved to be an **erratic** — pushed south by glaciers thousands of years ago and left as the ice retreated. By no means a "local" boulder.

Or is it? How long do you have to have been here to be local?

On 1 January 2000, German citizenship laws were relaxed. Ulla Neumann, Hamburg's commissioner for foreign immigrant affairs, seized on the immigrant boulder and the practices of city-residents visiting it. The poster (figure 13.1 in Massey's text), designed by Steffan Bohle, shows a gateway cut through the rock with the city visible behind it. Hamburg's established slogan was "Hamburg: gateway to the world." The new poster — gateway through immigrant — proposed: be open. Take the slogan at its word. Accept established immigrants as Hamburgers, "like the rock."

## What it does rhetorically

Massey reads the poster as the **political artefact corresponding to her ontology**. The poster:

1. **Reframes the local** as always-already mixed, including its geology. The presumption of indigeneity is broken by the rock's own immigrant status.
2. **Mobilises a place-making practice** (visits to the boulder) into a political moment.
3. **Acts on a "political cosmology"** in Fabian's sense — the imagined background of how time, space, and belonging fit together.
4. **Demands negotiation** rather than appeal to authority. The boulder doesn't prove the immigrants belong; it dislodges the certainty that anyone in particular belongs more.

## Why it matters for the argument

The vignette sets up the central question of Part Five:

> "Place as an ever-shifting constellation of trajectories poses the question of our [[throwntogetherness]]." (Massey 2005: 151)

The Hamburg rock is not unique — every place is a constellation of trajectories of different ages and origins. The poster makes a *particular* place's mixedness visible at a *particular* political moment. The general point: every place could, in principle, be read this way. The political work is making the reading *visible*.

## What the rock and the law-reform have in common

Both make the unchosen-coexistence of trajectories explicit. The rock didn't choose Hamburg; Hamburg didn't choose the rock; but the rock is here, the city is here, and they've found a way to share this place. The 2000 citizenship reform does the same conceptual work on a human scale: the immigrants who are here are here; the city is here; the political question is the terms of their being together, not whether they belong to a pre-given essence.

## A formal note

The vignette is also methodologically interesting. Like the [[the-cortes-vignette]], it is **non-academic** in form — concrete, particular, image-led. Massey opens both Parts One and Five with such a vignette, suggesting that the *form* of her political argument requires the singular case as much as the general thesis. The general thesis without the case is empty; the case without the thesis is mute. The vignette is the place where the two meet.
