# Forms of Sensemaking

Klein et al. (2007) identify **seven distinct forms of sensemaking** (Figure 6.1), each operating differently and requiring different support. The common error is treating all sensemaking as equivalent.

## The Seven Forms

### 1. Connecting Data and a Frame
The basic data-frame match. Data and frame mutually recognize each other. In many cases this happens preconsciously, through pattern matching and recognition — as in Level 1 of the [[Recognition-Primed Decision Model]] (Klein 1998). Not every successful sensemaking episode requires deliberate effort.

*Frame drives*: what data are sought; what counts as relevant
*Data drives*: which frame is activated; when anomalies are noticed

### 2. Elaborating the Frame
As more data arrive with no surprises, people extend the frame — adding more details, filling in slots (Minsky's "slot-filling"), inferring data that haven't yet been observed. The brigadier general in the reconnaissance example elaborates a tank company frame from one observed tank, populating expected positions for other elements.

Elaboration can shade into preservation (see below) when it becomes a mechanism for explaining away anomalies rather than deepening understanding.

### 3. Questioning the Frame
When data are inconsistent with the current frame — violated expectancies — a person begins to question the frame's accuracy. Lanir (1991) distinguishes:
- **Fundamental surprise**: realizing you may need to replace the frame entirely
- **Situational surprise**: only needing to adjust details within the frame

Emotional reactions (uncertainty, distress) are important initiators of frame questioning. Experts notice violations faster than novices because their frames generate more specific expectancies.

### 4. Preserving the Frame
When inconsistent data appear, the typical response is to explain them away — discarding them as noise, attributing them to instrument error, invoking special cases. This is often *adaptive*: not every anomaly should destabilize a frame.

Pathological preservation = [[Fixation Error]]. Vaughan's (1996) study of the Challenger launch decision shows how organizations routinize deviance — preserving frames by reclassifying alarming data as normal.

Feltovich, Coulson & Spiro (2001) catalog "knowledge shields" used in this mode:
- Arguing from authority
- Resorting to bad analogies
- Ignoring secondary effects
- Arguing special cases
- Arguing restricted applicability of a principle

### 5. Reframing
Replacing the current frame with a new one. Both processes must happen simultaneously: the replacement frame guides the search for new data, and new data suggest the replacement frame. One cannot simply abandon a frame without having an alternative — the interlude of framelessness is cognitively untenable.

Key feature: during reframing, data that were previously explained away or discarded become newly significant. Weick (1995): "during reframing people consider data elements that had previously been discarded, so that formerly discrepant cues now fit."

Duncker's (1945) radiation problem is a classic case: subjects who could reframe the problem ("minimize radiation intensity except at the tumor site") discovered solutions invisible to those holding the original frame.

### 6. Seeking a Frame
When no frame is available and the situation is genuinely novel, a person must construct one. Tactics include:
- Searching for anchors in the data stream
- Using analogies from other domains
- Running mental simulations to test candidate frames

This is the most cognitively demanding form. See [[Anchoring in Sensemaking]].

### 7. Comparing Multiple Frames
Deliberately tracking two or more competing frames simultaneously. Research suggests people can track up to 2–3 frames at once; beyond that, performance degrades.

Experts use "logical competitor sets" (LCS — Feltovich et al. 1984): interconnected memory units representing related diseases or scenarios that share symptoms. Activating one member of the LCS activates the full set, supporting systematic differential diagnosis. The strategy changes how information is sought: rather than confirming the most likely frame, the expert seeks data that will sharpen distinctions between competing frames.

## Failure Modes by Form

| Form | Characteristic Failure |
|------|----------------------|
| Data-frame match | Pattern matching on irrelevant features (novice) |
| Elaborating | Shading into preservation; never questioning |
| Questioning | Questioning without a replacement (framelessness) |
| Preserving | Knowledge shields preventing necessary reframing = Fixation |
| Reframing | Too rapid cycling = "diagnostic vagabonding" |
| Seeking | No anchors available; paralysis |
| Comparing | Cognitive overload; anchors lock into one frame |

## Training Implications

Different sensemaking forms require different training:
- Training for elaboration: expanding richness of frames in a domain
- Training for questioning: recognizing anomalies and violated expectancies
- Training for reframing: practicing transitions, not premature commitment prevention
- Training for comparison: developing logical competitor sets

Generic "open-minded" training that prevents early frame commitment is counterproductive — it removes the expectancy-generating function that makes anomalies detectable.
