# Operations, Not Substance

The core ontological shift in [[levi-bryant]]'s [[onto-cartography]]: instead of asking what a thing *is* — its essential properties, its predicates, the qualities inhering in a subject — we ask what it *does*. "The first question to ask of any machine is not 'what are its properties?', but rather 'what does it *do*?'" (*Onto-Cartography*, p. 38–39).

## The Shift

Classical ontology characterizes entities through substance and attribute: the tree has certain *invariant* properties (its essence) and *accidental* properties (changeable qualities). This leads us to think of objects as static lumps defined by what they *are*.

A machinic ontology reverses this. Confronted with a machine, "our first thought is not of its properties or qualities, so much as its *operations*" (p. 38). Operations are basic processes that take inputs and perform transformations, producing outputs. The tree is not a set of qualities but a system of operations — drawing water from soil, transforming carbon dioxide via photosynthesis, producing oxygen, cellulose, fruit.

"Far from being a static lump that just sits there, machines are processual through and through." (p. 38)

## Bogost's Definition

Bryant builds on Ian Bogost: "an *operation* is a basic process that takes one or more inputs and performs a transformation on it" (Bogost 2006: 7). Bryant adds that every operation also *produces an output*. Together: *a machine is a system of operations that perform transformations on inputs thereby producing outputs* (p. 38).

## The Lucretian Precedent

Lucretius offers an early machinic intuition: atoms have no color, but their arrangements *produce* color through operations. "Color is not a property that the water *has*, but is rather the result of operations taking place within the water through wave action and wind" (p. 43). Qualities are not substances; they are *activities* — manifestations of operations.

## Virtual Proper Being vs. Local Manifestation

The operations-not-substance move implies a split within any machine:
- **Virtual proper being**: the system of operations the machine is *capable of* (its powers)
- **Local manifestation**: the outputs it actually produces in a given context and coupling

This split explains why entities can possess powers without exercising them (a match in vacuum), and why the same machine produces different outputs in different environments (grapes producing wildly different wines depending on region).

## Spinoza's Challenge

Bryant invokes Spinoza: "we do not know what a machine can do" (Spinoza 2002: 280–1, p. 46). The powers of a machine are not knowable through a priori inspection of its properties; they are discovered only through acting on it and varying its couplings with other machines. This makes ontology fundamentally experimental.

## Synthesis Seeds

- Operations-not-substance directly parallels the "world-machines" framework: both refuse to define entities by fixed essences and insist that what matters is relational-operational profile.
- Contrast with Aristotelian hylomorphism, where form *is* the essential determination of matter. Bryant's move is anti-essentialist but not anti-realist.
- Tension with phenomenological traditions that emphasize *lived* qualities (Husserl's qualia, Merleau-Ponty's embodied perception) — Bryant would say qualities are real but they are *manifestations*, not intrinsic properties.
