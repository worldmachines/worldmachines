# Lexicographic Richness

## The Argument

From [[A Good Dictionary]], channeling [[John McPhee]] via [[James Somers]]:

> "Modern dictionaries have taken all the fun out and left all the crud in. The old way is the proper way to play with words."

Modern digital dictionaries (phone, computer) are optimized for one task: determining what a word means in a specific context. They are disambiguation tools. They are not **generative** — they do not open new possibilities or suggest directions you hadn't considered.

## What Rich Dictionaries Do

A rich dictionary (the [[1913 Webster's Dictionary]] being the canonical example):
- Provides literary, contextually-embedded definitions that make the word *live*
- Suggests synonyms and near-synonyms with nuanced distinctions
- Includes etymology that illuminates the word's conceptual history
- Functions as a *companion* to writing, not just a fact-checker

Per [[John McPhee]]'s account: using the dictionary well means *arriving at words you didn't know you were looking for*.

## The Documentation Parallel

This concept directly extends [[Documentation as Empathy]] and [[Divine Documentation]]: a good dictionary is also a form of documentation — one written with care for the future reader-writer, not just for disambiguation efficiency. The 1913 Webster's is "divine documentation" for language.

## The Anti-Modern Stance

There is a mild technoskeptic thread here: the digitization of reference tools has been a net loss for generative use, even as it improved accessibility and search. The best tool (1913 Webster's) requires deliberate installation and is 110 years old. The [[Small Web]] longing at the end of the essay is its cultural counterpart.
