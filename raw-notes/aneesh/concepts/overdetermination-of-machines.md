# Overdetermination of Machines

## Definition

Overdetermination, as Bryant deploys it in [[onto-cartography]], is the thesis that every local manifestation, movement, or becoming of a machine is the product of *multiple* causal agencies acting conjunctively — not one master cause or signifier. The logic is that of the "and": genetics *and* soil *and* weather *and* incorporeal farming practices *and* insect ecology together produce the qualities of a grape. No single agency overcodes or determines the rest.

Bryant is explicit that this is Freud's overdetermination from *The Interpretation of Dreams* — not Althusser's, and explicitly *opposed* to Deleuze and Guattari's "overcoding."

## Against Overcoding

Deleuze and Guattari's **overcoding** refers to phenomena territorialized on a *single* sovereign agency: God, Capital, a transcendent signifier. "All things flow to and from that agency." (Bryant, p. 175, summarizing Deleuze and Guattari 1983: 199). Overcoding is a unification around the One.

Overdetermination, by contrast, follows the logic of conjunction — the "and" — and has no center. There is not one machine that overcodes all others; there is a collaborative affair of many machines producing novel outcomes through their interactions.

## Freud's Model

Freud introduced overdetermination in his account of dreams (*Interpretation of Dreams*). The manifest dream — what we remember — expresses a *multiplicity* of conflicting desires simultaneously through condensation (many desires into one image) and displacement (desire transferred from its direct object onto a setting). Freud's patient who dreams of her sister's son's funeral: the dream simultaneously satisfies the wish to see a beloved man again, the wish to be punished for that desire, and the wish to receive his sympathy — all through one scene. No single wish determines the dream; all are at work together (pp. 175–177).

## Roy Bhaskar and Open Systems

Bhaskar's **generative mechanisms** provide the scientific realist version of overdetermination. Machines have powers or capacities that may be:
- **Dormant**: not yet triggered by appropriate inputs
- **Masked**: suppressed or disguised by interactions with other machines
- **Out of phase**: powers that exist but whose expression is blocked by the open-system context

In closed laboratory systems, single generative mechanisms can be isolated and their powers identified. But the natural and social world is composed of *open systems* in which many generative mechanisms act simultaneously. The local manifestation of any machine is thus a *conjunction* of its own powers with those of the many other machines it interacts with.

> "The task of the experimental scientist is to create a closed system where a particular generative mechanism is isolated from interaction with other generative mechanisms or machines so that the scientist might trigger the mechanism to discover what operations or powers belong uniquely to it." (Bhaskar 2008: 35, quoted at p. 178)

Oxygen and hydrogen are individually gaseous and highly combustible; bonded, they form water that extinguishes fires. Neither behavior predicts the other. Open-system conjunction produces novel effects neither machine would produce in isolation.

## Jane Bennett's Assemblage Topology

Bennett's assemblages illustrate the distributed nature of overdetermined agency:

> "Assemblages have uneven topographies, because some of the points at which the various affects and bodies cross paths are more heavily trafficked than others, and so power is not distributed equally across the surface. Assemblages are not governed by any central head: no one materiality or type of material has sufficient competence to determine consistently the trajectory or impact of the group." (Bennett 2010: 24, quoted at p. 180)

Emergent effects — blackouts, hurricanes, wars on terror — are distinct from the sum of their individual machine-contributions.

## Ethical Prescriptions

Overdetermination generates two ethical injunctions for onto-cartography:

**1. Never reduce a machine to its relations.** Every machine carries an excess — a virtual proper being — capable of breaking with its current relational circumstances. Treating machines as *nothing but* their interactions eliminates agency and makes change impossible. (p. 181)

> "Never reduce a machine to its interactions or relations to other machines, but always recognize that each machine carries an excess capable of breaking with its circumstances." (p. 181)

**2. Never ignore the role of relations in local manifestations.** Individualism — treating local manifestations as arising solely from a machine's intrinsic essence — is the ideology of reactionaries. It attributes to essence what is in fact a by-product of relational context. Lucretius: slavery is not an intrinsic property of persons but a by-product of how they are related to social systems. Relations are contingent and external; they can be severed. (p. 182)

> "All subsequent revolutionary thought has consisted in arguing [that relations are external], while all reactionary thought ... has argued for the internality of relations, for the relational or 'natural' being of machines." (p. 182)

**3. Be cautious.** Overdetermination means that even well-understood machines behave differently when introduced into new open-system conjunctions. Spinoza: "we never know what a body can do." Introducing a machine into a new assemblage may produce entirely unexpected local manifestations. (p. 183)

## The Traffic and Corn Examples

Traffic patterns are overdetermined by: incorporeal machines (traffic laws, signs), corporeal machines (road layout, vehicle states), driver dispositions, random events (tire blowouts, swerving). No single machine determines traffic.

The qualities of corn arise from: genetic code *and* soil conditions *and* weather *and* farming practices *and* insects *and* birds *and* mice *and* wavelengths of light. The corn's local manifestation is the result of its *trans*-corporeal encounters.
