# Panglossian Optimism

## Origin

Named for **Dr. Pangloss** in Voltaire's *Candide* (1759), the tutor who insists "this is the best of all possible worlds" through every catastrophe — earthquake, slavery, shipwreck, syphilis, auto-da-fé. Pangloss is Voltaire's parody of [[gottfried-wilhelm-leibniz]]'s theodicy in the *Theodicy* (1710), which argued that God, being omnipotent and good, must have created the best possible world, and thus all apparent evil is ultimately justified by its role in the larger design.

## The Philosophical Structure

Panglossian optimism has a specific logical structure:
1. **Teleological endpoint**: There is a destination toward which things are progressing (God's design; humanity reaching the stars; Moore's Law; the stars)
2. **Retroactive justification**: Any intermediate harm is justified by its role as a step toward the endpoint
3. **Scale shift**: The harm looks like a harm at one scale (individual suffering); at a larger scale (species, cosmos) it is a necessary contribution
4. **Immunity to falsification**: No harm can disconfirm the optimism, because all harms are reinterpreted as steps

## Gould & Lewontin's Critique (1979)

In "The Spandrels of San Marco and the Panglossian Paradigm: A Critique of the Adaptationist Programme" (1979), Stephen Jay Gould and Richard Lewontin identified **Panglossian thinking** as a structural fallacy in evolutionary biology: the tendency to assume every feature of an organism is optimally adapted for a function, rather than considering it as a byproduct, a structural constraint, or a historical accident.

The **spandrel** (the triangular space created by fitting arches into a rectangular frame in San Marco's basilica) is decorated beautifully — but it was not designed as a decorative element; it is a structural *spandrel* that was then given a decorative function. The adaptationist would ask "what is the spandrel for?" The proper question is "why does the spandrel exist?" — i.e., what structural constraint produced it.

Lohse's implicit extension in [[The Best of All Possible Worlds]]: Horto's techno-optimism ("child labor in chip supply chains is a stepping stone to humanity reaching the stars") has the same structure as adaptationist Panglossianism — assuming every feature of the historical trajectory is optimally justified by its eventual function.

## Contemporary Techno-Optimism

"Horto's" argument in Lohse's essay:
- Elon Musk, Trumpism, elderly people killed by Teslas, right-wing authoritarianism = "slightly darker patches on a poster from a Science Fiction movie"
- The movie is about "the great journey of mankind reaching for the stars"
- Moore's Law as the teleological spine: capabilities double every two years, therefore "any kind of remaining local suffering is equally halved every two years"
- "Who are we to stop humanity on this path?"

This is structurally identical to Pangloss: the zoom level is set to make any local harm disappear into the larger trajectory.

## The Zoom Problem

Lohse stages the refutation not as a logical argument but as a story: José Antunes's life has the *shape* of Panglossian fortune (disaster → disaster → disaster → modest stability in a Munich restaurant) but without the teleological grace. Things happened through contingency, violence, and luck — not because they were necessary for a larger plan. José's grandmother: "The complications of life are many, but greater is the power of constant misunderstandings."

The Gardener in Part III represents the anti-Panglossian alternative: not "every step is justified by the destination" but "I have no idea where this leads; I tend what is in front of me."

## The Voltaire-Leibniz Axis

Voltaire's *Candide* was the definitive literary attack on Panglossian optimism — specifically on Leibniz's theodicy. The final line, "*il faut cultiver notre jardin*" (we must cultivate our garden), is the direct counterpoint: instead of zoom-out speculation about optimal trajectories, zoom in to local, particular, tended things.

This puts Panglossian optimism in direct tension with:
- [[Kulturkritik]] (which at least acknowledges the wound, even if its cures are illusory)
- [[The Thicket]] (Sathe's concept of illegible local care as resistance)
- [[The English Garden as Epistemic Object]] (which tries to reconcile nature and culture but at least doesn't claim the reconciliation is globally optimal)
