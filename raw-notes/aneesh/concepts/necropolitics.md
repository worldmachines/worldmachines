# Necropolitics (Mbembe)

**Source**: Achille Mbembe, *Necropolitics* (2003; full book 2019); deployed in [[aneesh-sathe]], *We Need Homes in the Delta Quadrant* (2025), §III

## The Concept

Achille Mbembe's necropolitics extends and critiques Foucault's concept of biopolitics. Where Foucault analyzed power as the management of *life* — organizing populations, regulating their fertility, productivity, and health — Mbembe argues that an equally fundamental form of modern power is the determination of *death*: who may be let die, who must be killed, and who may be exposed to lethal conditions without political consequence.

Sathe's deployment:

> "Achille Mbembe's concept of *necropolitics* offers a stark rendering of this dynamic. For Mbembe, the most fundamental expression of power is the authority to determine who may live and who must die — not just biologically, but spatially. A person denied a stable place — be it in legal terms, economic structures, or cultural recognition — is exposed to systemic vulnerability. They are rendered invisible, disposable, or subject to unending surveillance. In this framework, place becomes not a refuge but a rationed privilege, administered according to hierarchies of race, class, and citizenship. To be placeless is to be exposed to risk without recourse." (§III)

## The Spatial Dimension

Mbembe's key move, for Sathe's purposes, is extending necropolitics beyond biological death to *spatial* death. This is not just about who gets killed but about who gets *a place*:

- **Legal placelessness**: statelessness, undocumented status — legal non-existence denies access to the protections that only legal place-membership confers
- **Economic placelessness**: neighborhoods disinvested by capital; workers without labor protections; people whose labor is invisible to the formal economy
- **Cultural placelessness**: communities whose practices, languages, and histories are not recognized — rendered illegible to the dominant order

In each case, the absence of place produces exposure: to violence, to disease, to exploitation, without the buffer that legal, economic, and cultural place normally provides. Place, in this reading, is not an amenity but a protective layer — its denial is a form of violence.

## Contrast with Harvey

Where Harvey analyzes placelessness through capital's production of uneven space — a structural economic process — Mbembe analyzes it through the explicit politics of racial and colonial power. Both identify place as a site of power contest; Mbembe identifies the extreme end of the spectrum where placelessness becomes a mechanism of organized exposure to death.

The two frameworks are complementary: Harvey explains the economic logic of uneven spatial production; Mbembe shows how that unevenness maps onto racial and colonial hierarchies and reaches its most severe form in the spatial stripping of populations rendered as threats or surplus.

## Contemporary Extensions

Mbembe's framework has been extended to:
- **The camp** (drawing on Agamben's *bare life*): refugee camps, detention centers — spaces of exception where legal protections do not apply
- **Gaza** and contemporary conflict zones: the logic of the siege as necropolitical spatial management
- **Algorithmic governance**: predictive policing and facial recognition as neo-necropolitical spatial surveillance — marking certain bodies in certain places as threat-candidates

## Relation to Bryant's Gravity

Bryant's [[bryant-gravity-as-power]] framework provides a material complement to Mbembe's political account. The necropolitical effect is not simply ideological — it is gravitationally sustained by infrastructure: who has access to hospitals, which neighborhoods have functioning food systems, where police presence concentrates, where legal aid is available. These are machinic gravitational facts, not merely representations. Bryant helps explain *how* necropolitical spatial exclusion is maintained materially even when it is not explicitly administered discursively.
