# Loose Ends and Missing Links

**Source**: [[doreen-massey]], *For Space*, Ch. 11 (2005: 107)

## The Phrase

> "If you really were to take a slice through time it would be full of holes, of disconnections, of tentative half-formed first encounters. [...] there are always connections yet to be made, juxtapositions yet to flower into interaction, or not, potential links which may never be established. Loose ends and ongoing stories." (Massey 2005: 107)

And:

> "Loose ends and ongoing stories are real challenges to cartography." (Massey 2005: 107)

## What It Means

The phrase is Massey's corrective to the holistic version of "everything is connected to everything else." That slogan is useful as a political reminder of unintended consequences, but it can mislead: it suggests a completed, already-constituted interconnectedness — a totality where all relations are in place.

Massey insists instead on the irreducibility of incompleteness. At any given moment:
- Some connections that could exist do not yet
- Some that could exist never will
- Some exist tentatively, not-yet-fully-formed
- Some have been severed

The spatial is not a completed system but an ongoing process of making and not-making connections. "Space can never be that completed simultaneity in which all interconnections have been established, in which everywhere is already (and at that moment unchangingly) linked to everywhere else" (Massey 2005: 107).

## The Cartographic Challenge

Maps aspire to represent space as a coherent, complete surface. Loose ends challenge this aspiration fundamentally. The Situationist cartographers attempted to represent the incoherences and fragmentations of urban space rather than its order — "a mimesis of incoherence" (Levin 1989, cited Massey 2005: 109). This is closer to what space is actually like. Even maps that do not pretend to coherence — deconstructive feminist and postcolonial remappings — still work within the surface-imagination, using the metaphor of the palimpsest (layers of suppressed pasts) rather than representing the *simultaneous* loose ends of a fully contemporaneous multiplicity.

## The Relation to the Chance of Space

Loose ends are the structural basis for **the chance of space** — the element of encounter, surprise, and the unexpected that Massey identifies as one of space's most important characteristics. It is precisely because connections are not fully established, because juxtapositions have not yet flowered into interaction, that space retains the capacity to surprise. De Certeau argued that spatialisation (representation) eliminates surprise by pre-coding all encounters; Massey argues that space itself — real space, not its representation — is constitutively open to the unexpected.

The "accidental neighbour" is the figure for this: you did not choose your neighbour. Space has brought together trajectories that might never have connected. This is neither randomness nor order, but the specific mix Massey associates with spatial configuration — "labyrinthine clarity" (van Eyck) or, more precisely, the intersection of multiple-and-structured trajectories that are each by no means chaotic but whose superimposition does produce the genuine indeterminacy of the chance encounter.

## Relation to Political Closure

"Loose ends and ongoing stories" are also a political concept. The aspiration to close off a place — to give it a finished, coherent identity — is a political act. Science parks (Massey 2005: 143–145) illustrate: they are designed to be controlled, disciplined places, to contain and direct their trajectory-character. But "The containment is impossible" (Massey 2005: 145). The loose ends of the trajectories composing the science park (class struggle, gender contestation, alternative knowledges on the shop floor) persist and will erupt.

More broadly: any politics that claims place has a settled identity requiring defence (defensive localism) is claiming that the loose ends have been tied up, that the story is complete. This is always false, and politically dangerous.

## Relation to Place-as-Event

Loose ends are what make place-as-event genuinely *open*. If all connections were already established, place would be a completed synchronic totality — the map's fiction. The event-character of place ([[places-as-events]]) depends on the constitutive incompleteness that loose ends name.
