# Noble Savage

## The Standard Misreading

The "noble savage" is commonly understood as Rousseau's idealized primitive — a claim that pre-civilized humans were happier, more virtuous, more whole. This reading makes Rousseau appear naively primitivist and his Kulturkritik a simple nature-vs-culture binary.

Per [[Franz R. Kempf]]'s analysis, this is wrong on several counts.

## What Rousseau Actually Meant

The *bête* (the solitary brute of the state of nature) is **an a priori construct**, never presented as historically real. Rousseau himself states in the preface to the *Discourse on the Origin of Inequality*: the brute "no longer exists, ... perhaps never existed [and] probably never will exist," but it is "nevertheless necessary to have precise Notions in order to judge of our present state correctly" (*CW3*, p. 13).

The *bête*'s function is entirely rhetorical: to create the extreme contrast needed for a comprehensive attack on the *bourgeois*.

## The Noble Savage as Nascent Society

The ideal Rousseau actually endorses is not the solitary *bête* but the inhabitant of **nascent society** — a middle condition "at equal distances from the stupidity of the brutes and the fatal enlightenment of Civil man" (*CW3*, pp. 48–49). This is described as "the happiest and most durable epoch."

Closest existing approximation: the Swiss peasant. In the *Second Discourse*, Rousseau calls this state the "nascent Society" and praises it as "free." In the *Reveries*, Switzerland is imagined as a "mixture of wild nature and human industry," with inhabitants "independent, self-sufficient, yet community minded."

## The Oxymoron Structure

"Noble savage" is an oxymoron — exactly the kind of contradictory articulation that Kulturkritik uses to capture the paradox of reconciling nature and culture. As Kempf notes, it is an "anthropological... manifestation of the paradox of Kulturkritik."

The noble savage is "noble" through natural virtue but "savage" through freedom from culture's refinements. The oxymoron holds the tension rather than resolving it.

## The "Imperial Eyes" Problem

Rousseau draws the frontispiece image for the *Discourse on Inequality* from a travel account describing a native who "returns to nature" after being raised as a European Christian. Kempf notes: "we feel leery about cultural critique's use, if not instrumentalization, of nature to unmask the depravity of culture" — the image views the "other" through "imperial eyes."

This problem is built into the concept from the start: the noble savage is always viewed from a European standpoint that turns non-European peoples into vehicles for European self-critique.

## Relation to the English Garden

The noble savage and the English gardener are, per Kempf, "oxymoronic articulations of a reconciliation of nature and culture that the eighteenth century celebrates and questions as a remedy for modern self-alienation." Both are "anthropological and horticultural manifestations of the paradox of Kulturkritik."

Both use artifice to simulate what they claim is natural. Both are ultimately illusions — but productive, regulative illusions.
