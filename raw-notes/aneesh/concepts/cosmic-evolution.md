# Cosmic Evolution

## Definition

Cosmic evolution is the study of "the many varied developmental and generational changes in the assembly and composition of radiation, matter, and life throughout all space and across all time." (Chaisson, 2009)

It is the broadest possible evolutionary framework — encompassing all change from the Big Bang to the present — and proposes that **energy** is the fundamental driver of complexity across all domains.

## The Unifying Claim

> "Evolution, broadly considered, has become a powerful unifying concept in all of science."

Biological evolution (neo-Darwinism) is just one subset of a broader evolutionary scheme. What Darwinian change does for plants and animals, cosmic evolution aspires to do for *all things*: galaxies, stars, planets, cells, organisms, societies, machines.

Key implication: evolution does not require genes, reproduction, or natural selection in the strict biological sense. It requires only:
1. Open systems with energy flowing through them
2. Variation and selection (broadly construed)
3. Time

## Seven Phases

Particulate → Galactic → Stellar → Planetary → Chemical → Biological → Cultural

All phases continue simultaneously — stars still form, galaxies still evolve, even as the Cultural phase dominates current attention.

## Heraclitus as Progenitor

The ancient claim that "everything flows and nothing stays" (πάντα ῥεῖ) anticipates the cosmic-evolutionary view. The universe is fundamentally processual, not substance-based.

Modern scientific backing: post-Renaissance observational data confirms change at every scale, from galaxies to snowflakes.

## Anti-Anthropocentrism

Cosmic evolution explicitly rejects the anthropocentric reading of the Arrow of Time:
- Humans are not the "culmination" of the cosmic-evolutionary scenario
- We are not necessarily the only technologically competent life to have emerged
- The arrow merely depicts a mixture of chance and necessity — it does not point *at us*

Yet the Life Era thesis implicitly re-centers intelligence: the transition to a life-dominated universe is cast as cosmologically significant precisely because technologically intelligent life is its agent.

## Relation to Big History

Big History (David Christian, Fred Spier) runs parallel to cosmic evolution. Chaisson credits them as using energy-flow diagnostics for historical analysis — but cosmic evolution goes further by explicitly grounding the framework in nonequilibrium thermodynamics and providing quantitative complexity metrics.
