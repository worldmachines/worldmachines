# Expertise

## The Two-Component Model

Sathe (see [[On Being Good vs. Knowing Good]], [[Dancing on the Shoulders of Giants]]) implicitly constructs a two-part model of expertise:

1. **Knowledge** — the structured accumulation of facts, frameworks, and relationships. Transmissible through text, education, and increasingly through knowledge graphs. Approximatable by LLMs.
2. **Taste** — the experiential capacity to make good judgments, to distinguish the right option, to know when the question is the wrong question. Develops through imperfect experience. Not yet approximatable by AI.

This split maps onto the [[Taste as Epistemic Capacity]] concept and drives the [[AI as Tool vs. Advisor]] distinction.

## The Network Structure of Knowledge

"Dancing on the Shoulders of Giants" adds structural depth: expertise is less about total knowledge held than about *how* knowledge is organized and *how flexibly* it can be traversed. The expert's advantage is a denser, better-organized knowledge network — which allows faster navigation and more efficient "muddling through" (see [[Muddling Through]]).

This is why [[Knowledge Graphs]] matter: they externalize the relational structure of expert knowledge into a traversable artifact. The gap between novice and expert is, at its core, a gap in knowledge network density and structure — and that gap is partially bridgeable if you can give novices access to a well-structured external network.

## The Acceleration Claim

"Expertise can be accelerated by giving people with base training access to new networks of knowledge." The [[Composite AI Architecture (Experts + LLM + KG)]] operationalizes this: KGs encode expert knowledge structure; LLMs make it accessible; domain experts provide taste to adjudicate outputs.

## The Data-Frame Theory Extension (Klein et al, 2007)

[[Gary Klein]]'s [[Data-Frame Theory of Sensemaking]] adds a third dimension to expertise not covered by the knowledge/taste binary: **frame repertoire and expectancy richness**.

Experts and novices use *identical* sensemaking processes (data-frame cycles, elaboration, preservation, reframing). What differs is the quality of the raw material:
- **Richer causal mental models**: experts know more about *why* things happen in their domain — more causal relationships
- **Larger frame repertoire**: experts have more ready-made explanatory structures plus more *fragments* from which to construct new ones
- **More specific expectancies**: because experts know more about what *should* happen, they detect violations (anomalies) faster
- **Functional vs. abstract orientation**: experts sensemake *for action*, not just for understanding

This suggests a third component of expertise beyond knowledge and taste: **the ability to rapidly construct, elaborate, and abandon explanatory frames** — a dynamic skill rather than a static accumulation.
