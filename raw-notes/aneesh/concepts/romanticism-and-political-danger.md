# Romanticism and Political Danger

## The Problem Berlin Identifies

The [[Counter-Enlightenment]] tradition produced two very different political offspring. One — exemplified by [[Johann Gottfried Herder]] — is a pluralist liberalism: different cultures should flourish side by side, none subordinating another. The other — exemplified by [[Joseph de Maistre]] and 19th-century nationalism — is authoritarian, aggressive, and eventually fascist.

Understanding *how* the same set of insights generated such different political conclusions is one of the central tasks of *Against the Current*.

## The Creative Self

The Romantic movement, drawing on Hamann's insistence that all truth is particular and on Kant's cult of moral autonomy, arrived at a doctrine of the creative self: values are not found but made, not discovered but created. "I create as I do, whether I am an artist, a philosopher, a statesman, not because the goal that I seek to realise is objectively beautiful, or true, or virtuous, or approved by public opinion, or demanded by majorities or tradition, but because it is my own."

This is in its first form — the form of Byron, Hugo, and the defiantly Romantic writers and painters — an assertion of radical individual self-expression. It is a declaration of war on the Enlightenment's pre-established rules, universal norms, and mimetic aesthetics.

## The Collectivization

The crucial dangerous move: the "creative self" is identified not with the individual mortal person but with a super-personal organism — nation, class, race, history itself. The individual is merely an element or member of this organism, a spark in the great central flame.

Once this move is made:
- Aggressive nationalism becomes self-realization of the nation's creative self
- Imperialism becomes the nation's organic growth and expansion
- Self-identification with class or race becomes a form of cosmic self-expression
- The wave of future-directed dynamism — "the forces of history" — becomes an entity whose demands override individual morality

"Aggressive nationalism, self-identification with the interests of the class, the culture or the race, or the forces of progress — with the wave of a future-directed dynamism of history, something that at once explains and justifies acts which might be abhorred or despised if committed from calculation of selfish advantage or some other mundane motive — this family of political and moral conceptions is so many expressions of a doctrine of self-realisation based on defiant rejection of the central theses of the Enlightenment."

## The Maistre Lineage

[[Joseph de Maistre]]'s organic nationalism feeds directly into this. Maistre's "nations are born and die like individuals; they have a common soul" combined with Romantic heroism and the distinction between "historic" and "unhistoric" peoples (those with a national spirit capable of self-realization and those without) "duly inspired nationalism, imperialism, and finally, in their most violent and pathological form, Fascist and totalitarian doctrines in the twentieth century" (p. 32).

## Berlin's Diagnosis of the Slide

The movement from Herder's peaceful cultural pluralism to Maistre-derived fascism is not accidental — it runs along a specific track:

1. Counter-Enlightenment insight: cultures are organic, irreducible, incommensurable
2. Romantic extension: the creative self generates its own ends
3. Collectivization: the nation/race as creative self
4. Political demand: the organic community must be defended against contamination
5. Violence: the defense of organic purity justifies extermination of the inorganic, the alien, the "unhistoric"

Each step follows from the previous if the initial premises are accepted without Berlin's own limiting clause — that value pluralism is consistent with a minimum floor of human rights that no organic community can override.

## What Berlin Does Not Explain

The slide from Herder to fascism is traced genealogically but the *logical* connection is left underspecified. Why did cultural pluralism produce nationalism rather than cosmopolitan respect? Berlin notes that "the seeds of nationalism are unmistakably present" in Herder but does not fully account for why the seeds germinated in the direction they did rather than in the direction of liberal multiculturalism.

Critics like Charles Taylor argue that this represents a failure in Berlin's own liberal framework — he cannot fully account for what legitimate claims of cultural community might be, given his individualist starting point.

## Contemporary Relevance

The Romantic self-creation doctrine has contemporary echoes in:
- **Populist nationalism**: the organic people as creative subject whose will overrides liberal institutional constraints
- **Platform tribalism**: the algorithmic amplification of identity-group self-assertion
- **AI-generated content**: the creative self as template for AI "personhood" claims
