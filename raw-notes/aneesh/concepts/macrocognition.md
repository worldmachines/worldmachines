# Macrocognition

## Definition

**Macrocognition** refers to the study and understanding of complex, high-level cognitive functions as they operate in real-world settings: sensemaking, planning, decision-making, coordination, problem detection. The term was formalized by Klein, Ross et al. (2003) in the context of naturalistic decision-making research.

Opposed to **microcognition**: the investigation of comprehension at the level of atomic mental processes (recognizing individual stimuli, processing single words, measuring millisecond reaction times).

> "All of these musings, theories, and research studies addressed psychological phenomena from a microcognitive perspective. That is, the goal has been to understand sequences of mental events down to a level of what are believed to be atomic processes and content, such as long-term memory in support of recognition of individual stimuli." (Klein et al., 2007, p. 113–114)

## The Microcognition Critique

Psychology's experimental tradition focused on simple stimuli precisely because they are tractable. But this creates a gap: the practitioner in a real-world domain — weather forecaster, military commander, ICU nurse — must understand complex, dynamic, evolving situations rich with multiple, interacting meanings.

Crucially: in naturalistic settings, there is nothing like an "endpoint" because understanding itself must be dynamic. The frozen, stored "meaning" of information-processing models is inadequate.

> "The domain practitioner's higher purpose is not to perceive stimuli, but is, simply put, *to make sense of things*." (Klein et al., 2007, p. 114)

## What Macrocognition Studies

Key functions identified by Klein et al. (2007):
1. **Problem detection** — identifying that a pattern requires attention
2. **Problem identification** — characterizing the nature of the problem
3. **Forming explanations** — diagnosing how a situation came to be
4. **Anticipatory thinking** — projecting future states
5. **Projecting future states** — scenario simulation
6. **Finding the levers** — identifying what can be done
7. **Seeing relationships** — using maps and models to understand position
8. **Enabling problem identification** — finding ways to represent a problem

## Sensemaking as the Core Macrocognitive Act

[[Karl Weick]] (1995) introduced sensemaking as one of the central cognitive functions that people must carry out in all kinds of natural settings. Klein et al. extend Weick's organizational sensemaking theory with a cognitive mechanism (the data-frame model), making it tractable for applied research, training design, and decision support systems.

Sensemaking is initiated by inadequacy — when the current understanding of events fails to account for what is being observed. It is often a response to surprise, but it can also be proactive (the brigadier general continuing to explore even after a satisfactory account exists).

## Macrocognition vs. Microcognition: A Research Gap

The research tradition Klein et al. address spans:
- NICU nurses (Crandall & Getchell-Reiter, 1993)
- Firefighters (Klein, Calderwood & Clinton-Cirocco, 1986)
- Weather forecasters (Pliske, Crandall & Klein)
- Navy AEGIS commanders (Kaempf, Klein, Thordsen & Wolf, 1996)
- Military intelligence analysts

In each domain, standard microcognitive models fail to capture what practitioners actually do. The practitioner is not assembling atomic inferences from clean data packets. They are constructing data and meaning simultaneously.

## Implications for System Design

A macrocognitive perspective reorients design priorities for decision support systems:
- The DIKW hierarchy (data → information → knowledge → wisdom) is misleading — it assumes data are pre-given, not constructed
- More data does not help past a threshold; better frames help
- Supporting abductive inference is more important than supporting logical inference
- Systems should support anomaly detection and frame transition, not just data presentation
