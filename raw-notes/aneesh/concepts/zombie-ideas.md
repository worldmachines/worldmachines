# Zombie Ideas

## Rao's Energetic Zombie

[[venkatesh-rao]] in [[The Divergence Machine]] (2026) describes **late modernity** as populated by **energetic zombies**: institutions, behaviors, and ideas that carry the zombie quality of the Korean horror film *Train to Busan* — energetic, mobile, consuming, but no longer carrying the "evolutionary intelligence" of the world's developmental direction.

> "Arguably, the bulk of the energy of the world will continue to flow through late-modern pathways for at least our lifetimes, even if very little of the evolutionary intelligence of the world flows through those pathways (hence 'zombie' or perhaps 'energetic zombie' would be a better term)."

Examples: Trumpism, reactionary nationalism, most institutional politics, mainstream media narratives, much of mainstream academic production. These are late-modern phenomena: they have the energy of the old machine, not the aliveness of the new one.

The zombie distinction: **energy** (mass behavior, resources, institutional weight) vs. **evolutionary intelligence** (the capacity to generate new solutions, genuine novelty, liveness).

## Lohse's Extension: Zombie Ideas in History

[[Florian Lohse]] in [[Shitty Castles]] (2026) extends this to historical case studies:

- **Leibniz's Monad theory**: built knowing it wouldn't hold against Spinoza's rising influence; a philosophical fortification that consumed enormous intellectual energy but couldn't generate the evolutionary intelligence needed for the new world
- **Château de Chambord**: a massive castle built after gunpowder had made castles militarily obsolete; enormous energy (220,000 tons of stone, 1,800 workers) without functional military intelligence
- **OpenAI's Jony Ive acquisition**: possibly zombie-idea-scale investment in a product category with no successful precedents

Lohse's key insight: "The Monads, Chambord Castle and maybe soon the weird video of Sam Altman and Jony Ive drinking coffee in San Francisco may be considered zombie ideas in their time, but sometimes it is hard to tell."

## The Diagnostic Challenge

The problem is telling zombie ideas from:
1. **Shitty castles** (intentionally flawed constructions that serve legitimate bridging purposes)
2. **Legitimate ongoing projects** that are still generating real value
3. **Early-stage divergent things** that look like zombies because they're not yet legible

Lohse proposes the **moat lifecycle** as a diagnostic tool: a 13th-century moat had honest functional value; a 16th-century moat had performative value; a 19th-century moat was purely ornamental; the Monty Python swamp castle's moat was satirized. The more functional flaws you can find, the more likely you're facing a zombie.

Rao's four-stage filter from [[divergence-machine]]:
1. Can the thing be traced back to genuine seeds ~1600?
2. Is it better understood as a zombie late-modern thing?
3. Is it better understood as an intelligent ghost of postmodernity?
4. Is it better understood as metamodern ennervated necromancy?

## The Zombie vs. Ghost vs. Necromancy Distinction

From Rao's taxonomy:
- **Energetic zombie** (late modernity): massive energy, little evolutionary intelligence
- **Intelligent ghost** (postmodernity): strong intellectual currents, little energy — still organizing attention and mapping debates long after peak
- **Ennervated necromancy** (metamodernity): neither energy nor intelligence, but the drive to resurrect dead patterns
