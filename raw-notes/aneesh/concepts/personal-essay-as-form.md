# Personal Essay as Form

## Origin

The personal essay was invented by [[Michel de Montaigne]] in the 1580s. The word "essay" comes from the French *essai* — an attempt, a trial. The form is structurally a *trying-out* of ideas rather than a demonstration of conclusions already reached.

## Defining Characteristics

1. **First-person voice**: the writer's consciousness is both subject and instrument.
2. **Meander as method**: associative rather than strictly logical progression. The essay follows attention.
3. **Provisional thinking**: conclusions are held lightly. Montaigne's motto — *Que sais-je?* ("What do I know?") — is the form's epistemological posture.
4. **Frank self-disclosure**: the essayist does not perform a curated self. Montaigne wrote about his kidney stones as readily as his philosophy.
5. **Non-teleological**: the essay is not trying to *sell* anything. It does not optimize for conversion.

## Why the Form Resists Storyselling

[[Byung-Chul Han]]'s diagnosis in *The Crisis of Narration* is that narrative has become commodified — stories are now written to create consumers. The personal essay's structural features resist this: meandering is bad for conversion funnels; provisional thinking is bad for persuasion; frank self-disclosure can't be scaled into a brand.

This is why Montaigne, dead 500 years, reads as more alive than influencers. See: [[Storyselling vs. Storytelling]], [[Looking Forward to Montaigne]].

## The Essay as Self-Construction

The personal essay is also the paradigmatic vehicle for [[Self-Construction Through Writing]]. Montaigne did not write about a pre-existing self — he wrote himself into existence iteratively. Each *essai* changed the person who would write the next one.
