# Space as the Dimension of Multiplicity

The constructive heart of [[doreen-massey]]'s *For Space* (2005), assembled in Chapter 5 ("The life in space") out of materials prepared by the diagnostic chapters of Part Two.

## The thesis

> "On this reading neither time nor space is reducible to the other; they are distinct. They are, however, co-implicated. On the side of space, there is the integral temporality of a dynamic simultaneity. On the side of time, there is the necessary production of change through practices of interrelation." (Massey 2005: 55)

Two co-implicated propositions:

1. **Without space, no multiplicity.** Space is the dimension in which more than one trajectory can coexist. To have multiplicity is to have things that are distinct-yet-coeval; that requires spacing.
2. **Without multiplicity, no time.** Time, on Massey's reading, is the change produced by the interaction of distinct trajectories. A pure One does not change; it has no other to react to. "We cannot 'become' . . . without others." (56)

The dual of Bergson's famous formula: where Bergson said "time prevents everything from being given at once" (1959: 1331), Massey adds that **space provides the condition for the relations that generate time.**

## Why the term "dimension"?

Not "space is the place where multiplicity occurs" — that would be the container image she rejects. Rather, space is the dimension *along which* coexisting multiplicity is possible. To remove space is not to compress everything into a single point but to remove the very possibility of plural trajectories. Multiplicity is **constitutive of**, not contained by, space.

## Constraints this thesis imposes

If space is the dimension of multiplicity, then:

- Space is **always open**. A closed space (in the sense of [[prison-house-of-synchrony]]) would contain no irreducible multiplicity and so would not be a space at all — it would be a single fully-interlocked system, a synchrony.
- Space is **always under construction**. Trajectories continuously meet, reshape each other, and produce new constellations. Cf. [[space-as-always-under-construction]].
- Space is **not representable** in any mimetic sense. Like time, it is the **happening** of the world, not a layout of the world. Cf. [[space-as-representation-critique]].
- Space is **integrally temporal**. Each of its constituent trajectories is itself a "story so far"; the spatial slice is a [[dynamic-simultaneity]], not a frozen snapshot.

## Political stakes

The thesis is not metaphysical neutrality. If space is the dimension of multiplicity, then any politics that reduces multiplicity (to one history, one voice, one developmental queue) is also a politics that **closes space**. Conversely, opening politics requires holding space open — holding the door for trajectories not yet articulated. This is the bridge to Part Three's critique of [[aspatial-globalisation]] and the "cosmopolitan time queue."
