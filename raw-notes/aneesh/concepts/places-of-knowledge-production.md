# Places of Knowledge Production

**Source**: [[doreen-massey]], *For Space*, Ch. 12, boxed section (2005: 143–145)

## The Science Parks as Event-Place

The science park is one of the most controlled, planned, and deliberately coherent types of place in contemporary capitalism: "Demarcated, landscaped enclosures dedicated to the production of science (usually, specifically, commercialisable science)" (Massey 2005: 143). They are easily recognisable, replicated globally, each a bid to create another Silicon Valley. Their design elements — enclosed separate space, landscaped environment, proximity to elite university, absence of industrial ruins, clustering to reassure investors — are known and repeated.

Massey uses them to apply her event-theory of place concretely. The apparent coherence of the science park is an achievement, not a given — and the trajectories that constitute it include far more than the current economic calculation.

## The Three Trajectories

**Trajectory 1: The mind/body distinction and the knowledge relation**

The separation of "conception" from "execution" in modern industrial labour — propelled by forces of class and a particular notion of knowledge (as removable from the shop floor, as separable rather than tacit, as distanced rather than embedded and embodied) — is deeply spatialised. "A dispersion of industrial sites emerged, with clearly distinct characteristics (a particular spatial division of labour), the spatiality being integral to the proliferation of divisions among the workers" (Massey 2005: 144). This recapitulates an old story: the desert for early Christian thinkers, monasteries as elite places of knowledge production, medieval universities — all materialise the separation of Mind from Body in spatial form.

**Trajectory 2: Gender**

"Through Western history, they have been part and parcel of the struggle around the creation of intelligible genders, of certain forms of 'masculine' and 'feminine'" (Massey 2005: 144). The establishment of each of these elite knowledge-spaces was bound up with the expulsion of women. Peter Brown on the desert fathers: "Fear of women fell like a bar of shadow across the paths that led back from the desert into the towns and villages" (cited Massey 2005: 144). David Noble's *A World Without Women* (1992) traces this gendered exclusion from desert monasticism through the medieval university to modern science. The "masculinity" of science parks today is not simply a demographic fact (predominantly male employees) — it is the precipitate of this longer history of contested gender construction.

**Trajectory 3: Legitimation of authorised knowledge**

These are "elite places of the production of legitimate, recognised, authorised knowledge" (Massey 2005: 145). Alongside them, always, were other forms of knowledge: in the villages along the edges of the desert, in the society beyond the monastery walls, on the shop floor. The histories of resistance — "from the battles within early Christianity, through Paracelsus, through the riot of dissidence over centuries in Europe (Lollards, Anabaptists, Muggletonians, early Swedenborgians, Brownists, Baptists, Quakers, Ranters ...) to the Lucas Aerospace workers" (Massey 2005: 145) — are woven into the contested history of these places.

## The Conclusion

> "These, then, again, are places as temporary constellations where the repercussions of a multiplicity of histories have been woven together. Knowledge production and legitimation function here as practices which generate space-times (as well as concepts of space-time). Place as event. Ironically, these high-tech places are controlled and planned events. Their components are disciplined, down to the enrolment of the non-human, in suitable, domesticated forms ('tasteful' landscape, watered lawns), to bolster their cachet. 'Ironically' because these 'places of innovation' seem designed to limit their potential character as places of innovation. And yet of course, in the end, the potential event of place remains. The containment is impossible." (Massey 2005: 145)

## Connection to Isabelle Stengers

Massey's analysis of science parks connects to her engagement with Stengers on the sociology of science. Stengers argues that "the way in which a science, or a conception of science, participates in the organization of the social field, and in particular induces a division of labor, is part of that science itself" (Deleuze and Guattari 1988: 368–9, cited Massey 2005: 143). The spatial organisation of knowledge production is not external to knowledge itself — it is constitutive of what counts as knowledge.
