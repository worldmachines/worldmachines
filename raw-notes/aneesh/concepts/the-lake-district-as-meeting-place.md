# The Lake District as Meeting Place

**Source**: [[doreen-massey]], *For Space*, Ch. 12 (2005: 130–142)

## The Scene

A winter stay in Keswick, in the northern Lake District. Massey's sister lives there. Just outside the town looms Skiddaw — "a massive block of a mountain, over 3000 feet high, grey and stony; not pretty, but impressive; immovable, timeless. It was impossible not to consider its relationship to this place. Through all that history, it seemed, it had presided." (Massey 2005: 131)

This is a provocation. Massey has argued throughout *For Space* that places are events — temporary constellations of on-going trajectories — but a friend has persistently objected: all right for human activity, but what about the mountains? The mountains seem to escape the event-logic.

The Lake District scene is Massey's answer: she reads the geology.

## The Geological Argument

Skiddaw's rocks were laid down in a sea that existed approximately 500 million years ago — in the southern hemisphere, about a third of the way from the equator to the south pole. (The sea is now called Iapetus.) The bit of crust we now call the Skiddaw slates has since:

- Drifted northward across the equator (crossing it approximately 300 million years ago)
- Been subjected to volcanic activity during the Ordovician period (reminders visible today in resistant volcanic rocks to the south)
- Been folded, eroded, and overlaid by other strata over hundreds of millions of years
- Had plutonic igneous rocks intruded from below
- Been carved by the glaciers of successive ice ages (the last retreating about 10,000 years ago)
- Only emerged as something resembling a mountain about 10 million years ago

Continental drift continues today — a few centimetres per year (roughly the rate at which fingernails grow). The whole of northwest Britain is still rising in relief after the removal of the great weight of ice. The landscape is still being eroded by weather and by the "constant tramp of hiking boots" — themselves a significant erosion force.

## The Conclusion: Immigrant Rocks

> "Immigrant rocks: the rocks of Skiddaw are immigrant rocks, just passing through here, like my sister and me only rather more slowly, and changing all the while. Places as heterogeneous associations." (Massey 2005: 136)

The geological argument extends the event-logic of place beyond human trajectories. Even Skiddaw — the paradigm case of natural immovability — is a trajectory-in-process. There is no stable ground. Not even under the mountains.

Massey draws the explicit conclusion: "If we can't go 'back' home, in the sense that it will have moved on from where we left it, then no more, and in the same sense, can we, on a weekend in the country, go back to nature. It too is moving on." (Massey 2005: 136)

## The Failure of "Natural Fixity" Arguments

Arif Dirlik (2001) is Massey's interlocutor here: he rightly argues that the social-constructionist emphasis in place-theory needs to bring nature back in, but he wants to use nature as a source of *fixity* — to ground place against the apparent dissolution of relational accounts. Massey responds: even this will not work. Nature is not fixed. The great temporalities of geological time are not evidence of permanence but of perpetual motion at a different pace.

The Lake District argument also counters the Romantic tradition that uses mountains as foundations for a sense of the eternal — as escape from the flux of human history into something unchanging. Two hundred years ago (before Lyell's geology), the argument would have been incomprehensible. Geology opened up deep time and made this alternative reading possible.

## Nonhuman Trajectories in Place

The scene at Keswick is composed of multiple trajectories at multiple temporalities:

- The rocks of Skiddaw: 500 million years of migration
- The drumlins (egg-shaped hills deposited by retreating glaciers): 10,000 years old
- The U-shaped valleys: carved in the last ice age advance
- The longstanding farmers and their hill-farming practices
- The grey-stone country houses of eighteenth/nineteenth-century aristocratic incomers
- The Roman remains
- The Romantic poets (Wordsworth, Coleridge) and the discourse of the sublime
- The ancient mining heritage
- The international tourist trade (including Massey and her sister)
- The swifts migrating between Kilburn and southern Africa

> "Reading Bruno Latour hints at how social scientists can dispense with our awe of natural science's 'truth' while still [...] integrating Skiddaw and weekend tourism as histories/trajectories whose co-formation participates in the event of Keswick." (Massey 2005: 138)

All of these are stories-so-far. The "event of Keswick" is their current, always-changing conjuncture.

## "Here" as Conjuncture

The philosophical conclusion:

> "'Here' is where spatial narratives meet up or form configurations, conjunctures of trajectories which have their own temporalities [...] Layers as accretions of meetings. Thus something which might be called there and then is implicated in the here and now." (Massey 2005: 139)

And: "It is, irretrievably, here and now. It won't be the same 'here' when it is no longer now." (Massey 2005: 139)

Place is the irreducible here-and-now of a specific conjuncture of trajectories. It is genuine — not a fiction — but it is not eternal. The Polaris star is the north pole star *now*, but it was not when the pyramids were built. The poles have wandered. Even orientation is a trajectory.
