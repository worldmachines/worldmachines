# Space as Relational Product

Massey's first proposition in *For Space* (2005): "that we recognise space as the product of interrelations; as constituted through interactions, from the immensity of the global to the intimately tiny." (Massey 2005: 9)

## The Claim

Space does not pre-exist the entities within it. It is not a neutral stage on which events play out. It is produced *through* the practices of relation between entities. Identities/entities, the relations between them, and their spatiality are **co-constitutive**: none is foundational; all emerge together.

> "Space does not exist prior to identities/entities and their relations. More generally I would argue that identities/entities, the relations 'between' them, and the spatiality which is part of them, are all co-constitutive." (Massey 2005: 10)

This claim operates at every scale: from the "immensity of the global" (the geopolitical relations that constitute continents as spatial entities in relation to one another) to the "intimately tiny" (the micro-practices of bodies in a room constituting that room's spatial character).

## Political Corollary

The relational production of space mirrors anti-essentialist politics. Rather than accepting pre-constituted identities and arguing for their rights, this politics takes the *relational construction of identities* as the central political stake. Chantal Mouffe (1993, 1995) is the key reference: identities and interrelations are constituted together, and spatiality may be integral to this constitution from the beginning.

If space is a product of interrelations, then spatial exclusions (borders, property lines, residential segregation) are not given features of the world but produced through practices that can be contested. This radically politicises geography.

## What This Rejects

- The Newtonian/Euclidean view of space as an empty, absolute container
- Container metaphysics: the idea that places are regions of a pre-given space into which things are deposited
- The "surface" imaginary: space as a neutral background across which actors move
