# Defenestration of Prague

The **Defenestration of Prague** (1618) was the act of throwing several Catholic royal representatives from the windows of Prague Castle by Protestant Bohemian nobles. The representatives fell ~70 feet into a dung heap and survived. The event precipitated the Thirty Years' War.

## Etymology

The event was so notable that it coined the word *defenestration* — "to throw someone out the window." The term became generalized for the political removal of officials.

## Aneesh Sathe's Use

In [[royalty-administration-and-antimemetics|Royalty, Administration, and Antimemetics]], Sathe uses the Defenestration as a paradigm case of how **memetically visible** power absorbs accountability for invisible administrative failures:

> "Defenestrating happened to important, visible, people held responsible for mismanagement leading to widespread discontent. While the defenestrated may represent the idea, surely we can't imagine that it was that specific person who was going around causing the suffering. No, they had minions."

The minions — the [[Antimeme|antimemetic]] administrative layer — caused the suffering but were structurally invisible. The royal representatives, visible and attributable, were defenestrated.

## General Principle

The Defenestration illustrates the **lightning-rod function** of memetic power: the visible figure exists in part to be displaced when the invisible machinery fails. This is not a bug in pre-modern governance design but a feature — it protects the administrative machinery by giving popular anger a target it can reach.
