# Shitty Castle Pattern

**Coined by**: [[Florian Lohse]], [[Shitty Castles]] (2026)

## Definition

A **shitty castle** is an elaborate construction — intellectual, material, or strategic — built by someone who knows it will not hold against the forces already in play. The pattern describes when and why people build these constructions anyway.

The name comes from Lohse's childhood taxonomy of castles: a castle that was "not gritty enough," built not to defend anything but "only to resemble actual fortifications." The definitive shitty castle: the 19th-century palace villa with walls that "would fall faster to a battering ram than dark age palisades."

## Three Case Studies

### Leibniz's Monad Theory
Leibniz visited [[baruch-spinoza]] in 1676, was "impressed and unsettled" by Spinoza's radical pantheism (God = Nature), but later publicly positioned himself as Spinoza's opponent. His Monad theory — each monad contains its own immortal soul subject to divine judgment — was widely recognized as insufficient:
- **Lessing**: suspected Leibniz was "a Spinozist by heart"
- **Bertrand Russell**: "Leibniz fell into Spinozism whenever he allowed himself to be logical; in his published works, accordingly, he took care to be illogical"

Lohse's charitable reading: the Monads were a **pragmatic shitty castle** — a defensive structure Leibniz built knowing it wouldn't hold, but which kept him aligned with religious patrons during a period of radical intellectual transition. Leibniz's own letters reveal his primary motivation was "assuring my security in the future."

### Château de Chambord (1519–)
François I began building Chambord after being defeated at Pavia by gunpowder units that would destroy such walls instantly. The castle was never meant to be a real fortress — the official explanation is it was "an architectural citation of bygone times," nostalgic attachment to chivalry.

But it *worked*: Charles V (the very king who had defeated François) visited in 1539 and called it "a synthesis of all that human effort can achieve." The castle served as a power demonstration — its sheer scale (220,000 tons of stone, 1,800 workers) was itself a signal of royal capacity.

### OpenAI / Jony Ive Acquisition (2025)
OpenAI acquired Jony Ive's "io" design company for $6.5 billion to build an "AI-native personal gadget" — in a product category with no successful precedents. Lohse's reading: a strategic shitty castle, signaling direction to investors when the core valuation ($500B for "a chatbot, an API and the buttery voice of Sam Altman") needed strategic buttressing.

## The Moat Lifecycle

Lohse proposes a general lifecycle for defensive structures (and by extension, ideas and frameworks):

| Period | Moat Type | Function |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| 13th century | Honest functional | Actual defense |
| 16th century (Chambord) | Performative | Power demonstration |
| 19th century (Neuschwanstein) | Ornamental | Patron pleasure |
| Monty Python (Holy Grail) | Satirical | Ridicule |

**Pattern**: functional → performative → ornamental → ridicule.

The more functional flaws you find in a construction, the more likely you're facing "a dead facade, not a working fortification."

## The Charitable Reading

The standard moral judgment of shitty castles: hypocrisy, self-deception, cowardice. Lohse resists this. Building a shitty castle can be:

1. **Temporally rational**: when the paradigm is shifting and no solid alternative exists, building something imperfect is better than building nothing
2. **Power-demonstrating**: the act of costly construction itself signals capability
3. **Security-maintaining**: allows continued operation until a genuinely solid idea arrives

The closing argument: "Why would you build a castle that is not a castle? To use the power you still have to create something fancy that is good enough to keep yourself sane until you have a better idea."

## Self-Implication

Lohse turns the pattern on himself: "I think with some of my past writing I have been digging fake moats for myself. I wrote some fancy essays while deep inside I knew they would not withstand the contact with reality, in particular the honest critique of my smartest friends."

The essay endorses a Spinozist alternative — "staying with the discomfort of not knowing" — while acknowledging this is genuinely hard.

## Relation to Zombie Ideas

Lohse explicitly connects shitty castles to [[venkatesh-rao]]'s concept of "energetic zombies" from [[The Divergence Machine]]: ideas from the past that still channel enormous energy while carrying little evolutionary intelligence. The challenge is distinguishing zombie ideas from legitimate shitty castles (things that are flawed but serve current purposes) from things worth genuine engagement.
