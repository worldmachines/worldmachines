# Algorithmic Management

The deployment of automated systems — recommendation algorithms, performance dashboards, incentive structures — to perform the functions historically carried out by human supervisors and factory overseers. Algorithmic management does not require the worker to be present in a particular place or to follow a clock; it governs through incentives and visibility rather than physical presence.

## Core Features

- **Incentive alignment without presence**: platforms don't need workers to show up; they pay only for output that sticks, perfectly matching reward to market impact
- **Elimination of classical management problems**: embezzlement, shirking, time theft — problems that occupied Landes's factory owners — are dissolved when the worker controls their own inputs and the platform controls distribution
- **Upside capture with downside insulation**: the platform participates only in successful outcomes; the worker absorbs all risk of failure
- **Opacity of criteria**: workers often cannot see or audit the algorithm that governs their rewards, creating an asymmetric information relationship

## Historical Precedents

- Factory overseers (18th–19th c.) — physical enforcement of productivity norms
- [[Putting-Out System]] — the merchant-manufacturer's version: incentivize production without direct supervision
- Scientific Management / Taylorism — measurement-based productivity maximization as precursor to algorithmic scoring

## Critiques

- Workers cannot contest or audit algorithmic decisions
- Gamification of incentives distorts output toward engagement over quality
- The algorithm's "neutrality" is ideological cover for platform power
- Creates [[Financialization of Creativity]] by making monetization legible and constant
