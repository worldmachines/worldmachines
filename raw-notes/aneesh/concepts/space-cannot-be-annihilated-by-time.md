# Space Cannot Be Annihilated by Time

The argument of Chapter 9 of [[doreen-massey]]'s *For Space* (2005), aimed at the widely-repeated Marxian-Harveyan thesis that capitalism's speed-up has "annihilated space by time."

## What the trope claims

Marx, then Harvey, then a generation of postmodern theorists (Bauman, Castells in some moods, the cyberspace utopians), have argued that contemporary capitalism progressively eliminates the friction of distance. With containerships, jet planes, fibre-optic cables, and the internet, "space doesn't matter anymore." Geography is history. The world is flat.

## Why this is wrong

Massey's countermove: **space is more than distance**.

1. **What is actually being reduced is *time* (and distance-as-time-cost), not space.** Marx's own formulation: capital seeks to "annihilate space by time." But space is not distance — space is the dimension of multiplicity. Reducing the time needed to traverse a distance does not reduce the multiplicity of trajectories that are co-present.

2. **Multiplicity persists, therefore space persists.** "No one is proposing (I assume) that screens, or instantaneous financial transactions, or even cyberspace, are abolishing multiplicity." (Massey 2005: 91) Telephone calls are instantaneous; the speakers do not merge into one being. Multiplicity is the heart of space; speed-up does not touch it.

3. **The two halves of the trope contradict each other.** The postmodern position says simultaneously that *this is the age of space* (Jameson's depthlessness, the spatial turn) *and* that *time has annihilated space*. Both can't be right. Massey reads the contradiction as a symptom of muddled spatial thinking. See [[instantaneity-depthlessness]].

4. **Cyberspace is itself spatial.** The fibre-optic networks, the data centres, the unevenly-distributed bandwidth — cyberspace has its own intensely material geography. See [[power-geometry]]. "Cyberspatial connections and bandwidth . . . are unequally distributed [spatially]" (Kitchin 1998, quoted 96).

## What is actually happening

Massey grants that speed-up reduces certain effects of distance and reorganises the experience of mobility. What is changing is not the existence of space but the **power-geometry** of space — who can move, who is moved, who is immobilised, who chooses their connections, who has connections imposed.

> "Whatever is happening to the former [distance] the latter [space-as-multiplicity] is very far from being annihilated." (96)

## The "happenstance juxtaposition" point

A related target: the celebration of "purified" non-contiguous communities (digital tribes, gated enclaves, science parks). Material spatial co-presence produces an unchosen friction — the "different neighbour" you didn't choose, the corner you turn into surprise. Massey argues this productive friction is what cyberspace utopianism dreams of escaping, and that escaping it is politically corrosive. See [[happenstance-juxtaposition]].
