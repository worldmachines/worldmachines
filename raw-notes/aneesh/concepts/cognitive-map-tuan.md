# Cognitive Map (Tuan)

Source: [[Yi-Fu Tuan]], *Space and Place* (1977), Chapter 6, pp. 67–84. See also [[tuan-2002-space-and-place-ch4-6]] and [[route-vs-survey-knowledge]].

## Definition

A cognitive map (in Tuan's usage) is an internal spatial representation that:
1. Organizes the spatial relations among places simultaneously rather than sequentially.
2. Exceeds what any individual can directly perceive or bodily experience.
3. Enables navigation by multiple routes and supports communication of spatial knowledge to others who have not made the journey.
4. Is culturally elaborated — it draws on language, diagrams, artifacts, social transmission, and formal instruction, not only individual experience.

The cognitive map is the endpoint of a developmental process that begins with bodily spatial skill (movement and kinesthesia) and passes through [[route-vs-survey-knowledge|route knowledge]] before arriving at the survey representation.

## Characteristic Distortions

The cognitive map is not a copy of geographic reality. Subjects who have learned to navigate a maze by route-following cannot reproduce it accurately in a drawing — they simplify, distort angles and lengths, omit turns, and delete information that was not relevant to the practical task of forward movement. "The drawn pattern departs so far from the actual course that it cannot be used as a map" (p. 72).

More broadly: people tend to discount angular departures and to treat all forward movement as movement toward their goal. The map is a rationalized sketch, useful enough for re-navigation but inaccurate as a survey. People carry "a general sense of direction to the goal, and [know] what to do next on each segment of the journey" — but the map itself is sketchy (p. 72–73).

## The Gap Between Ability and Map

Crucially for Tuan, **spatial ability does not require a cognitive map, and possessing a cognitive map does not guarantee spatial ability**. Many animals navigate expertly without anything resembling a conscious survey representation; a transcontinental migratory bird has spatial ability far exceeding most human achievements, yet presumably without a map in any cognitive sense. Conversely, a person might be able to describe the city's street plan with reasonable accuracy yet get hopelessly lost on foot.

This gap is illustrated by the contrast between the typist (knows the keyboard by finger, cannot draw it) and the explorer with map and compass (can traverse unknown terrain with minimal personal experience of it, because spatial knowledge has been abstracted and stored in material form).

## Cultural Determinants of Map-Complexity

Tuan's cross-cultural survey identifies conditions that favor the development of complex cognitive maps:

1. **Ecological demand**: Eskimos, Pacific Islanders, and Siberian hunters all live in environments where direct visual cues are sparse or absent (snow fields, open ocean, taiga at night) and where errors of orientation are lethal. They develop rich spatial-geometric vocabularies and map-making practices (p. 76–79).

2. **Social individuation**: Cultures that place a premium on individual initiative and self-reliance (Eskimo society, Puluwatan islanders) develop spatial-skill and map-competence more fully than those where group conformity provides orientation (Temne farmers whose social group constitutes their "place") (p. 79).

3. **Material culture of representation**: Map-drawing is not just a product of spatial knowledge; it is also a technology that develops and stores it. The Chukchi of northeastern Siberia drew detailed maps of river deltas in reindeer blood on wooden boards; these maps stored communal geographical knowledge and compared favorably with 1900 Russian naval charts (p. 77). The map as artifact is a form of cognitive extension.

4. **Need for transmission**: "The most common occasion [calling for a map] is the need to transmit efficiently geographical knowledge to another person" (p. 77). Purely individual navigation rarely requires a map; social coordination does. Maps are instruments of knowledge-sharing before they are instruments of wayfinding.

## Mythical Space as the Map's Halo

Cognitive maps are always embedded in a wider halo of "mythical space" — the hazily-known, imaginatively-filled region beyond the frontier of reliable knowledge. Tuan introduces this at the end of Chapter 6 and develops it in Chapter 7: every cognitive map has a periphery that shades into uncertainty and mythology. The Saulteaux Indians of Manitoba know their winter hunting and summer fishing grounds in minute detail; beyond that small world, "knowledge of terrain becomes hazy and inaccurate" (p. 87). Mythical space is the cognitive map's irreducible complement.

## Etak: The Richest Case

The *etak* system of Puluwatan navigation (Fig. 7) represents the most sophisticated integration of bodily route knowledge with abstract cognitive mapping. The navigator conceptualizes the journey as the canoe stationary and the reference island and destination moving past it against a fixed star-bearing grid. This is a genuinely allocentric frame — the navigator has stepped outside his own body-relative perspective — but it is encoded in kinesthetic and multisensory terms (feel of wave swells, sound of water, color of the sea surface). "The ocean is not a fearsome expanse of unmarked water… but a network of sea-ways linking up numerous islands" (p. 82–83).
