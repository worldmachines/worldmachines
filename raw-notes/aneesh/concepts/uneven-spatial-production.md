# Uneven Spatial Production (Harvey)

**Source**: David Harvey, *Social Justice and the City* (1973); *The Condition of Postmodernity* (1989); deployed in [[aneesh-sathe]], *We Need Homes in the Delta Quadrant* (2025), §III

## The Concept

David Harvey's core insight, developed across decades of Marxist geographical analysis: under capitalism, **space is not produced evenly**. The production of place — the investment in infrastructure, institutions, and visibility that makes certain locations inhabitable, productive, and meaningful — follows the logic of capital accumulation, not the logic of human need.

Sathe's formulation:

> "David Harvey arrives at a similar critique from a different angle. For Harvey, the production of space under capitalism is inherently uneven. Capital concentrates selectively, building infrastructure, institutions, and visibility in certain regions while leaving others disinvested, fragmented, or erased. Some places are made to flourish because they are profitable; others are sacrificed because they are not. Entire neighborhoods, cities, and ecosystems are subjected to cycles of speculative construction and abandonment. In this schema, place is commodified — not lived. It becomes a product shaped less by the needs of its inhabitants than by the imperatives of financial flows." (§III)

## Key Mechanisms

**Primitive accumulation of space**: Harvey's concept of "accumulation by dispossession" — capital accumulates not just through production but through the conversion of previously non-commodified resources (common land, public housing, natural ecosystems) into commodities. Space is a primary site of this accumulation.

**Speculative construction and abandonment**: Investment in place is tied to anticipated return. When returns decline, capital does not sustain the place — it abandons it, leaving disinvested, fragmented neighborhoods. Detroit's deindustrialization is the paradigm case; the "shrinking city" is the recurring form.

**Time-space compression**: In *The Condition of Postmodernity*, Harvey shows how capital's drive to reduce the time of circulation leads to technological and organizational transformations that "compress" space-time — some places are brought into tighter proximity with capital flows; others are rendered effectively more distant even as communication technology accelerates.

## Place as Commodity vs. Place as Lived Reality

Harvey draws a sharp distinction between:
- **Place as use-value**: the lived, inhabited, meaningful place constituted through inhabitation, memory, and social relation
- **Place as exchange-value**: the commodity — land as investment vehicle, property as financial instrument, neighborhood as site of return

Capitalism systematically subordinates use-value to exchange-value. The result is places that are shaped primarily by their exchange potential — gentrification, displacement, the transformation of neighborhoods from lived communities into financial assets.

## Relation to Lefebvre

Harvey explicitly extends Lefebvre's spatial triad. In Lefebvre's terms, capitalism's domination by abstract space (representations of space — the planner's grid, the developer's model) over representational spaces (the lived, symbolic, embodied spaces of actual inhabitants) is the form that uneven spatial production takes ideologically. Harvey adds the material-economic mechanism: it is capital's selective investment logic that drives this domination.

## Bryant's Gravitational Extension

Harvey's account is complemented by Bryant's [[bryant-gravity-as-power]]: the unevenness Harvey identifies is gravitationally maintained. Infrastructure — highways, fiber networks, hospitals, schools — bends the space-time of opportunities around it. Where capital has invested in infrastructure, the gravitational field shapes all subsequent movement; where capital has not, the field is flat or actively repulsive. Harvey names the political economy; Bryant names the ontological mechanism.
