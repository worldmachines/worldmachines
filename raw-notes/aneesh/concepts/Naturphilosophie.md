# Naturphilosophie

Naturphilosophie (philosophy of nature) was the German Romantic-idealist approach to natural science, primarily associated with Friedrich Schelling and developed within [[The Jena Circle]] milieu.

## Core Claims

- Nature is not dead matter governed by mechanical laws but a **living, self-organizing process**
- Mind and nature are expressions of the same underlying reality — consciousness emerges from nature, not from a separate substance
- The observer is part of the system observed, not external to it
- Organic holism over Cartesian/Newtonian reductionism — the whole is prior to the parts

## Influence

**[[Alexander von Humboldt]]** praised Naturphilosophie as revolutionary for empirical sciences. He acknowledged familiarity with Schelling's system and rejected the view that philosophy "can be destructive for the advance of empirical science." However, he never adopted the full speculative apparatus — retaining empirical grounding while absorbing the holistic orientation.

**Goethe's morphology** — his study of plant metamorphosis and color theory — shared Naturphilosophie's commitment to organic form and the observer's participation in phenomena, though Goethe was more empirically disciplined.

**Romantic medicine** drew on Naturphilosophie's emphasis on the organism as a self-regulating whole.

## Criticism and Legacy

Positivists dismissed Naturphilosophie as speculative pseudo-science — and its more extravagant claims (nature as unconscious spirit working toward self-consciousness) are difficult to defend on empirical grounds.

However, its core emphases anticipate several developments:
- **Complexity theory** — self-organization, emergence, non-linear dynamics
- **Systems biology** — the organism as an integrated system, not a collection of mechanisms
- **Ecological science** — interdependence, reciprocal causation (which Humboldt developed empirically)
- **Observer-dependent physics** — the role of measurement in quantum mechanics
