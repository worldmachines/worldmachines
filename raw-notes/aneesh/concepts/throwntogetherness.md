# Throwntogetherness

**Source**: [[doreen-massey]], *For Space*, Ch. 12 (2005: 140–142)

## The Term

Massey's most concentrated positive statement about place:

> "what is special about place is precisely that throwntogetherness, the unavoidable challenge of negotiating a here-and-now (itself drawing on a history and a geography of thens and theres); and a negotiation which must take place within and between both human and nonhuman." (Massey 2005: 140)

The word **throwntogetherness** carries a double resonance:

1. **Heidegger's "thrownness"** (*Geworfenheit*): to be thrown into a world not of one's choosing, with pre-given conditions (body, language, culture, geography). Massey extends this: not only persons but *all trajectories* find themselves thrown into configurations they did not choose and did not create.

2. **Together**: the thrown are not alone. The here-and-now is constituted by the meeting of multiple trajectories — some human, some nonhuman, each with its own story-so-far, each continuing independently of the others.

The compound "throwntogetherness" is Massey's way of saying: place is not a chosen or designed assembly; it is an always-already-happening conjuncture that demands a response.

## What Throwntogetherness Is NOT

Massey is insistent on this:

> "I must insist here, quite passionately, on one thing. This is not, as it is on occasions understood to be, a position which is hostile to place or working only for its dissolution into a wider space." (Massey 2005: 140)

Throwntogetherness is *not*:
- A deconstruction of place, merely exposing incoherence
- A denial that place matters
- A purely discursive move
- An argument that "there is nothing special about place" (she explicitly repudiates Dirlik's characterisation of her position this way)

What it *is* is an alternative positive understanding: places are special *because of* their throwntogetherness, not *in spite of* it. The romance of a pre-given collective identity or "the eternity of the hills" is the false alternative Massey is rejecting.

## The Political Dimension

Because throwntogetherness cannot be presumed coherent, and because it brings together trajectories that may conflict, it *necessitates negotiation*. This is the political payload:

> "the throwntogetherness of place demands negotiation. In sharp contrast to the view of place as settled and pre-given, with a coherence only to be disturbed by 'external' forces, places as presented here in a sense necessitate invention; they pose a challenge." (Massey 2005: 141)

The specific political challenges throwntogetherness creates:
- No assumption of pre-given community or collective identity
- Implication in the lives of human others (and in our relations with nonhumans)
- The necessity of responding to "our temporary meeting-up with these particular rocks and stones and trees"
- The impossibility of "purifying" spaces/places
- "The fact that you cannot (even should you want to, and this itself should in no way be presumed) 'purify' spaces/places"

This connects to Massey's broader political argument in Part Five of *For Space* about the responsibilities of place — particularly the responsibility of powerful places (London, the USA) for what they produce in and as the global.

## Nonhuman Trajectories

A crucial feature of throwntogetherness is that it includes nonhuman trajectories. Massey draws on Latour, Sarah Whatmore, and the geological record to insist that the here-and-now of Keswick includes: the rocks of Skiddaw (moving at centimetres per year), the swifts that nest in London (returning from 7000 miles away in southern Africa), the drumlins deposited by retreating glaciers, and the weekend tourists. All are trajectories in the event of this place. "History is no longer simply the history of people, it becomes the history of natural things as well" (Latour 1993: 82, cited Massey 2005: 138).

## The Science Parks Example

The boxed section on science parks (Massey 2005: 143–145) applies throwntogetherness concretely. A science park is not simply the product of current economic calculation — it is "a place as temporary constellation where the repercussions of a multiplicity of histories have been woven together" (Massey 2005: 144): class histories, gender politics, legitimation of elite knowledge-production from desert monasteries to Silicon Valley. The apparent coherence of the planned enclosure is an achievement, not a given — and "The containment is impossible" (Massey 2005: 145).

## Relation to Tuan and Bryant

**Tuan** and **Massey** agree that place is constituted through *event* rather than *property* — Tuan's pause, Massey's throwntogetherness, are both ways of saying place arises from something that happens rather than something that simply is. The difference: Tuan's account is phenomenological and human-centred (the pausing agent is always a human); Massey's throwntogetherness is ontological and includes nonhuman trajectories.

**Bryant**'s topology of worlds can be read as offering a structural parallel: places (for Bryant, "spaces") are milieus constituted by the paths of machines through phase-space. The intersection of machine-paths at a conjuncture is not unlike Massey's throwntogetherness, though Bryant's vocabulary is more rigorously non-phenomenological.
