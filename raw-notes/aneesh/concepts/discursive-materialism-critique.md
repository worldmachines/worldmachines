# Discursive Materialism (Bryant's Critique)

## What Bryant Means by "Discursive Materialism"

Bryant targets the mainstream of late twentieth-century critical theory — post-structuralism, certain strands of historical materialism, cultural studies, ideological critique — under the label "discursive materialism" or "discursivism." The unifying feature is the reduction of material phenomena to their discursive, semiotic, or ideological dimensions.

In this tradition, "materialism" had come to mean: something is historical, socially constructed, involves cultural practices, and is contingent. "It has nothing to do with processes that take place in the heart of stars, suffering from cancer, or transforming fossil fuels into greenhouse gases." (Bryant 2014, p. 2) Matter became a *terme d'art* — a theoretical gesture with no grip on actual stuff.

Žižek's formulation crystallizes the problem: "[m]aterialism means that the reality I see is never 'whole' — not because a large part of it eludes me, but because it contains a stain, a blind spot, which indicates my inclusion in it." (Žižek 2006: 17, cited at Bryant 2014, p. 2.) Bryant's response: "What need does matter have to be witnessed by anyone? What does a blind spot have to do with matter? Why is there no talk here of 'stuff,' 'physicality,' or material agencies?"

## The Genealogy

Bryant traces the collapse to Marxist political economy and its reception:

1. Marx correctly identified commodity fetishism: when we buy a shirt, we are participating in a network of social relations involving production, distribution, and consumption (Marx 1990: 165). The shirt conceals real social relations.
2. Marx was right: the commodity is, in *reality*, a crystallization of social relations (Marx 1990: 128).
3. But his successors drew the wrong conclusion: things are *only* crystallizations of the social. The powers of things themselves — as material entities with physical properties producing physical effects — were treated as naïve or politically dangerous to affirm.
4. The result: "To speak of the powers of *things* themselves, to speak of them as producing effects beyond their status as vehicles for social relations, became the height of naïveté." (Bryant 2014, p. 3)

The discursivist move applied not just to commodities but to everything: scientific findings were "discursive social constructions," ecological phenomena were interpreted through their cultural representations, and the agency of physical things was systematically dismissed as a vehicle for ideology.

## Analytic and Political Consequences

**Analytic**: discursivism could explain only half of how power works. The critical-theoretical tradition taught how fashion exercises power through symbolic capital, how ideologies distort consciousness, how discourse normalizes. But it could not explain how turnstiles in subways physically prevent access, how mountain ranges channel migration patterns, or how ocean currents distribute climate — physical organizations of power that operate regardless of what anyone believes about them.

**Political**: the sole strategy for change became exposing discursive construction and showing its contingency. "The idea of removing 'turnstiles' as one way of producing change and emancipation wasn't even on the radar." (Bryant 2014, p. 4) An emancipatory politics that cannot address material infrastructure is necessarily incomplete.

**Climate**: most urgently, discursivism made climate change theoretically unthinkable. "Because we had either implicitly or explicitly chosen to reduce things to vehicles for human discursivity, it became impossible to theorize something like climate change because we only had culture as a category to work with." (Bryant 2014, p. 4) Ecotheorists in the humanities developed preferences for discussing *representations* of the environment in literature and film, rather than the actual role bees play in agriculture.

## What Bryant Retains from the Discursivist Tradition

Bryant does not discard the discursivist tradition wholesale. Drawing on Whitehead: "the chief error in philosophy is overstatement." Discursive theories are not false; they are overstated in scope. The goal is to integrate their discoveries within a broader framework that also accommodates non-discursive agencies. Discursive machines (signifiers, texts, ideologies, cultural norms) are real machines with real effects — they are incorporeal machines in Bryant's ontology. The error is treating them as the *only* kind of machine.

## Relation to Correlationism

Bryant deploys Quentin Meillassoux's term [[correlationism]] to characterize the deeper philosophical error underlying discursive materialism: the assumption that we can only ever think the correlation between thought and being, never being in itself. Correlationism is the post-Kantian premise that makes it difficult to speak of matter's powers without immediately translating them into human experience or discourse. Bryant's [[renewal-of-materialism]] is, in part, a systematic attempt to break this correlation.

## Relation to This Wiki

The discursive-materialism critique is a useful conceptual tool for several wiki threads:

- **AI and knowledge**: a purely discursivist account of LLMs would analyze them as texts, training-data discourse, and signification. Bryant's framework demands also attending to the material substrate: server farms, energy grids, mineral supply chains, the physical-political geography of data centers.
- **Platform critique**: the [[protocol-gods-and-platform-demons]] framework implicitly operates at the discursive level (attention, meaning, architecture-of-values). Bryant would push toward the physical: the energy costs of serving the feed, the material infrastructure of the internet, the bodily effects of screen light on circadian rhythms.
- **Technology as place-maker**: the [[technology-as-place-maker]] synthesis evaluates technologies by whether they create orienting places or disorienting space *for humans*. Bryant would expand this evaluation to include what technologies do as media for non-human machines.
