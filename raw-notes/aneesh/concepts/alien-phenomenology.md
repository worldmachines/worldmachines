# Alien Phenomenology

## Origin of the Term

The term was introduced by Ian Bogost (*Alien Phenomenology, or What It's Like to Be a Thing*, 2012) to denote a form of phenomenology that examines how *non-human* entities experience the world. [[levi-bryant]] adapts it as a central methodological and ontological commitment of [[onto-cartography]] (pp. 62–74).

"Alien phenomenology includes, as I understand it, traditional phenomenology, but goes beyond it. Where traditional phenomenology investigates *our* lived experience of the world, alien phenomenology seeks to investigate how other entities such as mosquitoes, trees, rocks, computer games, institutions, etc., encounter the world about them." (p. 62)

## The Core Claim

Every machine has a *world* — defined by its [[structural-openness-operational-closure]]. Because machines are selectively open to particular flows and always transform those flows through their own operations, each machine inhabits a version of reality shaped by its own operational structure. The world looks different from inside the submarine's sonar, the mantis shrimp's visual system, the insurance-company's profit-maximization logic.

Alien phenomenology is the methodological practice of investigating:
1. To what flows is this machine structurally open?
2. How does the machine operate on those flows as they pass through it?
3. What local manifestations take place in the machine as a result?
4. What is the world *like* for this machine?

(pp. 62–63)

## Not Panpsychism

Bryant is explicit: alien phenomenology is not the claim that all beings possess consciousness, awareness, perception, or cognition. "Rather, the claim is that all machines, regardless of whether they're living, are selectively open to particular flows and operate on these flows in ways unique to that type of machine." (p. 64)

The rock does not *think*, but it has operations — it responds differentially to heat, pressure, acid. The insurance company does not *care*, but it is operationally organized around profit-maximization criteria that determine what flows it registers and how it processes them. In this sense, each machine has something like a perspective — not consciousness, but an operational "for-itself."

See [[for-itself-machine]].

## Post-Vitalist Ethology

Bryant distinguishes alien phenomenology from Uexküll's *ethology* (the study of animal umwelts) by its **post-vitalistic** scope: it includes not just living organisms but all machines — rocks, cameras, mathematical theorems, institutions. "A post-vitalistic ethology is not an ethology that *excludes* the living, just as post-humanism does not exclude the human." (p. 64)

This contrasts with Uexküll, who restricts phenomenological investigation to living beings. Bryant, following Bogost, extends the investigation to carbon atoms, cameras, computers, rocks.

## Methodology: Second-Order Observation

Alien phenomenology is what Luhmann calls "second-order observation" (Luhmann 2002b): not observing how an entity appears to *us*, but seeking to observe how the world appears to *that entity*. "We ask what the world is like for a cane toad, for example, rather than what cane toads are like for us." (p. 62)

The Nagel objection: it is impossible to fully experience the world of a bat, a computer chip, or a corporation. Bryant acknowledges this but insists that "while it is entirely true that I cannot *experience* the world of a bat, computer chip, or corporation because I am not a bat, computer chip, or corporation, I can nonetheless make all sorts of inferences about what the world is like for these other machines" (p. 63). Knowledge of physics, biology, and behavior allows inference about structural openness and operations.

## Examples in Practice

- **Mantis shrimp**: structurally open to a far broader electromagnetic spectrum than humans; their visual apparatus plays distinct roles in mating and predator detection (pp. 54, 63–64)
- **The bat** (Nagel): sonar as the primary mode of navigating and hunting
- **Insurance company**: structurally open to forms and profit-loss signals; operationally blind to individual human suffering
- **Plants** (Pollan's *Botany of Desire*): from an evolutionary alien-phenomenological perspective, plants have "seduced" humans to maximize their reproductive fitness — a perspective-flip on the human/nature hierarchy (pp. 65–67)
- **Cows**: from a bovine evolutionary perspective, humans function as instruments of bovine reproductive success (pp. 67–68)
- **Technology** (Kelly's *What Technology Wants*): technologies have tendencies and vectors arising from materials and economics rather than human intentions (pp. 68–69)

## Three Stakes

**Analytic**: mapping relations between machines requires knowing their openness and closure profiles. Without alien phenomenology, onto-cartography is blind to the actual operational contours of the machines being mapped (p. 69).

**Ethical**: "A great deal of human cruelty arises from the failure to practice alien phenomenology." (p. 71) Colonial genocide, sexism, ableism — all involve measuring others solely in terms of our own operational assumptions. Alien phenomenology opens space for more compassionate engagement: Cesar Millan thinking like a dog, Temple Grandin adopting cow phenomenology (pp. 70–71).

**Political** — thermodynamic politics: if institutions have their own operational closure, we must engage with them in terms of the flows they actually register, not the moral flows we wish they responded to. Boycotts work; ethical appeals to corporations mostly do not (pp. 72–73). See [[thermodynamic-politics]].

## Synthesis Seeds

- **Alien phenomenology ↔ Yi-Fu Tuan's experiential perspective**: Tuan's concept of [[experiential-perspective-tuan]] keeps "experience" and "perspective" firmly in the human domain — places become places through human consciousness, feeling, and meaning-making. Bryant's alien phenomenology radically generalizes "perspective" to all machines, including non-conscious ones. The productive tension: Bryant dissolves the special status of human experience; Tuan insists on it. A synthesis might argue that human place-experience is a *high-plasticity* alien phenomenology — the most elaborate form of operational transformation of spatial flows.
- **Alien phenomenology ↔ Sathe's "world-machines"**: world-machines in the wiki framework are defined operationally — by what they do to humans and other machines. This is structurally identical to alien phenomenology: both ask "what does this machine do?" rather than "what is it?"
