# The For-Itself of a Machine

## The Concept

In [[levi-bryant]]'s [[onto-cartography]], every machine has something like a *for-itself* — not in the Sartrean sense of conscious self-relation, but in the minimal operational sense: every machine has a world constituted by its [[structural-openness-operational-closure]]. The world appears to a machine *as that machine's operations transform it*, not as it is in itself.

The for-itself of a machine is the world-as-operationally-processed: the mantis shrimp's world shot through with twelve primary colors and polarized light; the insurance company's world reduced to profit-and-loss signals; the rock's world of pressures, temperatures, and chemical affinities.

"Luhmann remarks that '[r]eality is what one does not perceive when one perceives it' (Luhmann 2002a: 145). We perceive the world, of course, because our perceptions are initiated by flows that affect us. Nonetheless we do not perceive the world because what we experience — and what all sentient beings experience — are these flows transformed through operations. From the standpoint of the subject, its *lived experience* of the world is indistinguishable from the world itself." (p. 60)

Bryant extends this from sentient beings to *all machines*. Each machine only has access to "operationally transformed flows it encounters in its internal world" (p. 60).

## The Non-Conscious For-Itself

The concept deliberately separates the *for-itself* (an operational perspective) from *consciousness* (subjective awareness). The claim is not panpsychist — Bryant does not assert that rocks feel or that corporations experience pleasure. The claim is weaker and more structural: every machine transforms flows according to its own operations, and this transformation constitutes a *perspective* on the world, a way of encountering it, even in the absence of any inner light of experience.

This is the minimal sense in which "there is something it is like" to be any machine: not a qualitative inner experience, but an operational selectivity and transformation that functions as a perspective.

Compare [[alien-phenomenology]]: "the claim is that all machines, regardless of whether they're living, are selectively open to particular flows and operate on these flows in ways unique to that type of machine" (p. 64). The for-itself is what this selectivity and transformation constitute.

## Roots: Kant Extended

The for-itself concept follows from Bryant's generalization of Kant's Copernican revolution. Kant: objects must conform to the conditions of our cognition; we never encounter things in themselves. Bryant: what Kant says about cognitive machines holds for all machines (pp. 57–58). Every machine, like the Kantian subject, imposes its own conditions on the flows it receives.

The "firewalls" metaphor (crediting Harman): all machines encounter one another behind firewalls — the operations of each machine. No entity directly encounters another; each encounters the processed version. The for-itself is the interior side of this firewall (p. 58).

## The Ghost of Hegel

The Hegelian resonance is intentional but limited. Hegel's *für sich* (for-itself) marks the moment of self-relation in consciousness. Bryant's for-itself strips away the self-relation and the consciousness, retaining only the notion that a machine has an interior organization that it brings to bear on flows — a kind of immanent standard by which flows are processed.

The key difference: Hegel's for-itself is a feature of Geist developing toward self-knowledge. Bryant's for-itself is a feature of *any* operational system, including sub-personal, non-organic, and purely formal machines.

## Implications

1. **Every entity counts ontologically**, not merely instrumentally. A rock has a for-itself in the minimal operational sense; its resistance to certain pressures and responsiveness to others is a genuine perspective.

2. **Moral extensibility**: the for-itself grounds the ethical stakes of [[alien-phenomenology]] and [[non-human-ethics]]. If every machine has a for-itself — a way in which things can matter to it operationally — this provides a basis (however minimal) for moral consideration beyond conscious subjects.

3. **Political**: institutions have for-themselveses. A corporation is not just a legal fiction through which humans operate; it is a machine with its own operational world, its own firewalls, its own for-itself organized around profit. This is why personnel changes rarely change institutional behavior. See [[thermodynamic-politics]].
