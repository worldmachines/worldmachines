# Six Relationships of Modernity

**Originator**: [[venkatesh-rao]] (The Modernity Machine, Jan 2025)

## Framework

The [[Modernity Machine]] can be analyzed as the restructuring of six bilateral relationships among four social classes:

| Class | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Monarch** | Sovereign with divine authority |
| **Oligarchy** | Upper nobility with large land-holdings of sufficient size to be politically consequential |
| **Technocracy** | The knightly and priestly classes (overlapped in cases like the Templars); holders of codified knowledge |
| **Workers** | The peasantry; the laboring class |

The six relationships: Monarch ↔ Oligarchy, Monarch ↔ Technocracy, Monarch ↔ Workers, Oligarchy ↔ Technocracy, Oligarchy ↔ Workers, Technocracy ↔ Workers.

**Secular trend across all six**: the previously weaker party gained significant and persistent new agency. Power shifted noisily but steadily downward in the four-layer hierarchy.

## The Six Relationships in Detail

### 1. Monarch ↔ Oligarchy
**Pivotal event**: Magna Carta (1215)  
**Transformation**: From divine-right monarch to contractually constrained monarch. The monarch as "a regular human" rather than "an individual with divine rights."  
**Parallel**: Venice's hereditary Doge model (precocious modern republic — no land-based agrarian estates, early commercial technologies).  
**End-state**: Monarchies have "all but disappeared" or survive only as *de facto* rather than *de jure* power.

### 2. Monarch ↔ Technocracy
**Pivotal event**: Philip IV's move against the Templars (1307)  
**Transformation**: Established that political power can arise from *codified knowledge* (military or theological) as much as from land. Established *portable* technocratic loyalties — the knightly class could pivot from feudal estates to high-ranking court officials.  
**Downstream**: Mercenary companies (John Hawkwood's White Company), condottieri, the professional managerial class.  
**Note**: This relationship evolved "faster and further in the Islamic world" — the Ottoman *sipahi* system and the "slave" system of high-ranking military-court officials of Eastern European descent.

### 3. Monarch ↔ Workers
**No single pivotal event** (the relationship is "varied and messy")  
**Candidates**: Assize of Arms (1252) — required all English yeomen to become proficient with the longbow, acknowledging importance of working-class knowledge capital. War of Chioggia (1378) — forced the general population to learn technical sailing skills.  
**Military marker**: Robin Hood and Swiss William Tell legends date to this era.  
**End-state**: The Battle of Crécy (1346) as landmark — English longbow archers prevailed over knight-dominated French army.

### 4. Oligarchy ↔ Technocracy
**Defining dynamic**: The growing challenge to primogeniture from the clergy (younger sons acting through religious laws to constrain elder brothers' inheritance).  
**The indulgences economy**: Clergy gained growing influence over oligarchic society through increasingly restrictive religious rules — and manufactured exceptions (indulgences) to ease them. An "economy of indulgences" at least 800 years old.  
**Key precursor**: John Wycliffe (1330-1384) criticized clerical corruption a *century* before Gutenberg (1450s) and nearly two before Luther (1517).  
**Gutenberg twist**: "One of the earliest uses of the printing press was in fact the mass production of standardized fill-in-the-blank indulgences." The press accelerated religious corruption *before* enabling religious reform — analogous to how textile machinery initially strengthened slavery by driving up cotton demand.  
**Gender dimension**: Women gained property rights in part because childless widows could be persuaded to leave land holdings to the church. Nunneries rose alongside monasteries.

### 5. Oligarchy ↔ Workers
**Pivotal event**: Black Death (1347)  
**Transformation**: Newly scarce labor gained extraordinary bargaining power; terminal decline of serfdom in Western Europe.  
**Urban dimension**: Rise of cities as exit option for freed labor. Urban economies were cash-based, turning the working class into a financial asset-owning class. Stock markets begin here. Venice example: even the poor could buy shares in voyages.

### 6. Technocracy ↔ Workers
**Described as "perhaps the most important of the six"**  
**Military marker**: Battle of Crécy (1346) — English longbow archers prevailed over knight-dominated cavalry. The longbow is simpler than the crossbow and prefigures modern patterns of manager-worker relations.  
**Urban marker**: Rise of guild-organized workers within a sumptuary economy based on status.  
**End-state**: The "Professional Managerial Class" is far older than James Burnham would have you believe. AI as the contemporary equivalent: "Centuries of accumulated technocratic knowledge, gifted to the working classes in one fell swoop."

## The End-States of 700 Years

Running the machine for 700 years produces:
1. Monarchies mostly disappeared or reduced to shadows
2. Oligarchies evolved from large landholders with serfs to modern monopoly capitalism
3. Technocracy evolved from military/theological knowledge to scientific knowledge as power source; but the *social structure* remains (the indulgences economy evolved from religious sins to carbon credits and land acknowledgements)
4. Working class made steady, secular gains; AI and crypto continue this trend

## Relationship to Velitchkov's Four-Class Version

[[Ivo Velitchkov]] in "Three Engineers of Modernity" uses a four-relationship version (omitting Monarch-Workers and Oligarchy-Workers). Rao's original essay identifies six. The six-relationship version is more granular but both describe the same secular trend.
