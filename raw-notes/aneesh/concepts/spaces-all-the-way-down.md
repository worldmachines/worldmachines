# Spaces All the Way Down

**Source**: [[aneesh-sathe]], *We Need Homes in the Delta Quadrant* (2025), §IV — Spaces All the Way Down

## The Claim

> "Every place, no matter how concrete or intentional, overlays a prior space. A home rests on a plot of land that once held other meanings. A software tool is coded atop prior protocols, abstractions, languages. A startup's culture is built not from scratch, but from accumulated social assumptions, inherited metaphors, and the ghosts of previous institutions. No place begins in a vacuum. It begins by coalescing around an earlier ambiguity." (§IV)

> "To say 'it's spaces all the way down' is not a paradox but a recognition: that all our structuring of the world rests on foundations that were once unstructured. And those, in turn, rest on others. Beneath every home is a history. Beneath every habit is a choice. Beneath every heuristic is an unspoken story of why something worked once, and perhaps still does." (§IV)

## The Geological Image

"Spaces all the way down" inverts the philosopher's image of the infinite regress as a problem. Here it is a *feature*: places are sedimentary. They accumulate layers. The present structure is always an overlay on prior structures, which are themselves overlays on prior structures. There is no bedrock — or rather, the bedrock is itself a prior place with its own history.

This connects to Tuan's concept of [[growth-rings-of-place]]: cities acquire successive defensive walls, each becoming the outline of a subsequent neighborhood; the layers are readable in the urban morphology. The geological metaphor applies equally to non-physical places: software systems, organizational cultures, legal codes, daily habits.

## The Invisible Infrastructure Argument

What feels natural is often just deeply buried infrastructure. This is Sathe's most pointed observation from the insight: the apparent neutrality of existing structures — the way "how things are done" feels self-evident — is the invisibility of their sedimentary history. The structure has been there so long that the prior spaces it was built on are forgotten. 

This has critical implications:
- What appears to be the "obvious" way to organize a team may be a 20-year-old workaround to a problem that no longer exists
- What appears to be "neutral" technical protocol may encode assumptions from a particular cultural moment
- What appears to be "natural" social order may be the calcified result of past contestation and settlement

Making these layers visible is an act of historical consciousness and political analysis.

## Examples Sathe Deploys

- Setting up a **calendar system**: encodes assumptions about time, priority, and labor that are not universal
- **Onboarding processes**: scaffold new entrants into existing assumptions; the invisible assumptions are re-instantiated in every new employee
- **Refactoring code**: an act of making the invisible history visible — prior decision layers are examined and sometimes reverted
- **Reorganizing a room**: what was furniture arrangement becomes revealed as a now-inappropriate choice from a prior need

## Relation to Bryant

Bryant's [[onto-cartography]] provides a technical vocabulary for "spaces all the way down." Every machine (in Bryant's sense) is built from prior machines — prior endo-relational structures that are now the operational environment for the current one. [[infrastructure-inheritance]] names this in the wiki's technology thread. Bryant's analysis of how "black-box" machines (whose internal structure has become opaque) exercise gravity without revealing their inner history is the onto-cartographic version of Sathe's invisible infrastructure argument.
