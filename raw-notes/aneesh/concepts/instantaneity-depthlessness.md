# Instantaneity / Depthlessness

The target of Chapter 7 of [[doreen-massey]]'s *For Space* (2005). The "spatial turn" of late-twentieth-century cultural theory celebrated space — but in a particular way: as the flat surface of all-at-once interconnection, the "global village," the depthless present.

## What the celebration claims

- Everything is connected to everything.
- Everything happens *now*.
- Depth, history, and trajectory are obsolete; what matters is the surface of relations.
- This is liberatory: the prison of linear time has been broken.

The locus classicus is Jameson's "depthlessness" in *Postmodernism, or, the Cultural Logic of Late Capitalism* (1991) — though Jameson is critical of the phenomenon, much downstream literature is uncritical.

## Why Massey thinks this is another taming

Massey grants that some changes have occurred in the experience of space-time. But she reads the **celebration of depthlessness as a new spatial closure**, not a liberation:

1. **A space without trajectory is a space without time** — but a space without time is the structuralist [[prison-house-of-synchrony]] all over again. Just the marketing has changed.
2. **Replacing one history with no history is not pluralism.** Massey directly: "in the gadarene rush to abandon the singularity of the modernist grand narrative . . . what has been adopted in its place is a vision of an instantaneity of interconnections. But this is to replace a single history with no history." (Massey 2005: 14)
3. **The "global village" image flattens [[power-geometry]].** If everyone is equally connected to everyone else, the actual asymmetries of who-moves-who and who-talks-to-whom disappear from view.
4. **Depthlessness erases coevalness.** Coevalness ([[coevalness]]) requires that others have their own trajectories, their own durations, their own stories. Instantaneity reduces them to nodes in a present surface.

## The cyberspace version

The cyberspace utopians (Negroponte, Mitchell, Gates 1995) celebrate the instant-everywhere as the end of distance and therefore of constraint. Massey's reply (developed in Ch. 9): they treat space as distance and distance as constraint. But space is multiplicity; distance is what enables some kinds of multiplicity; instantaneity does not abolish multiplicity, only one of its modes. See [[space-cannot-be-annihilated-by-time]].

## What survives critique

Massey is not nostalgic for deep slow time. She is not making the conservative complaint that "things move too fast now." Her objection is that **the dominant celebrations of speed-up obscure the real political-spatial questions** — who is empowered by the new geometries, who is excluded, what new closures are produced under the rhetoric of openness.
