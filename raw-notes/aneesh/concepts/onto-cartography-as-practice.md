# Onto-Cartography as Practice

## Core Claim

Bryant's central political argument: onto-cartography is not a representationalist enterprise. Maps are not neutral descriptions. The point of mapping machine ecologies is to enable strategic action — to identify points of intervention, to expose contingency, to guide deconstruction and terraformation. "The first step in producing any sort of change or new assemblages lies in having a good map" (p. 257).

This makes onto-cartography irreducibly practical. The rationale for mapping "lies in the fact that we cannot effectively intervene in situations without knowing the lay of the land or how machines are put together, the gravity they exercise, and what flows between them" (p. 257). Physicians must know anatomy; generals must know supply lines; geophilosophers must know assemblages.

## The Four Map Types as Modes of Practice

Bryant distinguishes four interrelated map types, none of which is merely descriptive:

| Map Type | Temporal Orientation | Political Function |
|---|---|---|
| Topographical | Present snapshot | Identifies machines, gravity, flows — what holds an assemblage together |
| Genetic | Past to present | Reveals contingency; defamiliarizes the present; opens imagination of alternatives |
| Vector | Present to likely future | Determines where intervention is necessary now |
| Modal | Possible futures | Plans specific interventions: what to add/subtract to produce a desired world |

The modal map is the most explicitly practical — it is the cartographer's translation of topographical and vector analysis into actionable strategy (p. 266). It asks: "how would we have to change the existing assemblage to produce this world?"

## Against Abstractions: Mapping the Concrete

The first principle of practical onto-cartography: "avoid abstractions!" (p. 257). "Capitalism," "patriarchy," "racism" are shorthand for assemblages; they do not cause anything. The cartographer must descend to the *specific machines* and *specific relations* that compose and sustain these assemblages. This is not to deny that patriarchy or capitalism are real — it is to insist they are real as *assemblages*, as effects of machine interactions, not as sovereign agencies.

Abstract master-terms generate political paralysis:
- **Theoretical pessimism**: capitalism is everywhere and nowhere; you can't fight a ghost (p. 258)
- **Revolutionary quietism**: no intervention short of total abolition is worthwhile (p. 258)

Both positions fail to recognize that events unfold over duration as multiplicities, and that mediated, partial interventions accumulate into change.

## Cartographical vs. Normative Politics

Bryant distinguishes two political modes:

- **Normative politics**: specifies what ought to be done, what goals ethics demands. These are expressive machines — necessary but insufficient.
- **Cartographical politics**: maps how gravity is actually structured in an assemblage. It seldom tells us what to do, but shows *why* things are the way they are and *where* intervention is possible. Marx (*Capital*) and Foucault (*Discipline and Punish*) are exemplary cartographical politicians — their maps are not prescriptions but analyses of gravitational structure (pp. 283–285).

The limitation of normative politics without cartographical politics: denunciations, at this point, are "of limited value. We know them, we've heard them. What we need are modal maps that would allow us to transform these normative machines into actualities" (p. 283).
