# Slices through Space

**Source**: [[doreen-massey]], *For Space*, Ch. 11 (2005: 105–129)

## The Image

The conventional Western map presents space as a "flat surface, a continuous surface. Space as the completed product. As a coherent closed system" (Massey 2005: 105). It operates like the synchronies of structuralism: all relations already-established, everywhere already-interconnected.

Massey's counter-image: if you really were to take a slice through time — a cross-section through what is happening at any given moment — "it would be full of holes, of disconnections, of tentative half-formed first encounters" (Massey 2005: 107). The apparently coherent horizontal surface is, when properly understood, a slice through multiple ongoing trajectories, each with its own temporality, its own direction, its own story-so-far. The spatial is this simultaneous multiplicity — not a finished product but an arena of process.

## What This Means for Maps

Maps are not wrong in themselves — they are representations. But the **dominant Western map** misleads by suggesting:
1. That space is completed and given (rather than always-in-production)
2. That all interconnections are already-established (rather than some absent, some tentative, many yet to be made)
3. That the observer occupies a god's-eye view outside the space being represented

The postcolonial critique of maps (Harley, Huggan, Rabasa) partially corrects this by showing how maps are technologies of power and how their apparent coherence suppresses other voices. But even this critique, when it deploys the figure of the **palimpsest** (layers of suppressed pasts beneath the dominant surface), remains trapped in a surface-imagination. Palimpsest suggests stratigraphic suppression — things from "before" lying hidden under a current surface. It does not capture coevalness: the simultaneous existence of multiple ongoing trajectories that are not beneath the surface but *alongside* each other in the same present.

## The Postcolonial Extension

The "slices through space" argument has a political dimension. When one imagines travel as moving across a surface, the people and places "out there" are rendered static — they wait to be discovered, to be named, to receive history. Jose Rabasa's reading of Michel de Certeau's treatment of Jean de Léry's Brazil (de Certeau 1988) exemplifies this: Europe is constructed as the agent of history (time), Brazil as the passive space (object of knowledge). Space, on this imagination, is what you traverse; history is what you make.

Massey's counter: what Cortés encounters at Tenochtitlán is not space-waiting-to-be-conquered but another history, another set of ongoing stories. "Conquest, exploration, voyages of discovery are about the meeting-up of histories, not merely a pushing-out 'across space'" (Massey 2005: 120). Eric Wolf's *Europe and the People Without History* (1982) is the canonical statement of this corrective.

## The Architecture Connection

Bernard Tschumi's deconstructivist architecture — especially the Parc de la Villette project, with its superimposition of three separate logical structures (point grid, coordinate axes, curve) — reaches for the undecidability of space. But Massey criticises it: Tschumi's indeterminacy is produced by layering *horizontal closed structures*, not by intertwining open-ended trajectories. "There is no temporality here. Space here is formed by putting together three closed horizontal surfaces" (Massey 2005: 113). Aldo van Eyck's "labyrinthine clarity" (Sadler 1998: 30) comes closer — it is a more multifarious order rather than a collapse into randomness or the superimposition of stable structures.

## Relation to Massey's Positive Thesis

The "slices through space" image is the critical clearing for Massey's positive concept of place. If space is properly understood as this simultaneous multiplicity of trajectories, then places are the conjunctures — the local articulations, the here-and-nows — where some of those trajectories meet. See [[places-as-events]] and [[throwntogetherness]].

## Productive Tensions

- **Against palimpsest** (which stays surface-bound): Massey's own account of "layers" of history must avoid the same trap. She resolves this by insisting on layers as *accretions of meetings* — temporal accumulation of spatial encounters — rather than as strata hiding something below.
- **The coherence assumption**: "places are conceived of as sites where a host of different social processes are gathered up into an intelligible whole" (Low and Barnett 2000: 58, cited Massey 2005: 141). Massey argues against this: the simultaneous multiplicity of trajectories in a place means it cannot be presumed coherent.
