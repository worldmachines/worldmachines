# Narrative-Descriptive Approach

**Source**: [[tuan-1991-language-and-the-making-of-place]]

## Definition

The narrative-descriptive approach is the third of three methodological stances toward language and place, as Tuan identifies them in his 1991 article. It draws on the first two approaches — the linguistic/grammatical and the sociolinguistic — but absorbs them "into its story line, without pausing for theoretical overviews or going into analytical detail" (p. 686).

In this approach, the explicit formulation of theory is not attempted — or only informally attempted. Theories and theoretical frameworks are always present, but they operate as background support rather than foreground argument. The phenomena themselves (the cases, the narratives, the descriptions) carry the primary explanatory weight.

## Why Tuan Prefers It

Tuan gives two connected reasons:

1. **The nature of the subject matter.** Human experience — especially cultural and historical geography's subject matter — is "almost always ambiguous and complex." A highly structured theory tends to become almost "solid," and thus able to cast "paradoxically a shadow over the phenomena it is intended to illuminate." When the theory is too visible, it drives rival interpretations out of mind and makes the object of study — a human experience — "turn into something schematic and etiolated."

2. **The limits of social-scientific theories on creativity.** Social theories aspiring to be scientific "seldom address the still baffling phenomenon of creativity — of power as it is understood by artists." The narrative-descriptive approach, by contrast, can accommodate the fact that we use language powerfully "without fully knowing how."

## Who Uses It

The approach is favored by "cultural and historical geographers, historians generally, and cultural anthropologists — scholars who are predisposed to appreciate the range and color of life and world" (p. 686). Its best works "tend to make a reader feel the intellectual pleasure of being exposed to a broad and variegated range of related facts and of understanding them a little better (though still hazily)."

## The Tradeoff

The narrative-descriptive approach is "more inclusive and faithful to the complexities of actual experience, but it is less analytically penetrating" than the linguistic or sociolinguistic approaches. It does not provide "the intellectual assurance of being offered a rigorous explanation of a necessarily narrow and highly abstracted segment of reality."

This is an honest tradeoff, not a failure. Tuan acknowledges it directly and accepts it.

## Relation to Other Methodologies

The narrative-descriptive approach does not reject the linguistic or sociolinguistic approaches. It absorbs them. This is a form of methodological inclusivism: you can use the insights of grammar (language reveals spatial metaphysics) and of sociolinguistics (language is power in social context) without foregrounding either. The approach is itself an enactment of what it describes — it proceeds by example, by case, by accumulation of evidence, in the way that storytelling proceeds.
