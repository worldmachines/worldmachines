# Structural Coupling

## Origin

The concept comes from Chilean biologists [[humberto-maturana]] and [[francisco-varela]], developers of autopoiesis theory. Structural coupling describes how an autopoietic (self-producing) system relates to its environment: the system is *operationally closed* — its internal operations refer only to internal states — but *structurally open* — its internal states are nonetheless shaped and perturbed by contact with the environment.

The key feature is selectivity: the system does not simply receive all inputs from the environment indiscriminately. Its structure determines which perturbations from the environment are relevant and how they will be processed internally. The environment *triggers* internal changes; it does not *determine* them. The system and its environment become mutually adapted — *coupled* — through ongoing interaction, but without the environment directly programming the system's operations.

## Bryant's Usage

In [[onto-cartography]], Bryant adopts structural coupling as the mechanism by which machines form endo-relations with each other and draw inputs from their environment. A machine is never sealed off from its environment; it is selectively open to flows from other machines. But this openness is structured — shaped by the machine's own operational constitution.

The assemblage theory depends on structural coupling: machines form [[machinic-assemblage]]s by entering into structural couplings where the output of one machine becomes the input that triggers operations in another, and vice versa. When these couplings are sufficiently intimate and sustained, endo-relations form — the machines become constitutive of one another rather than merely adjacent.

Bryant also uses the structural coupling concept (without always naming it) in discussing how machines maintain themselves against [[entropy-and-assemblage-maintenance]]: negentropic machines must draw energy and matter from their environment, which requires structural openness; but this openness involves *selective* couplings that filter what flows are relevant. A city is structurally coupled to coal mines in the countryside, farms, other cities — not to everything indiscriminately.

## Operational Closure and Structural Openness

The paradox structural coupling resolves: how can a system be both closed (autonomous, self-producing) and open (responsive to environment)?

- **Operationally closed**: the system's operations refer only to other operations of the system; there is no direct line from external state to internal state
- **Structurally open**: the system's structure is shaped by its history of environmental perturbations; the environment influences (but does not determine) which perturbations matter

For Bryant, machines have a *virtual proper being* (their powers and capacities as potentialities) and *local manifestations* (their actual states at given moments). Structural coupling is what mediates between these: the environment perturbs the machine's local manifestations, but through a filter set by the machine's virtual constitution.

## Political and Ethical Stakes (Extended Mind)

Andy Clark's extended mind thesis is, in structural-coupling terms, the claim that human minds are structurally coupled to external media (notebooks, phones, the internet) in ways that make those media genuinely part of the cognitive system. The coupling is not merely useful: it constitutes a *different type of mind* with different powers.

This grounds Bryant's political argument: assemblages (persons) coupled to different media (guns, internet, calculators) are structurally different beings with genuinely different powers. "Mind-body assemblages that have guns and steel possess different powers than assemblages that have swords and spears." (p. 92)
