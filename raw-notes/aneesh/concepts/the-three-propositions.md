# The Three Propositions (Massey)

Massey's *For Space* (2005) builds its argument on three interlocking propositions stated in Chapter 1 (pp. 9–11). They are not independent claims but a single package: each requires the others.

## The Propositions

### 1. Space as Product of Interrelations

> "First, that we recognise space as the product of interrelations; as constituted through interactions, from the immensity of the global to the intimately tiny." (Massey 2005: 9)

Space is not a pre-given container within which things are placed. It is produced through the practices of relation between entities. Entities, relations, and spatiality are **co-constitutive**: none can be deduced from the others; all three emerge together.

Political resonance: this mirrors anti-essentialist politics (Mouffe 1993, 1995), which contests the relations through which identities are constructed rather than arguing on behalf of pre-constituted identities.

→ See [[space-as-relational-product]]

### 2. Space as Coexisting Heterogeneity

> "Second, that we understand space as the sphere of the possibility of the existence of multiplicity in the sense of contemporaneous plurality; as the sphere in which distinct trajectories coexist; as the sphere therefore of coexisting heterogeneity. Without space, no multiplicity; without multiplicity, no space." (Massey 2005: 9)

Space is what makes it *possible* for distinct trajectories to coexist without collapsing into a single narrative. Multiplicity and space are mutually constitutive: abolish space and you abolish multiplicity; abolish multiplicity and you have no space, only a single time-stream.

Political resonance: the neoliberal reduction of space to time — rendering Mozambique as simply "behind" the West — is a spatial move that abolishes the contemporaneity of others. A fully spatialised social theory "can force into the imagination a fuller recognition of the simultaneous coexistence of others with their own trajectories and their own stories to tell." (Massey 2005: 11)

→ See [[coexisting-heterogeneity]]

### 3. Space Always Under Construction

> "Third, that we recognise space as always under construction. Precisely because space on this reading is a product of relations-between, relations which are necessarily embedded material practices which have to be carried out, it is always in the process of being made. It is never finished; never closed. Perhaps we could imagine space as a simultaneity of stories-so-far." (Massey 2005: 9)

Space can never be a completed totality. Because it is produced through material practices that are always ongoing, any spatial configuration is provisional. "This is a space of loose ends and missing links." (Massey 2005: 11–12)

Political resonance: a closed, completed space forecloses politics. Only if space is open — and with it, the future — is genuine political contestation possible. This aligns Massey with Laclau's (1990) insistence that politics requires radical openness of the future, though Laclau paradoxically assigned this openness to time and closure to space.

→ See [[space-as-always-under-construction]]

## Why They Form a Package

The three propositions entail one another:
- If space is relational (1), then it must include multiplicity (2): relations require at least two relata.
- If multiplicity obtains (2), space cannot be a static given: multiple coexisting trajectories in motion means space itself is always shifting (3).
- If space is always under construction (3), it must be relational (1): construction happens through practices of relation.

## What Massey Is Against

Each proposition targets a specific default conception:
- Against: space as empty Euclidean container → Proposition 1
- Against: space as a single historical timeline with latecomers → Proposition 2
- Against: space as closed, complete, fixed — the "dead, the fixed" of Foucault's retrospection → Proposition 3
