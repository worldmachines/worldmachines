# Bryant's Pluralism of Worlds

## Core Claim

[[onto-cartography]] begins from a plural ontology: there is not *one* world that all machines inhabit but a *pluriverse* — many worlds, each a distinct ecology of loosely coupled machines. This is not an epistemological or perspectival pluralism (the view that different subjects perceive the world differently) but an ontological one: different assemblages of machines constitute genuinely distinct worlds with no overarching totality to contain them all.

> "Onto-cartography begins with the premise that there is not *one* world, but rather a plurality of worlds." (Bryant 2014: 116)

## Two Grounds for Pluralism

**1. Machines are external to their relations.** No machine is related to all others. Since worlds are nothing more than their component machines in relation, a machine that relates to no others whatsoever would belong to no world, or constitute a world of its own. Unrelated machines imply separate worlds. (See [[inter-world-opacity]] for the epistemological consequence.)

**2. No action at a distance.** Machines can only interact through material mediators, and mediators take time to cross space. The relation between any two machines at a distance is triadic: p → q → r, where q is the medium. This means that machines separated by space and time by more than any plausible mediating chain simply cannot interact — and where interaction is impossible, we should conclude separate worldhood. A supernova going supernova millions of light-years away belongs to a different world from ours not because we do not know about it, but because no material interaction between it and us is possible within any relevant timeframe.

> "Where two entities are unable to interact we should conclude that they belong to two different worlds." (Bryant 2014: 119)

## Consequences

**Worlds are bounded but fuzzy.** No sharp line demarcates one world from another; the bounds are set by the reach of material mediators, which is itself variable and changing. New mediators — printing presses, satellites, smartphones — extend the reach of a world, folding previously separate or distant machines into interaction. This can create new solidarities but also introduces entropy into older arrangements.

**No world is the world.** The human lifeworld, the social world, the political world — each is a particular ecology of machines, not the ground from which other worlds are derived. Bee worlds, factory worlds, river-system worlds, stellar worlds are all equally real. Bryant's pluralism is post-humanist at the cosmological level: the scope of "world" is not determined by the reach of human cognition or social meaning.

**Dark objects as evidence.** Bryant introduces [[bright-dim-dark-rogue-objects]] partly to account for the possible existence of machines that are entirely unrelated to any machine in a given world — machines that are "dark" not because they are concealed but because no flow reaches them. The possibility of dark objects is itself evidence that not everything belongs to one relational totality.

## Anti-Correlationist Stakes

The pluralism of worlds is a weapon against [[correlationism]]. If worlds were constituted by their correlation to a subject — Dasein's structural openness, the cogito's intentional horizon, language's semiotic structuring — then there would be as many worlds as subjects, but all of them dependent on subjects. Bryant's pluralism is realist: worlds are real ecological assemblages independent of any observing machine, including human observers. We did not constitute the world we find ourselves in; we were thrown into it and can only ever observe it partially through our own structural couplings.

## Relation to Other Concepts

- **flat-ontology** — Bryant's worlds are not hierarchically ordered; there is no uber-world containing all worlds.
- **[[inter-world-opacity]]** — the epistemological face of ontological pluralism: machines from different worlds may be mutually illegible.
- **[[worlding]]** — each world actively "worlds," produces its own field of relevance and salience.
- **[[luminosity-of-machines]]** — within a given world, machines have variable salience (bright, dim, dark, rogue).
- **[[world-machines]]** — Rao/Sathe's use of "world machine" as a civilizational-historical unit; Bryant supplies the ontological underpinning this concept presupposes but does not theorize.
- **[[Container Metaphysics]]** — Bryant explicitly rejects the idea that a world is a container; "all worlds are flat" (p. 114).
