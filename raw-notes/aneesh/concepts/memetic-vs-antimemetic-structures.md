# Memetic vs Antimemetic Structures

Power in complex societies is divided between **memetic** and **antimemetic** layers. This distinction explains the organizational logic of pre-modern states and recurs in modern institutional design.

## Memetic Layer: The Face of Power

Memetic structures are *designed to be seen and remembered*:
- Royal ceremony and divine legitimacy
- Monumental architecture (Alhambra, Taj Mahal, Forbidden City)
- Public decrees and symbolic acts
- Named individuals who "own" outcomes (kings, presidents, CEOs)

The memetic layer absorbs public attention — and public blame. It is the lightning rod.

## Antimemetic Layer: The Engine of Power

Antimemetic structures do the actual work of governance without being perceptible:
- Bureaucratic hierarchies with diffuse accountability
- Interpretive authorities ([[Ulama]], grand secretaries, regulatory agencies) that shape outcomes through application of rules
- Matrix reporting structures that obscure decision loci
- "Merely recording" officials who actually set policy

## The Functional Complementarity

The two layers need each other:
- Memetic rulers give administrators a human face to absorb anger
- Antimemetic administrators free rulers from operational detail while maintaining their symbolic function

The [[Ming Dynasty Grand Secretaries]] model illustrates this: the emperor could not process all correspondence, so he delegated to secretaries who "only recorded decisions" — in practice governing the empire.

## Breakdown Conditions

The equilibrium breaks when:
1. **The memetic layer becomes too discredited** — the lightning rod stops working; anger spreads to the machinery (revolutions, institutional collapse)
2. **Administrators become visible** — through leaks, FOIA requests, investigative journalism, or political exposure
3. **The antimemetic layer captures the memetic** — the "obscure directors" of [[Yes, Minister]] become the actual government
