# Lefebvre's Spatial Triad

**Source**: Henri Lefebvre, *The Production of Space* (1974/1991); deployed in [[aneesh-sathe]], *We Need Homes in the Delta Quadrant* (2025), §II

## The Three Terms

Lefebvre proposes that all space is socially produced through the interaction of three dialectically related elements:

1. **Representations of space** (*espace conçu* — conceived space): the abstract, conceptual space of planners, cartographers, economists, architects, and urban theorists. This is space as it is *thought* — grid, zone, map, blueprint, formula. It is the space of "scientists, planners, urbanists, technocratic subdividers and social engineers" (*The Production of Space*, p. 38).

2. **Spatial practices** (*espace perçu* — perceived space): the everyday, material practices through which space is produced, reproduced, and inhabited — routes, flows, routines, habits of movement and arrangement. This is space as it is *lived through the body* in quotidian activity. It is what you do without thinking: the paths you walk, the corners you use, the rhythms of your day in space.

3. **Representational spaces** (*espace vécu* — lived space): the space of inhabitants and users — saturated with memory, symbol, imagination, and emotion. This is space as it is *imagined and symbolized* — not as planners conceive it but as it is felt, storied, and invested with significance. Artists, writers, and inhabitants access this dimension.

Sathe's formulation: "the representations of space — the conceptual abstractions of cartographers, economists, and urban planners; the spatial practices of everyday life — our habits of movement and arrangement; and representational spaces — the dreamlike, lived realities saturated with memory, symbol, and emotion." (§II)

## The Modernity Diagnosis

Lefebvre's critical claim: in modernity, the first term — abstract space — has come to dominate. Space is planned, capitalized, and monetized; the grid and zone swallow the story and sanctuary. Abstract space serves the accumulation of capital: it must be homogeneous (interchangeable), fragmented (divisible into salable parcels), and hierarchical (organized by exchange-value).

This domination has political and phenomenological consequences: the symbolic richness of representational spaces is eroded; the bodily reality of spatial practices is reorganized to serve abstract space's imperatives; the person becomes a subject of planning rather than an inhabitant of lived space.

## Sathe's Use

Sathe deploys the triad as an on-ramp for thinking about modernity's spatial condition — establishing the regime of abstract space that Massey, Casey, and Harvey all respond to, and that the essay's argument about place-making implicitly resists. The essay's affirmation of lived place (representational spaces) against abstract space (representations of space) is the Lefebvrean argument.

The triad also helps frame the violence of exploration (§III): when capital imposes abstract space — subdivides, zones, and clears — it destroys the representational spaces of prior inhabitants, erasing the lived geographies that organized their world.

## Relation to Harvey

David Harvey is the principal Marxist geographer who takes up and extends Lefebvre's spatial triad. Harvey's account of [[uneven-spatial-production]] is the spatial triad's political economy — showing how the domination of abstract space manifests in concrete capital flows, cycles of investment and disinvestment, and the commodification of place.
