# Structural Openness and Operational Closure

[[levi-bryant]]'s Ch 3 (*Alien Phenomenology*) is organized around two paired concepts that define how every machine relates to its environment. Together they constitute the ontological basis for [[alien-phenomenology]].

## Structural Openness

Machines are not open to all possible flows in the world. They "specify" the flows to which they are responsive — drawing only on a small subset of what exists. This selectivity is called *structural openness* (from Maturana and Varela 1998: 79, cited p. 54).

Examples:
- **Mantis shrimp**: structurally open to eleven or twelve primary colors and to polarized, infrared, and ultraviolet light — flows entirely invisible to human visual systems (p. 54)
- **Bureaucracy**: structurally open primarily to *forms* — structured documents that reduce human communication to pre-defined parameters (pp. 55–56)
- **Neutrino**: structurally open to almost nothing — its neutral electric charge renders it unable to interact with most matter we encounter (p. 56)

Consequence: "It is not the case that everything can relate to everything else, nor that everything is related to everything else." (p. 54) The environment of any machine is always *more complex* than the machine's openness to that environment (Luhmann 1995: 25). This asymmetry means openness is always a *wager* — events to which a machine is blind can nonetheless destroy it (p. 60).

## Operational Closure

Once a flow enters a machine, that machine never encounters it *as it is*. The machine always transforms the flow according to its own internal operations. This is operational closure: the machine works over flows "in terms of the internal structure of the machine" (p. 56).

The paradigm example: a submarine's sonar returns a ping that encodes size, shape, and velocity of an underwater object. Within the submarine, this ping takes on a *functional* meaning (turn left, avoid collision) entirely different from what it means — or doesn't mean — for the shark it bounced off. "The shark, for example, is just navigating the ocean to find prey or to insure that water continues to flow over its gills so it can continue to breathe. In other words, once a flow enters a machine it takes on a different functional value — causally or in terms of meaning — than it had for the machine from which the flow issued." (p. 57)

This is the machine-ontological extension of Kant's Copernican revolution: objects must conform to the conditions of our cognition, not the reverse. But Bryant radicalizes: **what Kant says about cognitive machines holds for all machines**. "Rocks, neutrinos, insurance companies, and capybara no more encounter things as they are in themselves, than our own minds." (pp. 57–58)

## Firewalls

All machines therefore relate to each other *behind firewalls* — the operations of each machine transform the flows they receive before those flows can exert effects (p. 58, crediting Harman). No entity directly encounters another; each encounters the other as processed through its own operational structure. This means direct communication is always structurally impossible — there is always a translation, a loss, a remainder.

Luhmann's formulation: machines "cannot see what [they] cannot see" (Luhmann 2002a: 129, p. 59). The lived experience of a machine is, from its own standpoint, indistinguishable from the world itself — it only has access to operationally transformed flows in its internal world (p. 60).

## Epistemological and Political Consequences

**Epistemological closure and confirmation bias**: when a machine is structurally open only to flows that confirm its operational presuppositions, it becomes unable to respond to environmental events that fall outside its selective openness (pp. 60–61). Political ideological bubbles are one instance; corporate blindness to non-profit information is another.

**Lacan's aphorism generalized**: "all communication is miscommunication" — because a smile for a human signals goodwill, while a chimpanzee encounters the same bared teeth as an aggressive gesture (p. 59). Communication between machines with different structural openness and operational closure is always partial translation, never perfect transmission.

**Thermodynamic politics**: if institutions are machines with their own operational closure, then changing them requires engaging with the flows they are actually open to — not appealing to ethical flows they cannot register (see [[thermodynamic-politics]]).

## Mutability: Expanding Structural Openness

Structural openness and operational closure are not fixed. Organic and cognitive machines (plastic machines) can develop new forms of structural openness through evolution (developing new sensory apparatus) or *learning* (p. 61). The psychoanalyst learns to be structurally open to slips of the tongue and bungled actions as meaningful signals — expanding the range of flows to which they are sensitive, and developing new operations (interpretive operations) to work on these flows (pp. 61–62).
