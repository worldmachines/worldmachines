# Maps as Technologies of Power

**Source**: [[doreen-massey]], *For Space*, Ch. 11 (2005: 105–111)

## The Critique of Maps

Massey begins Chapter 11 with a meditation on maps and their seductive power. But this seduction is part of the problem. Maps present space as:
- A flat, continuous surface
- A completed product
- A coherent closed system
- Instantly and completely interconnected

In the "dominant form of mapping," the observer "position[s] themselves, themselves unobserved, outside and above the object of the gaze" (Massey 2005: 106). This is not simply a problem of the "view from above" (Massey is not critiquing verticality as such). The problem is that maps suggest "space is a surface — that it is the sphere of a completed horizontality" (Massey 2005: 106).

The more fundamental concern: maps present space as though "there is no room for surprises" (Massey 2005: 111). They pre-code all encounters. They eliminate the chance of space, the loose ends, the disconnections.

## The Postcolonial Critique and Its Limits

Massey endorses the postcolonial and feminist critiques of maps as "technologies of power" (Harley 1988, 1992) — maps encode taxonomic and ordering procedures, suppress alternative voices, make claims to singularity, stability, and closure. She also endorses the deconstructive project of opening up the "synchronic essentialism" of such maps (Huggan 1989), challenging their claims to a "point of presence whose stability cannot be guaranteed."

But she identifies a persistent limitation: these critiques deploy the figure of the **palimpsest**.

> "Palimpsest is too archaeological. In this story, the things that are missing (erased) from the map are somehow always things from 'before'. The gaps in representation (the erasures, the blind spots) are not the same as the discontinuities of the multiplicity in contemporaneous space; the latter are the mark of the coexistence of the coeval." (Massey 2005: 110)

The palimpsest suggests that alternative voices are *beneath* the dominant surface — earlier layers that have been written over. This is a valuable figure for domination and potential disruption. But it imagines the heterogeneous multiplicity as *layers* (archaeological, vertical) rather than as *simultaneous* coexistence. It fails to establish coevalness — the fact that the suppressed voices are not in a past layer but in the same present, alongside the dominant map.

Jose Rabasa's reading of colonial cartography illustrates this: the colonial imagination (visible in de Certeau's reading of de Léry) constructs Europe as the agent of history (time) and the colonised as passive space (to be written upon, explored, named). The blank page as metaphor captures both the colonial other as "absence of culture" and the idea of representation as writing-upon-space. Both rely on imagining space as a surface awaiting inscription — a surface without its own ongoing histories.

## Alternative Cartographies

Massey notes several attempts to map space differently:

**Pre-Columbian maps**: on both sides of the Atlantic before the colonial encounter, maps integrated time and space — they told stories. The Codex Xolotl shows events choreographed on the page; the directionality of footsteps makes clear that "there is no reversibility here: you can't go back in space-time" (Massey 2005: 108). These are maps that recounted histories rather than freezing space.

**Situationist cartographies**: sought to disorient, to expose the incoherences and fragmentations of urban space rather than its order. "This is the opposite of the synchronies of the structuralists: a representation of geographical space, not an a-spatial conceptual structure" (Massey 2005: 109). A representation of space that leaves "openings for something new."

**Deleuze and Guattari's map vs. tracing**: the map "is entirely oriented toward an experimentation in contact with the real. ... It is itself a part of the rhizome" (Deleuze and Guattari 1987: 12). This is closer to what Massey wants, though the dominant Western map aims at the opposite — the closed, coherent tracing.

## The Connection to Colonialism

The surface-imagination of space connects directly to colonial practice. If space is a surface, then the people who inhabit it are not *in history* — they are part of the landscape to be crossed. The encounter with Tenochtitlán is figured as "conquest of space" rather than "meeting of histories." Eric Wolf's challenge — *Europe and the People Without History* — is the corrective: every "space" being traversed is inhabited by people with ongoing stories-so-far. See also [[nostalgia-as-spacialization]].

De Certeau's analysis of writing as "the concrete activity that consists in constructing, on its own blank space (un espace propre) — the page — a text that has power over the exteriority from which it has first been isolated" (de Certeau 1984: 134, cited Massey 2005: 121) is precisely the imagination Massey is fighting: space as blank page awaiting the inscription of (Western, historical) agency.
