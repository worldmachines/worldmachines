# Performative Polymathism

## Definition

The desire to be recognized as accomplished in a field other than (and typically less prestigious than) one's actual domain of excellence. Identified by [[Michel de Montaigne]] as a recurring pattern among the powerful; explored autobiographically by [[Aneesh Sathe]] in [[Better Company Than Caesar]].

Performative polymathism is distinct from genuine intellectual range: it is oriented toward *reputation management* rather than the pleasures of knowing.

## Montaigne's Catalogue

Montaigne observes:

> "They would rather talk at length about other people's trade, instead of their own, and so hope to be seen as accomplished in yet another field."

His examples:
- **Caesar** — documented his bridge-building and siege-weapon engineering at length; silent about his military courage. Wanted to be known as an engineer, not a general.
- **Dionysus the Elder** — great military leader who pursued poetry despite poor skill. His reputation as a poet outlived his desire for it.
- **Periander** — a good doctor who sought a reputation as a bad poet (faulted by Archidamus).

## The Pattern in Modern Life

Sathe extends Montaigne's observation to contemporary elites: "We have leaders who are CEOs or investors and want to be known or seen as being accomplished engineers or physicists. Fields they are rather bad at." The mania appears to concentrate among "movers and shakers of history" — those whose primary domains of competence are, perhaps, insufficiently prestigious in their own minds.

## Contrast: Curiosity-Driven Learning

Sathe argues that genuine curiosity is the healthy alternative. "I don't really care much about being seen as an expert in writing... I would like to know how to do it and how to do it well. I am led by the pleasures of intense curiosity."

The distinction:
- **Performative**: oriented toward the audience, toward being *seen*
- **Curiosity-driven**: oriented toward the subject, toward knowing

See [[Curiosity as Ego Dissolution]] for the positive formulation.

## Why This Matters for Creative Work

Performative polymathism is also a hazard in [[The Tortured Artist Is So Yesterday]] framing — the tortured artist suffers for *recognition* as much as for the work itself. The shift toward collaborative creative labs may dissolve this, distributing ego investment across teams.
