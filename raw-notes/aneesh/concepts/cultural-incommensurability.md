# Cultural Incommensurability

## The Claim

Cultural incommensurability is the thesis that different historical cultures and civilizations embody forms of human excellence that cannot be ranked on a single scale. There is no common metric by which:
- The Homeric epics, Roman jurisprudence, and 18th-century French classicism can be placed in order of "advancement"
- The religious experience of Judean shepherds, the civic virtue of the Roman Republic, and the rational enlightenment of 18th-century Paris can be judged as progressive stages toward a single goal

Each is authentically itself; later does not mean better in any dimension that matters.

## Vico's Formulation

[[Giambattista Vico]] argues that each step on the ladder of human development "has its own equally authentic means of expression." The Homeric poems are an unsurpassable masterpiece — "but they could spring only from a brutal, stern, oligarchical, 'heroic' society, and later civilisations, however superior in other respects, did not and could not produce an art necessarily superior to Homer."

This strikes "a powerful blow at the notion of timeless truths and steady progress." The natural sciences deal with the "relatively unaltering nature of the physical world viewed from 'outside'"; humane studies view "social evolution from 'inside' by a species of empathetic insight."

## Herder's Formulation

[[Johann Gottfried Herder]] generalizes: "For Voltaire, Diderot, Helvétius, Holbach, Condorcet there is only universal civilisation, of which now one nation, now another, represents the richest flowering. For Herder there is a plurality of incommensurable cultures."

Each culture has its own [[Herder's Schwerpunkt|Schwerpunkt]], its own internal standard of value. "No culture is a mere means towards another; every human achievement, every human society is to be judged by its own internal standards." The absolute criteria of progress fashionable in Paris are a form of provincial arrogance masquerading as universalism.

## Berlin's Elaboration

Berlin uses Vico and Herder to ground his doctrine of [[Value Pluralism]]. The incommensurability of cultures is one expression of the incommensurability of values in general. If different civilizations embody genuinely different goods — civic virtue vs. Christian humility, heroic greatness vs. contemplative wisdom — and if these goods cannot be reduced to a common metric, then no utopian project can simultaneously realize all of them.

This has immediate political consequences: utopian projects that claim to realize all values simultaneously "are necessarily fraudulent or self-deceived."

## Distinction from Relativism

Berlin, following Vico and Herder, insists that incommensurability is not relativism:
- **Relativism**: values are mere expressions of culture or power; no cross-cultural judgement is possible
- **Incommensurability**: values are objectively real and binding within their cultural context, but there is no common scale to rank them

You can make cross-cultural judgements of a certain kind (this culture's practices are cruel; this civilization produced magnificent art) without claiming to place the whole culture on a universal scale of advancement.

## The Homeric Example (as Berlin reads Vico)

"The Homeric poems were an unsurpassable masterpiece, but they could spring only from a brutal, stern, oligarchical, 'heroic' society... Each culture expresses its own collective experience, each step on the ladder of human development has its own equally authentic means of expression." (p. 6)

This is not romantic primitivism — it's a structural point about the conditions of possibility of different art forms. Mass literacy may produce certain goods (widespread education, critical thinking) while making Homer impossible. You can regret the loss without claiming the trade-off was bad overall.

## Contemporary Relevance

Cultural incommensurability poses a direct challenge to any universal AI alignment scheme. If different cultures embody genuinely incommensurable values, then a single utility function for AI alignment is a category error — it imposes one culture's value ranking as universal.
