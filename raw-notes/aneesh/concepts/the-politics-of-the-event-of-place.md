# The Politics of the Event of Place

The argument of Chapter 13 of [[doreen-massey]]'s *For Space* (2005). A place is not a setting on which politics later happens; **place is already a political event** because it is the unchosen co-presence of multiple trajectories that have to find a way to share this here-and-now.

## The thesis

> "Place as an ever-shifting constellation of trajectories poses the question of our [[throwntogetherness]]." (Massey 2005: 151)

Three claims compressed:

1. **Place is event.** Not substance, not container, not essence — but a happening of trajectories meeting. (See [[places-as-events]].)
2. **The event demands negotiation.** The trajectories meeting do not arrive harmonised. They are different rhythms, different durations, different aims. They have to find a way.
3. **The negotiation is political.** Not optionally political (after the constituents have arrived) but constitutively political — the place *exists* because the negotiation is going on.

## Why "event" matters

Calling place an event rather than a process foregrounds:

- **Contingency** — the constellation could have been otherwise; trajectories happened to arrive here.
- **Disruption** — events can be surprising; the chance of space is real.
- **Singularity** — each place's event is unique; no template covers them all.

This is the philosophical ground for Ch. 14's claim that there are [[no-rules-of-space-and-place]].

## The Hamburg vignette

Chapter 13 opens with the [[hamburgs-oldest-immigrant]] vignette: a glacial erratic boulder, brought south by the ice millennia ago, discovered in the Elbe in 1999 and adopted by Hamburg's official-for-foreign-immigrants on the eve of the 2000 citizenship reform. The poster — gateway cut through the boulder, city visible — asks: how long does one have to have been here to be "local"?

Massey reads this as the political artefact corresponding to her ontology. The boulder is itself an immigrant; the city's "indigeneity" was always already mixed; the political question of "who belongs" cannot be answered by appeal to ground-truth localness, only by a negotiation of throwntogetherness.

## The public-space dimension

Chapter 13 develops the argument through urban public space. Critique of the "decline of public space" rhetoric is partial: Massey grants that private appropriation (malls, gated communities) is real, but adds that the romanticisation of public space as **empty harmonious enclosure** misses the point. Genuine public space is the site of *conflict* — pigeons-vs-tourists in Trafalgar Square, day-vs-night uses of parks, contested terms of negotiated exclusion. Drawing on Lefort and Deutsche:

> "Conflict is not something that befalls an originally, or potentially, harmonious urban space. Urban space is the product of conflict." (Deutsche 1996: 278, quoted 153)

Democratic public space is not harmony achieved but the legitimacy of ongoing debate about what is legitimate.

## The world-city dimension

Chapter 13 also develops the **London-as-world-city** case (see [[place-as-a-clash-of-trajectories]] when written; for now, included here):

- London's "success" as a global financial centre is one element in the production of poverty and exclusion across the rest of the city.
- The conjunction should not be "London is successful **but** there is still poverty," but "London is successful **and partly as a result** there is poverty."
- The political question is not how to manage the symptoms (London Weighting, affordable housing) but whether to challenge the model of growth itself.
- A relational politics requires "reimagining the city as a clash of trajectories" rather than a coherent unity with marginal problems.

## The human-nonhuman dimension

Chapter 13 closes with Los Angeles and Amazonia — places whose politics of throwntogetherness includes negotiation with the nonhuman. Tectonic plates, river basins, bush fires, coyotes — all are trajectories that the city must negotiate. Massey's claim: the nonhuman has its trajectories too, and the event of place demands the same political care.
