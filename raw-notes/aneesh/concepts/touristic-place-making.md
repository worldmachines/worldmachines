# Touristic Place-Making

## Core Claim

**Touristic place-making** is the process by which places acquire identity and legibility through the attention of outsiders rather than through the lived experience of insiders. In Tuan's framework, it is one of the key mechanisms of [[visibility-and-the-creation-of-place]], but it comes with a paradox: the very act of making a place visible to tourists tends to extinguish the quality that made it worth visiting.

Tuan draws on Robert Pirsig's *Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance* for the sharpest statement of the paradox:

> "At the lake we stop and mingle affably with the small crowd of tourists holding cameras and children yelling, 'Don't go too close!' and see cars and campers with all different license plates, and see the Crater Lake with a feeling of 'Well, there it is,' just as the pictures show. I watch the other tourists, all of whom seem to have out-of-place looks too. I have no resentment at all this, just a feeling that it's all unreal and that the quality of the lake is smothered by the fact that it's so pointed to. You point to something as having Quality and the Quality tends to go away. Quality is what you see out of the corner of your eye." (*Space and Place*, p. 147)

The tourist apparatus — designated scenic spots, cameras, license plates from distant states — creates the conditions for pointed attention that destroys the quality it means to celebrate.

## The Tourist vs. the Insider

Tuan sets up a systematic contrast between the tourist and the native throughout Chapters 10 and 12:

| Tourist | Native |
|---------|--------|
| Sees frontally, evaluates consciously | Perceives peripherally, kinesthetically |
| Must see without the support of accumulated familiarity | Dwells within a world of known sights, sounds, smells |
| Becomes "a specialized and unanchored being, a sightseer who samples life effortlessly" | Inhabits the "real" — the familiar daily round |
| Experiences place as an object for contemplation | Experiences place as the ground of living |

"Seeing has the effect of putting a distance between self and object. What we see is always 'out there.' Things too close to us can be handled, smelled, and tasted, but they cannot be seen — at least not clearly" (p. 146).

This is not a simple condemnation of tourism. Tuan acknowledges that tourist perception can notice what the native overlooks. But the tourist's experience is structurally different — and structurally deficient as a form of place-knowledge.

## Outsider Naming and Imposed Identity

Touristic place-making can occur without the tourist's active participation. The outsider gaze, once institutionalized, can impose place-identity on residents who had none. Derogatory names like "Back of the Yard" and "Jew Town" — imposed by fearful outsiders on Chicago neighborhoods — eventually became real for residents, who came to understand themselves as living in bounded places with certain characters (p. 172).

Similarly, Greenwich Village "owes its Bohemian image not a little to promotion by outside media and real estate agents" (p. 172). The identity is not false — it becomes real through social recognition — but its origin is external.

Slums and skid rows in North American cities "are so peculiar from the standpoint of middle-class values that they become tourist attractions. Air-conditioned buses take upright small-town citizens through Chicago's skid row as if it were a titillating peep show" (p. 172). This is touristic place-making in its most extractive form: the place exists, for outsiders, as spectacle.

## The Tourist Visibility Loop

Touristic place-making operates as a feedback loop:

1. A place acquires visibility — through art, naming, a guidebook, or a significant event.
2. Visitors arrive expecting to see what has been represented.
3. Infrastructure (hotels, signs, designated viewpoints) is built to support the visit.
4. The infrastructure becomes part of the place's representation.
5. More visitors arrive, drawn by the accumulated representation.

At each step, the original intimate or accidental quality of the place is further subordinated to its touristic image. The place that was known through the corner of the eye becomes known through the camera lens.

## The Modern Platform Extension

Tuan could not have anticipated Instagram, but his framework predicts its dynamics precisely. See [[insta-places]] and [[algorithmic-curation-as-place-making]]:

- Places are photographed and shared, creating touristic visibility.
- The visible places attract more visitors who photograph and share.
- Places begin to be designed for their image-value — the mural wall, the colorful door, the perfectly lit dish.
- The designed-for-image place is the contemporary *place royale*: deliberate place-manufacture for external consumption.

## Synthesis Seeds

- **Sathe's tourism/explorer essay**: Tuan's tourist vs. native contrast directly feeds the explorer vs. tourist distinction that underlies the essay project. The tourist moves through a pre-made world of named, legible places; the explorer (pioneer, namer) creates place through attention and naming. The digital equivalent: the person who discovers a corner of the internet vs. the person who arrives after the algorithm has made it "a place."
- **Authenticity and the touristic gaze**: Once a place is touristically visible, can the original quality be recovered? Tuan's Pirsig example suggests no — not without withdrawing the pointing finger. This is an argument against certain forms of cultural preservation and heritage tourism.
