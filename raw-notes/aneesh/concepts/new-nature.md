# New Nature

## Definition

From [[venkatesh-rao]]:

> "Regimes of reality governed by technologically mediated laws that are nearly as inviolable, immutable, and persistent as those of nature."

New Nature is what technology becomes when it achieves the density, persistence, and competitive ecology of biological nature. It is not a metaphor — it is a claim that certain technological regimes genuinely constitute a new form of nature with its own evolution, murder, and illegible fitness.

## The Wrong Image

Most people, hearing "New Nature," picture the **solarpunk left**: robots in meadows with children and deer, idyllic harmony.

The correct image is the **mussel-covered pier piling at low tide**: layers of organisms competing fiercely for sunlight, colonizing every available inch, illegible fitness forces operating in near-invisibility. Or equivalently: the technological tangled bank.

## Technological Tangled Bank

Rao extends Darwin's famous tangled bank passage to technology:

> "It is interesting to contemplate a technological tangled bank, clothed with many technologies of many kinds, with mile-markers weaving by old railroads, with various vehicles flitting about, and with fiber crawling through the damp earth..."

The point: New Nature has the same structure as Old Nature — murderous competitiveness lurking beneath apparent harmony. The "murder" in New Nature is not BattleBots or military conflict. It is:
- Vicious competitiveness in near-invisibility
- Dark Forests of hidden conflict beneath protocol grammars
- Entities asserting [[Liveness]] against each other in routine, boring interaction

## What Murder in New Nature Is Not

The "Bloodsport Planet" aesthetics of stylized Darwinism — Platonic fitness ideals, guns and masculinity, sanitized spectatorship of violence — is exactly the *wrong* model. [[Werner Herzog]]'s critique applies: it is "badly pronounced and half-finished sentences out of a stupid suburban novel."

Real Darwinian competition is illegible, bleak, routine. The stylized version can rule a stadium; it cannot rule a live planet.

## Relation to Old Nature

If New Nature is like Old Nature, we should expect:
- A harmony of overwhelming and collective murder (Herzog)
- Illegible fitness criteria — not legible aesthetic hierarchies
- Inseparable entanglement of liveness and death

The useless machine is the "e. coli of artificial liveness" — the genesis species of New Nature. More than Conway's Game of Life, it captures the essence: implacably defended uselessness.

## Relation to Chaisson's Life Era

Chaisson's [[Life Era]] thesis describes civilization becoming a cosmological actor — technology and life merging into a "technoculture" that dominates matter as matter once dominated radiation. This is structurally the same as Rao's New Nature, though Chaisson frames it as progress and Rao frames it as ecology (with all of ecology's murderousness).
