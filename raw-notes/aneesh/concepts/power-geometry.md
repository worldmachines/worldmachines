# Power-Geometry

**Source**: [[doreen-massey]], *Space, Place and Gender* (1994) and *For Space* (2005), esp. the Part Three epilogue (pp. 95–103)

## The Concept

"Power-geometry" is Massey's term for the differentiated social relations of mobility and flow in globalised space. The key claim: not everyone stands in the same relation to the flows that constitute contemporary globalisation. While some people "initiate flows and are in charge of them," others are "at the receiving end of time-space compression" (Massey 1994: 149).

Crucially, it is not simply a matter of who moves and who stays. The homeless person sleeping in a railway station and the international business executive both experience spatial mobility; but their power-geometry positions are radically different. The mobile migrant worker is moved by others' decisions; the mobile jet-setter decides for themselves. Movement is not in itself liberating; power-geometry asks *who controls movement*.

## The Three Dimensions

Massey identifies multiple dimensions of power-geometry:

1. **Who initiates flows vs. who is the object of flows**: Some people and institutions create the movements (capital investment decisions, military deployments, migration policies); others experience those movements as external forces acting upon them.

2. **Who is on the move vs. who is not**: mobility is differentially distributed by class, gender, race, and nationality. The refugee who cannot move is as much a product of the same processes that produce the international financial trader who moves constantly.

3. **Who controls representation**: the power to name, map, and narrate space is itself unevenly distributed. Colonial mapping is a historical instance; contemporary media geography is another.

## The Political Geography of Places

Power-geometry is the conceptual tool for a *differentiated* politics of place and globalisation. Against two inadequate alternatives:

- **Undifferentiated cosmopolitanism**: space is now free, movement is liberation, place-attachment is backward. This ignores that mobility is a power, not a universal condition.
- **Undifferentiated defensive localism**: all places are victims of globalisation; we must defend the local against the global. This ignores that some places *produce* the global.

Massey's alternative: places stand in contrasting relations to the global, depending on their position within wider power-geometries:

> "Different places occupy distinct positions within the wider power-geometries of the global. In consequence, both the possibilities for intervention in (the degree of purchase upon), and the nature of the potential political relationship to [...] these wider constitutive relations, will also vary." (Massey 2005: 101)

Mali and London are not in the same position. The appropriate politics differs accordingly. London (or the USA) cannot simply "defend the local" — because the local *is* producing the global, and the local conditions are precisely what a politics against neoliberal globalisation must challenge.

## The Feminist Origin

The concept has its roots in Massey's feminist geography. The earliest clear statement is in her famous 1991 essay "A Global Sense of Place" (*Marxism Today*), where she notes that older white men and young Black women in the same London neighbourhood have completely different relations to the "time-space compression" that Jameson and Harvey celebrate. The jet-setting academic celebrating global flows is already in a specific power-geometry position.

This is part of Massey's broader critique of David Harvey's *The Condition of Postmodernity* (1989): Harvey's analysis of time-space compression is sharp, but it treats mobility and disorientation as universal experiences, blinding it to the differential power relations that constitute those experiences.

## The "Topologies of Power"

In *For Space*, Massey refines the concept: "Understanding space as the constant open production of the topologies of power" (Massey 2005: 101). The spatial configurations of power are not fixed maps but ongoing productions — topologies that are themselves constituted through practices and relations. This connects to [[bryant-topology-of-worlds]]: topology is a matter of which entities are near or far, connected or disconnected, in relational rather than Euclidean terms.

## Relation to the Chance of Space

Power-geometry also appears in the discussion of the "chance of space" (Ch. 11). The element of surprise and encounter in space is not equally distributed: who gets to have unexpected encounters, who is always already encountered by others (and on whose terms), who is rendered static while others move — these are power-geometry questions. The romanticisation of the flâneur's serendipitous city-wandering ignores that this wandering presupposes specific class, gender, and racial positions.
