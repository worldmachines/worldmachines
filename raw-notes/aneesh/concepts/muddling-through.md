# Muddling Through

## Definition

"Muddling through" describes the characteristic mode of expert problem-solving at the frontier of a domain — not the confident application of established technique, but careful, risk-managed exploration in genuinely uncertain territory. The expert picks between options that *seem* likely to succeed based on accumulated knowledge and judgment, without certainty.

The concept appears in [[Dancing on the Shoulders of Giants]] as the key distinction between novice and expert behavior at the knowledge frontier: both face genuine uncertainty, but the expert's denser knowledge network allows more reliable intuitions about which directions are more promising.

## Why It Matters

The standard picture of expertise emphasizes confident mastery. Muddling through acknowledges that at the frontier — where expertise is most needed — even experts don't know the answer. What distinguishes expert muddling from novice flailing is the quality of the heuristics guiding the exploration.

This matters for AI because:
1. AI systems do not currently muddle through — they either retrieve from training data or hallucinate. They lack the judgment about which direction is "likely" to succeed in unfamiliar territory.
2. The [[Composite AI Architecture (Experts + LLM + KG)]] preserves muddling through as a human function: the domain expert navigates uncertainty; the LLM+KG provides structured knowledge to reduce the search space.

## Relation to Wright's Law

In the industries that follow [[Wright's Law]], muddling through at scale is institutionalized: factory workers, engineers, and managers each apply local expertise in ways that aggregate into systematic efficiency gains. In healthcare, muddling through stays private — individual clinicians develop hacks that don't propagate.
