# Social Infrastructure

## Definition

Social infrastructure refers to the physical spaces, institutions, and services that enable people to come together, form relationships, and sustain community life. Examples include:

- Public parks and recreation facilities
- Public libraries
- Community centers and civic halls
- Subsidized performing arts venues
- Publicly maintained plazas and gathering spaces
- Swimming pools, sports facilities, playgrounds

Eric Klinenberg (in *Palaces for the People*, 2018) developed the concept in its sociological form, arguing that social infrastructure is as important to social cohesion as physical infrastructure is to economic life.

## The Case for Public Investment

The argument for subsidizing social infrastructure parallels the argument for physical infrastructure:
- Social experiences are goods that generate positive externalities (reduced loneliness, civic participation, trust) that markets undervalue
- Many social experiences are subject to [[Baumol's Cost Disease]] — their relative prices will rise indefinitely without subsidy
- The market alternative (private, for-profit social venues) systematically selects against genuine sociality in favor of throughput and efficiency

[[Alex Mayyasi]] asks whether the logic that justifies public pools and libraries should also extend to bowling alleys, dive bars, and sit-down restaurants — "pro-social businesses" whose value is not captured in market prices.

## The Subsidy Model

One proposed mechanism: tax-deductible donations to "pro-social businesses," modeled on the existing charity exemption for arts organizations. If a violin performance is treated as a cultural good worth subsidizing, why not a neighborhood bar that functions as the same community's third place?

This is admittedly a difficult policy to design — who decides which businesses qualify? — but the underlying logic is sound: social experiences have positive externalities that the market systematically underprices.

## Connection to Baumol

Social infrastructure spending is precisely the "dependable solution" that Baumol identified for cost disease: progressive taxation in a growing economy funds services that the market will not provide at accessible prices. Healthcare and education are the canonical cases; social life is the underexplored extension. See [[Baumol's Cost Disease]].
