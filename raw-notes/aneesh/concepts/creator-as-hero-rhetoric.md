# Creator-as-Hero Rhetoric

The discursive strategy through which platforms — Substack, YouTube, TikTok, Patreon — frame their role as empowering individual creators, positioning those creators as autonomous cultural heroes whose success is self-generated. The rhetoric naturalizes the platform's extraction of a revenue cut by making the platform's role invisible or incidental.

## The Specific Case

[[Aneesh Sathe]] cites Substack's fundraise announcement calling writers "the heroes of culture" as a paradigmatic example. The alarm it should trigger: the louder a platform's rhetoric of creator empowerment, the more thoroughly the platform has structured the underlying relationship to extract value while the creator bears risk.

## Structure of the Ideological Move

1. **Emphasis on creator agency** ("you set your own schedule, you build your audience")
2. **Platform as neutral background** ("we just provide the tools")
3. **Success as individual achievement** ("your voice, your audience, your business")
4. **Risk invisible** (failed creators are simply not talked about; they bore 100% of their own losses)
5. **Platform profit naturalized** ("the cut" framed as a fair exchange for access)

## Comparison to Historical Ideologies

- The putting-out merchant who praised the skilled handloom weaver while extracting margin on every yard
- Factory philanthropy (Carnegie, Pullman) — benevolence framing that obscured labor suppression
- [[Gig Economy]] framing: "be your own boss" as cover for zero-hours employment without protections

## Critique

The rhetoric is not merely false — it may be psychologically functional for creators who need motivation to endure the high failure rate of platform content. But it systematically prevents creators from analyzing and contesting the underlying economic structure.
