# Explorer and Pioneer Naming

**Source**: [[tuan-1991-language-and-the-making-of-place]]

## The Standard Account and Its Limits

Standard historical accounts treat place as a product of physical transformation: place emerged "with the first swing of the pioneer's ax." A clearing in the woods becomes a farm; a farm becomes a village; a village becomes a town. Place is the result of material labor on nature.

Tuan argues this is too late. The conversion of space into place — the "ordering of nature" — occurred with "the first ritual act of possession" (p. 687), long before any tree was felled.

## Columbus at Guanahani

When Columbus came ashore at Guanahani, "his captains beside him, the royal standard was displayed, the banners of the expedition were unfurled, one bearing an F and the other a Y for Ferdinand and Isabella, and over each initial there was displayed a crown." The crews knelt; they embraced the ground with ceremonial tears; two notary publics "solemnly recorded the words and the ceremony" (Jones 1964, 100).

This act established legal-political possession. But for the participants it carried "religious-baptismal significance" as well. Citing Mircea Eliade: when Spanish and Portuguese conquistadores raised the cross over new territories, they "consecrated them in the name of Jesus Christ and believed that, by doing so, they enabled the territories to undergo a 'new birth'" (Eliade 1959, 32). The newly discovered country was "recreated" by the cross — reinstated into God's cosmos. Prior existence was erased; a new place began.

## Naming as Navigation Prerequisite

Confronted by vast stretches of unfamiliar land, "explorers, in order to go forward at all, needed to use names and words to differentiate space, to call this feature a 'mount' and that feature a 'river'" (p. 688). The names used were often inappropriate to Australian reality — "mount," "meadow," "pasture" — carrying images from western Europe that fit poorly. But for the purpose of the explorer, fitness to local reality mattered less than fitness to navigational purpose: "an explorer needed to have home bases and rest stops along a path that opened to the horizon and led to an ultimate destination" (Carter 1988, 47–55, as cited in Tuan).

This is Paul Carter's argument in *The Road to Botany Bay*: naming is not a secondary act of labeling a known route; it is constitutive of the route itself. Without names to mark waypoints, forward movement is impossible — not just difficult but conceptually unavailable. The name creates the waypoint; the waypoint enables the journey.

## The Pioneer's Journal

The explorer who keeps journals, field notes, and eventually rewrites and publishes them achieves something further: his "private experiences — his temporary places of habitation — could gain access to and take hold on public consciousness and achieve thereby a higher degree of stability and permanence even though no physical manipulation of nature had occurred" (p. 688). "Mount Prospect" became not only a place for the pioneer — the spot where he once stood — but also "a virtual or possible place at which to gain a prospect for all who read his narrative and wished to follow his footsteps" (p. 688).

Writing transforms private, temporary place into public, permanent landmark. The map and the journal together accomplish a geographical act that no amount of physical clearing and building can fully replicate.

## The Transition to Settled Life

In the course of time, pioneer farmers moved in, built houses, and created material places. "Mount Prospect" or "Mount Misery" — now standing on a corner of the ranch — "would have lost much of its original meaning as the marker of a traveling narrative and a process of discovery" (p. 688). A place created by language (the explorer's naming narrative) is later partially superseded by a place created by material transformation (the rancher's built world). But it is not erased. The rancher might sit on the porch, "rehearse and thereby enhance his awareness of the hill's emotional coloring by retelling its story to a visitor" (p. 688) — returning to language to sustain what language first created.

## Textbook Geography and Regional Identity

Eventually, the route maps of explorers and surveyors gave way to spatial maps showing areas and sites rather than routes, and then to "compendious 'coffee-table' atlases." The standard school textbook emerged, depicting history of settlement impersonally. "In its own way, it could promote a sense of regional identity and of place" (p. 688) — a different kind of linguistic place-maintenance, public and official rather than narrative and personal.
