# Epistemic Monoculture

## Definition

Epistemic monoculture is the condition in which a population or community converges toward a single dominant set of:
- **Epistemic standards** (what counts as evidence, what counts as good reasoning)
- **Reasoning styles** (how inferences are drawn, what heuristics are privileged)
- **Discourse norms** (what counts as credible, authoritative, or well-expressed)
- **Problem framings** (which questions are worth asking, which variables are salient)

The agricultural metaphor is apt: monocultures are efficient and high-yield under normal conditions but catastrophically vulnerable to novel stresses that the dominant crop cannot handle.

## How It Arises

1. **Institutional convergence**: standardized education, credentialing, and professional norms create shared epistemic habits within fields
2. **Media homogenization**: mass media (and now algorithmic curation) creates shared information environments
3. **Network effects**: when a reasoning style or discourse norm becomes dominant, deviating from it becomes costly — even independent of its epistemic merits
4. **AI-mediated homogenization**: LLMs trained on dominant corpora actively push users toward the statistical center of their training distribution (see [[LLM Training Data Bias]])

## The LLM Case

USC researchers (Dehghani, Sourati, 2026) document a specific mechanism: LLMs favor [[Chain-of-Thought Reasoning]] (linear, explicit step-by-step logic) and the linguistic/cultural norms of WEIRD societies. As billions of users rely on the same handful of models, what counts as "credible speech, correct perspective, or even good reasoning" is being quietly redefined by the model's outputs. See [[USC AI Homogenization Research]].

## Consequences

- Groups lose the benefit of diverse cognitive search
- Novel or catastrophic problems encounter a population with fewer cognitive tools
- Minority reasoning traditions and epistemic practices are gradually displaced
- The appearance of consensus can mask suppressed disagreement

## Historical Analogies

- The dominance of positivism in 20th-century social sciences — which suppressed interpretive and hermeneutic approaches for decades
- The spread of neoclassical economics as the default framework for social analysis
- The marginalization of non-Western scientific traditions during colonial periods

## Relationship to Value Pluralism

[[Isaiah Berlin]]'s [[Value Pluralism]] is a normative claim that diversity of *values* is genuine and irresolvable. Epistemic monoculture extends this concern to *reasoning processes* — not just what people value but how they think. The counter-Enlightenment worry (Vico, Herder) was that rationalist universalism erases the distinctive cognitive and cultural achievements of particular peoples.
