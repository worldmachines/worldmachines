# Positive versus Negative Multiplicity

A distinction [[doreen-massey]] develops in Chapter 4 of *For Space* (2005), drawing on the Spinoza–Bergson–Deleuze tradition of affirmative philosophy.

## The two ontologies

**Negative multiplicity** (her diagnosis of [[jacques-derrida]]'s spacing): begin from a presumed unity — an integrity, a self-identity, a coherent structure. Show that this unity is constitutively dependent on what it has set aside. The "Other" is the constitutive outside that disrupts the One from within. Difference is produced **negatively** — by the expulsion that the One performs in order to be itself, and by the subsequent recognition that the expelled Other still inhabits the One.

**Positive multiplicity** (her own commitment): begin from plurality. Don't postulate a unity that gets disrupted; recognise that there were always many trajectories from the start. Difference is the **prior fact**, not the residue of an interrupted identity.

> "It is in their rejection of this negativity, their emphasis on affirmation, that the line of philosophy Spinoza–Bergson–Deleuze has more to offer a re-thinking of space." (Massey 2005: 51)

## Why it matters for space

If you start with the negative ontology, space becomes the medium of disruption — the trace of what has been set aside, the index of an irreducible exterior. This is Derrida's spacing. It is generative within the textual frame but it pulls toward a horizontal imagination of space-as-disrupted-surface. See [[horizontalities-of-deconstruction]].

If you start with the positive ontology, space is the *condition* of multiplicity, not the trace of its disruption. You don't need a One that fails; you can begin with the manifold itself. This is what Massey wants — see [[space-as-the-dimension-of-multiplicity]].

## The Houdebine exchange

Massey reads the Derrida–Houdebine exchange (in the *Positions* interview, Derrida 1972/1987: 80 ff) as a live attempt to make this distinction within deconstruction itself. Houdebine repeatedly presses Derrida on whether heterogeneity is fully captured by spacing-as-expulsion or whether spacing also marks the **positivity** of a position. Derrida gradually concedes the latter is "the newest and most important point" of the conversation (p. 94). But the concession is grudging; the default reflex of deconstruction is still negative.
