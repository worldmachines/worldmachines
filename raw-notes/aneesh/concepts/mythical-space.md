# Mythical Space

**Primary source**: Yi-Fu Tuan, *Space and Place* (1977/2002), Chapter 7, pp. 85–100
**See also**: [[tuan-2002-space-and-place-ch7-9]], [[sacred-and-profane-space-tuan]], [[cosmogonic-architecture]], [[mircea-eliade]]

---

## The Two Forms

Tuan distinguishes two structurally different but related kinds of mythical space.

### 1. Diffuse Mythical Space

Every empirical world — the immediate known environment — is fringed by a larger, symbolically charged field of hazy knowledge. This ambient mythic horizon is not mere ignorance; it is structurally necessary:

> "Facts require contexts in order to have meaning, and contexts invariably grow fuzzy and mythical around the edges." (p. 88)

In contemporary Western society, citizens of a neighborhood know their local area well but share a common "store of hazy knowledge (myths) concerning a far larger field — the region or nation — in which their own local areas are embedded" (p. 88). This hazy field is not redundant or merely erroneous; it provides the context within which empirical knowledge coheres.

This form is **pre-reflective and ambient**: it is not the product of systematic cosmological elaboration but rather the necessary penumbra of any particular known world.

### 2. Oriented Cosmological Space

The second kind is more deliberately articulated — a world-view or cosmology in which the human being stands at the center of a universe organized by cardinal points and qualitative associations. This is the spatial form of *homo microcosmus*.

Structural features that recur across cultures (Saulteaux, Chinese, European medieval, Hopi, Pueblo, Maya):
- **Anthropocentrism**: man at the center, the cosmos organized around human presence and need
- **Cardinal orientation**: the four directions as qualitatively differentiated places, not merely navigational vectors — "directions are primarily places — 'homes' — rather than courses of movement in space" (p. 92)
- **Resonance over causation**: in Chinese cosmology especially, things belonging to the same class affect each other not mechanically but sympathetically — change one and "all the others will be affected in a process like a multiple echo" (p. 97)
- **Infinite divisibility**: not only the cosmos but each nested unit — empire, city, house, single shelter — can be an image of the whole. "In mythical thought the part can symbolize the whole and have its full potency" (p. 99–100)
- **Personality imputed to space**: natural forces are located, named, and personified, "thus transforming space in effect into place" (p. 91)

## Two Schemata of Cosmic Relation

Tuan identifies two organizing schemata for how humans relate body to universe:

**Schema A — Body as Microcosm**: The earth (and cosmos) is the human body writ large. Rock as bone, water as blood, clouds as breath. This schema is widespread across the Dogon, medieval European, Chinese popular, and North American Indian traditions. It does not impose a clear spatial organization on the earth's surface but rather establishes qualitative analogies.

**Schema B — Man at the Cardinal Center**: Human settlement is oriented to the four cardinal points, each associated with colors, animals, seasons, elements, and moral qualities. This schema is spatially explicit. Its most fully developed forms appear in Pueblo Indian, Chinese, and Maya cosmologies. It is this schema that generates mythical space in the strict sense — a structured, differentiated, cardinally-organized spatial field in which every part has its location and significance.

## Mythical Space as Matrix

Mythical space is the conceptual predecessor of both pragmatic space and abstract geometric space. It is not a primitive error to be corrected but the originary form of spatial meaning-making:

> "Mythical space is an intellectual construct. It can be very elaborate. Mythical space is also a response of feeling and imagination to fundamental human needs. It differs from pragmatic and scientifically conceived spaces in that it ignores the logic of exclusion and contradiction. Logically a cosmos can have only one center; in mythical thought it can have many centers." (p. 99)

This openness to contradiction is a feature, not a bug: mythical space is capacious enough to hold micro and macro as mutual images, center and periphery as interpenetrating, past and mythic distance as coextensive.

## Architecture as Mythical Space Made Tangible

The chapter's closing move is the bridge to Chapter 8: architectural space is mythical space given material form and human scale. "Architectural space — a house, a temple, or a city — is a microcosm possessing a lucidity that natural features lack" (p. 100). Architecture does not merely represent the cosmos; it **is** a cosmos, scaled to human sensory access.
