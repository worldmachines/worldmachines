# Trajectories as Stories-So-Far

**Source**: [[doreen-massey]], *For Space*, esp. Chs. 11–12 (2005: 107–145)

## The Formulation

Massey's process-ontology finds its most accessible expression in the language of **trajectories** and **stories-so-far**. Rather than speaking of entities (places, people, institutions, landscapes) as things with properties, she speaks of them as ongoing processes — as trajectories through time-space that happen to be at a particular moment of their unfolding.

The phrase "stories-so-far" appears most explicitly in Chapter 12: "if space is rather a simultaneity of stories-so-far, then places are collections of those stories, articulations within the wider power geometries of space" (Massey 2005: 130). A place is where some of those stories — those ongoing trajectories — happen to intersect at a given here-and-now.

## Why "Stories" Rather Than "Processes"

The word "stories" carries a specific weight in Massey's vocabulary. Stories:
- Have a direction (they go somewhere; they are not reversible)
- Are not finished (every story is "so far" — its ending is not yet written)
- Carry their histories within them (where a story is now is shaped by where it has been)
- Are multiple and concurrent (many stories can be in progress simultaneously without collapsing into a single narrative)

De Certeau, whom Massey otherwise criticises, had noted that trajectories traced on a map are turned into static lines — movement becomes a trace, a record, something reversible. Massey insists (citing the Codex Xolotl's directional footsteps): "you can't go back in space-time" (Massey 2005: 101). The story cannot be unwound.

## Application to Persons

A person is a trajectory. The commuter on the train from London to Milton Keynes is not just "crossing space" — she is herself a continuing story, intersecting with the stories of London (which has moved on since she left), the train, the landscape outside the window, and Milton Keynes (which is also continuing in her absence). Movement is not traversal of a neutral surface but a weaving together of multiple trajectories.

## Application to Places

A place is a collection of trajectories, momentarily articulated. When Massey arrives back at Euston Station she "reimmerse[s] myself into (just a few of) the stories of London. I weave together the stories which make this 'here and now' for me" (Massey 2005: 130). The place is not a container for these stories; it *is* their current intersection. Each arrival at a place is a re-entering of an ongoing set of processes that have continued in one's absence.

## Application to Geology

The most dramatic extension of this language is to the geological: Skiddaw is a trajectory. Its rocks are "stories-so-far" — having been deposited in the southern hemisphere 500 million years ago, having drifted northward, having been folded and eroded and risen above sea level 10 million years ago, having been named, having been tramped over by hiking boots (which are themselves a significant erosion force). "Immigrant rocks: the rocks of Skiddaw are immigrant rocks, just passing through here, like my sister and me only rather more slowly" (Massey 2005: 136). See [[the-lake-district-as-meeting-place]].

## Application to Institutions

The science parks boxed section in Ch. 12 applies the same logic: a science park is not simply a planned enclosure but "a multiplicity of trajectories each of which has its own spatiality and temporality" (Massey 2005: 143) — class histories, gender histories, legitimation struggles from mediaeval monasteries to Silicon Valley. The apparent coherence of a science park conceals its constitution as a temporary constellation of ongoing processes.

## Contrast with Rival Frameworks

- **Container model of place**: places as bounded territories with fixed identities. The trajectories-language refutes this — there is no stable container, only ongoing processes.
- **Tuan's "pause"**: Tuan's account of place as constituted by the pause of movement ([[place-as-pause]]) is structurally similar but phenomenologically grounded in human experience. Massey's trajectories-language extends to non-human processes and to geological timescales.
- **Palimpsest**: the palimpsest figure suggests layers of suppressed histories beneath a current surface. Trajectories-language insists on coevalness — the stories are not beneath the present but *simultaneous* with it.
- **Structuralism's synchrony**: the structuralist synchronic slice pretends to capture a moment as coherent. The trajectories-language insists the slice would be "full of holes, of disconnections, of tentative half-formed first encounters" (Massey 2005: 107).

## Resonances in the Wiki

The trajectories-as-stories-so-far formulation is Massey's answer to the same problem that Bryant addresses through "machine-paths" and "topological distance." Both are trying to get away from a static, synchronic, surface-imagination of space. Where Bryant uses the language of machines and their paths through phase-space, Massey uses the more humanistically accessible language of stories and trajectories. The ontological move is similar: entities are defined by their processes, not by their properties.
