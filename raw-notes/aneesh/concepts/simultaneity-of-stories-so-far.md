# Simultaneity of Stories-So-Far

Massey's most compressed and evocative formula for her conception of space, introduced in Chapter 1 of *For Space* (2005):

> "Perhaps we could imagine space as a simultaneity of stories-so-far." (Massey 2005: 9)

Elaborated in the same chapter: "Space can never be that completed simultaneity in which all interconnections have been established, and in which everywhere is already linked with everywhere else." (Massey 2005: 11)

## Unpacking the Formula

**"Simultaneity"** — not a sequence. Space is what makes multiple trajectories contemporaneous. This is Massey's Proposition 2 (coexisting heterogeneity) in its most compressed form. Trajectories are not arranged in a single queue (first-second-third); they are alongside each other, happening *now*, together.

**"Stories"** — by which Massey means "simply the history, change, movement, of things themselves" (Massey 2005: 12). The connotations of narrative and interpretation are deliberately set aside. A geological formation has a story; so does a scientific attitude, a collectivity, a social convention. Stories are the processual character of anything that exists.

**"So-far"** — the crucial qualifier. Each story is mid-sentence. There is no final chapter. Space is the cross-section through ongoing becomings, not an endpoint. This is Massey's Proposition 3 (space always under construction) in its most elegant form.

The formula collapses space and time together in exactly the way the Aztec "place-moments" did: space is not a grid on which time-slices are displayed but the dimension of multiple ongoing durations.

## What the Formula Does

It gives spatial content to Bergson's commitment to duration without Bergson's mistake of relegating space to stasis. Space is *also* durational — it is the simultaneity of many durations, many stories, each still in progress.

It also blocks the "single narrative" substitution. Massey notes the danger: "in the gadarene rush to abandon the singularity of the modernist grand narrative... what has been adopted in its place is a vision of an instantaneity of interconnections. But this is to replace a single history with no history." (Massey 2005: 14) Her formula offers a third option: replace the single history with *many* histories, all ongoing, all now.
