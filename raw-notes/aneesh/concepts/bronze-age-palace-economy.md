# Bronze Age Palace Economy

The Bronze Age palace economies of Mycenae, Egypt, and Mesopotamia represent the dominant pre-Axial civilizational logic: centralized, redistributive systems in which a palace bureaucracy administered the production, storage, and allocation of virtually all economic surplus. There was no market exchange in the modern sense — the "machine" optimized for administrative control rather than efficiency, growth, or innovation.

## The Linear B Evidence

The decipherment of Linear B tablets from Mycenaean palaces (Pylos, Knossos) revealed the most detailed surviving picture of this system. The archives document a massive redistributive operation: every worker, every ration, every piece of equipment was administratively fixed. Shepherds were assigned specific flocks; weavers received specific allocations of wool; smiths received specific quantities of bronze. The tablets are not literature or law — they are accounting records of total economic administration.

This is a civilizational logic fundamentally different from anything that followed. The palace was simultaneously temple, warehouse, factory, and government. There was no separate "economy" to speak of — economic activity was indistinguishable from political administration.

## The Late Bronze Age Collapse

The Late Bronze Age collapse (~1250–1150 BCE) destroyed this entire interconnected system within a single century. Mycenaean palaces burned, the Hittite empire vanished, Egyptian power contracted, Ugarit was destroyed, and international trade networks dissolved. The collapse was systemic — it was not the failure of individual states but the simultaneous failure of the redistributive palace model across the entire Eastern Mediterranean.

The collapse is significant for the [[world-machines]] framework because it demonstrates that civilizational "machines" do not degrade gracefully. When the operating logic fails, everything that depends on it fails simultaneously.

## Wittfogel's Hydraulic Hypothesis

Karl Wittfogel's *Oriental Despotism* (1957) proposed that large-scale irrigation agriculture necessarily produced centralized bureaucratic despotism — the "hydraulic hypothesis." The argument: only a powerful central authority could coordinate the construction and maintenance of irrigation systems; this authority then leveraged its control over water into total political control.

The hypothesis is now heavily criticized. Archaeological evidence shows that many irrigation systems were locally managed without centralized control, and that centralized states often *followed* rather than *preceded* irrigation. But Wittfogel remains historically important as the most ambitious attempt to explain the palace economy's logic through environmental determinism, and his framework influenced a generation of comparative sociology.
