# Nakatomi Space

**Coined by**: [[Geoff Manaugh]], BLDGBLOG (2010)
**Source**: [[Bldgblog Nakatomi Space]] — summary

## Definition

Nakatomi space is the architectural condition in which buildings reveal **near-infinite interiors** capable of being traversed through all manner of non-architectural means — elevator shafts, air ducts, blasted walls, forced ceiling openings. Named after Nakatomi Plaza from *Die Hard* (1988).

The concept identifies a gap between two topologies of the same building:
1. **Designed topology**: the grammar of corridors, doors, stairwells — the architect's intended circulation
2. **Nakatomi topology**: the full set of physically possible routes through the structure, accessible only by abandoning the designed grammar

## The Two Founding Cases

### *Die Hard* (1988)
John McClane moves through Nakatomi Plaza by all means except its designed ones. The film asks: why follow the architect's grammar when you need to move? McClane's answer is that he doesn't.

### IDF in Nablus (2002) — Eyal Weizman, "Lethal Theory"
The IDF's "walking through walls" tactic: soldiers moved horizontally through party walls and vertically through blasted floors, using "none of the streets, roads, alleys... external doors, internal stairwells, and windows." More than half of buildings in Nablus's old city center had routes forced through them.

Commander's order: *"From now on we all walk through walls!"*

## "Walking Through Walls" as Militarized Parkour

Weizman notes this is ancient, not novel — Paris Commune defenders, Kasbah of Algiers, Hue, Beirut, Jenin all used the same technique. What is new is its theorization and deliberate application as doctrine.

The civilian version is [[Parkour]] — the practice of urban movement that ignores the designed grammar of built environments.

## Epistemic / Conceptual Extensions

Nakatomi space is not only an architectural concept. The logic applies anywhere that:
- A designed grammar constrains movement
- A non-designed topology exists beneath the surface
- An agent chooses to bypass the grammar

This maps onto:
- **Algorithmic systems**: the designed grammar is the recommendation/search interface; Nakatomi space is what you find when you move laterally through APIs, scraping, remixing
- **Social legibility**: the "grammar" is the legibility the system expects; Nakatomi space is the opacity of the [[The Thicket]]
- **Intellectual territory**: textbooks define the designed grammar of a field; actual research operates in Nakatomi space
