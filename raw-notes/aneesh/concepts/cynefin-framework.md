# Cynefin Framework

The **Cynefin Framework** (pronounced "ku-NEV-in," Welsh for "habitat" or "place") is a sense-making model developed by Dave Snowden at IBM in 1999. It categorizes problems and contexts into five domains based on the relationship between cause and effect.

## The Five Domains

| Domain | Cause-Effect | Approach |
|---|---|---|
| **Clear** (formerly Simple) | Obvious, immediate | Sense → Categorize → Respond |
| **Complicated** | Analyzable, expert-discoverable | Sense → Analyze → Respond |
| **Complex** | Emergent, retrospectively coherent | Probe → Sense → Respond |
| **Chaotic** | No perceivable relationship | Act → Sense → Respond |
| **Confusion/Disorder** | Unclear which domain applies | — |

## The Complicated/Complex Distinction

The distinction most relevant to intellectual work is between **Complicated** and **Complex**:

- **Complicated**: A jet engine is complicated. With enough expertise, its behavior is fully predictable. Multiple right answers may exist, but they can be determined through analysis.
- **Complex**: An ecosystem (or [[Dwarf Fortress]], or [[Emacs]], or [[Artificial Intelligence]]) is complex. No amount of analysis fully predicts emergent behavior. The right answer emerges from the system's dynamics.

This distinction drives the argument in [[Dwarf Fortress, Emacs, AI — Generative Complexity]]: these systems are valued not because they are complicated (which would make them boringly mastered) but because they are complex (which makes mastery itself generative).

## Cynefin and Knowledge Systems

The same framework applies to knowledge systems:
- A well-structured database is complicated (deterministic retrieval)
- The [[Universal Library]] project became complex (emergent meanings, unforeseen contradictions)
- [[LLM]] outputs are complex in the Cynefin sense — you cannot fully predict what a trained model will say
