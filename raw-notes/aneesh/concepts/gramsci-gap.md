# Gramsci Gap

## Origin

From Antonio Gramsci's *Prison Notebooks* (1929-1935):

> "The crisis consists precisely in the fact that the old is dying and the new cannot be born; in this interregnum a great variety of morbid symptoms appear."

The term "Gramsci Gap" is [[venkatesh-rao]]'s usage, naming the interregnum itself — the gap between paradigms, not just the morbid symptoms that characterize it.

## The Phenomenology of the Gramsci Gap

Living through a Gramsci Gap means:
- The old order has lost legitimacy but has not yet collapsed
- The new order has not yet crystallized enough to offer clear alternatives
- "Slightly monstrous" people embody unseemly levels of [[Liveness]] that don't fit the "hushed and reverential tones" appropriate to either funereal or prenatal ends of the spectrum
- The social expectation is to mourn the old or nurture the new; what Rao advocates is something wilder: asserting liveness in the gap itself

## Gramsci Gap and Rewilding

The Gramsci Gap is where [[Rewilding Technology]] becomes necessary. Civilization normally maintains itself by *never quite* exposing itself to wilderness. In a Gramsci Gap:
- More wildness is required than is comfortable
- Death must be allowed to operate — corpses surrendered to decomposition
- Protections must be removed from the barely-born
- The consolation: greater liveness

## Relation to History Machines

In Lohse's framework ([[History Machines in the Margins - Lohse]]), a Gramsci Gap is the moment when the history machine — components developing silently for centuries — suddenly lurches into motion. The morbid symptoms are the grinding of gears not yet calibrated to each other.

## Relation to Chaisson

Chaisson's three eras — Radiation, Matter, Life — each involve a Gramsci Gap transition. The period when matter began dominating radiation was one such interregnum: structure had not yet emerged, the universe was "uniform, symmetrical, informationless, and boring." The Life Era transition is the current Gramsci Gap at cosmic scale.
