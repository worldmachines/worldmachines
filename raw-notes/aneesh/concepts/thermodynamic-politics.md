# Thermodynamic Politics

## Definition

"We can call these forms of engagement *thermodynamic politics*. Thermodynamic politics is a form of political engagement that targets a machine's sources of energy and capacity for work." (*Onto-Cartography*, p. 72)

[[levi-bryant]] introduces thermodynamic politics as the practical consequence of [[alien-phenomenology]] applied to large-scale institutional machines. If corporations, governments, and other institutions are machines with their own [[structural-openness-operational-closure]] — their own "alien minds" — then effective political engagement must speak the language of those machines, not the language of human communicative ethics.

## The Logic

Most machines require work and energy to sustain themselves across time. A corporate-machine requires:
- Natural resources
- Electricity and fossil fuels
- Capital
- Labor

These are the flows to which corporations are *structurally open*. They constitute the machine's operational energy supply. Thermodynamic politics targets precisely these flows — starving the machine of what it needs to operate, or redirecting those flows to create leverage.

**Why boycotts work**: "Boycotts are implicitly aware of the flows and operations that animate corporate machines. They are aware that the flows to which corporations are structurally open are those of profit and loss." (p. 72) Cutting off profit flows sends a signal the machine *can actually register*, producing a response.

**Why strikes work**: a strike shuts down the operations of production themselves — preventing the corporate-machine from doing the work that generates surplus value. Workers create leverage by controlling the energy flows (labor) on which the machine depends (p. 72).

## Contrast with Semiotic Politics

*Semiotic politics* relies on signs, communicative persuasion, and critique — attempting to change institutions through ethical appeals, hermeneutics of suspicion, or ideological debunking (p. 73). Bryant's critique: semiotic politics is "confused in that it is premised on producing change through ethical persuasion, and thereby assumes that institutional-machines such as corporations, governments, factories, and so on, are structurally open to the same sorts of communicative flows as humans" (p. 73).

The corporation is not structurally open to moral flows. "At best, however, these entities are indifferent to such arguments, while at worst they are completely blind to even the occurrence of such appeals as machines such as corporations are only structurally open to information events of profit and loss." (p. 73)

"Persuading a corporation through ethical appeals is about as effective as trying to explain calculus to a cat." (p. 73)

## Semiotic Politics Is Not Useless

Bryant qualifies: semiotic politics is not entirely useless — but it misunderstands what it is doing. A protest is not really addressed to the corporation or government; it is addressed to *onlookers and other humans*, building collective awareness and solidarity. A critical debunking is aimed at raising awareness *among people*, creating a larger collective machine that can then exert thermodynamic pressure (pp. 73–74).

The goal of semiotic politics, in this reading, is to manufacture a *collective machine* (a social movement) large enough and organized enough to exert thermodynamic leverage. Critical mass is required: enough people withholding labor, purchases, or political support to actually interrupt the energy flows of the target machine (p. 74).

## Broader Application

The concept extends beyond corporations:
- **Ecological politics**: targeting fossil fuel infrastructure is thermodynamic politics (interrupting the energy flows of carbon-dependent civilization)
- **Institutional reform**: defunding a university department, cutting access to data, restricting resource flows — these speak the institution's language regardless of ethical intent
- **AI governance**: restricting compute access, limiting training data, imposing energy costs — thermodynamic interventions on AI development machines

## Synthesis Seeds

- **Thermodynamic politics ↔ algorithmic-management**: Platforms that manage workers algorithmically are machines with specific structural openness (to productivity metrics, user engagement signals). Labor organizing in the gig economy is discovering thermodynamic politics empirically: strikes and coordinated work stoppages work; appeals to platform ethics rarely do.
- **Thermodynamic vs. semiotic ↔ the Gramsci gap**: the wiki has a concept [[gramsci-gap]] — the disconnect between ideological critique and material power. Bryant's thermodynamic/semiotic distinction maps onto this: semiotic politics operates at the ideological level; thermodynamic politics operates at the material-energetic level.
