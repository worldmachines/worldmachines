# Intent Collisions

**Intent collisions** name the predicted phase of the liveness era in which intents wired to responsive substrate begin to encounter each other. Today the substrate has room for every intent to fill; every essay finds its niche, every founder finds adjacent space. This is the early phase, where an early arriver has the field. It does not last. As more brains and more resources are wired to the substrate, intents start to collide — and once they do, the question of which one gets executed is no longer settled by who shouts loudest. Whoever has the better model, the better taste, the better access to the substrate settles it. **Intents red in tooth and claw.**

## Definition

An intent collision is the encounter between two or more intents that, deployed through generative substrate, produce competing artefacts in the same niche. The collision is not a debate, a flame war, or a status fight in the older sense. It is a **production race**: two intents commission two pipelines, and the pipelines deliver finished, indistinguishable-from-canonical artefacts into the same channel of attention.

The canonical scene runs like this. Two people disagree about something — say, the legacy of a contested thinker. The first commissions an AI agent to write the counter-book in the disputed thinker's style: full length, with footnotes and an index, indistinguishable in production quality from a publisher's, in five days. The second deploys a different agent to write the refutation, also full length, also in five days, also indistinguishable. Neither needs an audience to authorise the move. The intent to disagree no longer needs an audience. **It produces artefacts, and the artefacts compete.**

## The Mechanism

Three conditions, each already largely in place by 2026, together produce the collision regime:

1. **Cheap execution.** Generative substrate has driven the marginal cost of producing a finished-looking artefact (book, app, paper, video, agent) toward zero. See [[bottleneck-reversal]].
2. **Distribution at scale.** The same channels that promote canonical artefacts will promote auteured ones at the same throughput. There is no separate slow lane.
3. **Many wired intents.** The substrate is no longer an asymmetric advantage held by a few. It is a commodity input. The number of intents capable of producing finished artefacts grows by orders of magnitude.

Under these conditions, two intents in the same niche produce two pipelines, and the pipelines run in parallel. Whoever has the better model, taste, and access to substrate finishes faster, finishes better, or finishes both. The artefacts compete on quality and reach; the underlying intents compete by proxy.

## From Performance to Production

The closest manual prototype was Venkatesh Rao's [[internet-of-beefs]] (2020), which described a stable low-grade civil war waged across social media: fights that were not about resolving anything, beefs as the medium through which identity and tribal belonging got transacted, knights performing for mooks. Intent collisions industrialise the underlying logic and shift its shape.

The shift is from **performance to production**:

| Internet of beefs (2020) | Intent collisions (~2030) |
|---|---|
| Knights perform for mooks | Auteurs commission pipelines |
| Beef is the medium | Artefact is the medium |
| Status is the prize | Niche capture is the prize |
| Replies, dunks, threads | Books, papers, agents, codebases |
| Performance holds the system together | Products hold the system together |
| Manual, attentional cost | Automated, substrate cost |

Disagreement becomes a production pipeline. The intent to oppose is no longer expressed by yelling at someone. **It is expressed by writing the better book, faster, and putting it into the same machinery that already promotes the original.**

## Time Horizon

The first generation of intent collisions is on the order of three to five years out from 2026. The shape of the era will be determined in that interval. Before three years, the substrate is still sparse enough that early arrivers have the field. After five years, the collision regime is the default, and the question is no longer whether intents collide but how the collisions are settled.

The wager is on the rough timescale at which (a) enough actors are equipped to commission full-pipeline artefacts cheaply, and (b) distribution channels can no longer distinguish auteured artefacts from canonical ones at the rate at which they arrive.

## What Settles It

Once intents collide, the older settlement mechanisms (audience size, platform power, who shouts loudest) no longer suffice. The substrate is finite, the resources behind it are finite, and the competition is over which intent gets executed at scale. Three things plausibly settle a collision:

- **Better model.** Access to a stronger generative substrate produces better artefacts faster. This is currently asymmetric and likely to remain so for some years.
- **Better taste.** Among artefacts of comparable production quality, the one that reflects sharper [[taste-as-epistemic-capacity]] wins more attention and survives more iterations of the loop. See [[auteuring]].
- **Better access.** Distribution channels, search surfaces, and citation graphs are not neutral; intents wired into them more deeply propagate further.

A collision is settled when one intent's artefact captures the niche — not when the other intent concedes. Concession is rare. Niches are usually captured by exhaustion of attention or capital, not by argument.
