# Fixation Error

## Definition

**Fixation error** occurs when a decision maker maintains an incorrect explanatory frame in the face of available evidence that should prompt revision. De Keyser and Woods (1993), studying the nuclear power plant industry, defined the criterion as: *the initial account was incorrect, plus the decision maker maintained this incorrect explanation in the face of opportunities to revise it.*

Fixation is not the same as simply persisting with a frame. Persistence is often appropriate — frames need stability to function. Fixation is persistence *past the point where revision is warranted*.

## The Mechanism

Fixation is a byproduct of [[Frames and Framing]]'s attention-directing function, not a separate bug. Because frames filter what gets noticed, a frame in good standing naturally directs attention away from data that contradict it. The same mechanism that enables efficient attention management becomes fixation when the frame is wrong.

> "The phenomenon of fixation can be seen as a simple result of using a frame to direct attention — which is one of the functions of frames. We call this fixation when we are working with a flawed frame, or a frame that is built around flawed data. We call it efficient attention management when our frames are accurate." (Klein et al., 2007)

## Knowledge Shields

Feltovich, Coulson & Spiro (2001) identified a set of "knowledge shields" — cognitive tactics that enable experts to preserve frames against countervailing evidence. These include:
- Arguing from authority
- Resorting to bad analogies
- Ignoring secondary effects
- Arguing special cases
- Arguing that a principle has restricted applicability

These shields minimize the importance of contradictory data. They are not always wrong (some contradictory data is genuinely noise), but they become pathological when protecting an incorrect frame.

## The Anchor Timing Effect

Klein et al. (2007) hypothesize that an inaccurate anchor early in a sensemaking sequence has a correspondingly greater negative effect on accuracy than one introduced later. Early anchors shape the entire frame; they corrupt the filter through which all subsequent data are interpreted. This is consistent with Tversky & Kahneman's (1974) anchoring heuristic, but located at the frame level rather than the numerical estimate level. See [[Anchoring in Sensemaking]].

## Garden Path Paradigm

Rudolph (2003) used the "garden path paradigm" — scenarios with salient initial anchors that suggest an obvious but incorrect explanation — to study fixation in anesthesiology residents. Key finding: fixation occurs as a byproduct of elaborating a plausible-seeming frame, not because people *seek* to fixate. People are skilled explanation machines; their very skill at elaborating and preserving explanations makes them vulnerable.

Feltovich et al. (1984) found that experts in pediatric cardiology escaped the garden path more readily than novices. Experts had more differentiated frames, generating more specific expectancies — so violations became more salient, not less.

## Fixation vs. Diagnostic Vagabonding

The opposite pathology is **diagnostic vagabonding** (Dörner, 1996) — continual reframing without sufficient commitment to test any frame. Rudolph (2003) found that vagabonding was nearly as dysfunctional as fixation. Decision makers need sufficient commitment to a frame to generate testable expectancies and to detect when the frame is wrong.

The implication: training people to "keep an open mind" by *delaying* frame commitment is counterproductive. It removes the mechanism by which anomalies become detectable.

## Organizational Fixation

Vaughan's (1996) study of the Challenger launch decision describes institutional fixation as "routinization of deviance" — organizations explain away anomalies repeatedly until the anomalies are reclassified as normal. The frame-preserving behavior of [[Sensemaking Forms]] scales from individual cognition to organizational culture.

## Navigation as Fixation Domain

Klein et al. found many examples of fixation in navigation incidents. People getting lost attach too much credibility to an inaccurate anchor, then interpret all subsequent data to fit the inaccurate position. The person "bends the map" — forcing geographic features to fit their incorrect position hypothesis rather than updating their position hypothesis to fit the terrain. This is a pure illustration of frame preservation overriding data.
