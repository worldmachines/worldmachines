# The Co-Implication of Space and Time

[[doreen-massey]]'s constructive thesis from Chapter 5 of *For Space* (2005). She rejects two common moves:

1. The **reduction** of space to time (Bergson's polemical inheritance: space is the bad spatialisation of duration).
2. The **subordination** of space to time (the broad twentieth-century habit: space matters only insofar as it serves temporal narratives).

The third option is **co-implication without reduction**.

## The two-sided formulation

> "On this reading neither time nor space is reducible to the other; they are distinct. They are, however, co-implicated. On the side of space, there is the integral temporality of a dynamic simultaneity. On the side of time, there is the necessary production of change through practices of interrelation." (Massey 2005: 55)

Two propositions in tandem:

- **Space contains time.** Every element of a spatial multiplicity is itself a [[stories-so-far]] — its own duration, its own ongoing process. The "snapshot" is therefore not really static; it is a [[dynamic-simultaneity]].
- **Time requires space.** Change is produced by **interaction** among distinct entities. Without others to react to, no change. Without space, no others. Therefore time presupposes the spatial. "We cannot 'become' . . . without others." (56)

She summarises Bergson's "time prevents everything from being given at once" with its dual: the role of space is to provide the condition for the relations that generate time.

## What it forbids

- **Four-dimensional spacetime as a single dimension.** Massey explicitly rejects "an easy four-dimensionality." Space and time remain distinct; their co-implication is not their identity.
- **Space as the medium in which temporal events occur.** That is the container image she rejects.
- **Space as the residue of time.** That is the negative definition she diagnosed in [[prison-house-of-synchrony]].

## What it enables

- **Place as event.** A place is a [[dynamic-simultaneity]] of trajectories meeting — both spatial (they are co-present) and temporal (they are each in motion).
- **A politics of co-presence.** If others share my "now" by virtue of space, and if their durations are not reducible to mine, then I am obliged to negotiate with their ongoing-ness — see Part Five and [[throwntogetherness]] (when written).
