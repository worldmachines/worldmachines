# Play as Problem-Solving

## The Claim

From [[Hack Hacky Hacker]]: "Being hacky, taking imperfect shortcuts, in the world is immensely satisfying. It is play disguised as problem solving."

The inversion matters: play is not the *enemy* of serious work but its disguise. The most effective problem-solving preserves the qualities of play — exploration, low-stakes experimentation, intrinsic satisfaction — while achieving real-world outcomes.

## Connection to Anxiety Motor

Play connects to [[Anxiety as Generative Force]] from [[The Plato Plateau]]: the anxiety → exploration → action → refreshed identity cycle *is* a form of play. Exploration is not frivolous — it is the mechanism by which identity and competence are built.

## Richard Stallman's Definition

[[Richard Stallman]]'s definition of hacking as "playfully doing something difficult, whether useful or not" puts play at the center of technical mastery. The author endorses playfulness and difficulty but rejects the "or not" clause — play should still be in service of something (even just a prank). Pure purposeless play is the tree felling in a forest: play without audience or effect.

## Button-Pressing as Play

[[Divine Documentation]] connects: hypothesis-driven button-pressing is the play form of technology exploration. It's inefficient, but "discovery is a fun way to spike interest." This is play in the learning context — inefficient but motivationally priming.

## What's Missing

The concept would benefit from engagement with literature on intrinsic motivation ([[Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi]]'s flow, [[Edward Deci]] and [[Richard Ryan]]'s self-determination theory). The claim that play *is* the best problem-solving has empirical support that isn't drawn on here.
