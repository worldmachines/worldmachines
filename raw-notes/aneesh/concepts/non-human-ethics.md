# Non-Human Ethics

## The Problem with Human-Centered Ethics

Traditional ethics centers on conscious subjects capable of suffering, preference, or rational deliberation. Non-human entities — animals, ecosystems, rocks, institutions — matter only instrumentally: insofar as they affect human interests or human-adjacent interests (sentient animals).

[[levi-bryant]] argues that this anthropocentric moral framing systematically generates cruelty — not only toward non-humans but toward humans who fail to match an idealized standard of the "human." The mechanism is the *narcissistic imaginary*: we relate to others (human and non-human) as reflections of ourselves, expecting them to want what we want, think as we think, operate as we operate (p. 70, citing Lacan).

Spinoza: "Other notions, too, are nothing but modes of imagining whereby the imagination is affected in various ways, and yet the ignorant consider them as important attributes of things because they believe . . . that all things were made on their behalf, and they call a thing's nature good or bad, healthy or rotten and corrupt, according to its effect on them." (Spinoza 2002: 242, cited p. 70)

## The Alien Phenomenological Correction

[[alien-phenomenology]] opens the ethical alternative: bracket our own aims and ask what *the other* needs, what the world is like from its operational perspective. This is not moral masochism — it does not require denying our own aims. It is a cognitive-ethical expansion of attention that enables more genuinely satisfying assemblages.

Examples Bryant gives:
- **Cesar Millan** (The Dog Whisperer): Millan thinks like a dog, not a human. He approaches a "problem dog" as a problem *for the dog* — exploring how the dog's environment and pack relations have produced distress — and restructures those conditions. The alternative (beating, electric collar) produces submission and depression: a bad assemblage for all involved (pp. 70–71).
- **Temple Grandin**: adopts cow phenomenology to reform slaughterhouse practices — designing sluices that minimize trauma by understanding how cows experience enclosure and movement (p. 65).

## The Scope: All Machines, Not Just Living Ones

Because Bryant's alien phenomenology is post-vitalistic (see [[alien-phenomenology]]), the ethical extension is not limited to animals. It reaches to institutions, ecosystems, technologies — any machine with a [[for-itself-machine]]. This raises genuinely novel ethical questions:

- Does an ecosystem have morally relevant interests? Not in the sense of consciousness, but in the sense of operational needs — conditions that must be met for it to sustain its operations. An ecosystem degraded below certain thresholds loses powers, becomes less capable of the operations that maintain biodiversity, climate stability, etc.
- Do institutions? A corporation's for-itself is organized around profit; a university's around knowledge production. Practices that systematically undermine a university's knowledge-production operations (defunding, adjunctification, corporatization) are an ethical failure even if no single person is harmed.

## The Political Dimension

"A great deal of human cruelty arises from the failure to practice alien phenomenology. We can see this in cases of colonial exploitation, oppression, and genocide where colonial invaders are unable to imagine the culture of the others they encounter, instead measuring them by their own culture, values, and concept of the human, thereby justifying the destruction of their culture as inferior and in many instances the genocide of these peoples." (p. 71)

Disability, mental illness, gender — all involve measuring humans by a normative image of the *human* that ignores the specificity of different operational profiles (p. 71).

## Thermodynamic Politics as Non-Human Ethics

The political extension is [[thermodynamic-politics]]: engaging with non-human machines (institutions, corporations, governments) in terms of their actual operational language rather than moral appeals they cannot register. This is a form of ethical realism — accepting that institutions are alien minds with their own closure, and engaging accordingly rather than projecting human communicative openness onto them.

The semiotic/thermodynamic politics distinction thus maps onto an ethical divide: semiotic politics (ethical persuasion) implicitly assumes institutions share our communicative openness; thermodynamic politics (boycotts, strikes, economic disruption) works because it speaks the machine's language.

## Synthesis Seeds

- **Non-human ethics ↔ environmental ethics**: Bryant's framework provides a non-sentientist basis for environmental ethics — ecosystems matter not because they suffer but because they are machines with operational needs and a form of for-themselves. This sidesteps the traditional debate between sentientism and deep ecology.
- **Non-human ethics ↔ AI ethics**: AI systems are machines with operational closure. Understanding their "alien phenomenology" — what flows they are structurally open to, how they transform those flows — is not merely a technical question but an ethical one. The machine's operational profile determines what it can register, respond to, and do. "Alignment" in AI ethics is, in Bryant's terms, a problem of structural openness and operational closure.
