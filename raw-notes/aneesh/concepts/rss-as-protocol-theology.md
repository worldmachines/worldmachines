# RSS as Protocol Theology

## The Myth

In [[The Small God of the Internet]], RSS is personified as **ArrEsEs**, a small god with an orange halo (three ripples), presiding over the River of Posts. His liturgy: "Mark All As Read" — absolution that "leaves you lighter and vaguely suspicious you've got away with something." His priests: librarians, tinkerers, persons who believe in putting things in order.

This is not mere whimsy. Assigning divine character to a protocol is an argument about what the protocol *means*: RSS is a covenant, a set of mutual obligations between publisher and reader that platforms cannot mediate or monetize.

## Theological Virtues of RSS

The god's virtues are the protocol's architectural properties:
- **Patience** — "He'll wait. He always has." RSS does not demand real-time attention.
- **Order** — brings things in the order they happened
- **Silence** — "He does silence properly." The feed is finite; absence of new items is meaningful.
- **Memory** — "He remembers what you finished."
- **Non-intrusion** — "He promises tomorrow will come with its own bundle, and if you happen to be away, he will keep the stack neat."

## Genealogy

ArrEsEs descends from **[[Old Mother Press]]** (the printing press), inheriting "text in tidy rows, dates that mind their place, headlines that know how to stand up straight." The lineage matters: RSS is in the tradition of the *broadside*, not the *town crier*. It distributes; it doesn't shout.

## Small Rites / Ritual Practice

The god is sustained by small liturgical acts:
- Linking to your feed with `feed` (not buried in a `<link>` tag)
- Maintaining a **[[blogrolls-and-the-personal-web|blogroll]]** — how "villages point at one another and remember they are villages"
- Sharing **OPML** files — "a pilgrim's list people swap like seed packets"
- Maintaining a sensible update cadence

## Displacement

Browser vendors eventually removed the votive lamps ("candles are unmonetizable"). Platform dams redirected the River of Posts into ornamental channels. See [[The Gloomwheel]] for what replaced it, and [[Google Reader's Shutdown]] for the historical moment of displacement.
