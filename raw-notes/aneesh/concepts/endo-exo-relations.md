# Endo-Relations and Exo-Relations

## The Distinction

In [[levi-bryant]]'s [[onto-cartography]], the most fundamental distinction for machine ontology is between two types of relations:

**Exo-relations** (external relations): Relations between two already-distinct machines that can be severed while the machines retain their being. A television connected to a wall socket stands in an exo-relation to it — unplugging does not destroy the television; it merely prevents the television from exercising certain powers. A frog in a vacuum chamber severs its exo-relation to oxygen; it remains a frog, at least for a time.

**Endo-relations** (internal relations): Relations *between* machines that are constitutive of a new machine — the relations that *make* the new machine what it is. The hydrogen-oxygen bonding in H₂O is an endo-relation: the water molecule is constituted by and through those bonds. Severing those bonds produces hydrogen and oxygen again, not water in a different condition.

"The claim that a machine is a complex of relations or an assemblage of machines is thus the claim that a machine is constituted by its *endo-relations*. Endo-relations are *internal* relations between machines that constitute or generate a new machine." (p. 76)

## Determining New Machines

The endo/exo distinction provides the criterion for when a coupling counts as producing a genuinely new machine:

1. If the coupling between machines A and B generates powers or capacities not present in A or B alone → the coupling involves endo-relations → a new machine (the assemblage A+B) has come into being
2. If the coupling between A and B merely allows A or B to exercise already-possessed powers → the coupling is exo-relational → no new machine has emerged

The man-horse coupling before the stirrup is exo-relational in important respects: the man retains his powers, the horse retains its powers, and the coupling allows the exercise of existing capacities. The addition of the stirrup changes this: the man-horse-stirrup assemblage generates the capacity for mounted shock combat that no component possesses — an endo-relational coupling producing a new machine.

## Essential vs. Inessential Parts

Not all parts of a machine stand in endo-relations to it. Bryant distinguishes essential from inessential parts:

- **Essential parts**: those whose removal would dissolve the machine's being or significantly alter its powers
- **Inessential parts**: those that can be removed while the machine retains its identity

A capybara can lose a leg and remain a capybara; the leg stands in a somewhat endo-relational but not absolutely essential relation. A television can lose its plastic casing and remain a television. However, the television cannot function as a television without the circuitry that constitutes its essential endo-composition.

This is an empirical question for each machine — there is no a priori principle determining which relations are essential.

## Implication: Scale-Independence of Machines

Because machines are constituted by endo-relations rather than by being "simple" or "basic," there is no privileged scale of machine. A molecule, a cell, an organism, a city, and a government are all machines constituted by their endo-relations. "Simple" is not a synonym for "substance" — jet planes, composed of other machines, are no less genuine machines for being decomposable.

This establishes the flat ontology of [[onto-cartography]]: every level of organization is equally real, and no level can be reduced to a more fundamental one.
