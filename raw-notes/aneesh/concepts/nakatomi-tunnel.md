# Nakatomi Tunnel

## Origin

Named after the Nakatomi Plaza ventilation shafts in *Die Hard* (1988), where John McClane navigates the building not through the front door or by overpowering the terrorists directly, but by crawling through the building's infrastructure — exploiting the system's own structure to move laterally.

## Concept

The Nakatomi Tunnel approach to problems:
1. Don't attack the hardest point directly
2. Don't walk away (that's neither fun, useful, nor hard)
3. Instead: try many approaches, probe the system's affordances, push buttons until "the alignment is just so and you slip through"

From [[Hack Hacky Hacker]]:
> "My favored way is to take the Nakatomi Tunnel through problems. Be hacky. Try enough approaches, push buttons that may do the thing you want until the alignment is just so and you slip through."

## Connection to System Design

The method is grounded in an observation about how most systems are built:
> "Most systems, especially computers are designed by people for people like you and me who are neither very bright nor very invested in the thing. We want to not have the problem."

If systems are designed for the average user, they are full of unintended paths — affordances that enable lateral navigation by anyone willing to explore rather than demand mastery.

## Relation to [[Hacky Mindset]]

The Nakatomi Tunnel is the operative metaphor for the hacky mindset in action. Where the [[Fox-Hedgehog Distinction]] provides the intellectual typology, the Nakatomi Tunnel provides the tactical image.
