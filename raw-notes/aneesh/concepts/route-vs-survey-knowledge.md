# Route vs. Survey Knowledge

Source: [[Yi-Fu Tuan]], *Space and Place* (1977), Chapter 6, pp. 67–84, drawing on Warner Brown's maze experiments. See also [[tuan-2002-space-and-place-ch4-6]].

## The Distinction

**Route knowledge**: spatial knowledge organized as a sequence of movements, cues, and actions tied to recognized landmarks. It is egocentric, procedural, and embodied. One knows *what to do next* at each decision point, not *where one is* in an abstract spatial field. Route knowledge is acquired through direct kinesthetic experience and does not require or produce an accurate mental map.

**Survey knowledge**: spatial knowledge organized as a simultaneous representation of places and their relations from an imagined vantage point — a "view from above." It is allocentric, propositional, and abstract. It enables one to navigate by many routes, to locate oneself when disoriented, to communicate spatial information to others who have not made the journey.

Tuan's formulation: most people operating in familiar environments rely on route knowledge supplemented by a sketchy and imprecise survey image. The two are not on a single developmental track; they are distinct cognitive achievements.

## Warner Brown's Maze Experiments

Brown's laboratory work is Tuan's main empirical case. Subjects learn a maze while blindfolded (wearing a gadget that blocks vision but allows perception of the larger room). The learning sequence:

1. **First trials**: only the entry point is clearly a recognized place; the rest is undifferentiated space.
2. **Subsequent trials**: landmarks accumulate — "a rough spot," "a tilting board," "a long straight stretch," "double turns." Each landmark triggers the next movement.
3. **Mastery**: the whole maze becomes "one locality with appropriate movements." "What begins as undifferentiated space ends as a single object-situation or place" (p. 72).

When asked to reproduce the maze in a drawing after learning it, subjects fail badly. They cannot state turns as "right" or "left" in abstract order; a subject says, "I don't know what comes next. I have to be *there* before I can tell you" (p. 72). The drawn maps are so distorted in angle and length that they "cannot be used as a map" (p. 72, Fig. 6). Route knowledge is real, detailed, and reliable — and yet it does not translate into survey knowledge.

The general implication: people in cities know a succession of movements appropriate to recognized landmarks; they do not acquire a precise mental map of the neighborhood. "The mental image is sketchy. Precision is not required in the practical business of moving about" (p. 72).

## Skill Without Knowledge

An important preliminary: spatial *skill* does not require spatial *knowledge*. A typist's fingers know the keyboard in the sense that they can find any key instantly; yet the typist typically cannot describe the keyboard's layout from memory. Riding a bicycle requires fine proprioception but not understanding of balance physics. "Self-conscious knowledge can even stand in the way of perfecting a skill" (p. 68).

Skill and knowledge can coexist and enhance each other, but they are logically separable. The distinction is not just academic: Tuan uses it to explain why people who are excellent navigators of familiar space are often hopeless at giving directions or drawing maps.

## When Space Becomes Place

"When space feels thoroughly familiar to us, it has become place" (p. 73). The key insight: **place is the outcome of successful route-learning**, not of map-mastery. Kinesthetic and emotional attachment to recognized landmarks is what converts anonymous space into inhabited place. Survey knowledge is a further, more abstract achievement that transforms place back into a navigable representation of space.

This is Figure 5 in Tuan's text: the progression from A (entry as the only recognized place; beyond = undifferentiated space) through B and C (landmarks multiply, paths articulate) to D (the whole maze = familiar landmarks and paths = place).

## Cultural Variation: Temne vs. Eskimo

John Berry's comparative study of the Temne of Sierra Leone and Eskimos of Arctic Canada illustrates the social determinants of which type of knowledge develops:

- **Temne**: rich visual environment, communal/authoritarian social structure, benign ecology. Individuals rarely face the task of orienting in unfamiliar space — the group and the familiar place are always present. They have meager spatial-geometric vocabulary and poor map-making ability. Their world is already "place."
- **Eskimos**: monotone visual environment (snow, ice, fog), individualistic and egalitarian social structure, harsh ecology requiring navigation over vast featureless terrain alone. They develop a spatial-geometric vocabulary comparable to Western technical discourse and are skilled map-makers and navigators. Their environment is structurally "space" that must be actively converted into survey knowledge to survive.

The key variable is the **ecological and social demand for spatial abstraction**: where survival requires knowing *where* things are (not just *how to get there*), survey knowledge develops. Where the group and the familiar environment constitute a pervasive "place," route knowledge suffices.

## Pacific Islander Navigation: The Integration

Puluwatan navigators combine bodily route knowledge with abstract survey knowledge in a system called *etak*. They know routes as sequences of felt wave swells, wind directions, color changes in water, and star bearings — deep kinesthetic knowing. But they also command a conceptual framework (Fig. 7) that organizes star bearings and reference islands across thousands of miles of open ocean, enabling them to locate themselves absolutely rather than just procedurally. Thomas Gladwin: "The Puluwatans picture themselves and their island in their part of the ocean much as we might locate ourselves upon a road map" (p. 82).

This is the fullest integration: the ocean is not threatening empty space but a network of routes and places.

## Synthesis Seeds

- **Data-frame theory** ([[data-frame-theory-of-sensemaking]]): Klein's distinction between recognition-primed (expert pattern-triggered) and analytical decision-making maps directly onto route vs. survey. Route knowledge is the expert practitioner's "recognition" — she knows what to do next because the situation triggers a practiced response. Survey knowledge is the analyst's frame — she knows where she is in the decision space. Expertise in most fields is primarily route-based even when practitioners believe they operate from a map.
- **LLMs as epistemic tools** ([[llms-as-epistemic-tools]]): LLMs have vast associative route knowledge — they know what comes next given any prompt — but structurally lack survey knowledge. They cannot locate themselves in an epistemic field; they can only follow familiar paths. The failure to draw the maze is exactly the failure mode: fluent performance, distorted representation.
- **Expertise** ([[expertise]]): The typist and the maze subject suggest that expertise is route-deep. Novices search for the survey map; experts trust their routes. The danger: route-expert practitioners may be blind to their own spatial distortions — they have to be "there" before they can tell you.
