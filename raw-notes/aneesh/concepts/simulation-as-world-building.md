# Simulation as World-Building

**Simulation as world-building** is the design philosophy that a simulation's highest purpose is not to represent specific scenarios or deliver predetermined experiences, but to implement rules faithfully enough that the simulated world generates its own stories.

## The DF Philosophy

[[Dwarf Fortress]]'s goal, as stated by [[Tarn Adams]]: simulate as many things as possible so that "the game had the ability to tell great stories." Not: design specific stories. Design the substrate from which stories emerge.

This produces unexpected outcomes that no designer authored — cats getting drunk from spilled ale, tantrum spirals cascading through a fortress's social graph, giant farting birds. These stories are *discoveries*, not designs. See [[Emergent Complexity]].

## Why MoMA Collected DF

The Museum of Modern Art acquired [[Dwarf Fortress]] for its permanent collection — and the acquisition changed how MoMA approaches software preservation. DF is recognized not as a game in the conventional sense but as a generative medium: a world that produces infinite, unrepeatable cultural artifacts (player stories, player-generated legends).

## Contrast: Authored World-Building

Most games (and most fiction) author specific experiences: scripted dialogue, designed levels, predetermined plot beats. The designer controls what the player encounters. Simulation-as-world-building inverts this: the designer controls the *physics* and the world figures out the rest.

## Implications for Narrative

If a simulation is rich enough, it produces narratives with a quality that designed narrative cannot: genuine surprise, even for the creator. Boatmurdered is not a story Tarn Adams told — it's a story the simulation told through players.

## Broader Applications

- **Agent-based models** in social science: simulate individual behavior, observe macro patterns
- **Procedural generation** in games (Minecraft, No Man's Sky): rule-based world creation
- **Climate models**: simulate physical laws, observe emergent climate behaviors
