# Chinese Garden and the Literary Completion of Landscape

**Source**: [[tuan-1991-language-and-the-making-of-place]]

## The Case

In China, painting and gardening were understood as sister arts: one composes a garden as one might compose a picture. But unlike European landscape art — where the visual image is (ideally) self-sufficient and the title is a brief label — Chinese landscape art treats the written word as a *constituent element* of the work:

> "The Chinese connoisseurs have not actually said that words call objects into being, wake up rocks and flowers, imbuing them with life and meaning, and yet something of that belief is there." (Tuan 1991, p. 691)

Words on scrolls and inscriptions are not addenda to the visual experience. They are part of what creates the visual experience — they "complete" the garden in the sense that without them the garden remains humanly unfinished. "The visual alone does not rise to the full potential of words. Words must also be used" (p. 691).

## The Mechanism

Words enhance the visual scene in two ways:

1. **They direct attention to sensory effects that cannot be directly shown** — sounds, fragrances, temporal sequences (rain from the hills "from time to time," mist "morning and evening"). The poem supplies a temporal dimension that the static visual image cannot.

2. **They deepen the meaning of what is visually present** — a gnarled tree already connotes age, "but one can do far more for that tree, making it even more forcefully present and worthy of respect, through the use of a literary allusion" (p. 692). Li Rihua's six-line poem identifying six trees with six ancient wise men is an extreme case: the visual presence of the tree and the cultural presence of the sage are merged through the inscription.

## The Great Garden Novel

The novel *The Story of the Stone* (eighteenth-century China) describes a great family building a garden to accommodate an Imperial Concubine. The physical construction is completed — then comes the true work: choosing the inscriptions. "These inscriptions are going to be difficult," says the head of the family. The inscriptions are not decorative. They are what transform a constructed space into a humanized place.

The novel is itself a further completion: "the great garden is constructed and completed is told in a novel, written in the middle of the eighteenth-century" (p. 691). The garden exists at three levels simultaneously — physical (rocks, water, pavilions), inscriptional (poems on scrolls and plaques), and literary (the novel that narrates the garden's creation).

## The Colophon

In Chinese pictorial tradition, the owner of a scroll can increase its value by asking famous literary figures to add colophons — written commentaries inscribed on the picture itself. "The more colophons are inscribed on the picture, the greater will be its value, even though the spread of words may threaten the integrity of the pictorial image itself" (p. 692). This is revealing: the written word is so valued that it can be worth encroaching on the visual space of the painting. Words do not merely supplement; they constitute.

## The Chinese Scholar's Studio

Tuan cites the *literati* tradition (Li Rihua, 1565–1635) and Li's poem inscribed on a fan painting: "Rain from the hills comes from time to time; Light mist appears morning and evening. I open my book without knowing where to turn, and find moss all over my desk." The poem supplies what the visual image cannot show pictorially: the *mood* of a poet listlessly opening his book. "One may not be able to show, but one can tell, and this the Chinese artist has always been quite willing to do" (p. 691).

## Implications

The Chinese case demonstrates that "literary completion of landscape" is not a marginal or exotic practice. It is a systematic cultural technology for linking verbal and visual worlds — one that European culture has largely suppressed in favor of the self-sufficient image. The Chinese tradition offers an alternative epistemology of art: a scene is not complete when visually rendered; it is complete when *named, described, and situated within literary culture*.

This extends Tuan's core thesis: not just pre-literate peoples singing their world into being, but sophisticated literary civilizations in which the written word is acknowledged as constitutive of the landscape it describes.
