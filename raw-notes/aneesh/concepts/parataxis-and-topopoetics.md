# Parataxis and Topopoetics

**Source**: [[aneesh-sathe]], *We Need Homes in the Delta Quadrant* (2025), §II — What is Place?

## Parataxis

*Parataxis* (Greek: "arrangement side by side") is the rhetorical and grammatical form of placing things alongside one another without connective tissue — no "because," no "therefore," no subordination of one element to another. It is the list, the inventory, the declarative sequence.

Sathe borrows the term to describe a spatial operation:

> "Places are not necessarily large or fixed. Often they are forged in fragments — through a method of thought called *parataxis*, the act of placing things side by side without hierarchy or explanation. Plates, tables, menus — listed without commentary — already conjure a restaurant. North is the river, east is the village: already we are somewhere." (§II)

The restaurant is not assembled by defining what a restaurant is and then listing its components. The list itself *is* the place. Adjacency does the work of definition. This is place from enumeration, not from description.

## Topopoetics

*Topopoetics* names the broader practice: **spatial coherence from adjacency**. Where traditional place-description explains, justifies, and situates, topopoetics allows place to emerge from the arrangement itself. The word has antecedents in Paul Carter's spatial history (*The Road to Botany Bay*) and in landscape poetry, but Sathe uses it more broadly — any act of creating felt spatial coherence through side-by-side presentation rather than logical derivation.

The key insight: a place need not be explained to be felt. The felt geography of *Zork* — "To the west is a locked door. To the north, a forest" — is pure parataxis. No map was provided; no geometry was specified. The spatial structure was *carried* by the syntax.

## Relation to Language as Place-Making

Parataxis and topopoetics sit at the intersection of [[language-as-world-making]] and place-making. Tuan's account of [[naming-as-place-making]] emphasizes the constitutive power of single names; parataxis extends this to sequences and inventories. A name summons an entity; a paratactic list summons an environment.

This also connects to the logic of exploratory cartography: the first act of mapping is often paratactic — landmark here, landmark there, no connecting road yet drawn. The map emerges from the inventory.

## Design Implications

For designers and writers, parataxis is an orientation tool. An interface that lists affordances without explanation can produce a felt sense of possibility before the user understands the system. A sentence that places two things side by side invites the reader to construct the relationship — and in constructing it, they have made a small place. The constructive work is done by the reader, not the author; the author creates the conditions for place rather than the place itself.
