# Western Canon

## Bloom's Argument

In *The Western Canon* (1994), Harold Bloom mounts a defense of canonical literary selection against what he calls the "School of Resentment" — deconstruction, New Historicism, feminist theory, postcolonial criticism, and multiculturalism as practiced in academic literary study.

The argument is not primarily political, though it has been received as such. It is fundamentally about **memory** and **aesthetic selection**:

> "Nothing is so essential to the Western Canon as its principles of selectivity, which are elitist only to the extent that they are founded upon severely artistic criteria."

The criterion for inclusion is not demographic representation, cultural significance, or historical importance. The criterion is: **does the work continue to influence writers?** Are living writers still returning to this text as a [[Precursor and Ephebe|precursor]], still being made anxious by it, still producing misreadings that generate new work?

This criterion makes the canon self-enforcing: if artists stop reading Shakespeare, Shakespeare exits the canon. The canon is not a fixed administrative list but a living system of [[Anxiety of Influence|influences]].

## Vico's Historical Structure

Bloom organizes the canon according to [[Giambattista Vico]]'s historical cycles:
- **Aristocratic Age**: Dante, Chaucer, Shakespeare, Cervantes, Montaigne, Molière, Goethe, Dr. Johnson
- **Democratic Age**: Wordsworth, Austen, Dickens, Whitman, Dickinson, George Eliot, Ibsen, Tolstoy
- **Chaotic Age**: Freud, Proust, Woolf, Joyce, Kafka, Pessoa, Borges, Neruda, Beckett

Each age has its characteristic tone: aristocratic confidence, democratic breadth, chaotic fragmentation. The canon's trajectory is not progress but increasing burden.

## The Canon as Anti-Forgetting

Bloom's deepest claim is not about culture wars but about **the role of memory** in civilization. The canon is the mechanism by which a tradition maintains continuity with its best productions against the natural tendency toward forgetting:

> "There is only so much life and only so much time; because all that has been written can't be read, there has to be at least some mechanism for selecting and bequeathing the products of any particular tradition."

This is essentially a Vichian argument: civilizations that lose access to their literary inheritance enter a new barbarity — sophisticated, technological, literate, but cut off from the depth of their tradition.

## Academic Reception

The book made Bloom the figurehead for discontent with the university English department's turn toward theory. Academics attacked the book for:
- Its dismissal of identity politics as a criterion for inclusion
- Its canonical judgments (all white, mostly male, mostly European-American)
- Its apparent certainty about aesthetic value

But the source essay's observation: the *form* of the attack (reducing Bloom's argument to politics) is itself a symptom of the anxiety Bloom diagnosed. Academic critics need to limit Bloom's influence for the same reason they need to limit Shakespeare's — the debt is embarrassing.

## What's Missing

- No account of non-Western canons or whether the concept of canon applies there
- The criterion "do writers continue to be influenced?" is circular if applied before a tradition has had time to determine influence
- The implicit assumption that "the West" is a coherent enough tradition to have a single canon is contestable
