# The Chance of Space

**Source**: [[doreen-massey]], *For Space*, Ch. 11 (2005: 111–117)

## The Core Claim

Space, understood as a dynamic simultaneity of multiple ongoing trajectories, is constitutively open to the unexpected. The "chance of space" is "that – sometimes happenstance, sometimes not – arrangement-in-relation-to-each-other that is the result of there being a multiplicity of trajectories" (Massey 2005: 111). In spatial configurations, "otherwise unconnected narratives may be brought into contact, or previously connected ones may be wrenched apart. There is always an element of 'chaos'" (Massey 2005: 111).

The figure for this: the **accidental neighbour**. Space has brought together trajectories that might never have connected. You did not choose who lives next door; space made this juxtaposition.

## What This Is Not

Massey is careful to distinguish the chance of space from:

**Pure randomness or chaos**: street markets look chaotic but are in fact "intricate constructions of multiple routines, rhythms, and well-worn paths — ordering systems" (Massey 2005: 112). The council estate looks ordered and dreary, the street market looks spontaneous and vital — but this is a class-inflected reading. The indeterminacy of space is not general ontological randomness; it is the specific indeterminacy that arises from the juxtaposition of multiple structured trajectories.

**Postmodern celebration of indeterminacy**: it has become fashionable to revel in chaos, contingency, and undecidability. Massey is sceptical: what looks like randomness to one observer may be perfectly ordered to another. The deregulated city may look chaotic from below and look like a well-understood game from the boardroom. The situationists were right to disdain the Surrealists' reliance on pure chance — "An insufficient awareness of the limitations of chance, and of its inevitably reactionary use" (Debord 1956, cited Massey 2005: 116).

**Tschumi's architectural indeterminacy**: Tschumi's Parc de la Villette superimposes three coherent logical structures (point grid, coordinate axes, curve) to produce undecidability. But Massey argues this stays within a surface-imagination: the indeterminacy arises from layering *horizontal closed structures*, not from the superimposition of *coeval trajectories*. "There is no temporality here. Space here is formed by putting together three closed horizontal surfaces" (Massey 2005: 113).

## The Correct Account: Labyrinthine Clarity

Aldo van Eyck's formula — "labyrinthine clarity" — is closer to what Massey wants. Van Eyck's Amsterdam Children's Home was designed as "a place of chance encounters and of the imagination" (Glancey and Brandolini 1999: 16); his sculpture pavilion at Arnhem aimed for the effect of "Bump! — Sorry. What's this? Oh hello!" (van Eyck, cited Jencks 1973: 316). This is the specific indeterminacy of encountering the genuinely unforeseen — not general chaos, but the encounter with alterity that space makes possible.

The correct account of the chance of space: "Not the mutual interference of (horizontal) closed structures, but intertwined openended trajectories" (Massey 2005: 113). Each trajectory is structured and by no means chaotic; but the superimposition of multiple structured trajectories produces the genuine indeterminacy of unexpected encounter.

## The Cournot Definition

Massey invokes Cournot's definition of chance: "the intersection of two or more chains of causality" (cited Massey 2005: 116). This is not pure randomness but the meeting of trajectories that are each causally ordered but whose intersection was not itself caused by either trajectory individually. The accidental neighbour is not a random event but the product of two separately-caused moves that happened to result in adjacent locations.

The situationists worked with a version of this: their *dérive* was not aimless wandering but a structured attention to the unplanned psychogeographical effects of urban form. Van Eyck's labyrinthine clarity maintained, against total indeterminacy, that "Not that the drift of the pedestrian confounded all logic" (Sadler 1998: 30).

## The Political Dimension

The chance of space is not available equally. The street-market flâneur's experience of spontaneous encounter presupposes specific class, gender, and racial positions. Massey's power-geometry argument applies here too: who gets to have the serendipitous encounter, who is always already the object of others' encounter (on others' terms), who is held still while others drift. The romanticisation of the flâneur ignores this.

At the same time, the chance of space offers a kind of hope: the impossibility of closing space, of reducing it to order, means there are always "cracks in the carapace." The containment is always incomplete.

## Relation to De Certeau

De Certeau argued that "spatialisation" (the strategy of the proper place) eliminates surprise — it "avert[s] surprises" (de Certeau 1984: 89). For de Certeau, resistance lies in the tactical improvisations of the street, which elude the ordered strategies of power. Massey agrees that space is not the realm of completed strategy — but she thinks de Certeau's spatial imagination is itself still trapped in the time/space binary. See also [[slices-through-space]].
