# Dreamtime Songlines as Place-Making

**Source**: [[tuan-1991-language-and-the-making-of-place]]; see also Bruce Chatwin, *The Songlines* (1988)

## The Strongest Case

Tuan treats Australian Aboriginal Dreamtime as "perhaps the strongest case" for his thesis that language is constitutive of place. Unlike European or even other pre-literate cultures where naming and storytelling operate alongside material transformation, for the Aborigines the relationship between word and world is more fundamental:

> "Dreamtime ancestral tracks may thus be called, as Bruce Chatwin (1988) has, 'songlines.' The ancestors have sung the world into existence." (p. 687)

The world — the humanized world of meaningful place — was not first made materially and then named. It was *sung first*. The song is primary.

## What a "Place" Means Here

In standard geographic and economic understandings, place is defined by resource locality: a waterhole, a food source, a shelter site. For Aboriginal Australians:

> "Place is not primarily the locality of an economic resource — a waterhole, for instance; rather it is wherever an ancestor has stopped to perform some action, which may be quite ordinary like cooking a meal, or extraordinary like the institution of a particular rite." (Elkin 1964, 151–55, as cited in Tuan, p. 687)

Place is wherever narrative crystallized. A clump of trees, a cave, a billabong — these acquire meaning not from their economic function but from the story attached to them. Natural features "acquire visibility because of Dreamtime actions, as these are told in myths" (p. 687). Objects that have no economic importance but high mythic importance are fully present in the world; economically important objects without attached narrative may be effectively invisible.

## The Songline as Infrastructure

Aboriginal Australia is "criss-crossed by hundreds of paths, left behind by totemic ancestors in their Dreamtime wanderings" (p. 687). These paths — songlines — are not physical roads. They are narrative routes: to follow a songline is to sing the song associated with it, to re-perform the ancestral creation event at each named place along the route. The songline *is* the path; the path exists because the song exists.

This is infrastructure in a radically different sense from Western infrastructure. Western infrastructure (roads, pipes, rails) enables movement. Songline infrastructure enables *recognition* — the ability to identify where you are within the humanized world. Without the song, the land is foreign; with the song, it is home.

## Relation to Chatwin's Account

Bruce Chatwin's *The Songlines* (1988) is Tuan's primary reference. Chatwin documented how Aboriginal Australians maintain an entire continental-scale geography through oral song — that the continent is effectively a library of interlocked narratives, where each geographic feature is a text. The Aboriginal person who "knows their country" knows the songs; geography and literature are not separate domains.

## Implications for Language and Place

The Dreamtime case is theoretically important because it provides the limit case. If one were to argue that language merely *helps* constitute place — that it is an important but secondary force — the Dreamtime case makes that position difficult. Here, language (song) is the primary act of creation. The material features of the landscape exist; their existence as *places* — as nodes in a meaningful human world — is entirely a function of whether an ancestor's song passes through them.

This supports Tuan's broader thesis: "It is their place — their world — through the casting of a linguistic net" (p. 687).
