# The Earth as Onto-Cartographic Scale

## The Earth as Plane of Immanence

In Bryant's geophilosophy — his name for the practical-political deployment of onto-cartography — the Earth functions as the ultimate scale of analysis. An ontology of immanence "is committed solely to the existence of earthly worlds, rejecting any transcendent beings" (p. 237). The Earth is not a metaphor; it is the literal plane on which all machines — inorganic, organic, social, incorporeal — operate and interact.

This is a specific philosophical claim against two dominant alternatives:
- **Vertical ontologies** (Platonism, ontotheology, transcendental idealism, certain strains of structuralism): one machine overcodes all others from a position that is itself unconditioned. God, eternal forms, the transcendental subject, Language-as-Structure each overcode being from without.
- **The nature/culture divide**: treats culture as outside of or above nature, thereby bracketing the material, non-human agencies that shape social formations. This bracketing is both philosophically confused and politically damaging.

## Flat / Horizontal Ontology

The Earth as onto-cartographic scale implies a flat or horizontal ontology: there is a single plane of machines in which all entities affect and are affected by one another. No machine stands outside the plane — there is "no *outside* to these planes or plateaus of immanence" (p. 238). This does not mean all machines are equally powerful; the sun exercises more influence over the Earth than the Earth over the sun. But differential power does not imply transcendence — it is a matter of degree of bilateral conditioning, not unilateral overcoding.

Bryant formalizes this with Table 8.1 (p. 238):

| | Type of Ontology | Relations |
|---|---|---|
| Transcendence | Vertical ontology | One machine overcodes others; sovereignty |
| Immanence | Flat/horizontal ontology | Overdetermination, bilateral conditioning; anarchy |

## Geophilosophy: Culture as Formation of Nature

The key move: "geophilosophy proposes that there is only nature, that culture is itself a formation of nature, and that there are no transcendent or vertical terms outside of nature" (p. 256). Nature is not eternal necessity but contingent history — evolutionary, geological, cosmological. The atoms were forged in stars; the laws of physics might have been different had the universe cooled at a different rate after the Big Bang (p. 252).

This dissolves the standard critical theory gesture: the claim that something is "socially constructed" rather than "natural" no longer has the political weight it once did. If nature is historical and contingent, then "you cannot go against nature / Because when you do / Go against nature / It's part of nature too" (Love & Rockets, cited p. 251). The critical gesture must shift from "this is constructed, not natural" to "this came to be through specific machines in a contingent history and can be otherwise."

## The Earth and Ecological Politics

Bryant's ecological turn: the Earth is not only the philosophical frame but the political stake. Climate change is a crisis of the Earth-scale machine ecology in which human social assemblages are embedded. As climate intensifies, "we need to overcome the bias of human exceptionalism we find in our social and political thought, so as to take into account the manner in which human social assemblages are embedded in a broader ecology" (p. 286).

The Earth as scale means political thought must attend to:
- Energy flows between social assemblages and their broader natural environments
- Waste outputs that travel through and affect the broader natural world
- Non-human machines (climate systems, ocean currents, microbes, geological formations) that actively shape the form social assemblages take

## Developmental Systems Theory as Biological Model

Bryant grounds the Earth-scale flat ontology in biology: developmental systems theorists (Oyama, Lewontin, Sterelny) show that even the development of individual organisms is not governed by a single transcendent agency (the genome) but by a massively distributed, bidirectional developmental system:

(DNA ↔ RNA ↔ Protein) ↔ structural maturation ↔ function, activity, or experience ↔ organism ↔ environment (p. 243)

This model — in which all arrows are bidirectional — is the biological analog of onto-cartography's flat ontology. No single level unilaterally determines the others.
