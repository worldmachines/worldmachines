# Maintenance of Place Through Language

**Source**: [[tuan-1991-language-and-the-making-of-place]]

## The Claim

Language does not only *create* places — it must *sustain* them. A place named and established will fade from consciousness if the name is not passed on, if stories are not "continuingly elaborated" (Tuan 1991, p. 689). Place-making is not a one-time act; it is an ongoing practice of verbal maintenance.

"'Mount Misery' will fade from consciousness if it is not kept alive by social support — if the name is not passed on by word of mouth or written on a map that is periodically consulted" (p. 689). Without the maintenance infrastructure of language, even a named place reverts toward undifferentiated space.

## The Mole and the Rat: Oral Maintenance

The canonical illustration of oral place-maintenance is Tuan's reading of Kenneth Grahame's *The Wind in the Willows* (1944). Mole has entertained Rat in his delightfully cozy rowing boat. When it is Mole's turn to reciprocate, he is ashamed of his house — "a 'poor, cold little place.'" Rat's response is to verbally recreate the house for Mole:

> "So compact! So well planned! Everything here and every thing in its place! ... No bread! ... No pâté de fois gras, no champagne! ... Ah, that little door at the end of the passage? Your cellar, of course! Every luxury in this house! Just you wait a minute." (pp. 689–690)

The physical house does not change. But Rat's verbal reconstruction of it — his narration of what the house *is* — transforms it from a source of shame into "the jolliest place I ever was in." "The kind of place that Rat recreates for his friend Mole is within the powers of most people, although the act does call for skill and wisdom, including moral sensitivity" (p. 694).

This is place-maintenance at the most intimate scale: warm conversation, as such, brightens a room. "Violent confrontation, as such, makes it look sinister" (p. 694). The same physical space becomes different places under different verbal registers.

## Literary Works: Public Maintenance

In literate societies, the written text largely supplants storytelling as the primary place-maintenance technology. Literary works have two distinctive features that oral narrative lacks:

1. **Permanence as physical objects** — "books are there on the shelf, potentially accessible to all who can read" (p. 690)
2. **Shared resonance across readers** — "all who read the same book share the basic material furnishing and feeling-tone of a common world" (p. 690)

Updike's description of London (from *The Music School*, 1980) is Tuan's central example. A literary person who has absorbed Updike's London experiences the city through a lens that makes certain features vivid and others invisible. "London has an unmistakable personality, thanks in part to the influence of its rich literature, and yet, at the same time, the city can seem unreal precisely because it is so thoroughly transformed by the literary imagination" (pp. 690–691).

"221B Baker Street is more vividly present to some Londoners than are the apartment homes of their maiden aunts, and more real by far to tourists than are the hotels they temporarily occupy" (p. 690). Literary place-maintenance can produce a *more real* version of a place than the physical experience of it.

## The Colophon Tradition in China

In Chinese landscape art, the maintenance of a place's meaning occurs through the accumulation of colophons — written commentaries added by successive readers/viewers to a pictorial scroll. "The meaning of a place deepens with the gradual swelling of coordinated voices — the colophons, which are written with not only the picture and its artist in mind but also in response to the verses that are already there" (p. 692). Place-meaning is not a fixed property of a physical location; it accumulates through the layers of verbal testimony that surround and interpret it.

## The Two-Phase Account

Tuan's full account of place and language thus has two phases:

| Phase | Act | Example |
|-------|-----|---------|
| Creation | Naming, singing, narrative | Songlines; Columbus; Adam naming the animals |
| Maintenance | Retelling, inscription, literature | Mole/Rat; Updike's London; Chinese colophons |

Places that are not maintained verbally fade. Places that are actively maintained verbally — through gossip, oral history, literary representation — deepen and become "more real" over time.
