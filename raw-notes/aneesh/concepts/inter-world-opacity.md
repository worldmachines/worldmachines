# Inter-World Opacity

## The Problem

If Bryant's [[bryant-pluralism-of-worlds|pluralism of worlds]] is correct — if there are genuinely distinct ecologies of machines separated by the absence of material interaction — then a structural consequence follows: machines from different worlds may be mutually opaque to one another.

Inter-world opacity is not merely epistemic (we don't happen to know about them). It is *material*: because machines can only interact through material mediators, and because mediators take time to traverse space, machines that cannot materially reach each other have no operational access to each other's states. They exist, but for the purposes of their respective worlds, they might as well not.

This is distinct from *intra-world* opacity — the opacity that any machine faces with respect to its world, since every machine only encounters the world through its own structural couplings. Inter-world opacity is stronger: there is no possible coupling at all, not merely a selective one.

## Bryant's Account

Bryant does not name "inter-world opacity" as a single term — the concept is assembled from several moments in Ch. 5:

1. **Dark objects** as machines present in a world but unreachable by its flows — a local case of opacity within a world that, at the limit, becomes an inter-world boundary.
2. **The locality condition**: "In order for machines to relate or interact they must be capable of touching in some way" (Bryant 2014: 116). Where touch is impossible, worlds separate.
3. **The supernova case** (p. 119): a supernova going off right now in a galaxy millions of light-years distant is effectively in a different world because no material interaction can be established within any human-relevant timeframe.
4. **World-specific mediaspheres**: each world is organized through the particular material mediators it contains. Different mediaspheres produce different kinds of interactions, which means machines from worlds with different mediaspheres face structural barriers to communication even where physical proximity exists.

## Social and Political Instances

Bryant's framework invites application to human-scale inter-world opacity. Consider:

- **Class worlds**: different economic classes may inhabit the same geographical space but be organized through such different machines (legal regimes, media, spatial arrangements, temporalities) that their ecologies are only partially overlapping. A "translation" apparatus — journalists, social workers, NGOs — is required to function as a mediating machine between them.
- **Disciplinary worlds**: academic disciplines organized around different machines (citation networks, laboratory instruments, mathematical vocabularies) can become opaque to one another even within a single institution. Inter-disciplinary work is the attempt to forge new mediators.
- **Technological generational worlds**: the world of a pre-digital community and a fully networked community are organized through such different mediaspheres that their worldings are only partially translatable.

## Relation to Content and Expression

Bryant's distinction between the plane of content (corporeal machines) and the plane of expression (incorporeal, semiotic machines) adds a layer. Inter-world opacity can operate at either plane:

- Two worlds may share a physical environment (plane of content) while operating through entirely different semiotic machines (plane of expression) — the colonial encounter as paradigm.
- Alternatively, two worlds may share expressive machines (speak the same language, share legal categories) while being organized through profoundly different material machines (plane of content).

True opacity — the strongest form — arises when the mediating machines connecting expression to content differ so radically that even shared language does not produce shared worlding.

## Synthesis Seed

Inter-world opacity ↔ Sathe's cognitive-diversity thesis: if different worlds world differently, then maintaining cognitive diversity is not merely a normative preference but an ontological hedge. Different worldings constitute different fields of salience; destroying them through homogenization (see [[epistemic-monoculture]]) collapses the pluriverse toward a single worlding and forfeits whatever machines were only legible within the lost worlds.
