# Clock as Incomplete Discipline

In [[bayo-akomolafe-the-untimely]], the clock is not merely an instrument of timekeeping. It is a disciplinary machine: plantation bells, shipboard rhythms, punctuality demands, time management, and progress narratives coordinate bodies into legible productivity. But Akomolafe's crucial claim is that this discipline is **incomplete**. Every downbeat creates an upbeat; every mark creates a not-mark; every tick-tock contains an interval.

## Mechanism

1. **The clock counts** — it establishes punctuality, sequence, productivity, and obedience.
2. **The count disciplines bodies** — on the slave ship, enslaved people are forced to dance to keep them economically viable; on the plantation, the bell commands eating, labor, stopping, starting.
3. **But the count produces gaps** — the forced dance creates syncopation; the bell's command creates songs; the ledger's mark creates what escapes the ledger.
4. **Those gaps become culture and fugitivity** — Akomolafe gestures toward jazz, blues, hip-hop, reggae, and fugitive practices as emerging from intervals in imposed time.

## Contrast with Alternative Clocks

Akomolafe is suspicious of simply replacing the clock with another clock. The question is not only how to leave clock time, but how clock time is already leaking. This makes the clock a site of decolonial possibility from within: not because the clock is good, but because no disciplinary system is complete.

## Contradictions / Conflicting Data

The strongest counter-position in the wiki is **David Landes**, *Revolution in Time* (1983). Where Akomolafe says the discipline is *incomplete*, Landes's Introduction states the completion thesis flat: the mechanical clock made possible "a civilization attentive to the passage of time," and crucially, **"punctuality comes from within, not from without"** ([[rit-02-introduction]]). For Landes the hinge from *time obedience* (the public bell that summons) to *time discipline* (the internalized punctuality imprinted from infancy) is the portable, privately owned watch — and that hinge, once crossed, is treated as a near-total interiorization. The book's last page restates it almost as Akomolafe's mirror image: time is "a deep habit, the price of modernization" ([[rit-24-the-quartz-revolution-ii]]). This is the canonical statement of the very thesis the incompleteness claim pushes against.

Landes is the more useful antagonist precisely because he is not naive about the remainder — the book is full of it, even as its overt argument is about completion. The remainder-instances Landes records form a ready-made evidence chain *for* the incompleteness thesis, against his own framing:

- **The China natural experiment** — the device arrived stripped of its social machinery and disciplined nothing, sitting inert as a chiming "toy" for two centuries; discipline lives in social relations (whose time is it?), not in the escapement ([[rit-04-why-are-the-memorials-late]]). The clearest demonstration that the clock alone is not the discipline.
- **Devils in monks' dreams** — Raoul Glaber's demon urging a sleep-in; Brother Alger woken by the devil at the *wrong* time so he sleeps through the real bell. The fugitive operates precisely in the seams of the schedule, an anxiety the disciplinary regime secretes but cannot govern ([[rit-05-are-you-sleeping-brother-john]]).
- **Saint Monday** — "Sunday was holy, Monday was holy, and Tuesday was often needed to recover"; the backward-bending labour supply of workers with a "strong preference for leisure" who bought *less* work with higher wages; and the defensive watch, workers arming themselves with the discipliner's own instrument to contest stolen hours ([[rit-16-ups-and-downs-of-international-competition]]).
- **The régleurs "at funerals"** — when quartz humiliated their craft, the suspended chronometry competitions left the master adjusters meeting "once a year for dinner; also at funerals": the human residue of a discipline whose metric outran it ([[rit-23-the-quartz-revolution-i]]).
- **Alarms ringing twice** — the book's final image of incompleteness at the level of bodies: even at total infrastructural saturation, the alarm must sound *twice* for the unwilling riser, and a "timeless vacation" is impossible only because one would have to renounce the time-bound media of modern life ([[rit-24-the-quartz-revolution-ii]]).

The synthesis: Landes documents the leak at every turn while narrating the completion. The disagreement is therefore one of *register*, not fact — Landes reads the residue as friction on the way to internalization; Akomolafe reads the same residue as the structurally inevitable upbeat that no count can absorb. The two are reading the same archive in opposite directions.
