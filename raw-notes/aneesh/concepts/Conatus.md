# Conatus

**Source**: [[baruch-spinoza]], *Ethics* (1677), Part III
**Deployed by**: [[Aneesh Sathe]], [[The Architecture of Resistance]] (2025-12-23)

## Definition

*Conatus* (Latin: striving, effort) is Spinoza's name for the fundamental drive of every entity to persist in its own being and, where possible, to expand. It is not a conscious choice but a metaphysical fact — the essence of every thing is its striving to continue existing.

In Spinoza's framework:
- Everything that exists strives to persist
- This striving is the very definition of the thing's essence
- It cannot be overcome by the thing itself — only by external causes

## The Capture Mechanism

Dominant systems capture *conatus* by converting it from active self-determination into passive reaction. Sathe identifies this across three historical regimes:

**Theological** (Spinoza's Amsterdam): The "Scriptural Average" converts the *conatus* — the drive to persist and expand — into the passive states of **hope** (that the external judge will reward you) and **fear** (of punishment). The subject's energy is directed outward toward an imagined authority rather than inward toward self-authorship.

**Nationalist** (mid-20th c. Levant): Identity frozen as a "Citizen-as-Monument" — the subject's *conatus* channeled into reproducing a fixed national or religious form.

**Algorithmic** (2025): The statistical mean captures *conatus* by predicting and surfacing expected behavior. "If every response is predicted, the individual ceases to be a cause and becomes merely a consequence of the architecture."

> "Like Spinoza, our conatus withers under the umbrella of the statistical mean."

## Spinoza's Counter

Spinoza's move: remove the external judge. Freedom consists in the **intellectual mastery of the causes that move us**. The *conatus* cannot be expanded by desire alone; it requires understanding the relational structure of causes. This is why Spinoza connects freedom to adequate ideas — not willpower but comprehension.
