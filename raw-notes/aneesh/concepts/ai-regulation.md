# AI Regulation

## The Core Tension

Sathe (see [[On Being Good vs. Knowing Good]]) frames AI regulation in healthcare as a classic double-bind:
- **Too little regulation**: AI tools of unknown quality get deployed on vulnerable populations before their failure modes are understood. Historical clinical trial abuses are cited as a cautionary precedent.
- **Too much regulation**: Beneficial applications are locked out. The healthcare system foregoes real efficiency and quality gains that could reduce harm.

This is not unique to AI — it is the eternal dilemma of innovation governance in safety-critical industries. What Sathe adds is an AI-specific argument for a more exploratory posture.

## The Correctability Argument

"Unlike drug development and other biotech fields, AI and its software can be corrected relatively easily." A drug with an adverse effect requires recall, regulatory review, and years of remediation. A software system with a flaw can be patched, retrained, or replaced quickly. This lower irreversibility cost suggests AI regulation could tolerate more exploratory deployment than pharmaceuticals.

The caveat Sathe doesn't address: software failures can scale much faster than drug failures. A bad drug affects the patients who took it; a bad AI system deployed at scale can affect millions simultaneously before the flaw is detected.

## The Quality Selection Mechanism

Sathe's preferred governance mechanism is not primarily regulatory but cultural: "Users who recognize what is good will elevate the collective ability to achieve excellence." Creating a selective environment — practitioners who can discriminate between good and bad AI tools — drives quality through market/professional dynamics.

This is a [[Taste as Epistemic Capacity]] argument applied to governance: the solution to bad AI is practitioners with sufficiently developed taste to reject it.
