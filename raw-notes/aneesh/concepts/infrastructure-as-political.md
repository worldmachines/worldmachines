# Infrastructure as Political

## Core Thesis

Political power is primarily a matter of infrastructure — of which machines are built, how they organize access to necessities, and what paths of movement and becoming they make available or foreclose — rather than primarily a matter of ideology, discourse, or normative consensus.

This is not the claim that ideas do not matter. It is the claim that **the material organization of a social assemblage is more determinative, harder to change, and less studied than the ideational organization** — and that political theory has systematically inverted this priority by focusing on meaning, belief, and discourse while treating infrastructure as background.

## Bryant's Formulation

Levi Bryant develops this argument most systematically in Chapter 7 of *Onto-Cartography* (2014) through his concept of [[bryant-gravity-as-power]]. His key intervention is to show that "power" as used in critical theory (including Foucauldian power/knowledge, Gramscian hegemony, and Marxist ideology critique) is systematically biased toward the discursive and representational.

What these frameworks miss: mountains, river systems, disease ecologies, fossil fuel deposits, transportation networks, food distribution systems. These exert what Bryant calls **gravity** on the machines that inhabit assemblages — they bend the field of possible action regardless of what anyone believes about them. A railroad does not require ideological consent to determine where cities grow. A food supply chain does not require belief to determine what populations are materially dependent on what territories.

The paradigm case in Chapter 7 is company scrip (pp. 203–205). Workers in coal-mining towns were paid in scrip redeemable only at company stores, making them entirely dependent on the company for necessities. This is not primarily an ideological phenomenon — it is an **infrastructural lock-in**. The workers may have had perfectly accurate beliefs about the injustice of their situation. What they lacked was an alternative material infrastructure through which to exit. The political solution was not ideology-critique but legal infrastructure: laws mandating federal-currency payment.

> "The question therefore is one of *building* alternatives that might generate lines of flight or paths of escape from these orbits." (Bryant 2014, p. 205)

## Precursors and Allies

This claim has precursors across disciplines:

**Fernand Braudel**: The *Annales* school's attention to long-duration geographical and material factors (climate, soil, transport routes, disease) as the real determinants of historical trajectories. Braudel's "structures of the longue durée" are heavy machines in Bryant's sense.

**David Harvey**: Harvey's spatial Marxism insists that capitalism has a geography — that accumulation requires physical infrastructure, that crises have geographical expressions, that spatial organization is not merely a by-product but a constitutive element of capitalist power. Bryant cites Harvey's Lockean-property analysis (p. 196): under the Lockean theory, factories should become workers' property once surplus value has paid back the original investment — an argument made structurally rather than ideologically.

**Michel Foucault**: Foucault's later work (especially *Discipline and Punish* and the lectures on biopolitics) is already more infrastructural than it appears — the Panopticon is an architecture, not a discourse; the clinic is a building; the asylum is a spatial arrangement. Bryant's move is to extend this material attention beyond institutions to the built environment, energy systems, and biological ecologies.

**Bruno Latour**: Actor-network theory insists that non-humans are genuine actors in social assemblages — door-closers, speed bumps, and vaccination campaigns share agency with politicians and ideologues. This is the flat ontology Bryant builds on.

**Jane Bennett**: *Vibrant Matter* (2010) argues for the political agency of material things — electricity grids, food, metal, trash. Bennett's politics of thing-power is closely allied with Bryant's gravity concept.

## The Strategic Inversion

Mainstream political theory (ideology critique, discourse analysis, hegemony theory) operates on the assumption that:
1. What primarily holds social arrangements in place is belief, consent, ideology.
2. Therefore, what primarily changes social arrangements is changing beliefs — enlightenment, demystification, consciousness-raising.

Bryant's framework inverts this:
1. What primarily holds social arrangements in place is infrastructure — the material organization of access to necessities and the gravitational fields that structure movement.
2. Therefore, what primarily changes social arrangements is changing infrastructure — building alternative material systems.

This is not to say that ideas are irrelevant. Ideas can function as **light gravity** — they organize fields of action, though more easily than heavy infrastructure. But heavy infrastructure does not require ideological maintenance; it maintains itself by being materially necessary. This is what makes it more durable and more resistant to change.

## The Problem for the Left

Bryant's argument has a pointed implication for left politics: the dominant strategy of ideological critique is insufficient for structural change. You can expose the contingency of capitalism as a system of representations without building alternative material systems. The critique may be correct and still leave the gravitational topology intact.

Gramsci's concept of the "war of position" recognized this: hegemony is not just ideological but institutional, embedded in the capillary structures of civil society. Bryant's framework extends Gramsci's insight further — the capillary structures are not just institutions (schools, churches, trade unions) but physical infrastructure (roads, energy grids, food supply chains). The war of position includes a war of construction.
