# Material Power vs. Discursive Power

## The Distinction

In *Onto-Cartography* (2014), Levi Bryant constructs a systematic critique of what he elsewhere calls [[discursive-materialism-critique|discursive materialism]] — the dominant tendency in late-twentieth-century critical theory to reduce power to its discursive, representational, and ideological dimensions.

The distinction:

**Discursive power** operates through:
- Representation, signification, meaning-making
- Normative frameworks, ideologies, belief systems
- Discourse in the Foucauldian sense — regimes of truth, subject-formation, the dispositif
- Cultural hegemony in the Gramscian sense — the production of consent
- Symbolic capital in the Bourdieuan sense — the invisible prestige-economy structuring fields

**Material power** (Bryant: gravity) operates through:
- Physical organization of access to necessities (food, shelter, water, energy)
- Transportation networks that determine where things and people can move
- Energy regimes that determine what kind and scale of activity is possible
- Financial systems that determine what transactions can occur
- Biological ecologies — disease vectors, soil fertility, river systems
- These operate **regardless of belief or representation**

Bryant's claim is not that discursive power is unreal. His claim is that it has been treated as exhaustive when it is only partial. The concept of [[bryant-gravity-as-power|gravity]] is designed to capture both:

> "The concept of gravity proposed here includes all of the discursivist and practice-based elements of concepts of power proposed by theorists such as Foucault and Bourdieu, while also helping us to recognize the power that things themselves exercise — by virtue of what they *are*, not by virtue of how we symbolize or represent them." (Bryant 2014, p. 194)

## The Foucault Question

This distinction bears directly on the reception and limits of Foucault. Foucault's analysis of power is, in certain respects, already more material than it appears. The Panopticon in *Discipline and Punish* is an architectural structure; the clinic is a spatial arrangement; disciplinary power is enacted through timetables, spatial distributions, and examinations that organize bodies in space. The material is present.

But Foucault's analytics of power are primarily analytics of the **plane of expression**: regimes of truth, subject-formation, discourse, biopolitics as a management of populations *through signification and norm*. What Foucault does not systematically develop is how power works through the plane of content — through fossil fuel reserves, through railroad routes, through disease ecologies, through the gravitational topology of mountain ranges and ocean currents.

Bryant's framework is in this sense an extension *beyond* Foucault's analytics, incorporating them into a wider ontological framework where non-discursive, non-human agencies are equally real actors in power relations. This does not render Foucault wrong — it renders him systematically incomplete on one side.

## The Gramsci Question

Similarly, Gramsci's distinction between the "war of maneuver" (direct confrontation with state power) and the "war of position" (long-term work within civil society to build hegemonic consent) implicitly recognizes that power is not located solely in the state apparatus. The war of position is fought in schools, churches, trade unions, media institutions — civil society's institutional infrastructure.

Bryant's framework extends this insight further: the war of position is also fought in the physical infrastructure of energy, transport, and food. Gramscian hegemony theory still focuses primarily on discursive and institutional dimensions. Bryant's gravity framework adds the material-infrastructural layer Gramsci's Italian prison context made it harder to theorize directly.

## The Ideological vs. Infrastructural Test

Bryant provides an implicit test for whether a power relation is primarily ideological (discursive) or primarily infrastructural (material): ask whether changing the beliefs of the dominated would change their situation.

Company workers paid in scrip have, in many cases, accurate beliefs about the injustice of their situation. They know the company is exploiting them. This knowledge does not free them — because what holds them is not a false belief but a material gravitational field: the scrip system controls their access to necessities, and there is no alternative access point they can reach. Ideology-critique leaves the gravitational field intact.

Contrast: a person who believes that their low social status is natural and deserved may indeed be held partly in place by that belief. Changing the belief — consciousness-raising — may release some of the hold. But even here, the material organization of access to education, professional networks, and capital will continue to operate as independent gravitational fields regardless of what the person now believes about their situation.

The practical upshot: both forms of power are real; neither alone is sufficient. But political theory has been far better at developing tools for the discursive and far worse at developing tools for the infrastructural.

## Relation to Bryant's Six Objects

The material/discursive distinction maps onto Bryant's six-object typology:

- **Bright objects** can be material (oil, rice, highways) or incorporeal (financial systems, legal codes, cultural norms). The political question is which type predominates in a given assemblage.
- **Dim objects** are often those whose material agency is unregistered by the dominant discursive framework. The 2010 BP oil spill revealed entire ecosystems as dim objects that had been structuring livelihoods without recognition.
- **Rogue objects** can be material (the bubonic plague, Hurricane Katrina) or discursive (a new ideology, a rogue political movement). Bryant refuses to privilege the discursive rogue objects (Badiou's events in science, love, politics, art) over the material ones.

## Implications for This Wiki's Threads

**AI and platform power**: Analysis of platform power frequently focuses on the discursive dimension — algorithmic curation as meaning-making, attention economy as the production of subjectivities. Bryant's framework pushes toward the material: the physical infrastructure of data centers, undersea cables, GPU supply chains, the energy demands of training runs. These are bright objects in the full sense.

**World machines framework**: The [[modernity-machine]] and its successors are analyzed in the Rao/Velitchkov framework primarily at the level of civilizational logic and cultural form. Bryant's framework demands also attending to the material-infrastructural layer: what energy regime underlies each machine, what physical infrastructure it requires and produces, and how that infrastructure constrains the possibilities available to machines born within it.

**Spatial epistemology**: The [[spatial-epistemology-the-master-thread]] argument that knowledge is fundamentally spatial can be read as a discursive claim (our conceptual frameworks are organized spatially) or a material-infrastructural claim (physical geography determines what can be known, communicated, and transmitted). Bryant's framework supports the latter reading.
