# Sturm und Drang

**Translation**: 'Storm and Stress'
**Period**: c. 1760s–1780s, Germany
**Named after**: Klinger's play *Sturm und Drang* (1776)

## Origin and Character

The *Sturm und Drang* movement is the literary expression of the [[Counter-Enlightenment]] in German culture. It was inspired directly by [[Johann Georg Hamann]] and [[Johann Gottfried Herder]], and fed by Jean-Jacques Rousseau's passionate pleas for direct vision and natural feeling. Its characteristic cry, from Herder, is: "I am not here to think, but to be, feel, live! Heart! Warmth! Blood! Humanity! Life!"

The movement represented "outbursts against every form of organised social or political life" — attacks not just on the Francophile rationalism of Frederick the Great's Prussia, but on the entire "tidy ordering of life by the principles of reason and scientific knowledge advocated by the progressive thinkers of France, England and Italy."

## Key Figures and Works

- **Lenz**: regarded nature as "a wild whirlpool into which a man of feeling and temperament will throw himself if he is to experience the fullness of life"
- **Klinger, Gerstenberg, Leisewitz**: dramatic works as acts of self-assertion against conventional forms
- **Friedrich Schiller**: *Die Räuber* (1781) — the tragic hero Karl Moor's revolt against rational reform; the play shows that knowledge is not enough; a fatal Rousseauian break between spirit and nature has occurred that cannot be healed
- **Goethe**: in the 1770s reacted against Holbach's *Système de la nature* as "Cimmerian, corpse-like"; saw the Gothic cathedral at Strasbourg (under Herder's guidance) as one of the noblest expressions of the German spirit
- **Heinse**: *Ardinghello und die glückseligen Inseln* — violent, radical individualism; an early form of escape from imposed rules whether of reason, politics, or ecclesiastical authority

## The Core Attitude

Art and literature as "passionate forms of self-assertion which look on all acceptance of conventional forms as but 'postponed death.'" The Romantic pronouncement at the heart of the movement: "To dissect is to murder." The seamless whole of reality is misrepresented by the static, spatial metaphors of mathematics and natural science. The living body of passionate, poetical human existence cannot be imprisoned in French rationalist categories.

## Relation to Kant

The paradox Berlin notes: it was the "profoundly rational, exact, unromantic Kant" — through his doctrine of moral autonomy — who inadvertently fed the individualism the *Sturm und Drang* celebrated. By insisting that only those who are true authors of their own acts can be held responsible, Kant created a "cult of moral autonomy" that the Romantics radicalized into unbridled self-assertion: I create as I do not because the goal is true, beautiful, or virtuous, but because it is my own.

## Legacy

The *Sturm und Drang* was the precursor to full-blown Romanticism. Its descendants include:
- The contrast between critical analytic intellect (Enlightenment) and primary imagination (Romanticism) — in Fichte, Hegel, Wordsworth, Coleridge, Goethe, Carlyle, Schopenhauer
- The Romantic identification of the creative self with nation, class, or race — which fed aggressive nationalism and, ultimately, fascism (see [[Romanticism and Political Danger]])
- Anti-positivist schools of thought culminating in Bergson
