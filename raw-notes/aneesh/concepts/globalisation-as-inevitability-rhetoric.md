# Globalisation as Inevitability Rhetoric

The political-discursive operation at the centre of [[doreen-massey]]'s critique of neoliberal globalisation. The dominant globalisation narrative does not just describe a process; it **enforces** the process by representing it as inevitable.

Clinton's 1998 line — "resisting globalisation is like resisting gravity" — is Massey's exemplary case (cited in the book's first rumination, p. 5; revisited in Ch. 8).

## How the rhetoric works

1. **Naturalisation.** Globalisation is presented not as a political-economic project but as a force of nature — like gravity, like weather, like the tide.
2. **Temporalisation.** Those who oppose it are not different but *backward*. They will eventually be swept along. See [[cosmopolitan-time-queue]].
3. **Closure of the future.** There is no alternative path. The future is already fixed by the gravitational logic.
4. **Heroic impotence.** The most powerful actors (the IMF, the WTO, finance ministers) deploy a rhetoric of *powerlessness*: "we can't help it; it's globalisation." Yet they are simultaneously the most active producers of the very phenomenon they claim is uncontrollable.

> "World economic leaders gather (in Washington, Paris or Davos) to congratulate themselves upon, and to flaunt and reinforce, their powerfulness, a powerfulness which consists in insisting on powerlessness — in the face of globalising market forces there is absolutely nothing that can be done. Except, of course, to push the process further. It is a heroic impotence, which serves to disguise the fact that this is really a project." (Massey 2005: 83)

## Why it's also a spatial move

The rhetoric is the [[spatialising-modernity]] operation in contemporary dress. It converts the world's spatial multiplicity into a single line — the line of globalisation, with "advanced" economies ahead. The political consequence: alternative globalisations, alternative economic arrangements, alternative trajectories cannot be imagined because there is "only one" globalisation, and it is inevitable.

## The structural-adjustment payoff

In the global South, the inevitability rhetoric is the political infrastructure for the imposition of structural adjustment, export orientation, the prioritisation of foreign capital, and the dismantling of the state. "It is this understanding of the unavoidability of this form of globalisation which legitimises the enforcement of export orientation on the economy of country after country." (83)

## How to break it

Massey's response, developed across Part Three:

- Recognise that this globalisation is **a project** — produced, not inevitable.
- Recognise that *another* globalisation is possible. There is no single trajectory.
- Refuse the doubleness (see [[aspatial-globalisation]]): if free trade is virtuous, so is free movement of people; if border discipline is necessary, so is border discipline on capital.
- Insist on coevalness ([[coevalness]]) — the world's societies are contemporaries, not stages.
