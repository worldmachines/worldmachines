# The Horizontalities of Deconstruction

Chapter 4 of [[doreen-massey]]'s *For Space* (2005). The most charitable of her three diagnostic chapters: [[jacques-derrida]] is, unlike [[ernesto-laclau]] and [[michel-de-certeau]], **not** guilty of leaving space as residual negativity. Derrida gives spacing explicit positive attention. And yet, Massey argues, the *practice* of deconstruction still pulls the imagination of space toward something flat and textual.

## Where Derrida is right

*Différance* contains both deferral (temporal) and differentiation (spatial). Spacing for Derrida is "a productive, positive, generative force . . . not only the interval, the space constituted between two things . . . but also spacing, the operation" (Derrida 1972/1987, fn 42 to Houdebine/Scarpetta interview, p. 106; quoted Massey 2005: 50). Space is explicitly temporalised — the "a" in *différance* adds time to the "e." This is genuinely close to what Massey wants.

## Where Massey's complaint bites

Two residual problems:

### 1. The textual imagination is horizontal

Deconstruction emerged from work on texts and writing. Texts are two-dimensional. As deconstruction generalised to "the world is like a text," the imagery of a flat surface — laid out, side-by-side, available for the deconstructive eye — came with it. The aim of deconstruction is to show that horizontal integrities are not as integral as they seem; but the **mode of seeing** that makes this possible is itself the spatialised gaze that Massey is trying to dislodge. She prefers the inverse formulation: "a text (even in the broadest sense of that term) is just like the rest of the world" (54). The world is not a flat surface to be deconstructed but a thick simultaneity of ongoing trajectories.

The deeper issue: deconstruction privileges *differentiation* (the spatial pole of *différance*) over *deferral* (the temporal pole), and so produces images of disrupted-but-still-flat surfaces rather than multi-temporal becoming. Cf. [[simultaneity-of-multiple-trajectories]].

### 2. Spacing-as-expulsion is a negative ontology

Derrida defines spacing as "the movement of setting aside . . . what is set aside from itself, what interrupts every self-identity" (Derrida 1972/1987: 107, quoted 51). Difference is produced **negatively**: from a presumed self-identical One that is then disrupted, decentred, decomposed. The constitutive outside is also the internal disruption.

Massey thinks this is the wrong ontology for space. It privileges:

- internal fragmentation over external relatedness (cf. Robinson 1999, cited 51)
- the binary Same/Other over **positive multiplicity**
- the postulation of (illusory) coherent identities that get deconstructed — rather than the recognition of multiplicity from the start

She aligns instead with the **Spinoza–Bergson–Deleuze** line of philosophical affirmation, which begins with positive plurality rather than with a One to be broken open.

## The Houdebine exchange

A telling moment Massey reads at length: Derrida's interlocutor Henri Houdebine repeatedly tries to get Derrida to acknowledge that heterogeneity is not exhausted by spacing-as-expulsion — that spacing also names the *position* of a positive something (Derrida 1972/1987: 81 ff). Derrida initially misses the point; the two talk past each other in the interview, the footnotes, and a follow-up letter exchange. By p. 94 Derrida concedes that this is "the newest and most important point." Massey reads the exchange as evidence that the negative-ontology default is deep enough in deconstruction's reflexes that even Derrida had to be argued out of it.

> "Position, location, is the minimal order of differentiation of elements in the multiplicity that is co-formed with space." (Massey 2005: 53, summing up Houdebine's recovered point)

## The diagnosis

Deconstruction *could* be spatial in Massey's sense (Doel 1999 argues it already is). But its inheritance — the textual imagination, the negative ontology, the focus on disrupting hypothesised integrities — makes it hard to "shift the imagination from a mission to disrupt the supposed integrity of spatial structures towards an ever-moving generative spatio-temporal choreography" (54).

What is needed is not the wholesale rejection of deconstruction but a reorientation: from looking at a text to **recognising one's place within continuous and multiple processes of emergence**.
