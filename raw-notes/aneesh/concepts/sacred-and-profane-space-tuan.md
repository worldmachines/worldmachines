# Sacred and Profane Space (Tuan)

**Primary source**: Yi-Fu Tuan, *Space and Place* (1977/2002), Chapters 7–8, pp. 85–117
**Primary debt**: Mircea Eliade, *The Sacred and the Profane* (1959)
**See also**: [[tuan-2002-space-and-place-ch7-9]], [[mircea-eliade]], [[mythical-space]], [[cosmogonic-architecture]]

---

## Eliade's Framework, Tuan's Application

Tuan inherits from [[mircea-eliade]] the foundational distinction between sacred and profane space. For Eliade, sacred space is qualitatively differentiated — it has an absolute character, a center, an axis mundi — while profane space is homogeneous, undifferentiated, and relative. Sacred space is not merely "religiously significant" space but the very condition of **orientation**: it provides the fixed point around which a world can be organized.

Tuan applies this framework not as religious phenomenology per se but as a description of how mythical space is structured. The cardinally-oriented cosmological space he describes in Chapter 7 is, in Eliade's terms, sacred space: it has a privileged center (the human settlement, the cosmos-middle), cardinal points that are qualitatively charged, and a vertical axis (the axis mundi connecting earth to heavens and underworld).

---

## Sacred Space as Qualified Space

In both Eliade and Tuan, the key move is that sacred space is **qualitatively heterogeneous**. It is not neutral metric space with certain locations designated "important" — it is space that has intrinsic directional values baked in:

- East: light, sunrise, life, beginning (in Greek, European medieval, and Chinese cosmologies)
- West: darkness, sunset, death, completion
- South: summer, fire, warmth, the living world
- North: winter, cold, darkness, the underworld

These associations are not arbitrary labels but felt qualities — "Oriented mythical space... It imputes personality to space, thus transforming space in effect into place" (p. 91). The cosmos is already a place-system, not a neutral coordinate grid.

---

## The Profane as Undifferentiated

Profane space, in this framework, is undifferentiated and therefore disorienting. It is the space that cannot be oriented because it lacks the sacred center and axis. The modern experience of "abstract space" — the homogeneous, isotropic, geometrically equivalent space of Cartesian coordinate systems — is in these terms profane space: technically precise but experientially evacuated. It has no "here" that is intrinsically different from any "there."

Tuan's account in Chapter 8 of the modern diminishment of architectural meaning tracks a related historical trajectory: as buildings lose their function as cosmic texts (as they become "profane" in Eliade's sense — merely functional, not world-disclosive), they lose their capacity to teach and orient.

## Sacred Space in Practice: The Pygmy Case

The Pygmy camp (Fig. 14, p. 115) provides a revealing inversion of the standard case. For the Pygmies, the social space of the camp (the clearing) is emphatically not sacred. Their sacred sentiments are identified with the surrounding forest, not with their own constructed environment. "Man-made things cannot, as it were, carry the weight of religious meaning" (p. 114). Social and religious spaces are thus separate.

This case matters because it shows that the equation of **built = sacred** is not universal. The Pygmies' religious world is the uncultivated forest, not the human artifact. What distinguishes Western technological society is not that it separates social and religious space (the Pygmies do too) but that its built environment is "pervasive and dominant, nonetheless has only minimal cosmic or transcendental significance" (p. 114) — the built world is everywhere, but it encodes nothing beyond functional utility.

## Sacred Space and the Building Act

Tuan notes that in many traditional societies the act of building is itself sacred:

> "To build is a religious act, the establishment of a world in the midst of primeval disorder. Religion, since it is concerned with stable truths, contributes to the conservatism of architectural form." (p. 104)

This is pure Eliade: the founding of a settlement repeats the cosmogonic act, the creation of ordered space out of chaos. The builder participates in a "momentous and primordial act" even when following a fully established tradition. The ceremony of construction elevates awareness even when the form is completely conventionalized.

## Rudolf Otto: The Numinous as Pre-Predicative Sacred

Adjacent to Eliade's work is Rudolf Otto's concept of the *numinous* — the pre-predicative, awe-inducing encounter with the sacred that precedes all theological articulation. Tuan's architecture-as-crystallized-awareness implies a related structure: the cathedral does not teach the worshipper about God by means of an argument; it induces a pre-discursive sense of divine presence through light, mass, height, and enclosure. The numinous is architecturally produced before it is theologically named.
