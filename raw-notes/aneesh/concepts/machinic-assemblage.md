# Machinic Assemblage

## Core Definition

A machinic assemblage, in [[levi-bryant]]'s [[onto-cartography]], is a machine composed of other machines whose internal (endo-) relations generate emergent powers or capacities that cannot be found in any of the component machines taken individually. The assemblage is not a mere heap or aggregate — it constitutes a genuinely new machine insofar as it "is able to act on other entities in the world in ways that its parts cannot." (p. 77)

This adapts Deleuze and Guattari's *agencement* (usually translated "assemblage") but grounds it more explicitly in a flat ontology of machines: every entity, from atoms to ecosystems to governments, is a machinic assemblage at some level of scale. There is no privileged level.

## Endo-Relations vs. Exo-Relations

The core technical distinction underpinning the concept:

- **Exo-relations**: external relations between two already-distinct machines. These can be severed without dissolving either machine. A television remains a television when unplugged; a frog remains a frog (at least briefly) when severed from its oxygen supply.
- **Endo-relations**: internal relations *between* machines that together constitute a new machine. These are the relations that make a machine what it is. The hydrogen-oxygen bonding that produces H₂O is an endo-relation: water is constituted by those bonds, not merely adjacent to them.

The assemblage frame establishes new machines wherever endo-relations generate new powers. This means machinic assemblages proliferate ontological types: the man-horse-stirrup-lance is a machine distinct from the man, horse, stirrup, and lance.

## Individuation by Powers/Affects, Not Qualities

Following the Spinozist/Deleuzian tradition, Bryant argues that assemblages should be individuated by their *affects* (powers), not by their qualities or appearances:

> "Latitude is made up of intensive parts falling under a capacity, and longitude of extensive parts falling under a relation." (Deleuze and Guattari 1987: 256–7, quoted p. 81)

- *Passive affects*: ways the machine is selectively open to causal input (a bat's sonar sensitivity)
- *Active affects*: capacities to produce differences in other machines (a knife's ability to cut)

A racehorse shares more affects with a draft ox than with a thoroughbred — not anatomically, but functionally. This has radical implications: we should classify the world by what things can *do*, not how they *look*.

## Emergence Without Mysticism

Bryant explicitly rejects emergentist mysticism: the emergent powers of an assemblage do not "share no relation to their parts." Water's fire-extinguishing capacity depends entirely on the properties of hydrogen and oxygen — but only as coupled *this way*. Without those bonds, the powers do not come into being. "The point is that these parts must be coupled in this way in order for the powers of water to emerge. Without those relations, these powers do not come into being." (p. 78)

Emergence is thus relational and non-reductive simultaneously: you can't predict water's properties from the properties of isolated hydrogen and oxygen, but you also can't invoke magical substance over and above the parts.

## Machinic Genesis: Reversible and Irreversible

When machines couple to form assemblages, the genesis can be:
- **Reversible**: component machines can be recovered intact (electrolysis breaks H₂O back to its elements; disassembling an automobile recovers its parts)
- **Irreversible**: component machines are destroyed or transformed in the coupling process (metabolism: a body cannot be decomposed back into the corn it has eaten)

Whether a given genesis is reversible or irreversible is an empirical question, not resolvable in advance. Many cases involve degrees of reversibility rather than a binary.

## Machinic Problems

Because component machines retain their own structural openness and operational closure, parts are never perfectly harmonized into the whole. Every assemblage faces *machinic problems*: the tension between the requirements of the unified machine and the semi-autonomous trajectories of its components.

Latour: "[n]one of the actants mobilized to secure an alliance stops acting on its own behalf." (Latour 1988: 197, quoted p. 79) Evolution is a *bricoleur*, assembling machines from pre-existing structures that served different functions, leaving every organism jerry-rigged. No assemblage is ever fully coherent; the parts always retain some capacity for "subterranean plots, machinic intrigues, tiny acts of treason" (p. 81).

## Scale Independence

Machinic assemblages exist at all levels of scale simultaneously and each level is equally real:
- Particles → atoms → molecules → cells → organs → organisms → persons → institutions → states → ecosystems
- No level is more fundamental than another — each possesses genuine powers not present below it
- Social machines (governments, universities) are no less machines than biological bodies

This is the basis for Bryant's critique of social reductionism (neoliberal reduction of society to individuals) and of humanist political theory (which ignores the assemblage-character of "humans").

## Extended Mind as Assemblage

Andy Clark's extended mind hypothesis serves as a paradigm case: mind is not contained within the skull but is a relation between brain, body, and physical media. The assemblage of person + notebook + internet has different cognitive powers than the assemblage of person + brain alone. "Cognition that takes place with pencil, paper and Arabic numerals is *different* from cognition that takes place purely inside the head." (p. 87)

This has political stakes: assemblages coupled to different media (guns, calculators, internet) possess genuinely different powers. Political equality cannot be assumed from biological similarity.

## Examples in Bryant

| Assemblage | Emergent Powers |
|---|---|
| H₂O (H + O bonded) | Fire suppression, liquid at room temperature, higher density than either element |
| Man-horse-stirrup-lance | Mounted shock combat, lance charge, medieval chivalric warfare |
| Human + notebook (Alzheimer's patient) | Functional belief-storage equivalent to normal memory |
| Human + internet | Different epistemic capacities than human + library |
| City (people + infrastructure + norms + energy flows) | Social differentiation, division of labor, sustained collective action |
| University | Knowledge production impossible for any single scholar |
