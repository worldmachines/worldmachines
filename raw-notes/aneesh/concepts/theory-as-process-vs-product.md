# Theory as Process vs. Product

## Core Distinction

[[Karl Weick]] distinguishes between:
- **Theory** (product): a finished explanatory structure with generality, accuracy, and simplicity in some combination
- **Theorizing** (process): "activities like abstracting, generalizing, relating, selecting, explaining, synthesizing, and idealizing"

Most of what appears in journals is approximation — texts created *"in lieu of"* strong theory. These approximations may result from laziness OR from "interim struggles in which people intentionally inch toward stronger theories." The products look identical; the difference is process and trajectory.

## Theory as a Continuum

Drawing on Runkel & Runkel (1984): "Theory belongs to the family of words that includes guess, speculation, supposition, conjecture, proposition, hypothesis, conception, explanation, model." Theory is a **continuum**, not a dichotomy. Social scientists should "use theory whenever they are theorizing" — not reserve it for their ultimate triumph.

The four Mertonian approximation types (not yet full theory but not nothing):
1. General orientations — broad frameworks specifying variables without relationships
2. Analysis of concepts — clarified/defined concepts not yet interrelated
3. Post-factum interpretation — ad hoc hypotheses from single observation
4. Empirical generalization — isolated two-variable proposition

## The Five "Not Theory" Elements (Sutton & Staw, via Weick)

| Element | Distance from Theory | Theoretical Content |
|---------|---------------------|---------------------|
| References | Far | Only if ideas are extracted, not merely pointed to |
| Data | Far | Closer if tied to treatment-symptom logic (Starbuck) |
| Lists | Medium | Tacit messages about causality, priority, simultaneity |
| Diagrams | Medium | More explicit about sequence and influence pathways |
| Hypotheses | Close | Lack only connections to propositions/concepts |

## The Starbuck Intervention

William Starbuck (1993): the best theorists, like the best doctors, may prescribe directly from symptoms (data) *without* theorizing as an intermediary. Theory comes last, summarizing observed treatment-symptom relationships. This is not laziness — it is a recognition that theory "does not capture all the information in data" and introducing theory prematurely discards information.

Weick's Mann Gulch paper illustrates this: data as symptoms, thought experiments as treatments, theory emerging afterward (sensemaking collapses when role structures collapse).

## The Key Evaluative Question

Not "is this a theory?" but: **"is this an interim struggle that is clearly moving from one stage to the next, with past and future articulated?"**

If yes: revise and resubmit.
If no (diagrams preceded this paper, diagrams will follow): the theorizing is less robust.

## The Ultimate Tradeoff

"Perhaps the ultimate tradeoff is the one between process and product, between theorizing and theory, between doing it and freezing it." You cannot fully honor both at once. Publication biases toward freezing — but freezing too early truncates the developing insight.

## Application to the World Machines Project

The [[world-machines-framework]] is explicitly an "interim struggle" — "sensemaking triage and shared conceptual language, not a Grand Unified Theory." The WMP framework's epistemic posture is Weickian: theorizing as process, raw historical phenomenology first, loose ends acceptable.

The WMP's collective book project is an attempt to institutionalize theorizing-as-process — to publish the interim struggle itself, not just its outputs.
