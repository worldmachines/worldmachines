# Heuristics and Biases

## Definition

The **Heuristics and Biases** (H&B) program in cognitive psychology, developed primarily by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky in the 1970s, catalogues systematic departures from normative rationality in human judgment and decision-making. These departures are labeled "biases" — anchoring bias, availability heuristic, representativeness heuristic, [[Confirmation Bias]], framing effects, and dozens more.

The program's claims: human cognition uses fast, automatic heuristics that work well in some domains but systematically fail in others, producing predictable errors. The prescription: awareness of biases + deliberate "slow thinking" (System 2) can improve decision quality.

Popularized by Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011).

## The Methodological Critique

The [[Naturalistic Decision Making]] (NDM) tradition, led by [[Gary Klein]], mounts a fundamental methodological challenge:

H&B findings are produced by:
- **Subject pool**: undergraduate students — the least expert possible population
- **Task type**: toy problems designed to elicit specific errors
- **Setting**: controlled laboratory — zero ecological validity for real-world decisions
- **Measure**: deviation from normative models of correct reasoning

NDM findings come from:
- Expert practitioners (fireground commanders, NICU nurses, military intelligence analysts)
- Real tasks in real settings under real stakes
- Observed performance, not deviation from abstracted norms

Klein et al's key claim: the *same cognitive processes* that produce "biases" on toy lab problems produce *excellent performance* when deployed by experts on naturalistic tasks. The processes are not errors — they are adaptive heuristics, correctly deployed.

## Confirmation Bias as Paradigm Case

The H&B tradition's treatment of confirmation bias (the tendency to seek confirming rather than disconfirming evidence) is the sharpest point of disagreement:

- H&B: confirmation bias is a widespread cognitive weakness; mitigation requires deliberate counter-strategies ("keep an open mind")
- NDM: in naturalistic settings, what looks like confirmation bias is rational frame-guided information seeking; the "keep an open mind" strategy consistently produces *inferior* performance compared to early frame commitment + anomaly monitoring

Klein et al pose a falsifiable challenge: find a *single expert* in a naturalistic setting performing at the top of their domain while using the "keep an open mind" strategy. If you find one, the Data-Frame Theory needs revision.

## The Futility Admission

In 2021, David Weiss and James Shanteau published "The Futility of Decision Making Research" — a paper by senior figures in the H&B tradition lamenting that decades of bias-correction research had produced negligible real-world improvement in decision quality. Kahneman himself has acknowledged that he does not believe cognitive biases can be reliably corrected through training.

Meanwhile, NDM-informed training programs continue to produce measurable performance improvements in military and medical training contexts.

## What's Right About H&B

The NDM critique does not invalidate the existence of the cognitive processes H&B identifies:
- Anchoring, availability, representativeness — these are real phenomena
- They genuinely produce errors in domains where the practitioner has no domain expertise
- Novice decision-making in unfamiliar domains is correctly described by H&B models

The dispute is about: whether these processes are *defects* to be corrected, or *tools* whose quality depends on the expertise and domain context of deployment.
