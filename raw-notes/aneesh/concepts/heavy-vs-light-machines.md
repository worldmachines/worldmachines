# Heavy vs. Light Machines

## The Distinction

Levi Bryant does not use "heavy" and "light" as explicit technical terms in *Onto-Cartography*, but the distinction is implicit throughout Chapter 7 and is essential to his political framework. It can be reconstructed from his typology of gravitational objects and his account of how assemblages resist change.

**Heavy machines** are those whose gravitational fields are:
- Spatially extensive (they organize large territories)
- Temporally durable (they persist across regime changes)
- Practically irreplaceable in the short term (no readily available alternative)
- Ecologically entangling (many other machines are dependent on them)

**Light machines** are easily displaced, relocated, or replaced without transforming the broader field of action for surrounding machines.

The distinction maps onto Bryant's bright-object taxonomy: the brightest objects are always the heaviest — oil, nuclear plants, railway systems, river systems, financial clearing infrastructure. These resist being detached from the assemblages they organize because so many other machines have evolved in dependence on them.

## The Political Weight of Infrastructure

Bryant's analysis of bright objects and satellites makes the political consequence explicit. Satellites are trapped in bright-object orbits not because they are deceived into staying but because the **material cost of exit** is prohibitive — the bright object controls access to necessities, and no alternative gravitational field exists close enough to migrate toward.

The company-scrip example is the clearest instance (pp. 203–204). The coal-mining company issued its own currency (scrip), redeemable only at company stores. This is a heavy machine in the relevant sense:

- It organizes access to food, clothing, shelter, and energy.
- It has no readily available substitute (workers cannot exchange scrip for federal currency).
- It entangles the entire worker household.
- It is maintained by a corporate apparatus with legal, financial, and physical force behind it.

The scrip-system is far heavier than, say, the company's ideology about the natural order of capital and labor. You can debunk the ideology without freeing anyone from scrip. You cannot debunk the scrip — you can only build an alternative payment system or pass legal tender laws.

## Implications for Political Analysis

Bryant's argument is that political thought has systematically focused on *light* machines — ideologies, signifiers, cultural norms, beliefs — while underestimating the weight of *heavy* machines — energy systems, transportation networks, food supply chains, monetary systems. This is partly an artifact of the discursive/academic milieu in which political theory is produced: theorists work with texts, not with physical infrastructure, so they naturally attend to the former.

But the heavy machines are precisely those most resistant to change and most determinative of the field of possible action. A political analysis that maps the ideology of a system without mapping its heavy machines has missed the gravitational topology that ideology floats on.

The strategic implication is that transformative political change — going beyond reconfiguration of discourse and into structural change — requires engaging with heavy machines directly:
- Dismantling existing ones (rerouting supply chains, retiring fossil infrastructure)
- Building alternatives (constructing new energy regimes, cooperative financial systems, distributed food networks)
- Creating "gravitational escape routes" for machines currently captured as satellites

## Relation to Constructal Law

There is a productive parallel with [[constructal-law]] (Bejan): natural and artificial systems evolve to minimize resistance to flow. Heavy machines are those that have been extensively optimized by this evolutionary pressure — they have become maximal flow facilitators and are correspondingly entrenched. The highway system, the electricity grid, the TCP/IP internet — each is a product of extended constructal optimization; each is correspondingly hard to dislodge.

## Relation to Infrastructure Inheritance

[[infrastructure-inheritance]] names the pattern by which each civilizational regime inherits the previous machine's physical apparatus and repurposes it. The heaviest machines are precisely those that survive regime transitions — railways, river systems, port cities, electrical grids — because their replacement cost is so high that each new regime finds it cheaper to inherit and repurpose than to build anew. This confirms that heavy machines are the continuity beneath ideological discontinuity.

## Relation to Modernity Machine

[[modernity-machine]] in the Rao/Velitchkov framework names the convergent civilizational machine of 1600–2000. That machine's legacy is precisely a stack of heavy infrastructure: fossil energy grids, interstate highway systems, containerized global supply chains, the nuclear arsenal, the financial clearing networks. The Divergence Machine and Liveness Machine inherit this stack. Bryant's framework explains *why* this inheritance is not optional: the heavy machines generate gravitational fields too strong to escape without the construction of alternative heavy machines (renewable energy infrastructure, distributed computing, etc.).
