# Limits of Experiential Geography

## The Self-Critical Move

Ch. 14 (Epilogue) of *Space and Place* (1977/2002) is Tuan's rarest move: a sustained reflection on what his method cannot do. Having spent thirteen chapters building an experiential account of space and place, he turns on the method itself and asks: what escapes it? The answer is not a simple caveat but a philosophical diagnosis of why experience resists being captured in any systematic account — including an experiential one.

## The Articulation Problem

The foundational limit is that most human spatial experience operates **below the threshold of articulation**. Learning is "rarely at the level of explicit and formal instruction" (p. 199). A child learns to navigate the route to school without a map, without being able to describe the route. "Abstract space, lacking significance other than strangeness, becomes concrete place, filled with meaning. Much is learned but not through formal instruction" (p. 199).

The problem deepens: not only is this learning tacit, but the social and scientific apparatus for articulating experience systematically selects *against* the most important experiential data. "We attend to what we know about; we are aware of a certain kind of reality because it is the kind that we can easily show and tell" (p. 201). At a party, asked "How do you like Minneapolis?", the typical response is "It's a good city, a good place to live in, except perhaps for the winter." The personal, subtle, tactile experience is compressed into a cliche; "our personal and subtle experiences are misrepresented time and again" (p. 201).

Tuan's sharper formulation: **"What we cannot say in an acceptable scientific language we tend to deny or forget"** (p. 200). This is not merely a sociological observation about academic convention; it is a claim about the cognitive economy of attention. The tendency to equate "knowing" with "knowing about" (Lord Kelvin: "we do not really know anything unless we can also measure it") colonizes experience itself.

## What Experiential Geography Specifically Cannot Do

Tuan's critique of the discipline is precise. The conventional geographer "writes as though people were endowed with mind and vision but no other sense with which to apprehend the world and find meaning in it" (p. 201). Books, maps, aerial photographs, and field surveys constitute the data. This produces a geography of space that systematically occludes the geography of place.

"A large body of experiential data is consigned to oblivion because we cannot fit the data to concepts that are taken over uncritically from the physical sciences" (p. 201). The physical-science model works by positing a simple, measurable subject. "The simplicity of human beings is an assumption, not a discovery or a necessary conclusion of research" (p. 203).

Tuan's own experiential method is not immune. By systematizing experience — by writing *about* the feel of place, the sense of attachment, the intensity of encounter — it risks performing the same reduction it diagnoses. "Our understanding of human reality suffers as a result" when data is made to fit pre-formed concepts, and the experiential approach, if it substitutes its own set of pre-formed concepts (pause, path, attachment, rootedness), commits the same error at a higher resolution.

## Specific Open Questions

Tuan catalogs the questions his method poses but cannot answer — these are the limits as agenda:

- What is the connection between **space awareness** and the idea of **future time and goal**?
- What are the links between **body posture** and **personal spatial values** and **distance relationships**?
- How do we describe **"familiarity"** — that quality of "at-homeness" felt toward a person or place?
- What kinds of **intimate places can be planned**, and what cannot — at least, no more than we can plan for deeply human encounters?
- Are **space and place** the environmental equivalents of the human need for adventure and safety, openness and definition?
- **How long** does it take to form a lasting attachment to place?
- Is **sense of place** a quality of awareness poised between being rooted (unconscious) and being alienated (exacerbated consciousness)? (p. 202)

## The Deepest Limit: Consciousness Annuls the Past

The final paradox Tuan articulates: "The effort to evoke a sense of place and of the past is often deliberate and conscious. To the extent that the effort is conscious it is the mind at work, and the mind — if allowed its imperial sway — will annul the past by making it all present knowledge" (p. 198). The very act of studying place-experience from the outside risks converting the pre-reflective density of place into an object of contemplation — which is to say, into space.

This is not a defect of Tuan's method specifically but of reflective method as such. Consciousness extended to cover experience transforms experience into content. The experiential geographer who succeeds too well at making experience articulable has converted lived place into represented place — which is precisely what the museum does.

## The Humanistic Resolution

Tuan's response is not to abandon method but to redefine its ambition. The essay is "one attempt to systematize human experiences of space and place. It can claim success if it has made the reader see the range and complexity of experience, and if in addition it has clarified some of the more systematic relationships between and among the wealth of experiential components" (p. 202).

The larger purpose is to enter the debate of **environmental design** — to make planners and designers ask: What connection is there between space awareness and the idea of future time? What kinds of intimate places can be planned? These questions "do not make the life of the social scientist and planner any easier. They make it, for the time being, more difficult by opening up facts that both professionals and non-professionals have found convenient to forget" (p. 203).

The ultimate ambition: **"to increase the burden of awareness"** (p. 203). The experiential method's limit is also its gift — it demands that its own blind spots remain in view.

## Contrast: Bryant's Flat Ontology

Tuan's limits are precisely the site that Bryant's onto-cartography (in *Onto-Cartography*, 2014) attempts to occupy. Where Tuan's method is first-person and experiential — it can reach the phenomenology of the subject inhabiting place but cannot adequately account for the non-human, non-experiential dimensions of spatial reality — Bryant's flat ontology refuses the subject/object distinction and treats all entities (human and non-human, animate and inanimate) as machines with equal ontological standing.

Bryant's project is not a rival to Tuan's but a complement: it addresses what the experiential method leaves out — the way non-human forces, material structures, and supra-personal systems shape the spaces and places that subjects then experience. The growth-rings city is not only a experiential phenomenon; it is also a material-historical object with causal powers independent of any subject's experience of it.
