# Pace and Rhythm as Political

## The Core Insight

In [[onto-cartography]], pace — the rate at which a machine can engage in operations — is not a neutral property. The differential between machine temporalities produces systematic political effects: slower machines cannot register signals from faster processes as information; faster machines overwhelm slower ones; and temporal saturation (fatigue) functions as a mechanism of domination more material and pervasive than ideological belief.

The political valence of time is expressed most sharply in Bryant's claim:

> "If time is a central site of the political, then this is because time is the possibility of carrying out operations. A life that is saturated by the exigency of demands ... finds it tremendously difficult to explore the possibility and construction of other operations and social relations. It is a life trapped in a web of existing relations of power." (*Onto-Cartography*, p. 174)

## Three Mechanisms

### 1. Misregistration Between Machines at Different Rates

Machines can only register flows from other machines that fall within their operational rate. Outside this range — too fast or too slow — a flow does not become *information* for the receiving machine (Bateson: information is a difference that makes a difference). This produces systematic blindness at scale:

- **Climate change vs. governmental/media machines**: Geological-rate change cannot appear as information to machines operating on electoral and news cycles. Special technologies (satellite measurement, data accumulation) are required to translate slow processes into fast-machine-legible information.
- **The fly vs. the human**: The fly registers human arm motion; the human cannot swat the fly because the fly's movements are too fast to register.
- **GPS satellite vs. ground vehicle**: Satellites run faster due to their distance from Earth's mass (relativity); without clock-compensation, they accumulate ~10km positional error per day.

### 2. Temporal Capture — Slower Machines Structuring Faster Ones

Fast machines can be captured in the rhythms of slower ones. A human's ability to respond to grief is structured by the State Department's pace of passport processing. A soldier's strategy is determined by the speed of messenger horses. A politician's planning horizon is set by electoral cycle length.

Braudel's grain example: the temporal rhythm of agricultural crops structures entire social formations. Wheat's one-harvest-per-year rhythm creates long fallow periods, necessitating food preservation, enabling family farms, generating boom-bust cycles. Rice's two-to-three harvests per year require collective labor, generate priestly classes managing reserves, and saturate farmer time. Grain temporality is not background to social organization — it is constitutive of it.

### 3. Fatigue as Domination

**Fatigue** is a real ontological feature of non-trivial machines, not a merely subjective experience. Two dimensions:

**Operational saturation**: Every machine has a finite number of operations it can conduct simultaneously. When a machine is called upon to exceed this, operations crowd each other out and nothing is attended to fully. Human beings facing excessive simultaneous demands reach a point where "the world becomes a maelstrom demanding activity" (p. 170).

**Energetic exhaustion**: Organization requires energy. Order does not come for free. A person whose daily schedule is fully occupied by work, childcare, debt service, and basic maintenance has no energetic remainder for reflection, political organizing, or imagining alternatives.

> "Operations never come for free, but always require some sort of energy to take place." (p. 170)

This is why Bourdieu's *skholè* — leisure as condition of possibility of "pure thought" — is a class privilege. The academic who theorizes emancipation forgets the temporal freedom their position provides. The emancipatory theory then alienates the people it seeks to liberate twice: first by their energetic capture in survival, second by condemning them morally for "tolerating" oppression or being "duped" by ideology (p. 173–174).

Latour's speed bump: a concrete slab in the road achieves speed reduction as reliably as a moral injunction, without depending on belief. Non-human machines distribute temporal-energetic constraints across populations. Changing the machines is more effective than changing the beliefs.

## Creating Time as Emancipatory Project

If temporal saturation is a mechanism of domination, then *creating time* — opening operational capacity — is a project central to any emancipatory politics. Idle time is threatening to dominant power:

> "It is often said that idle hands are the devil's tools. From the standpoint of dominant power, this is entirely true, for with the emergence of open time, with freedom from labor and the exigencies of life in a world, people have the means to begin questioning existing social relations, existing spatial networks, and the time to begin building those networks." (p. 174)

This reframes emancipatory politics from a primarily discursive project (changing beliefs, ideology critique) to a temporal-energetic one: what arrangements of machines would open time rather than saturate it?

## Pace-as-Political in Different Domains

| Domain | Fast machine | Slow machine | Political effect of mismatch |
|---|---|---|---|
| Climate governance | Geological change | Electoral cycle + news media | Systematic incapacity to respond |
| Poverty | Opportunity structures | Survival demands on time | Energetic capture; no operational surplus for exit |
| Military | Radio/satellite | Horseback messaging | New forms of warfare coordination |
| Bureaucracy | Human grief/need | State processing rates | State temporality structures human life |
| Academia | Contemplation | Working-class daily schedule | Scholastic disposition misreads behavioral causes |
