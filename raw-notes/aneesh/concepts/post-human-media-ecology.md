# Post-Human Media Ecology (Bryant)

## Source

Levi R. Bryant, *Onto-Cartography* (Edinburgh, 2014), Ch. 1: "Towards a Post-Human Media Ecology," pp. 15–36.

## The Core Move

Bryant radicalizes McLuhan's "the medium is the message" in two directions:

### 1. Beyond Sense-Organs

McLuhan argues that every medium is an extension of a human organ or faculty — writing extends the ear, the automobile extends the foot, the camera extends vision. Bryant grants this as a subset but insists the category must be broader:

> "*A machine functions as a medium for another machine not only when it amplifies or extends a sense-organ, but also whenever it modifies the activity or becoming of any other machine.*" (Bryant 2014, p. 33)

Examples: Vitamin B functions as a medium for human bodies when it modifies moods. Cigarettes function as a medium for lung cells when they modify how those cells maintain themselves. The temperature of a crocodile's nest is a medium for the developing eggs insofar as it determines their sex. Smart phones are media for humans because they modify the sorts of activities humans engage in.

### 2. Beyond Humans

McLuhan's account of media is implicitly anthropocentric — media are defined as extensions *of man*. Bryant rejects this restriction: "A medium is any machine that modifies the becoming or activity of another machine, or that extends the sense-organs of another machine. This holds no less for non-humans than it does for humans." (Bryant 2014, p. 34)

The electric light is no less a medium for the insects and lizards that reorganize their predator-prey relations around it than it is for humans enjoying night baseball. Remora fish use sharks as media. Ocean currents and electro-magnetic fields are media for birds navigating migration. The rotting carcasses of animals are media for fungi and bacteria.

Most pointedly: in Kafka's *Amerika*, the stoker is a medium *for the steam engine* — he extends the engine's energy flows — not the reverse. The engine has made him its extension (Bryant 2014, p. 34).

## McLuhan Recovered and Corrected

Bryant is deeply sympathetic to McLuhan's project — the recovery of the Latinate sense of *medium* as "intermediary," emphasizing the materiality of the medium rather than its content. McLuhan's example of writing vs. speech as legal media illustrates the point: what gave written law its permanence, spatial reach, and capacity to create shared identity among strangers who had never met was not the content of the law but the material properties of inscription (Bryant 2014, pp. 31–32).

But McLuhan remains too restricted. Bryant retains: materiality matters; medium shapes field of action; medium is message, not content. Bryant adds: the field of relation extends to all machine-to-machine couplings, not only human ones.

## Why "Post-Human"

The theory is post-human "in the sense that it is not restricted to how various entities function as media for human beings." It also investigates:
- How non-human machines function as media for other non-human machines
- How human artifacts function as media for non-humans
- How humans function as media for non-humans

"As such, it presents an ecological vision of relations between machines that elides any fundamental distinction between the kingdom of the human and the kingdom of the non-human." (Bryant 2014, p. 35)

## Media Ecology as Ecology

The investigation of media is "closer to ecology than to the investigation of what we ordinarily refer to as 'mass media.'" Structural couplings between machines form ecologies — webs of mutual modification without a centre, without a designer, without a human subject at the apex.

This gives onto-cartography its ecological dimension: mapping a world is mapping the structural couplings between the machines that compose it, and understanding how those couplings organize the possible movements, becomings, and activities of the machines involved.

## Relation to This Wiki

Bryant's post-human media ecology bears a direct resonance with the wiki's [[technology-as-place-maker]] synthesis, but operates at a more foundational ontological level. Where "technology as place-maker" asks whether a technology creates habitable, orienting places or disorienting spaces *for humans*, Bryant's framework asks how any machine modifies the field of possibility for any other machine — prior to the question of human experience.

This opens a critical question: can Bryant's ecological framework be reconciled with the spatial-epistemological evaluation criterion (does this technology help humans make places)? Bryant would likely say: that criterion is legitimate but anthropocentrically scoped. A full onto-cartography would also ask what the smart phone does as a medium for the server farms that power it, for the cobalt-mining ecosystems that supply its batteries, and so on.
