# Recognition-Primed Decision Model

## Definition

The **Recognition-Primed Decision (RPD) model** (Klein, 1998) describes how experienced practitioners make decisions in naturalistic settings. Instead of enumerating options and comparing expected utilities, practitioners:

1. **Recognize** the situation as belonging to a familiar type
2. This recognition immediately activates: *goals* (what matters here), *cues* (what to look for), *expectancies* (what should happen next), and *action scripts* (how to respond)
3. The first recognized option is typically good enough — it is mentally simulated and either executed or adjusted

The model has three levels of increasing deliberateness:

### Level 1: Simple Match
A situation is recognized; an action is activated. No conscious deliberation. This is the basis of experienced intuition — what Klein later calls "seeing the invisible" (connections that experts see immediately and novices miss entirely). Corresponds to the basic data-frame match in [[Frames and Framing]].

### Level 2: Deliberate Sensemaking
The decision maker is uncertain about the nature of the situation and deliberately constructs an interpretation — a story or frame — to account for the data. This is the domain of the [[Data-Frame Theory of Sensemaking]]. Klein et al. (2007): "At Level 2, the decision maker *does* deliberate about the nature of the situation, sometimes constructing stories to try to account for the observed data. At Level 2, the decision maker is engaging in the different forms of the sensemaking."

### Level 3: Mental Simulation
The decision maker evaluates an action option by mentally simulating how it would play out. If it fails in the simulation, the option is modified or abandoned, and the next option is retrieved and simulated.

## Why It Matters

The RPD model challenges classical rational choice models (enumerate all options, evaluate all outcomes, maximize expected utility) by demonstrating that expert practitioners rarely if ever do this in time-pressured naturalistic settings — and that they don't need to, because recognition exploits experience more efficiently than exhaustive analysis.

**Not the same as "gut feeling"**: RPD is pattern recognition based on genuine expertise — learned from extensive experience in a domain. It is *not* irrational intuition. The patterns recognized are real; the action scripts activated have been repeatedly refined. When intuition fails, it is usually because the current situation differs from past situations in ways the practitioner didn't notice.

## RPD and the Data-Frame Theory

The RPD model and the data-frame theory are complementary:
- Level 1 RPD is the automatic data-frame match — preconscious pattern recognition
- Level 2 RPD is deliberate sensemaking — the domain of the seven [[Sensemaking Forms]]
- The data-frame theory provides the cognitive mechanism underlying Level 2

Klein et al. (2007): "The data-frame theory is an extension of the story-building strategy described in this Level 2."

Also: "The concepts of frames and routines are linked, as described by the recognition-primed decision model. Once a person adopts a frame for understanding a situation, the frame will frequently include a set of routines for typical ways of taking action." The RPD model's action scripts are part of what frames activate.

## RPD and Expertise

The RPD model locates expert advantage in the *recognition stage*, not the *reasoning stage*. Experts don't reason better; they see more. Their richer pattern libraries allow them to:
- Recognize situations as familiar when novices see ambiguity
- Generate action scripts immediately when novices must deliberate
- Notice violations of expectancy (anomalies) that novices miss
- Achieve Level 3 mental simulation quickly because the option space is already constrained by recognition

This is the empirical basis for Klein et al.'s (2007) finding that expert and novice reasoning *strategies* are the same — both abductive, both connection-seeking — but expert outputs are far better.

## Sources and Domains

RPD was originally developed from studies of fireground commanders (Klein, Calderwood & Clinton-Cirocco, 1986) — commanders making attack decisions within seconds of arrival. Extended to: Navy AEGIS commanders, ICU nurses, chess players (via deGroot), military planners.
