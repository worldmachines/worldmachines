# Deliberate Archival Erasure

## Definition

Deliberate archival erasure is the strategic destruction of a civilization's records — maps, charts, logbooks, manuscripts — by a conquering or competing power. Unlike natural document loss (fire, flood, decay), erasure is intentional: the goal is to prevent future knowledge of what was known or accomplished.

## The Majapahit Case

The clearest documented example: the Portuguese Royal Decree of November 13, 1504, ordering destruction of "all maps, charts and logbooks connected with navigation south of the equator."

Consequences:
- Not a single indigenous Javanese cartographic specimen is known to have been preserved
- "Pre-Portuguese Indonesian cartography has never been the subject of any specific study"
- Javanese naval technology — including nine-decked ships potentially carrying 1,000 people — remains unmeasured and largely uncharacterized

This was not collateral damage. It was a deliberate effort to eliminate evidence of navigational capability that rivaled and pre-dated European maritime dominance.

## Why Erasure Happens

1. **Competitive threat**: Records of rival capability undermine the legitimacy of claims to supremacy
2. **Monopoly on knowledge**: Controlling navigation charts = controlling trade routes
3. **Retrospective authority**: Destroying pre-existing records allows the conquering power to define what counts as "discovered" and "advanced"

## Erasure and the History Machine

[[History Machines]] can be deliberately sabotaged through archival erasure. A history machine requires its components to survive long enough to assemble — destroying the records of those components can prevent future paradigm shifts from drawing on them.

The Majapahit case shows this is not hypothetical: an entire tradition of naval cartography was effectively removed from the historical record by decree. What would modern maritime technology look like if Javanese charts had survived?

## The Inverse: Notes in the Margins

Erasure is resisted by peripheral, undervalued documentation — [[Florian Lohse]]'s "notes in the margins" figure. The Majapahit court official's irrelevant personal observations survived *because* they were ignored; high-status records (navigation charts, royal archives) were targeted.

This suggests a structural irony: the most durable historical records are often the ones deemed least important at the time of their creation.

## Relation to Power-Knowledge (Foucault, not cited)

The dynamics here connect to Foucaldian power-knowledge analysis, though none of the source texts invoke Foucault: knowledge is not neutral, archives are political, and the production of ignorance is as active a project as the production of knowledge. What we don't know about Majapahit is a historical fact with a historical cause.
