# Worlding (Bryant)

## The Term

"Worlding" in Bryant's *Onto-Cartography* (2014, Ch. 5) is the gerundive, active form of "world" — treating world not as a static noun (a container or background) but as a *process*: the ongoing, dynamic act by which an ecology of machines maintains, reproduces, and transforms itself. A world is not a thing that *has* a structure; it is a *structuring* — a continuous activity.

The term is not Bryant's coinage. It has a history in Heidegger (the *Weltlichkeit* of Dasein, the "worlding of world" in *Being and Time* and the later essays), in feminist philosophy (particularly in the work of María Lugones, who uses "worlding" to describe the practices by which subjects inhabit and co-create social worlds), and in postcolonial studies. Bryant departs significantly from Heidegger: for him, worlding is not an event of Dasein's disclosedness but a *machinic* operation that has nothing essentially to do with human beings.

## What Worlding Means in Bryant's Ontology

For Bryant, worlding has several interrelated senses:

**1. Constitution of the field of relevance.** A world "worlds" by constituting what counts as significant, what flows circulate, what couplings are possible. A world does not represent significance — it *is* the structured ecology that makes certain interactions possible and forecloses others. The bee world is not *described* as centering on floral nectar flows; those flows *are* that world's worlding.

**2. Differential salience production.** Worlding produces the distribution of bright, dim, and dark objects — it is the process by which some machines become gravitationally dominant and others remain peripheral. See [[bright-dim-dark-rogue-objects]].

**3. Material mediation of interaction.** A world worlds through the mediators it contains. The kinds of media available within a world — soundwaves, texts, digital packets, road networks — determine what can interact with what, and how far that interaction can extend. Worlding is partly a function of the *mediasphere* (Bryant's term from Ch. 5, p. 118) of the world.

**4. Ongoing production, not given ground.** Worlds are not backgrounds or stages. They are themselves dynamic machines — though Bryant distinguishes worlds from machines in one crucial respect: the elements of a machine are internally related such that their separation destroys the machine, whereas the elements of a world are externally related and can be transferred between worlds. Worlding is therefore more fragile and contingent than machinic operation; it is perpetually at risk of dissolution through entropy or the introduction of rogue objects.

## Worlding as Anti-Correlationist Category

The main philosophical work of the worlding concept is anti-correlationist. Heidegger's account of world-disclosure ties worlding to Dasein: the world worlds through Dasein's thrownness and projection. For Bryant, this confuses one particular machine's structural openness to a world with the world itself. The world worlds regardless of whether any cognitively sophisticated machine is present to "disclose" it.

> "It is a failure to engage in alien phenomenology, posthumanist phenomenology, or second-order observation that leads us to miss the being of world." (Bryant 2014: 114)

Worlds indifferent to cognition: the Carboniferous forests constituted a world — an ecology of photosynthetic, geological, atmospheric, and biological machines in dense coupling — long before any entity capable of phenomenological disclosure existed. The worlding was real; its independence from intentionality is the ontological wager of [[onto-cartography]].

## Worlding and the Plurality of Worlds

Because worlding is a machinic process rather than a human-correlated event, and because machines are external to their relations, different assemblages world differently and independently. A bee world worlds through pheromone flows, waggle-dance communications, floral geography, and hive thermodynamics. A corporate world worlds through contract law, financial flows, physical infrastructure, and semiotic machines of brand and regulation. Neither is a subset or reflection of the other; each worlds in its own right.

This plural worlding is the basis for [[bryant-pluralism-of-worlds]]: there are as many worldings as there are loosely coupled assemblages of machines forming their own ecologies.

## Worlding and "Place"

The concept of worlding is in productive tension with Tuan's account of "place." For Tuan (1977), place is what space becomes when it acquires meaning — when human experience, memory, and care invest a location with significance. Place is, in Tuan's terms, a "pause" in movement where value is established. This is a human-experiential account of something Bryant would call worlding's experiential surface.

Bryant's worlding is the more general category: what Tuan calls "place" is the human-correlated subset of worlding — what worlding looks like to a cognitively and affectively open machine (a human). To say that a place is experienced as a "pause" is to describe how a human machine, as part of a richer worlding, picks out and dwells within a particular sub-ecology. The worlding is always broader than any individual machine's experience of it.
