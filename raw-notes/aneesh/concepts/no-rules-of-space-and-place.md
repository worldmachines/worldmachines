# There Are No Rules of Space and Place

The political-philosophical thesis of Chapter 14 of [[doreen-massey]]'s *For Space* (2005). Drawing on Laclau and Mouffe's claim that "there is no universal politics of topographic categories" (Laclau & Mouffe 2001: 180, quoted 165), Massey argues that no spatial principle — openness, closure, the local, the global, hybridity, rootedness — can be deployed as an *a priori* political guide. Each situation must be assessed on its own terms.

## The four cases

Massey demonstrates the thesis through four extended examples, each of which generates the *opposite* political verdict from a familiar topographic principle.

### 1. The Deni of Amazonia

Greenpeace works with the Deni Indians in western Brazilian Amazonia to **demarcate** their traditional territory — to draw a boundary. The political verdict: support the boundary. Closure here is anti-colonial. The progressive position is the bounded place.

### 2. Hamburg / EU immigration

The European left supports **relaxed** immigration into the EU; the "oldest immigrant" boulder ([[hamburgs-oldest-immigrant]]) is the rallying image. The progressive position is openness, against the closure of Fortress Europe.

### Combining 1 and 2

The European left holds *both* positions simultaneously: closure for the Deni, openness for Hamburg. There is no inconsistency *if* the principle is not a topographic one (openness=good, closure=bad) but a relational one (assess the specific power-geometry).

### 3. Isle of Dogs, London

A working-class East London area suffers under the City's overflow. Council housing is overcrowded; a 1993 by-election returns a BNP candidate. The "local voice" here is both racist and a real grievance. The local council's "Sons and Daughters" housing policy — preferring local-born families for housing — is criticised as exclusivist. But the area is, on every measure, under tremendous economic pressure. The political question is not "openness or closure" but **what are the power-geometries** producing this situation and what specific intervention they call for.

### 4. José Bové and Confederation paysanne / McDonald's-Millau

In 1999 Bové led the dismantling of a McDonald's in Millau, France. The action is supported by some as nationalist closure and rejected by others as anti-American xenophobia. Bové and Dufour spend years insisting:

- They are not anti-American.
- They are not pro-national-closure.
- They are pro-democratic-control of food and farming.
- They are *pro-local* in the sense of valuing local specificity in the human-nonhuman negotiation.
- They are *internationalist* in the sense of building alliances (Via Campesina) with small farmers worldwide.

Massey: this is "immensely politically productive . . . it forces one into the excavation of what are the real political issues in this (particular) situation" (172).

## The Derrida-hospitality formulation

Massey closes the chapter with Derrida's *On Cosmopolitanism and Forgiveness* (2001) on hospitality:

> "Within an historical space which takes place between the Law of an unconditional hospitality, offered *a priori* to every other, to all newcomers, whoever they may be, and the conditional laws of a right to hospitality, without which The unconditional Law of hospitality would be in danger of remaining a pious and irresponsible desire . . . Experience and experimentation thus." (Derrida 2001: 22–23, quoted 176)

The unconditional Law (radical openness, hospitality to every newcomer) and the conditional laws (the actual political negotiation, with all its limits) are **both required**, **mutually irreducible**, **inseparable**. Politics happens *between* them.

## What the thesis refuses

- "**Spatial fetishism**" (Massey 2005: 165) — the treatment of any topographic category (openness, closure, local, global) as bearing political value in itself. See [[spatial-fetishism]].
- The right's contradictions: free trade for capital, border discipline for migrants. The doubleness is the political work; it relies on two contradictory topographic universals.
- The left's mirror contradictions: anti-globalisation **and** open borders, on the basis of equally antinomic universals.
- The romanticisation of either openness or closure as such.

## What the thesis affirms

- **Assessment of [[power-geometry]]**: who is positioned how with respect to what flows; who bears the cost of which arrangement.
- **Specificity**: each situation must be examined anew; no template covers them all.
- **Experimentation**: politics is experimental, not deductive.
- **Coevalness**: the people in the situation have to be active participants in defining their own response. See [[coevalness]].
