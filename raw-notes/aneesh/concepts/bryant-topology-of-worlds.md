# Bryant's Topology of Worlds

## Core Claim

In [[onto-cartography]], Levi Bryant argues that space is not a pre-existent Newtonian container within which machines are placed, but something that *arises from* machines themselves through their paths, couplings, and interactions. This is the central shift from Euclidean/Cartesian geometry to topology as the appropriate framework for mapping worlds.

> "In a topological conception of space, space is conceived of as a network of paths between machines or nodes produced by machines." (*Onto-Cartography*, p. 144)

## Newtonian vs. Topological Space

| Dimension | Newtonian Space | Topological Space |
|---|---|---|
| Ontological status | Pre-existent container | Produced by machines |
| Distance | Invariant metric | Relational; depends on paths |
| Homogeneity | Uniform throughout | Varying density, fluidity |
| Change | Entities move within fixed space | Space itself changes as machines change |
| Political significance | Ignores structural exclusion | Reveals who can reach whom |

In Newtonian space, Austin, TX is necessarily closer to another US city than to Cairo because a constant metric grids all space. In topological space, a colleague in Cairo whom you email daily may be topologically nearer than a neighbor you never interact with.

## Paths as the Substance of Space

Space is composed of paths and flows. Paths are themselves machines — not just the surface along which other machines move, but "densities and fluid vectors produced by other machines" (p. 143). They include:

- **Corporeal paths**: roads, mountain passes, ocean currents, hallways
- **Technological paths**: radio waves, telecommunications networks, capital flows
- **Expressive/semiotic paths**: gendered signage, passports, national borders, DSM diagnostic categories, racial classifications

Lacan's two doors ("Men's"/"Women's") are semiotic machines that segregate human bodies into two topological spaces. Passports open or close paths between national territories. The DSM-IV generates paths of treatment — and also forecloses paths by institutionalizing.

## The Atlantic City Case

The casino example is Bryant's sharpest illustration. Low-income communities were metrically adjacent to Atlantic City casinos, so urban planners expected economic spillover. But the casinos were topologically self-enclosed: visitors arrived directly, spent money within the casino system, and departed via direct paths back. The surrounding city was *topologically remote* despite metric proximity. The lesson: metric distance is insufficient to analyze or predict relations between machines.

China Miéville's *The City & The City* dramatizes the inverse: two cities occupying the same geographical space while remaining topologically distinct through expressive machines of perception, law, and trained inattention.

## Three Network Topologies

Bryant follows Paul Baran's network typology (Figure 6.1):
- **Centralized**: all nodes equidistant from center; center is equally proximate to all others; peripheral nodes maximally distant from each other
- **Decentralized**: clusters of centralized hubs linked to each other; intermediate distances
- **Distributed**: dense lateral links; many short paths between most nodes

These topologies produce radically different proximity, power, and vulnerability structures — independent of physical location.

## Space as Process

Following [[doreen-massey]], Bryant insists topological fields are never static:

> "Space proper consists of discontinuous topological fields produced through interactions between machines. It is thus crucial to note that spatial fields are not static and fixed like Newtonian space, but are in a constant state of change, production, and becoming." (p. 147)

The printing press created new paths, collapsing topological distances between ideas and populations previously unconnected. Internet blogs created new nodes linking people who would never otherwise have met. New machines appear within topological fields, reconfiguring relations without changing Euclidean positions.

## Topological Becoming

A machine placed at a different node in a topological field will encounter different inputs, structural couplings, and flows — and will *become differently*. Kim Stanley Robinson's *Red Mars* illustrates: colonists' children develop taller and differently muscled in Martian gravity, because their bodies have been structurally coupled to a different topological-gravitational field. Powers are not intrinsic essences but capacities developed through enmeshment in topological milieux.

This demolishes biological essentialism: the capacities attributed as fixed essences of women, racial groups, or classes are by-products of topological fields that structure movement, education, and becoming.

## Political Stakes

Topological fields must perpetually struggle against entropy — they can therefore be changed. Even totalitarian regimes, which attempt to reduce entropy in topological fields to zero by rigidly controlling all paths, are paradoxically *more* fragile than loosely organized ecologies because:
1. Maintaining total control is energetically costly
2. Rigid systems generate resentment
3. Rigid expressive webs prevent adaptive response to novelty
