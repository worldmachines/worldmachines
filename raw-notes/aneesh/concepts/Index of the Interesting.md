# Index of the Interesting

The Index of the Interesting is [[Murray S Davis]]'s taxonomy of the 12 logical categories by which propositions can deny their audience's taken-for-granted assumptions, making them *interesting* rather than obvious or absurd.

## The 12 Categories

Each category operates on a different dimension of the [[Assumption-Ground]]:

1. **Organization** — What seems disorganized is actually organized (or vice versa). Pattern ↔ chaos.
2. **Composition** — What seems individual is actually holistic (or vice versa). Part ↔ whole.
3. **Abstraction** — What seems local is actually general (or vice versa). Particular ↔ universal.
4. **Generalization** — What seems heterogeneous is actually one species (or vice versa). Many ↔ one.
5. **Stabilization** — What seems stable is actually unstable (or vice versa). Static ↔ dynamic.
6. **Function** — What seems to function as X actually functions as Y. Manifest ↔ latent.
7. **Evaluation** — What seems good is actually bad (or vice versa). Value reversal.
8. **Co-relation** — What seems unrelated is actually correlated (or vice versa). Independent ↔ dependent.
9. **Co-existence** — What seems to co-exist cannot (or vice versa). Compatible ↔ incompatible.
10. **Co-variation** — What seems to co-vary positively actually co-varies inversely. Direct ↔ inverse.
11. **Opposition** — What seems opposed is actually similar (or vice versa). Different ↔ same.
12. **Causation** — What seems the cause is actually the effect (or vice versa). Cause ↔ effect reversal.

## The Sweet Spot

An interesting proposition denies *one specific assumption* while leaving the rest of the belief structure intact. Too little denial = obvious. Too much denial = absurd. The art is in targeting the right strand of the web.
