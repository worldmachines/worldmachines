# Rules of Thumb as Portable Place

**Source**: [[aneesh-sathe]], *We Need Homes in the Delta Quadrant* (2025), §II — Exploration and Navigation; §IV — Traditions, Tools, and Temporal Sediments

## The Formulation

Sathe introduces the concept in §II and develops it more fully in §IV:

> "Rules of thumb emerge here as quietly powerful. They encode accumulated wisdom without demanding full explanation. A rule of thumb is a kind of portable place — a local memory that survives relocation. It allows someone to move meaningfully through new terrain without starting from nothing." (§II)

> "They are the crystallization of hundreds of micro-failures, carried forward in language so that others may avoid or adapt. A rule of thumb is a place you can carry in your mind — a place where you briefly borrow the perspective of others, where their past becomes your foresight." (§IV)

## Why "Place"?

The place metaphor does real conceptual work here, not merely rhetorical. A rule of thumb is a *place* in the sense that it provides:

1. **Orientation** — in an unfamiliar situation, the rule provides a starting point, a direction to face
2. **Boundary** — it delineates which terrain is familiar (covered by the rule) from which remains unknown
3. **Memory** — the rule carries accumulated experiential deposit, the way a physical place carries the sediment of prior inhabitants
4. **Portability** — unlike a physical place, the rule travels with the person, can be applied in multiple contexts

The rule of thumb is a *collapsed* place — a three-dimensional history of situated experience compressed into a sentence or principle, which can be re-expanded into lived guidance when needed.

## Examples Sathe Uses

"Measure twice, cut once." "If it's not a hell yes, it's a no." "Always leave a version that works." These are not abstract truths but local condensations of experience in specific domains. They carry the implicit qualifier *in this kind of situation*. Their portability depends on pattern recognition — the ability to see that the new situation is relevantly similar to the situation that generated the rule.

## Connection to Ethnographic Engineering

Sathe pairs rules of thumb with **ethnographic engineering** — living among those you design for. The designer who has lived in a user's context has generated their own rules of thumb through situated experience. Those who haven't must borrow rules from others who have. In either case, the rule serves as a portable place: a device for navigating situations that exceed current knowledge.

The mantra "get out of the building" is itself a rule of thumb about when rules of thumb are insufficient — an acknowledgment that no portable place can substitute indefinitely for the real place.
