# Social Development Index

Ian Morris's *The Measure of Civilization* (2013) constructs a quantitative index of social development — defined as "a social group's ability to master its physical and intellectual environment to get things done in the world" — tracking civilizational capability across the last 15,000 years.

## Four Components

1. **Energy capture** — total energy harnessed per capita (food, fuel, feedstocks), measured in kilocalories/person/day
2. **Organization** — proxied by the size of the largest permanent settlement, on the grounds that maintaining large cities requires and reveals organizational capacity
3. **Information technology** — from no writing to digital telecommunications, scored on a scale reflecting the speed, range, and fidelity of information transmission
4. **War-making capability** — the scale and sophistication of military operations a society can sustain

Each component is scored on a 0–250 scale, giving a maximum composite score of 1,000.

## Key Findings

The index reveals a striking pattern of lead-changes:

- The "West" (broadly: Mesopotamia → Mediterranean → Atlantic world) led from the origins of agriculture through roughly the 6th century CE
- "East" (broadly: Yellow River → Chinese civilization) led from roughly the 6th through the 18th century
- The West regained the lead in the 18th–19th century and has held it since

Morris uses this to argue against both cultural-essentialist explanations ("the West is inherently superior") and accident-based ones ("it was all contingency"). Instead, geography and ecology set different constraints that made certain developments easier or harder in different regions at different times.

## Relation to World Machines

The Social Development Index provides the quantitative backbone for comparing "machines" across eras in the [[world-machines]] framework. Where Rao's framework is qualitative and structural, Morris supplies the measurement apparatus. The index also connects to [[energy-rate-density]] (Chaisson's complexity metric): both treat energy flow as the master variable of civilizational capability, though Morris measures it at the societal level while Chaisson measures it per unit mass.

## Limitations

The index is only as good as its proxies. Using city size for "organization" obscures non-urban forms of complexity (pastoral empires, distributed trading networks). War-making capability privileges states over stateless societies. And the East/West binary, while necessary for tractability, flattens enormous internal variation.
