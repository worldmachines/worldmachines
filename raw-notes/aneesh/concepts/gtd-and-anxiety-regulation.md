# GTD and Anxiety Regulation

## Reframing GTD

[[Getting Things Done (GTD)]], by [[David Allen]], is conventionally framed as a productivity methodology. [[The Plato Plateau]] reframes it as an **anxiety regulation system**:

- **Primary function**: Crash the anxiety overload by externalizing the signal inventory (capture everything, clear the mental RAM)
- **Secondary function**: Reintroduce *future* anxiety in a structured, tractable form via 10k/30k-foot reviews

The insight is that GTD is not a path to nirvana (zero anxiety, pure presence). The horizon reviews deliberately inject future concerns back into the system. GTD is the anxiety motor's maintenance interface.

## Two Failure Modes

1. **Too much anxiety**: GTD works well here — it provides a structured crash
2. **Too little anxiety**: GTD becomes identity-thinning. If there is nothing pressing, the system runs in idle and identity becomes ephemeral

This implies that GTD users who feel directionless despite a clean inbox are experiencing the second failure mode, not a GTD failure per se.

## Relationship to Identity

GTD interacts with identity through [[Anxiety as Generative Force]]. Clean capture prevents [[Identity Crash]] by ensuring anxiety stays in the productive range. But GTD cannot *generate* anxiety — it can only regulate it.

## Contrast with Pure Productivity Framing

The productivity framing (capture tasks, do tasks, feel productive) misses the affective and identity-level function. A person who uses GTD purely for throughput optimization misses the system's deeper value as identity maintenance.
