# Strategic Cartography

## Definition

Strategic cartography is the action-oriented pole of [[onto-cartography-as-practice]]. It names the two map types — **vector maps** and **modal maps** — that translate topographical and genetic analysis into forward-looking strategic intelligence. Bryant invokes physicians, generals, and engineers as his intuition pumps: these practitioners cannot act without knowing the "lay of the land" (p. 257). A general who knows troop positions but not supply lines, factories, and logistics is dangerously under-mapped. An activist who knows what capitalism is but not where it is going has no strategic leverage.

## Vector Maps

Vector maps chart "the trajectories along which worlds are unfolding" based on gravitational tendencies and machine interactions in the present (p. 264). They are always fallible projections — not prophecy but anticipation.

Exemplary vector cartographers:
- **James Hansen**: maps climate through emissions, population, feedback loops — projects where the world is going
- **Marx in *Capital***: examines dynamics of capital in the present to chart the future it was tending toward — wage depression, crisis, polarization of wealth

The political necessity of vector maps: without them, activists respond to the immediate — the demands of the day — rather than to what *ought* to concern them. Marx's point: teachers who fight for better testing, without knowing that standardized testing feeds a capitalist ecology of assessment corporations, inadvertently reinforce the gravitational structure they oppose (p. 265). The vector map reveals that the *real* issue is elsewhere.

## Modal Maps

Modal maps "map futures that *could* exist if we were to intervene in ecologies in particular ways" (p. 266). Where vector maps describe the likely future if an assemblage is left to run, modal maps describe possible futures conditional on specific interventions.

Modal maps are "a sort of synthesis of topographical maps and vector maps" (p. 266). The process:
1. Identify the current topology (topographical map)
2. Identify the trajectory (vector map)
3. Determine what machines would have to be added or subtracted to produce a preferred alternative
4. Build the modal map: the picture of the target world and the required interventions

The modal cartographer is an activist or engineer — they ask "how would we have to change the existing assemblage to produce this world?" (p. 266).

Exemplary modal intervention: the environmental activist noting that the current vector leads to mass extinction and climate catastrophe, then composing a modal map of a survivable world — what machines (energy systems, agricultural practices, transportation networks, economic institutions) would have to be added and subtracted.

## The Map Is Not the Territory

Bryant's crucial self-limitation: "The paradoxical danger of cartographical analyses is that they can blind us to the new forms social ecologies are taking. We forget that the map is not the territory and that, in any event, territories change" (p. 285). Marx and Foucault are both exemplars and cautionary tales — their specific maps were of specific assemblages under specific historical conditions, and cannot simply be transplanted.

This is strategic cartography's constitutive limitation: every map selects, and selection excludes. The map is always partial; the territory always exceeds. Cartographers must remain open to revising maps as the territory changes.

## Cartography vs. Normative Politics

Bryant distinguishes strategic-cartographical politics from normative politics. Cartographical politics does not tell you *what ought to be done* — that is normative politics, the domain of ethics. It tells you *how gravity is structured* and *where leverage is available*. "Cartographical politics is not a discourse about the norms that ought to direct our activity, nor a discourse about what we ought to do, but is instead a mapping of how power or gravity functions in particular social assemblages or worlds" (p. 283–284).

Marx and Foucault are both cartographical politicians: *Capital* presents a topographical map (how capitalism produces inequality), a genetic map (how it came to be), and a vector map (where it tends) — but does not prescribe. *Discipline and Punish* shows how power is internalized and how architectural and institutional arrangements produce subjectivity — again, a map.
