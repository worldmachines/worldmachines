# Counter-Enlightenment

## Definition

The counter-Enlightenment is [[Isaiah Berlin]]'s term for the intellectual tradition that challenged the core assumptions of mainstream Enlightenment rationalism from within the 18th and 19th centuries. Berlin's use is descriptive and rehabilitative — he argues these thinkers identified real problems that the dominant tradition suppressed, and that they deserve serious philosophical engagement rather than dismissal as irrationalist reaction.

## Core Enlightenment Assumptions Under Attack

The counter-Enlightenment thinkers targeted three interlocking assumptions:

1. **Monism**: there is one correct answer to all genuine questions — moral, political, metaphysical
2. **Universalism**: reason is universal, and therefore a single rational framework can adjudicate all human questions across all cultures and times
3. **Progressivism**: history is a story of rational improvement; obstacles to progress are ignorance or prejudice

## Key Thinkers

- **[[Giambattista Vico]]** — each civilization must be understood on its own terms, through imaginative participation (*fantasia*); there is no view from nowhere; *verum ipsum factum*
- **[[Johann Georg Hamann]]** — language precedes and shapes reason; Enlightenment abstraction distorts lived experience; "all truth is particular, never general"; "passion alone gives to abstractions hands, feet, wings"
- **[[Johann Gottfried Herder]]** — cultures have incommensurable but equally valid ends; coined *Einfühlung* (empathy) as the method for understanding other cultures; each culture has its own *Schwerpunkt*
- **[[Joseph de Maistre]]** — reactionary wing; reason cannot ground society; authority must be absolute because men are naturally evil; "all social order in the end rests upon one man, the executioner"
- **[[Edmund Burke]]** — analogously in the English tradition; the accumulated wisdom of traditions exceeds what any individual rational plan can capture
- **[[Justus Möser]]** — the first historical sociologist; *Zeitstil* and *Volksstil*; local variety as the precondition of freedom
- **The *Sturm und Drang*** — the German literary movement (Schiller, Lenz, Klinger, Goethe) that expressed the counter-Enlightenment spirit in literary form

## Berlin's Ambivalence

Berlin takes these thinkers seriously without endorsing their conclusions. He is most sympathetic to Vico and Herder — whose pluralism anticipates his own [[Value Pluralism]] — and most critical of Maistre and the authoritarian tradition. The counter-Enlightenment insights are valuable; the political consequences they were put to (nationalism, fascism, reaction) are catastrophic.

## Contemporary Relevance

The counter-Enlightenment critique resonates with contemporary concerns about:
- **AI homogenization**: LLMs trained on dominant linguistic patterns suppress minority reasoning styles and epistemologies — a technological enactment of the universalism the counter-Enlightenment opposed (see [[AI Homogenization and the Counter-Enlightenment]])
- **WEIRD bias**: the empirical finding that psychological research over-represents Western, educated, industrialized, rich, democratic populations — a structural analogue to Enlightenment universalism

## Genealogy of the Political Consequences

Counter-Enlightenment insights followed two diverging political paths:

| Path | Thinkers | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Pluralist | Vico, Herder | Cultural pluralism → liberal multiculturalism |
| Authoritarian | Maistre, Bonald | Organic nationalism → fascism and totalitarianism |

The Romantic movement was the hinge: depending on whether the "creative self" was identified with the individual or with the nation/race, it produced anarchic individualism or aggressive collective chauvinism. See [[Romanticism and Political Danger]].
