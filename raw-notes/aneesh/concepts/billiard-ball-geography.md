# Billiard-Ball Geography

[[doreen-massey]]'s image (Chapter 6 of *For Space*, 2005) for the dominant modernist conception of places: pre-formed, internally coherent, bounded entities that exist independently and only later come into "contact" with one another.

> "The modern, territorial conceptualisation of space understands geographical difference as being constituted primarily through isolation and separation . . . First the differences between places exist, and then those different places come into contact . . . It is an essentialist, billiard-ball view of place." (Massey 2005: 66)

## What the image carries

The billiard-ball image bundles several claims:

1. Places have **clean borders** — there is a definite inside and outside.
2. Place-identity is **internally produced** — characteristics belong to the place independent of its relations.
3. Contact is **external collision** — what happens between places does not constitute them.
4. Contact is **occasional** — places spend most of their time being themselves, undisturbed.

## Why Massey rejects it

The empirical historical record refuses the picture. Long before 1492, societies were extensively interconnected:

- Eric Wolf, *Europe and the People Without History* (1982): "Everywhere in this world of 1400, populations existed in interconnections" (p. 71, quoted 67).
- Gupta and Ferguson (1992): identity is produced *through* connection, not through prior isolation.
- Edwin Wilmsen (1989): "the Bushmen" became "Bushmen" through colonialism's discursive and material production of "remoteness," not by their prior isolation.
- Oakes (1993) on China: "Distinctive cultural spaces were maintained . . . through connections rather than disjunctions" (p. 63, quoted 66).

The "pre-modern unity of space and place" that Giddens and others periodise was never empirically real. The billiard-ball image is itself a *modern* projection backward.

## Place as meeting-place

Massey's positive alternative:

> "'Place' in consequence is necessarily meeting place, where the 'difference' of a place must be conceptualised . . . in the ineffable sense of the constant emergence of uniqueness out of (and within) the specific constellations of interrelations within which that place is set." (66)

A place is constituted by what touches it. Its identity is **relationally produced**, not internally generated. See [[places-as-events]] (when written), [[space-as-relational-product]].

## Political stakes

Billiard-ball geography is the philosophical infrastructure for:

- Exclusivist localism and nationalism (the place must defend its bounded identity against the outside).
- The denial of [[coevalness]] (different places at different "stages").
- The naturalisation of borders.
- The fetishisation of authentic "local" identity.

A relational geography of meeting-places blocks all of these.
