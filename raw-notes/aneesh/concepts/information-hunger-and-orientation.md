# Information Hunger and Orientation

## The Thesis

[[Hank Green]]'s claim: "We aren't addicted to content, but are instead starving for information."

The reframe: information hunger is not a metaphor. It is a biological need for *orientation* — an accurate internal map of "where I stand and what to do next." Organisms with accurate mental maps survive; disorientation is aversive.

Social media companies provide just enough signal-hits to keep users scrolling but not enough genuine information to provide orientation. The result: users are lost and hungry, glassy-eyed.

## The Signal-to-Orientation Ladder

```
Signal → Information → Orientation → Biology
```

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| **Signal** | Any pattern standing out from background noise — headline, notification badge, data point |
| **Information** | Signal parsed and interpreted; meaning and relevance attached |
| **Orientation** | Updated internal map; answers "where do I stand, what do I do next?" |
| **Biological need** | Evolutionary pressure toward accurate maps; disorientation = discomfort |

## Historical Parallel: Printing Press

The printing press also created information overload. Early modern Europe had too much data, no guides to quality. [[Giordano Bruno]] is the cautionary example: mystical click-bait + Copernican theory + no data = infinite universe (no empirical basis). Culture eventually adapted through place-making and comparison — which triggered the scientific revolution.

See: [[Printing Press and Information Overload]]

## Waldenponding as False Solution

"Waldenponding" (deliberate disconnection from the social internet) is the wrong response — analogous to giving up on books because there were too many. The internet does provide genuine orientation opportunities; the failure is in the *posture* of engagement.

See: [[Waldenponding]]

## The Corrective: Socratic Posture

Engaging with curiosity — an "information-shaped content diet" — satisfies the orientation need. Socratic inquiry transforms the dominant competitive emotion of online discourse into collaborative place-making. "Each of us and our thoughts are a place in the world."
