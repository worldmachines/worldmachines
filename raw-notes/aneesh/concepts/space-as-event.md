# Space as Event

A phrase [[doreen-massey]] borrows from [[ernesto-laclau]]'s *New Reflections on the Revolution of Our Time* (1990: 84) and turns against Laclau's own framework. Where Laclau uses "space" to denote causal closure (see [[coupure-and-causal-closure]]), he also concedes — perhaps inadvertently — that "**space itself becomes an event**" once we admit that all closure is ultimately fragile.

Massey takes the concession seriously. If space is event, then:

1. Space is something that **happens** — it is not a backdrop on which events occur; the spatial moment is itself an event in which trajectories meet.
2. Space is **eventful**: it is the site of dislocation, of the genuinely new, of the unrepresentable.
3. Space "**cannot be tamed**" (her phrase in Chapter 9) — it resists the standard moves of containment (representation, synchronic closure, queue-into-a-developmental-line, etc.).

This is the bridge from Part Two's critique to Part Four's positive theory of place (see [[places-as-events]] when written).

## Why "event" and not just "process"

"Process" suggests continuity, smooth becoming. "Event" foregrounds **happening** — discontinuity, surprise, the encounter that could not have been predicted from the trajectories alone. Massey aligns with the [[positive-versus-negative-multiplicity]] tradition (Spinoza–Bergson–Deleuze) for which events are the eruptions of the new, not the disruptions of a prior coherence.

## Cognates

- Bryant's machinic ontology: machines have **operations** (events) that bring new entities into being from out of pre-existing materials. The world is eventful at every level.
- Whitehead's "actual occasions" as the basic units of process metaphysics. Massey alludes to Whitehead via Prigogine (32).
- Latour's enactment ontology: things are events of being-enacted.
