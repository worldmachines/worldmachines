# Corporeal and Incorporeal Machines

Within [[levi-bryant]]'s [[onto-cartography]], all entities are machines, but machines divide into two fundamental types by their mode of operation and the nature of their inputs and outputs.

## Corporeal Machines

Corporeal machines are physical, material entities whose operations involve the transformation of material flows. Examples: rocks, organisms, factories, weather systems, chemical reactions. A tree performing photosynthesis is a corporeal machine — it takes in water, carbon dioxide, and sunlight as material flows and produces oxygen and glucose as outputs.

Corporeal machines are characterized by trans-corporeality: they are constituted through material flows from other machines, and their operations modify those flows while being modified in return (see [[structural-openness-operational-closure]]).

## Incorporeal Machines

Incorporeal machines are semiotic, symbolic, or linguistic entities whose primary mode of operation is not physical transformation but *meaning-production, instruction, or organization*. Examples: recipes, mathematical theorems, laws, languages, musical scores, religions, ideologies.

Bryant's key example: the Pythagorean theorem is an absolutely rigid incorporeal machine — it performs the same operations on any right triangle regardless of material, color, size, or substance (p. 51). It is impermeable to the material powers of wood, steel, string, or pencil. A recipe is an incorporeal machine that operates on a cook, leading the cook in turn to operate on utensils and ingredients (p. 39).

Incorporeal machines approach **absolute rigidity** on the spectrum of [[rigid-plastic-machines]]: they tend not to be modified by the material inputs that pass through them. The Pythagorean theorem does not change when applied to a steel triangle rather than a wooden one.

## The Key Interaction: Incorporeal Machines as Media

One of Bryant's central points is that incorporeal machines function as *media* for corporeal machines. An incorporeal machine provides the structured flow upon which a corporeal machine performs operations. The form (a legal document, an insurance form, a mathematical equation) is an incorporeal machine that mediates between humans and institutions, distilling human communication to pre-defined parameters (p. 55).

This means that symbolic and material reality are not separate domains but nested levels of machine-coupling. Ideologies, laws, and mathematical structures are machines that organize material-corporeal machines by specifying the inputs those corporeal machines receive.

## Political Implications

The distinction matters for political analysis. Changing a corporeal machine (a factory, a body, an ecosystem) requires different interventions than changing an incorporeal machine (a law, a cultural norm, a mathematical model). Semiotic politics — attempting to change institutions through communicative persuasion — operates on the incorporeal level. Thermodynamic politics targets the material-energetic flows on which corporeal machines depend (see [[thermodynamic-politics]]).

## Note on Sources

Bryant does not use the term "corporeal/incorporeal" in exactly this binary form in Ch 2–3; the distinction is implicit in his examples. He uses "incorporeal machines" explicitly when discussing mathematical entities, laws, and ideologies approaching absolute rigidity (p. 51). The Deleuze/Guattari influence is present: D&G distinguish between "machinic assemblages of bodies" and "collective assemblages of enunciation" — a parallel to the corporeal/incorporeal split.
