# Four Questions of Uncertainty

## Origin

Developed by [[Vaughn Tan]] over 2023–2024 in his *Not Knowing* series. Applied by Cedric Chin (Commoncog) to AI sensemaking in [[How to Make Sense of AI]] (2026).

## The Four Questions

When evaluating any piece of information — especially about a new technology or disruptive change — ask:

1. **What new outcomes are suggested here?**
   What has become possible that was not possible before? (Not just direct effects; also second-order changes in society/industry that can be exploited.)

2. **What actions can I take in response?**
   What does this information open up as a possible action for me, my company, or my situation? (The test of *affordance*.)

3. **What are the relative values of these outcomes to me?**
   Given who I am, what I do, what I value, and what my goals are — how much do these outcomes matter? (Personalizing the analysis to your context.)

4. **What are the causal relationships?**
   What mechanisms produce these outcomes? What levers exist? Which of those levers can I pull without others noticing?

## Why These Four

The questions operationalize [[Outcome Orientation]] and constitute a minimal sufficient framework for converting information consumption into actionable intelligence:

- Question 1: situates you in the landscape of possibility
- Question 2: converts possibility into agency
- Question 3: filters noise from signal for *your* context
- Question 4: gives you the causal model that allows strategic action rather than reactive adaptation

## Relationship to the Data-Frame Theory

The four questions can be understood as a **structured elaboration protocol** within the [[Data-Frame Theory of Sensemaking]]:
- The frame is: "How does this development affect my outcomes?"
- Questions 1–4 define the specific data elements (anchors) the frame requires
- The questions prevent the common failure of consuming information without building functional frames

## Applied Example (Chin, 2026)

A field report about Microsoft's autonomous coding experiments answers:
1. **Outcome**: Complex software producible without human-written code, given ~6 months of harness engineering
2. **Actions**: Experiment with agent harness construction; build boring tiny tools; find practitioners and develop relationships
3. **Values**: High-value for software companies; variable for others based on team size and software complexity
4. **Causal mechanism**: Best practices (TDD, CI/CD, high observability) + harness engineering = AI coding effectiveness

## Limitation

The framework presupposes that you have access to high-quality field reports. In domains without clear field reports (e.g., geopolitics, slow-moving institutional change), the framework needs modification — you may need to rely on analysis or inference rather than reports of use.
