# Scalability and Social Life

## The Core Asymmetry

Digital, solitude-inducing products are scalable: a single piece of content (a Netflix show, a TikTok video, an AI chatbot) can serve millions or billions of users without proportional increase in cost. The marginal cost of one more user is near zero.

Social experiences are not scalable: a dinner party, a live concert, a barbershop visit, a yoga class all require roughly the same amount of human time per participant as they always have. There is no technological lever that reduces the labor content of genuine togetherness without eliminating what makes it valuable.

## Economic Consequences

This asymmetry has predictable consequences for capital allocation:

- **Investor incentives**: venture capital flows toward scalable products with exponential return profiles. Social experiences — cafes, theaters, community centers — don't attract this capital because their margins are thin and their growth is arithmetic.
- **Marketing budgets**: a Marvel movie can justify a $100M marketing budget because it serves a global audience. A local restaurant cannot.
- **Survival rate**: anti-social businesses survive and grow; pro-social businesses operate at the margin.

## The Efficiency Trap

When social businesses *do* achieve efficiency gains, they typically do so by reducing their social character:
- Fast-casual restaurants replace servers with kiosks, uncomfortable seating that discourages lingering
- Social media platforms replace peer-to-peer connections with algorithmically curated professional content
- Theaters and concert halls cut costs by reducing performance frequency or staff

This creates a systematic pressure: the economically surviving version of a "social" business is progressively less social than its predecessor.

## Facebook as Case Study

Facebook's evolution is a clean illustration. In its early phase, value was created by users' social connections — but this was labor-intensive and didn't scale efficiently. To achieve productivity gains, Facebook shifted from social graph content to algorithmically selected content from professional creators globally. It became more productive by becoming more anti-social. See [[Anti-Social Business Model]].

## Connection to Baumol

The scalability asymmetry is what makes [[Baumol's Cost Disease]] bite hardest in the social domain. Not only do social experiences become relatively more expensive — they also receive no capital investment, face competition from cheap digital alternatives, and employ the most precarious workers (service labor). See [[Baumol Cost Disease and Toxic Solitude]].
