# Content and Expression: The Two Planes (Bryant / Deleuze-Guattari)

## Origin

The content/expression distinction is Deleuze and Guattari's radical reworking of Louis Hjelmslev's linguistics (*Prolegomena to a Theory of Language*, 1969). Originally a distinction within language (expression = signifier-side; content = signified-side), Deleuze and Guattari liberate it from the semiotic domain entirely, extending it to all machines: natural, social, technical, biological.

First developed in *Kafka: Towards a Minor Literature* (1986) and systematized in *A Thousand Plateaus* (1987), the distinction is adopted by Bryant in *Onto-Cartography* Ch. 5 as a key structural feature of many — though not all — worlds.

## The Two Planes

### Plane of Content
The plane of corporeal machines: "actions and passions, an intermingling of bodies reacting to one another" (Deleuze and Guattari 1987: 88). Content encompasses everything material — physical interactions, biological bodies, the smith's muscles, the factory floor, the roads, the prison architecture, the bacteria, the weather.

Content has both a **form** (the manner in which machines are organized or structured) and a **substance** (the specific materials or bodies that are organized). Example: the prison (form of content) and the prisoners (substance of content).

### Plane of Expression
The plane of incorporeal machines: "acts and statements, incorporeal transformations attributed to bodies" (ibid.: 88). Expression encompasses semiotic machines, norms, laws, classifications, signs, and the transformations they effect on bodies — not by changing their material constitution but by changing how other machines *relate* to them.

Expression also has **form** (grammar, operational structure of the semiotic system) and **substance** (the abstract objects and categories produced — "delinquency," "citizenship," "racial category").

Deleuze's example from *Foucault* (1988: 47), cited by Bryant: "The content has both a form and a substance: for example, the form is prison and the substance is those who are locked up, the prisoners... The expression also has a form and a substance: for example the form is penal law and the substance is 'delinquency' in so far as it is the object of statements."

## The Independence of the Two Planes

Crucially, neither plane determines or overcodes the other:

- Content is not "infrastructure" that expression passively reflects (contra vulgar Marxism).
- Expression is not the signifier that produces content as its effect (contra Lacan's "the universe is the flower of rhetoric").
- Each plane has its own organization, objects, and processual logic.

Evidence of independence: the prison can be rebuilt in entirely different materials while penal law remains unchanged (content changes, expression stays); penal law can be rewritten while the architecture and population of the prison stay (expression changes, content stays).

## Incorporeal Transformations

The signature concept of the expressive plane. An incorporeal transformation changes how other machines relate to a corporeal machine without changing its material constitution. Bryant's examples:

- Marriage: "Nothing changes here materially in their bodies, in their corporeal being, yet how they relate to one another, how others relate to them, their legal status, all change significantly." (p. 131)
- "I love you": materially unchanged, yet relational position shifts dramatically.
- Racial categorization, citizenship grants, judicial verdicts, military declarations — all incorporeal transformations.
- Lacan's two doors (Hommes / Dames): the signs change which bodies may pass through which door without changing the material doors themselves.

Key formulation: "*Incorporeal transformations transform not the thing itself, but rather how other machines relate to the machine or thing.*" (Bryant 2014: 129)

## Synchronic and Diachronic Dimensions

Both planes have a **synchronic dimension** (how machines on each plane are organized at a given moment) and a **diachronic dimension** (how machines on each plane produce and assemble other machines over time — "double articulation").

The diachronic dimension is the *historical* face of the planes. Bryant adapts Deleuze and Guattari's concept of **double articulation** (from *A Thousand Plateaus*: 40–1): a first articulation selects matters to become formed substances; a second articulation organizes those substances into stable functional structures. Example: a river selects sand and pebbles (first articulation) → their accumulation produces sedimentary rock (second articulation).

Applied to social machines: an educational-machine uses a legal/normative machine to select which children enter (first articulation) → the curriculum, physical arrangements, and expressive machines then form those children cognitively and corporeally (second articulation).

## Worlds Without Expression

Most worlds contain only content. On Neptune there is nothing but corporeal machines — material bodies affecting each other. Expression is not a universal feature of worlds; it is an emergent property of worlds that contain sufficiently complex semiotic machines. This makes the plane of expression the exception rather than the rule in Bryant's pluriverse, though it is the plane that most directly concerns social and political theory.

Expression may extend beyond human worlds — cetaceans, cephalopods, bee-dance, wolf packs — but it is not coextensive with the world as such.

## Bryant's Political Extension

Bryant uses the content/expression framework to diagnose a systematic bias in continental social theory: since the Frankfurt School and through the post-structuralists, theory has focused overwhelmingly on the plane of expression (ideology, discourse, signs, norms) at the expense of the plane of content. Bryant argues this produces blind spots: material machines — roads, resources, infrastructure, climate — play an equally significant role in social form.

World War II/women's liberation case: corporeal transformations (women in factories) preceded expressive transformations (gender norm change) by a generation, demonstrating that the planes develop independently and that content can outpace expression.

## Critique of Luhmann

Bryant uses content/expression to criticize Luhmann's thesis that societies are composed *solely* of communications. Communications are expressive machines — incorporeal transformations. But roads, disease vectors, resource distributions, and physical infrastructure are content machines that shape social form just as powerfully. See [[niklas-luhmann]].
