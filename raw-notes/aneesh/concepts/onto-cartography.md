# Onto-Cartography (Bryant)

## Etymology and Definition

"Onto" from Greek *onto*, meaning "thing" or "being"; "cartography" meaning "map." Bryant's coinage: onto-cartography is "a map of relations between machines that analyzes how these assemblages organize the movement, development, and becoming other machines in a world" (Bryant 2014, p. 7).

In its initial formulation (Ch. 1): "onto-cartography is the investigation of structural couplings between machines and how they modify the becomings, activities, movements, and ways in which the coupled machines relate to the world about them. It is a mapping (cartography) of these couplings between machines (onta) and their vectors of becoming, movement, and activity." (Bryant 2014, p. 35)

## The Central Thesis

Social relations and ecologies take the form they take due to the *gravity* — Bryant's term for "power" — that physical and discursive machines exercise on the elements that inhabit assemblages, worlds, or ecologies. Form is not imposed from above by human intention or ideology alone; it emerges from the distributed press of machine-ecologies on one another.

## What Onto-Cartography Does

1. **Analyzes how worlds are organized** — why power functions as it does, why forms of social organization persist and resist change, why societies don't simply disintegrate through entropy.
2. **Provides strategies for changing oppressive social systems** — by understanding what machines (physical and discursive) maintain oppressive configurations, we can identify which machines to deconstruct and which new machines to build.
3. **Integrates discursive and material agency** — it does not deny that signifiers, ideologies, and cultural practices structure reality; it refuses to let these exhaust the account.

## Distinction from Geographic Cartography

Geographical cartography maps space; onto-cartography maps relations or interactions between machines and how these structure the movements and becomings of one another. But Bryant thinks geography is "the queen of the social sciences" precisely because it resists dematerializing the world — recognizing that social relations are always embedded in particular spaces and places, that communication takes time and requires media to travel. "Social and political philosophy needs to become more geographical." (Bryant 2014, p. 7)

## Relation to Critical Theory

Onto-cartography is explicitly non-reductive with respect to existing critical frameworks. Drawing on Whitehead's observation that "the chief error in philosophy is overstatement" (1978: 7), Bryant argues discursive theories are overstated rather than false. The framework aims to "port" existing political preoccupations — Marxist critiques, feminist critiques, ecological critiques, post-colonial critiques — into a more capacious ontological framework, revealing their blind spots without discarding their insights (Bryant 2014, p. 8).

## The Book's Scope

The concept is developed across eight chapters. Key extensions beyond Intro/Ch. 1:
- Ch. 2: The full ontology of machines (operations, flows, outputs).
- Ch. 3: Alien phenomenology — how machines observe other machines.
- Ch. 4: Machines as assemblages; the problem of entropy.
- Ch. 5: Worlds as ecologies of machines; content and expression (Deleuze/Guattari).
- Ch. 6: Topological conception of space and time.
- Ch. 7: Gravity as power; dark/bright/dim/rogue/satellite/black-hole objects.
- Ch. 8: Geophilosophy — cartography, deconstruction, terraformation.

## Relation to This Wiki's Concepts

Onto-cartography as a practice resonates with the wiki's spatial-epistemology thread. Bryant's claim that "social and political philosophy needs to become more geographical" is structurally parallel to [[spatial-epistemology-the-master-thread]]'s claim that knowledge is fundamentally spatial. But Bryant is making an *ontological* claim about where power lives, not primarily an epistemological claim about how we know.

The mapping metaphor also connects to [[technology-as-place-maker]]: onto-cartography is itself a kind of place-making act — it converts the undifferentiated assemblage of the social world into a navigable map.
