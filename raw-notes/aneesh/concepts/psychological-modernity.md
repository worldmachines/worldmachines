# Psychological Modernity

**Originator**: [[venkatesh-rao]] (The Modernity Machine, Jan 2025)

## Definition

**Psychological modernity** is the emergence of a recognizably modern cognitive mode — a way of thinking, organizing politically, and relating to other minds — that Rao argues appeared globally and in parallel between 1200 and 1600, preceding the typically cited markers of modernity (Renaissance, Reformation, Scientific Revolution, Enlightenment).

The case is analogous to the physical anthropologists' case for *anatomical* modernity: "Sometime between 1200 and 1600, psychologically modern humans emerged (not counting a possible earlier leap to unicameral minds that Julian Jaynes argued for)."

## The Santa Claus Test

An informal heuristic: Can you talk to people from a given era as rough *cognitive peers*, despite all differences in knowledge, technology, philosophy, and aesthetics? Or would you have to humor them like children?

Applied examples:
- **Venice, late 13th century**: Passes. "I feel like I could comfortably talk to Venetians."
- **Constantinople, same era**: Fails. "I would have trouble talking to Byzantines in any adult way."
- **Muhammad bin Tughlaq** (Delhi Sultanate, 1325-1351): Passes — "arguably the first psychologically modern ruler of India," despite (or through) his "madness."

Venice outgrew Constantinople not just militarily, economically, or politically — it *outgrew it psychologically*, "in the sense that a child might outgrow a parent trapped in the mindset of their youth."

## The Global Parallel Emergence

Psychological modernity appeared independently at multiple loci, driven by a few shared causes:
- The Black Death and Mongol expansion
- The reframing of political relationships around negotiated contractual logic
- The beginnings of secular pluralism in the wake of religious encounter and reform
- The rise of free cities and accountability mechanisms beyond popular insurrection

This means the "specifically European experience of modernization" — comprising the Renaissance and Enlightenment — is "the making of the specifically *European* mind, which did not in fact go global at all, and has remained restricted to the West."

## Distinguishing Westernization from Modernization

A corollary: non-Western populations became technologically modern through science and technology acting on a landscape of *regional early modernities*. Philosophical/cultural "Westernization" has been largely cosmetic. Science and technology approach 100% penetration.

People from non-Western cultures who are technologically modern are "culturally distinguishable modernists" rather than Westernized traditionalists.

## The Traditionalist Response

Traditionalists worldwide want to reverse the process — to recover versions of themselves who are "pre-modern, and largely decoupled from the rest of the planet, genetically and memetically." Almost all traditionalists want to rewind to roughly 1100 — when, by their reckoning, the global cultural entanglement and secularization began. Islamists rewind to the First Caliphate; American reactionaries to a pre-1960s America; Hindu nationalists to a pre-Islamic India.

> "It is no accident that all traditionalists seem to want to rewind to roughly 1100 or so."

## Implications for Intellectual History

If psychological modernity preceded the philosophical and scientific revolutions, then those revolutions should be understood as "decoupled leaps that irrupted into *already modern* societies and minds worldwide, rather than acting as modernizing forces." This flips the usual causal story: the Enlightenment did not make people modern; modern people produced the Enlightenment.
