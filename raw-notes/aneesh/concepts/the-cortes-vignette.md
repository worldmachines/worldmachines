# The Cortés Vignette (Tenochtitlán Encounter)

Massey opens *For Space* (2005) with the November 1519 encounter between Hernán Cortés's army and Moctezuma's Aztec city of Tenochtitlán. The vignette runs for four pages (pp. 2–6) and does multiple things simultaneously: it establishes the book's political stakes, exemplifies the dominant spatial imagination, and offers the first counter-resource.

## What the Vignette Does Rhetorically

Massey narrates the encounter *from both sides*. This is itself a formal enactment of her thesis: there are two trajectories approaching the same point in time-space. The story is not Cortés's story with the Aztecs as background; it is a meeting of two coexisting histories.

The conventional retelling — "Cortes voyaged across space, found Tenochtitlan, and took it" — is her target. She identifies it as the "voyages of discovery" imaginary:

> "Space, in this way of telling things, is an expanse we travel across... it makes space seem like a surface; continuous and given. It differentiates: Hernan, active, a maker of history, journeys across this surface and finds Tenochtitlan upon it." (Massey 2005: 4)

The Aztecs, on this reading, are simply "on" the surface, awaiting Cortés's arrival, "without their own trajectories." The spatial imaginary of surface/discovery **dehistoricises and immobilises** non-Western actors.

## The Aztec Counter-Resource

Massey draws on Townsend (1992) and Soustelle (1956) to show that Aztec cosmological space-time was fundamentally different: "concrete complexes of space and time, heterogeneous and singular sites and events... 'place-moments' [*lugares momentos*]" (Massey 2005: 6). Direction, time, cosmological cycle, and specific place were fused — not abstractions but singular configurations.

The *Codex Xolotl*: a hybrid manuscript representing events through itineraries of footsteps and dotted lines between places. "The manuscript is read by locating the origin of the footprints and deciphering the place signs as they occur on these itineraries." (Massey 2005: 6, citing Harley 1990) — these are representations of time and space together, not of abstract space alone.

Massey does not romanticise the Aztec system. She reads it as pointing toward a *different spatial imagination* — space as trajectories-in-relation, not surface-awaiting-discovery — that is a resource for her own argument.

## The Rhetorical Stakes

By placing the Aztec cosmology alongside the Western surface imaginary on the first pages of the book, Massey performs two moves:

1. **Defamiliarises** the "obvious" Western conception of space by showing it was one imaginary among (at least) two.
2. **Grounds** her theoretical propositions in a politically consequential historical encounter: the stakes are not just philosophical but colonial/postcolonial.

The vignette sets up the book's core political claim: the imagination of space as a surface was not innocent — it was one of the conceptual weapons of conquest.

## What It Sets Up

The Cortés vignette anchors all three of Massey's propositions:
- **Proposition 1** (space as relational product): the encounter is a meeting of trajectories, not a discovery on a surface.
- **Proposition 2** (coexisting heterogeneity): Aztec and Spanish histories coexisted; neither was reducible to the other.
- **Proposition 3** (space always under construction): Moctezuma's dilemma was the challenge of responding to an open, unforeclosed future.
