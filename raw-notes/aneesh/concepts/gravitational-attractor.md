# Gravitational Attractor

## Definition

A **gravitational attractor** is a machine that functions as a [[bryant-gravity-as-power|bright object]] in a given assemblage — one whose gravitational field substantially organizes the movements, local manifestations, and becomings of the other machines in that assemblage. The term synthesizes Bryant's "bright object" concept with the dynamical-systems notion of an attractor: a region of state-space toward which trajectories converge.

Bright objects "capture other machines in their orbit" (Bryant 2014, p. 202). Machines caught in a bright object's orbit become *satellites*: their field of available choices is pre-delineated by the bright object, not by some neutral space of possibility. The question the satellite can ask is not "what do I want to do?" but "which of the options this bright object makes available do I choose?"

## The Asymmetry of Capture

The defining feature of a gravitational attractor is **asymmetry**: it is far more dependent on its satellites than they are on it (satellites need the attractor for access to necessities), yet its satellites are far more dependent on it than it is on them (the bright object can generally continue in a modified form without any particular satellite). This asymmetry is precisely what makes the relation politically significant.

As Bryant writes of oil: "There is nothing on the planet that is not somehow caught in its gravitational pull and transformed into a satellite by it... In one way or another, all life on the planet is currently structured by paths created by oil." (p. 204)

## Major Examples from Bryant

**The Sun** (pp. 202–203): The paradigm bright object. Not bright because it illuminates the sky, but because it captures all planets in their orbits, drives all atmospheric and biological processes, and is the ultimate source of the energy stored in fossil fuels. "Within the frame of reference of the Earth, all politics is ultimately, in one way or another, solar politics." (p. 202)

**Oil** (p. 204): "Perhaps the brightest object of all today." Oil organizes transportation infrastructure, agricultural systems, economic fluctuation, geopolitical conflict, climate change, and the physical composition of everyday objects (plastics). Nations go to war over it; climate is transformed by it; suburban life is structured by the paths it creates.

**Rice** (p. 203): For agrarian communities organized around rice cultivation, the crop organizes labor rhythms, body postures, tool fabrication, social relations, feast and famine cycles. Rice-planting in water creates disease vectors requiring further adaptation. The seasonal rhythm of the crop organizes the space-time of entire cultures.

**The coal company** (pp. 203–204): In company towns, "the company" owned the mine, the general store, the homes, and everything else. Company scrip — currency redeemable only at company stores — became the system's gravitational instrument, making workers entirely dependent for food, clothing, shelter, and energy. A perfect closed attractor.

**Parents** (p. 203): In early childhood, parents function as bright objects. They don't determine what the child becomes, but they "substantially organize the paths along which the child moves and develops." The child navigates "warps and depressions in the world" created by parental desires, regrets, values, and neuroses.

## Extension: Contemporary Attractors

Bryant's framework invites extension to contemporary systems:

**Screens and attention economies**: Digital platforms are gravitational attractors in Bryant's sense — they organize the field of attention, information access, social connection, and commercial activity. The question of what you can find, who you can contact, what opinions you can encounter is substantially pre-delineated by the platform's algorithmic architecture. See [[protocol-gods-and-platform-demons]].

**Highways**: Highway systems determine where cities grow, where commerce flows, what suburban patterns develop. Cities built around highways cannot easily function without them; the highway is a bright object that has drawn an entire urban ecology into its orbit.

**Financial clearing infrastructure**: The payment systems, credit markets, and clearing networks that organize the flow of capital are gravitational attractors for economic activity. Entities unable to access these systems (the unbanked, nations under financial embargo) experience their gravitational force most acutely.

**Food supply chains**: Industrial food distribution networks have made most of the world's population into satellites dependent on fossil-fuel-powered supply chains for caloric survival. Like the company-scrip system at global scale.

## The Quilting Point

Bryant draws on Lacan's concept of the **quilting point** (*point de capiton*) to capture the assembling function of bright objects (p. 195). A quilting point is a signifier that stitches other signifiers together into a stable field of meaning. Bryant extends this: any machine can function as a quilting point that stitches other machines together into a stable assemblage. The Brazil nut tree quilts together bees, orchids, agouti, indigenous peoples, export economies, and territorial conflicts into a single assemblage. The attractor does not merely influence — it *constitutes* the assemblage by providing the node around which other machines assemble.

## Relation to Tuan's Place

There is a structural resonance between Bryant's gravitational attractor and Yi-Fu Tuan's account of **place** in [[space-and-place-philosophy]]. For Tuan, a place is a node of meaning and orientation that organizes spatial experience — it converts undifferentiated space into a field with a center. Bright objects do something structurally analogous at the ontological level: they convert undifferentiated assemblages into fields with a gravitational center.

The difference: Tuan's places are primarily constituted by human meaning and experience; Bryant's attractors are constituted by material gravitational force, regardless of human experience. But the structural parallel suggests that **visible, meaningfully constituted places ARE gravitational attractors** in Bryant's sense — and that Bryant's framework provides the ontological grounding for Tuan's phenomenological observation. ## Political Analysis

Onto-cartographical political analysis begins by identifying the bright objects in an assemblage — "to locate the bright objects that structure gravity in a particular assemblage" (p. 210). From there, the analysis proceeds to:
1. How bright objects influence the movements and becomings of satellites
2. What the symmetrical and asymmetrical flows between satellites and bright objects are
3. Which machines function as dim objects (neglected actors)
4. What rogue objects might reconfigure the assemblage

The political question — "how do we produce more satisfying and just assemblages?" (p. 197) — is answered by understanding which bright objects to dismantle and which alternative attractors to build.
