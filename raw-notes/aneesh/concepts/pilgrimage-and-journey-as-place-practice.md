# Pilgrimage and Journey as Place Practice

**Primary source**: Yi-Fu Tuan, *Space and Place* (1977/2002), Chapters 7 and 9, pp. 85–135
**See also**: [[tuan-2002-space-and-place-ch7-9]], [[time-in-experiential-space]], [[mythical-space]], [[dreamtime-songlines-as-place-making]]

---

## Movement as the Genesis of Place

Tuan's foundational formula — "if we think of space as that which allows movement, then place is pause" — establishes that movement and place are co-constitutive. The journey is not merely travel through pre-existing places; it is the activity that creates the space-time structure within which places emerge.

This is most visible in the cases of directed, purposive movement: the journey to a sacred site (pilgrimage proper), the cosmogonic journey of founding ancestors, the explorer's penetration into unknown territory, and the daily commute. All share a temporal structure that simultaneously organizes space.

---

## Cosmogonic Journey: The Walbiri Dreamtime Tracks

The most striking case Tuan presents is the Walbiri (and broader Australian aboriginal) tradition. Topographical features are not merely landmarks; they are "a record of 'who were here, and did what'" and simultaneously "a record of 'who are here now'" (p. 132). The landscape is a frozen journey — the paths of founding ancestors are inscribed in the terrain itself.

> "By conceiving of the heroes as wanderers and by periodically reenacting the mythic journeys, Australian aborigines impart a directional time sense to their land." (p. 132)

The reenactment of these journeys (the Dreamtime tracks, Fig. 16) is not a commemoration of past events; it is the continued production of the place-structure of the landscape. The journey makes place not once but repeatedly. Remove the reenactment and the landscape reverts from cosmogonic place to undifferentiated terrain.

See [[dreamtime-songlines-as-place-making]] for the fuller treatment.

## Directed Journey and Historical Space

Tuan's analysis of the everyday journey (Chapter 9) shows that even mundane movement creates historical time and oriented space:

- The morning journey to work is **forward movement** — the office lies ahead in space and time
- The evening return is backward in space (retracing) but forward in personal time — home is familiarity, origin, past
- European emigration to the New World is spatial movement (across the Atlantic) that is simultaneously temporal projection (into the future — the New World as beckon)
- Exploration of Africa or Asia is spatial penetration that is experienced as temporal regression (into ancient time)

> "The intention to go to a place creates historical time: the place is a goal in the future." (p. 130)

The destination creates both the temporal horizon (future) and the spatial vector (direction). Goal and place are the same thing seen from different angles of the same experience.

## Pilgrimage as Sacred Journey

The pilgrimage is the journey form most explicitly structured by sacred-profane spatial categories. The pilgrim moves from profane home territory through an increasingly charged spatial field toward a sacred center — a site of theophany, ancestral presence, or cosmic axis. The journey is not incidental to the destination; it is constitutive of the arrival's meaning.

Tuan does not develop pilgrimage at length in Chapters 7–9, but the structural logic is embedded in his discussion of mythical space. In cultures with oriented cosmological space, movement toward the cosmic center (the kiva, the sacred mountain, the holy city) is a movement toward intensified place — toward the site where space is maximally charged and maximally real.

Jerusalem as "the navel of Christ and of the world" in medieval *orbis terrarum* maps (p. 96) is the paradigm: all directional movement in Christian sacred geography is organized in relation to this center. The pilgrimage to Jerusalem is a cosmogonic journey — a reenactment of the movement toward the axis mundi.

## The Journey as Place-Making Technology

What unites these cases — Walbiri Dreamtime tracks, European pilgrimage, Hopi ceremonial movement, daily commute — is that **directed movement through space produces place**. The journey is not an episodic event that happens between places; it is the process by which space acquires the directional structure, the temporal depth, and the felt significance that makes it place.

The opposite of journey-as-place-making is the undirected drift: Tuan's "presentic" unoriented space (citing Erwin Straus) induced by dance and music, where each step is not a step toward something but an entry into open, undifferentiated space (p. 129). Dance de-places; pilgrimage places.
