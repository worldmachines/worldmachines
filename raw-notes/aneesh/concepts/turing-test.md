# Turing Test

## Original Purpose

Proposed by [[Alan Turing]] in "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" (1950). Turing reframed the philosophically intractable question "Can machines think?" into a behavioral test: can a machine produce responses indistinguishable from a human in text-based conversation?

## Sathe's Reframing

In [[Chatbots, Bats & Broken Oracles]], Sathe uses the Turing Test as an illustration of [[Problems vs. Questions|problem vs. question reduction]]:

- **Original question**: "What is it to think?"
- **Turing's conversion**: "How do you detect thought?" (a problem)
- **The consequence**: LLMs pass the test; we remain unconvinced about machine thinking.

The failure of the test to settle the question is evidence that the conversion *lost something essential*. The original question was not dissolved — it was only operationalized. The philosophical question "What is thinking?" remains fully open.

> "He turned the question of what is it to think into the problem of how do you detect thought."

## Why LLMs Pass But Don't Convince

LLMs passing the Turing Test means:
- They produce human-indistinguishable conversational outputs. ✓
- They therefore think (by the problem's definition). ✓

But we remain unconvinced because:
- The test was designed to answer a question by converting it to a problem.
- Passing the problem does not answer the original question.
- We sense that the operationalization missed something — phenomenal consciousness? First-person experience? Genuine intentionality?

This gap is precisely what [[Thomas Nagel]]'s bat argument explores: the subjective, first-person character of experience that behavioral tests cannot capture.
