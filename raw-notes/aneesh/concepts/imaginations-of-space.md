# Imaginations of Space

Massey's diagnostic frame in *For Space* (2005): the claim that **implicit assumptions about space are politically consequential**. Most actors who operate with bad spatial imaginaries do so without consciously thinking about space at all. The damage is done below the level of explicit argument.

> "One of the recurring motifs in what follows is just how little, actually, space is thought about explicitly. None the less, the persistent associations leave a residue of effects." (Massey 2005: 7)

## The Three Dominant (Failed) Imaginations

### 1. Space as Surface

The "voyages of discovery" imaginary: space is a flat expanse across which agents move and on which things are found. Cortes voyages; Tenochtitlán is found. This immobilises those who are "there" — they await discovery, lacking their own trajectories.

> "It differentiates: Hernan, active, a maker of history, journeys across this surface and finds Tenochtitlan upon it... by this means they are deprived of histories." (Massey 2005: 4)

This is the "surface" spatial imagination: continuous, given, passive. It underlies colonialism, the development-as-geography fantasy, and much everyday cartography.

### 2. Space Reduced to Time

The neoliberal globalisation rhetoric converts geography into history: places that are spatially different are rendered as temporally earlier. Mozambique is not a contemporary other but a laggard. This is a sleight-of-hand with immediate political consequences: if there is only one direction, resistance is futile.

> "The cosmology of 'only one narrative' obliterates the multiplicities, the contemporaneous heterogeneities of space. It reduces simultaneous coexistence to place in the historical queue." (Massey 2005: 5)

### 3. Place as Closed Authenticity

The nationalist-communitarian place imagination: place is a bounded, coherent, authentic enclosure defending itself against the spatial exterior. This underwrites exclusivist localisms, ethnic nationalism, and territorial parochialism. But it also shapes more progressive place-defense when conceptualised in the same closed terms.

## Why "Imagination" and Not "Ideology"

Massey's term of art is "imagination" rather than "ideology" — the spatial conceits are not necessarily held consciously or deployed cynically. They are embedded in practices, narratives, and forms of reasoning that reproduce themselves unreflectively. The political question is not to expose a lie but to displace a habit.

This is why the alternative requires philosophical as well as political work: you cannot simply argue people out of an unthought cosmology. You have to offer a different imaginary that becomes habitual in its turn.

## The Alternative Constellation

What Massey proposes is a re-embedding of space in a different set of concepts:

| Old constellation | New constellation |
|---|---|
| Stasis | Process / becoming |
| Closure | Openness |
| Representation | Materiality / practice |
| Fixed | Relational |
| Dead | Lively |

> "What is needed, I think, is to uproot 'space' from that constellation of concepts in which it has so unquestioningly so often been embedded (stasis; closure; representation) and to settle it among another set of ideas (heterogeneity; relationality; coevalness... liveliness indeed) where it releases a more challenging political landscape." (Massey 2005: 13)
