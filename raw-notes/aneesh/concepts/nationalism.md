# Nationalism

## Berlin's Account of the Concept's Origins

[[Isaiah Berlin]] treats nationalism not as a primordial human drive but as a historically specific intellectual product — one that grew directly out of the [[Counter-Enlightenment]] tradition. Understanding nationalism requires understanding the ideas it distorted.

## The Herderian Root

[[Johann Gottfried Herder]] was, in Berlin's phrase, "the greatest inspirer of cultural nationalism among the nationalities oppressed by the Austro-Hungarian, Turkish and Russian empires." But Herder himself was not a nationalist in the political sense. He believed different cultures should flourish "side by side like so many peaceful flowers in the great human garden." His attack was on imposed universalism — the Enlightenment's assumption that French rational culture was the apex of civilization to which all others should aspire.

The Herderian insights that fed nationalism:
- Each culture (*Volk*) has its own irreducible centre of gravity ([[Herder's Schwerpunkt]])
- "To belong to a given community... is a basic human need no less natural than that for food or drink"
- Cosmopolitanism is "the shedding of all that makes one most human, most oneself"
- Cultural contamination and imitation produce "artificiality, feeble imitativeness, degraded art and life"

From these insights it is a short step — though a logically invalid one — to: each Volk should have its own state, and that state should protect the culture's purity from contamination.

## The Maistrian Root

[[Joseph de Maistre]] contributed a darker strand: nations as organic wholes with "common souls," born and dying like individuals; racial purity as a social good; history and natural growth as the only legitimate sources of political authority. Maistre's ally Bonald explicitly lamented French racial mixing.

Where Herder's cultural pluralism was tolerant and anti-imperialist, Maistre's organic nationalism was aggressive and exclusionary from the start.

## The Political Logic of the Slide

The key move Berlin identifies: the "creative self" of Romantic individualism is collectivized. The individual artist who creates his own values becomes the nation/race as creative subject. Actions that would be abhorred as individual selfishness become heroic acts of national self-realization. The logic of Romantic self-assertion, applied to collective entities, licenses everything.

## The Historic/Unhistoric Nation Distinction

The most dangerous step: distinguishing "historic" from "unhistoric" peoples. Some nations have the organic spiritual capacity to realize themselves; others are mere masses that must be absorbed into historic nations. Hegel's philosophy of history gave this a theoretical form; the 19th-century nationalist movements applied it. This distinction is the immediate intellectual predecessor of racial hierarchy.

## Herder Contra the Nationalists

Berlin carefully notes that Herder rejected coercive nationalism: he "was no nationalist: he supposed that different cultures could and should flourish fruitfully side by side." His condemnation of Roman imperial conquest, of forced Christianization, of British missionary cultural destruction — these show his cultural pluralism was genuinely pluralist, not a cover for the dominance of any one culture.

The nationalists who claimed Herder's mantle had to ignore his actual position. The insight they appropriated — cultural identity as deep human need — was real; the political use they made of it was not what the insight logically required.

## Berlin's Own Position

Berlin's value pluralism leads him to a nuanced position on nationalism:
- Cultural belonging is a genuine human need, not mere false consciousness
- The demand for cultural recognition and preservation is legitimate
- But it does not follow that every culture must have a sovereign state, or that cultural purity must be enforced, or that "unhistoric" peoples may be exterminated
- These conclusions require additional premises that are false or indefensible

Berlin's liberalism tries to honor the Herderian insight while blocking the Maistrian political conclusion. Whether this is coherent — whether pluralism can be both culturally sensitive and universally rights-respecting — is the permanent tension in his thought.
