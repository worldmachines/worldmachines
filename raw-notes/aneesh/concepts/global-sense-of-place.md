# Global Sense of Place

**Source**: [[doreen-massey]], *Space, Place and Gender* (1994) and *For Space* (2005), esp. Ch. 12

## Origin

The term "a global sense of place" originates in Massey's 1991 essay "A Global Sense of Place" (in *Marxism Today*), reprinted in *Space, Place and Gender* (1994). It is the concept for which she is most widely known in cultural geography, urban studies, and feminist geography. *For Space* (2005) does not restate it from scratch but builds on it, deepening the ontological basis through the trajectories-and-events framework.

## The Concept

A "global sense of place" is Massey's shorthand for the alternative to two dominant but inadequate responses to globalisation:

**Response 1: Defensive localism** — place as a bounded, internally coherent identity, to be defended against external flows. This politics, while understandable, is structurally conservative: it treats local culture as authentic and prior, and globalisation as a threat from outside. It also rests on a false premise (places were never actually bounded and internally coherent).

**Response 2: Pure cosmopolitanism** — the celebration of flows, mobility, and the dissolution of place-attachments. This is the neoliberal fantasy that space has been conquered, that global capital flows freely, and that local attachments are merely backward. It ignores power: who is mobile, who is not, who gains from mobility, who is forced to move.

Massey's alternative: "a global sense of place" — place understood as:
- **Open** rather than bounded
- **Constructed out of particular sets of social relations** — including global ones — that happen to intersect at a particular location
- **Without a single essential identity** — its character is produced by the mix of relations, not by some prior local essence
- **Itself participating in producing the global** — places are not merely recipients of global forces; powerful places are nodes through which the global is constituted

## The Political Geography of Responsibility

A consequence of the global sense of place is a differentiated politics of responsibility. Places are differentially located in the wider power geometries:

> "On this view local places are not simply always the victims of the global; nor are they always politically defensible redoubts against the global. [...] Mali and Chad, most certainly, may be understood as occupying positions of relative powerlessness. But London, or the USA, or the UK? These are the places in and through which globalisation is produced." (Massey 2005: 101)

This is the concept of [[power-geometry]] at work: who controls movement, who is moved by the decisions of others, which places accumulate capital and which are drained. The global sense of place makes visible the *local production of the global* — and with it, a local responsibility for the global.

Bruce Robbins' observation about American nationalism (cited Massey 2005: 91–92) crystallises this: by treating capitalism as always arriving "from somewhere else," American progressives exonerate their own locality and refuse to see their complicity in producing the global they critique. The same applies to any powerful place that identifies itself as a victim of processes it is in fact constituting.

## What a Place Feels Like Under This Account

Massey is not arguing against the lived sense of place-attachment. She is herself a northerner who feels something specific when the train passes Cloud Hill beyond Congleton — "the hills get higher, the people get smaller, and I know that when I get off the train I will meet again the constant cheery back-chat which is south Lancashire" (Massey 2005: 122). That sense of home is real. What needs reworking is the *interpretation* of that sense — specifically, the tendency to freeze home in the moment one left it, to deny the place its continuing history.

"When you get 'there' the place will have moved on just as you yourself will have changed" (Massey 2005: 124). A global sense of place is one that registers this — that places are ongoing processes, not frozen essences. It is a sense of place that includes awareness of the global relations constructing the local.

## Relation to Throwntogetherness

The global sense of place is the political form taken by the ontological claim of [[throwntogetherness]]. If places are constituted by the meeting of multiple trajectories — local and global, human and nonhuman, historical and contemporary — then the appropriate political stance is one that acknowledges this constitution, including the global dimensions, rather than retreating to a fantasy of bounded local essence.

## Tension: Can Openness Ground Attachment?

The persistent challenge to Massey's account (from Dirlik, from some feminist geographers): can a place that has no essential identity, no grounding, support the kind of attachment and political mobilisation that defensive localism provides? Massey's answer: yes, but it requires a different kind of politics. The uniqueness of place, on her account, lies in the specific constellation of trajectories that come together at this here-and-now — not in a pre-given essence. That constellation is genuinely unique and genuinely matters; it is just not eternal.
