# AI Democratization Gap

## The Claim

AI-generated information tools (summaries, analysis) improve performance across the board — but they improve sophisticated users *more*, widening rather than closing the knowledge gap between expert and novice users.

First raised in [[Jan. 3, 2025]] via a paper by Eric So and colleagues on AI in financial decision-making.

## The Mechanism

The gap operates at two levels:

### 1. Signal Loss in Simplification

Making summaries accessible requires stripping technical precision:
- **Advanced**: "operating margins declined due to higher input costs caused by supply chain disruptions"
- **Simplified**: "reduction in profits"

The simplified version is accurate but loses causal structure. Decisions made on reduced signal are worse decisions.

### 2. Query Precision (Jargon as Compression)

See [[Jargon as Compression]]: experts who can precisely scope a question — using domain vocabulary to compress a complex problem into a precise query — extract more precise output from a model. The model cannot compensate for what the user doesn't know to ask.

> The result is a tool that amplifies existing advantage rather than distributing it.

## Key Quote

From the paper: *"AI widens the knowledge-driven disparities between sophisticated and unsophisticated participants."*

## Broader Implication

This directly challenges the democratization narrative embedded in [[Composable Knowledge]] and in much AI optimism. The question is whether composability is a **multiplier** (scales with existing capability) or a **leveler** (raises the floor for everyone).

The evidence here suggests it is primarily a multiplier.

## Open Questions

- Does this apply beyond finance? (Likely yes, most acutely in domains with high jargon density)
- Can prompt design or interface design mitigate this? (Reducing the burden of query precision on the user)
- Is there a category of tasks where simplification doesn't sacrifice essential signal?
