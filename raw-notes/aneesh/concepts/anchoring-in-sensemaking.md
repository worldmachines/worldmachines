# Anchoring in Sensemaking

## The Concept

When encountering a new situation or a surprise, the initial one or two key data elements sometimes serve as **anchors** for creating an understanding. These anchors elicit the initial frame, and the frame is then used to search for more data elements. The process is highly constrained: Klein et al. (2007) assert that people rely on *at most* three or four anchors in deriving a frame.

This is not a passive process. Anchors are not simply the most salient data in a stream — they are the data elements that have sufficient meaning to suggest an explanatory structure. Expert knowledge is required to know which data elements are worth anchoring on.

## Key Properties

**Economy**: Weather forecasters studied by Debra Jones relied on only 2–3 anchors when selecting frames for severe weather conditions. The helicopter accident (Example 3) was resolved through a sequence of successively better anchors: night conditions → bad weather → sling load → wind shift + U-turn complexity.

**Sequential replacement**: Each new anchor can suggest a new frame, displacing the previous one. Captain B. moved from one frame to the next without losing continuity — each anchor superseded the last as the organizing center.

**Anchor lock-in**: Once a data element has been used as an anchor in one frame, it is difficult to use in a competing frame. The rival uses of the same anchor create conceptual strain, limiting the ability to compare frames. Experts develop "logical competitor sets" (LCS) precisely to manage this constraint.

**Timing effects**: An inaccurate anchor early in a sensemaking sequence corrupts subsequent interpretation more severely than a late inaccurate anchor. Early anchors shape the entire filter through which subsequent data are interpreted. This has important implications for intelligence analysis: Patterson et al. (1998) found that initial misleading articles distorted all subsequent analysis, because the initial frame (anchored on the first articles) guided subsequent search.

## Anchoring vs. Anchoring Heuristic

Tversky & Kahneman's (1974) "anchoring and adjustment heuristic" describes how numerical estimates are biased toward an initial number — even an arbitrary one. Klein et al.'s sensemaking anchors are structurally related but conceptually distinct:

| Dimension | Kahneman anchoring | Klein sensemaking anchoring |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------------------|
| Domain | Numerical estimation | Explanatory frames |
| Content | Any salient number | Meaningful data elements |
| Function | Distorts estimates | Constructs the frame itself |
| Correction | Adjust away from anchor | Replace with new frame |

Footnote in Klein et al.: "The strategy of using anchors in this way is compatible with the anchoring and adjustment heuristic (Tversky & Kahneman, 1974)" — but it operates at the macro level of explanatory structures, not micro estimates.

## Building Frames From Anchors

Klein & Crandall (1995) describe **mental simulation** as the mechanism by which people assemble frames from anchors. People assemble data elements as anchors, gauge the plausibility of the assembly, and "run" the assembly to imagine how events might have been caused or to imagine how they might play out. The astronaut example: from a limited set of causal factors, people construct a story that can be projected into the future.

## The DSS Implication

Decision support systems should help users track available anchors, since people use so few and since anchor quality determines frame quality. More data does not compensate for poor anchors — it may actually worsen sensemaking by increasing information load without improving the anchor set.
