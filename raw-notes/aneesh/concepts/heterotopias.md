# Heterotopias (Foucault)

**Source**: Michel Foucault, "Of Other Spaces" (1967/1984); deployed in [[aneesh-sathe]], *We Need Homes in the Delta Quadrant* (2025), §III

## The Concept

In his 1967 lecture "Of Other Spaces: Utopias and Heterotopias" (published 1984), Foucault distinguishes between two kinds of "other" spaces:

- **Utopias**: places that have no real location — idealized, inverted, or perfected forms of society, but not actually existing anywhere
- **Heterotopias**: places that *do* exist — real, actually instituted spaces — but that operate under a different logic than the surrounding spatial order

As Sathe characterizes them:

> "Michel Foucault offers a vocabulary for this through his concept of *heterotopias*: places that operate under a different logic, outside the dominant spatial order. These may be institutional — cemeteries, prisons, libraries — or insurgent — subcultures, autonomous zones, speculative games. Heterotopias do not merely resist the prevailing map; they reveal that other maps are possible. They function as mirrors and distortions of the dominant world, reminding us that the spatial order is neither natural nor inevitable." (§III)

## Foucault's Six Principles

Foucault identifies six properties of heterotopias:

1. **Universality**: every culture probably produces heterotopias, though their form varies
2. **Historical variability**: the same heterotopia can shift function as a society changes (the cemetery moved from the center of the city to its edge)
3. **Multiple incompatible spaces**: a single heterotopia can juxtapose several spaces that would otherwise be incompatible (the theater, the cinema, the garden)
4. **Heterochronies**: heterotopias break with conventional time — either accumulating time indefinitely (the museum, the library) or ephemeral by nature (the fair)
5. **Opening and closing**: entry into heterotopias is regulated — you don't enter a prison or a sauna freely; entry is either obligatory (prison) or requires ritual (the hamam)
6. **Function relative to the rest of space**: they either create an illusion that reveals all real space as more illusory (like the brothel) or create a perfect, meticulous, well-arranged space to compensate for real space's disorder (like the colony)

## The Political Valence

For Foucault, heterotopias are not straightforwardly liberatory. They can be sites of confinement (prisons, psychiatric hospitals) as much as sites of resistance (autonomous zones, ship). The cemetery is a heterotopia: it concentrates the dead, separating them from the living — originally at the center of the city, then, as death became taboo, expelled to the periphery.

The political significance that Sathe draws out is subtler: heterotopias demonstrate that the dominant spatial order is *contingent*. If cemeteries, libraries, and ships operate under different rules, then the rules by which streets, offices, and homes are organized are not natural laws but conventions that could be otherwise. Heterotopias are counter-proofs — they make visible the artificiality of dominant space.

## Relation to Bryant

Bryant's [[onto-cartography]] conceives of space as produced by the relational coupling of machines. Heterotopias, in this light, are assemblages where the machine-couplings follow a different topology — different entry/exit protocols, different temporal structures, different gravitational hierarchies. The library accumulates time (heterochrony of indefinite accumulation); the prison regulates bodily movement through architectural gravity; the colony attempts to create a compensatory perfect space.

Bryant's framework gives heterotopias a material substrate that Foucault's purely discursive analysis sometimes lacks.
