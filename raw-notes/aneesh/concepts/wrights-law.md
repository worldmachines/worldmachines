# Wright's Law

## Definition

Wright's Law (also known as the experience curve or learning curve) states: for every doubling of cumulative production output, unit costs fall by a fixed percentage. The law was first observed in aircraft manufacturing (1936) and has been confirmed in solar panels (20% cost reduction per doubling of installed capacity), semiconductors, gene sequencing, and other technology sectors.

The efficiency gains come from multiple interlocking factors: labor efficiency, process improvement, better equipment utilization, and — critically — the diffusion of tacit knowledge among workers who share a common knowledge base.

## Why Healthcare Resists Wright's Law

Sathe (see [[Dancing on the Shoulders of Giants]]) identifies healthcare as a persistent exception:
- **Regulatory barriers**: each new procedure, drug, or diagnostic requires independent clinical evidence cycles
- **High research costs**: innovation is expensive to validate
- **Slow knowledge diffusion**: individual clinicians develop effective hacks, but those insights enter the broader system only through education, training, and regulation — a generational delay
- **No shared knowledge infrastructure**: in the technology sectors that follow Wright's Law, a shared culture across factory floors and corporate strategy enables learning to compound. Healthcare lacks this.

## The Composite AI Hypothesis

The [[Composite AI Architecture (Experts + LLM + KG)]] is implicitly an argument about how to apply Wright's Law dynamics to healthcare:
- [[Knowledge Graphs]] externalize expert knowledge so it doesn't die with the expert or remain siloed in one institution
- LLMs make that knowledge traversable without requiring technical expertise
- The result: practitioners can access the accumulated learning of the field without waiting for it to percolate through a generation of education

In other words, the composite architecture is an attempt to create a shared knowledge infrastructure for healthcare analogous to what factory floors have in manufacturing.
