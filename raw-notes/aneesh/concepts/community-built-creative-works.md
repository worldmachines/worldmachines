# Community-Built Creative Works

**Community-built creative works** are works where the primary author provides a core substrate (rules, engine, world) and an organic community fills complementary layers — art, music, documentation, tooling, fanfiction, shared lore — without formal mandate.

## Dwarf Fortress as Paradigm

[[Dwarf Fortress]] is the clearest example in contemporary game culture:
- Core simulation: [[Tarn Adams]] alone
- Graphics and tileset mods: community
- Music: community (now official)
- Third-party utilities (DFHack, Dwarf Therapist): community (many now on the team)
- Legend and lore documentation: community
- Webcomics, Let's Plays, narrative accounts: community

None of this was formally organized. It emerged because the substrate was deep enough to warrant the investment.

See [[the-best-game-ever-made|The Best Game Ever Made]].

## Why Communities Form Around These Works

1. **Depth**: the substrate has enough to explore that community members find value in extended engagement
2. **Open gaps**: the primary author leaves (by choice or necessity) gaps that community members can fill meaningfully
3. **Shared language**: a common substrate creates common reference points that enable communication and collaboration
4. **Non-zero-sum**: community contributions improve the experience for all without diminishing the original work

## The Transition from Community to Team

In DF's case, community contributors eventually joined the official team. This is a pattern in long-form open-source-adjacent projects: the boundary between community and creator blurs as the work matures.

## Contrast with Manufactured Fandom

Corporate IP attempts to replicate community formation through marketing. The results are typically shallower because the substrate was designed for audience consumption rather than genuine exploration.
