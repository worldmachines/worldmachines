# Far-From-Equilibrium Systems

## Core Concept

Far-from-equilibrium systems are a class of physical and chemical systems described by Nobel laureate Ilya Prigogine (and collaborators including Isabelle Stengers). Unlike systems approaching thermodynamic equilibrium — which tend toward maximum entropy, minimum free energy, and structural simplicity — far-from-equilibrium systems maintain organized, complex structures by continuously dissipating energy.

The key insight: organization can be *produced and sustained* by energy throughput, not despite it. Under sufficient energy flow, certain chemical and physical systems spontaneously develop self-organizing, macroscopic structures (Bénard cells in heated fluid; the Belousov-Zhabotinsky reaction; living organisms) that would never appear at equilibrium. These are Prigogine's *dissipative structures* — organized forms that exist only because energy continuously flows through them.

Thermodynamic equilibrium = death. Life, complexity, and organization are far-from-equilibrium phenomena.

## The Two Arrows

Prigogine's framework resolves what appeared to be a contradiction between:
- The Second Law of Thermodynamics (entropy always increases; systems tend toward disorder)
- The existence of highly organized biological and social structures (which seem to contradict the Second Law)

The resolution: the Second Law applies to *closed* systems. Open systems that exchange energy and matter with their environment can locally decrease entropy, at the cost of increasing entropy in the larger system. The organism is a pocket of low entropy sustained by continuous energy throughput — food, sunlight, chemical gradients — while exporting entropy (heat, waste) to the environment. The universe's overall entropy still increases; the local improbability is purchased at global cost.

## Relevance to Bryant

Bryant does not cite Prigogine extensively in Chapter 4, but the Prigoginian framework is the thermodynamic background for Bryant's entropy/negentropy analysis. When Bryant argues that machines are "improbable" states that require ongoing work to maintain themselves, he is pointing to far-from-equilibrium dynamics:

- Machines are dissipative structures: they maintain low entropy by continuously processing energy and matter flows
- The city's dependency on external energy flows (food, fuel, building materials) is a dissipative structure at social scale
- Organic bodies are paradigmatic dissipative structures — their autopoietic operations are also dissipative operations

Bryant's observation that cities draw energy from coal mines, farms, and oil fields reflects the openness-to-environment that defines far-from-equilibrium systems. Cities exist because they are thermodynamically open — they can't bootstrap their own energy supply from within.

## Prigogine's Philosophical Implications

Prigogine argued (with Stengers in *Order Out of Chaos*) that far-from-equilibrium physics has profound implications:
1. **Irreversibility is fundamental**: unlike classical mechanics (where time is reversible), far-from-equilibrium processes are irreversible. The arrow of time is real.
2. **Becoming over being**: stable, equilibrium structures are the exception; the world is fundamentally a world of processes, flows, and becoming.
3. **Bifurcation and contingency**: far-from-equilibrium systems pass through instability points (bifurcations) where small fluctuations can determine which of several possible structures emerges. History matters; the world is not fully determined.

These implications align with Bryant's process-ontology of machines.
