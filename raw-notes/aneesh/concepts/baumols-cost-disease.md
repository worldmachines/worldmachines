# Baumol's Cost Disease

## Definition

Baumol's cost disease (also: the Baumol effect) is the phenomenon whereby wages in labor-intensive sectors that *cannot* achieve productivity gains nonetheless rise over time in lockstep with economy-wide wages — because workers in those sectors must be retained against the outside option of higher-paying, more productive industries. The result is that the *relative price* of labor-intensive goods and services rises continuously, even if there is no inefficiency or deterioration in quality.

Named after economist **[[William Baumol]]**, who developed the theory in the 1960s while investigating the economics of performing arts for the Kennedy administration.

## Core Logic

1. Productivity growth in some sectors (agriculture, manufacturing, electronics) allows those sectors to pay higher wages while keeping prices flat or declining.
2. Economy-wide wages rise as a result.
3. Labor-intensive sectors (healthcare, education, childcare, performing arts, restaurants) cannot achieve comparable productivity gains — their output is irreducibly human.
4. Yet they must compete for workers against the more productive sectors by matching rising wages.
5. Without offsetting productivity gains, their prices must rise to cover higher labor costs.
6. Over time, these sectors become relatively more expensive even though nothing has "gone wrong."

## Baumol's Illustrative Case

> "The output per man-hour of the violinist playing a Schubert quartet in a standard concert hall is relatively fixed, and it is fairly difficult to reduce the number of actors necessary for a performance of Henry IV, Part II."

## Affected Sectors

- Healthcare
- Education
- Childcare
- Performing arts (opera, theater, classical music)
- Restaurants with table service
- Barbershops and salons
- Live entertainment generally

## Implications for Social Life

[[Alex Mayyasi]] argues that Baumol's cost disease is the underappreciated structural cause of rising [[Social Isolation]] in America. Because communal, in-person experiences fall almost entirely in the labor-intensive sector, they become progressively unaffordable relative to scalable, digital alternatives. See [[Baumol Cost Disease and Toxic Solitude]].

## The Proposed Cure

Baumol himself identified the standard solution: progressive taxation in a growing economy can be used to subsidize cost-disease sectors so that citizens can access them. This argument underpins the case for public subsidy of healthcare and education. The Mayyasi essay extends the logic to question whether social infrastructure — bowling alleys, dive bars, third places — should also qualify.
