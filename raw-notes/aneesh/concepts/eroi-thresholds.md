# EROI Thresholds

**Energy Return on Investment (EROI)** is the ratio of energy delivered by a source to the energy required to obtain it. An EROI of 30:1 means that for every unit of energy invested in extraction, 30 units are delivered to society. The concept, developed by Charles Hall and colleagues, reveals that not all energy is equal — what matters for civilization is **net energy**, the surplus available after the energy cost of energy acquisition is paid.

## The Hierarchy of Civilizational Functions

Hall's research establishes a hierarchy of minimum EROI thresholds below which specific civilizational functions become unsustainable:

| EROI Threshold | Civilizational Function |
|----------------|------------------------|
| ~3:1 | Basic resource extraction (grain, timber) |
| ~5:1 | Transportation of goods and people |
| ~7:1 | Supporting families and basic social reproduction |
| ~10:1 | Education, healthcare, and complex institutional life |
| ~14:1+ | Arts, science, and cultural surplus |

Below ~3:1, a society is spending so much energy acquiring energy that it cannot sustain even basic material production. Below ~10:1, the surplus is insufficient for the institutional complexity that characterizes modern civilization. The implication is stark: **complex societies require high-EROI energy sources**, and as EROI falls, civilizational functions are shed in reverse order of the hierarchy — arts and science first, then education and healthcare, then families, then transport.

## The Declining EROI of Fossil Fuels

Global oil and gas EROI has declined from approximately **30:1 in 1995** to approximately **18:1 in 2006**, with continued erosion since. The easy oil — shallow, onshore, high-pressure reservoirs — was extracted first. What remains is deeper, offshore, unconventional, and more energy-intensive to access. Tar sands operate at ~5:1. Tight oil (fracking) at ~5-10:1.

This decline is not a failure of technology but a geological inevitability: the best deposits are found and exploited first, and subsequent deposits require more energy to access. Technology can slow the decline but cannot reverse the underlying thermodynamic trend.

## The Cheap-Energy Subsidy

The 20th century's explosion of individual agency — mass education, healthcare, mobility, creative autonomy — was underwritten by fossil fuels with EROI ratios of 50:1 to 100:1 in the early petroleum era. This cheap-energy subsidy meant that an unprecedented share of economic activity could be directed toward complexity-building rather than energy acquisition.

As EROI falls, an increasing share of economic activity must be diverted to energy acquisition. The surplus available for everything else — the surplus that funds the institutions, infrastructure, and freedoms of modern life — shrinks. The agency explosion of the 20th century was, in thermodynamic terms, a one-time gift of geological inheritance, and that inheritance is being drawn down.

## Useful-Stage Correction (Brockway et al.)

The standard EROI numbers measure energy returned at the wellhead or busbar, not at the point of useful work. About 70% of fossil-fuel primary energy is wasted as heat in combustion before it does anything. Brockway and colleagues (2019) re-ran the EROI accounting at the **useful stage** — the point where energy actually performs work — and found:

- **Fossil fuels (useful-stage):** ~3.5:1
- **Road transport on fossil fuels:** 1.6:1
- **Estimated minimum useful-stage EROI for a complex society:** ~5:1

By honest accounting, fossil fuels are **already below the threshold** for sustaining the complexity they currently support. The visible primary-stage numbers (oil and gas at 18:1 in 2006) overstate the actual surplus available to civilisation by roughly the combustion-loss factor.

## Solar at the Useful Stage

Photovoltaics convert directly to electricity, which is itself useful-stage energy. The combustion-loss correction does not apply. Solar's useful-stage EROI is estimated at **25:1 to 30:1** — seven to nine times higher than fossil fuels at the same point of comparison.

The structural difference matters more than the numbers. Fossil fuels are a stock with diminishing returns to extraction; solar is a flow with increasing returns to manufacturing scale ([[swansons-law]]). PV cost per watt has fallen from ~$106 in 1976 to under $0.10 today — a thirteen-hundred-fold decline in fifty years. As cumulative installed capacity doubles, costs fall ~23%. Unlike fossil EROI, which declines geologically, solar EROI **improves with scale**.
