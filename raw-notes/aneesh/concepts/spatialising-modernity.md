# Spatialising Modernity

The signature diagnostic move in Chapter 6 of [[doreen-massey]]'s *For Space* (2005). The thesis: modernity's characteristic political-cosmological operation is the **temporal convening of spatial difference**. The geography of the world is rewritten as the *history* of the world.

> "Different 'places' were interpreted as different stages in a single temporal development. All the stories of unilinear progress, modernisation, development, the sequence of modes of production . . . perform this operation. Western Europe is 'advanced', other parts of the world 'some way behind', yet others are 'backward' . . . That turning of the world's geography into the world's (single) history is implicit in many versions of modernist politics, from liberal progressive to some Marxist." (Massey 2005: 68)

## The move in steps

1. Coexisting societies are observed.
2. The differences between them are read as differences of **stage**, not of **kind**.
3. A single developmental story is assumed in which one stage follows another.
4. The Western model is positioned as "ahead"; everyone else is "behind."
5. The future of the "behind" is already known: become the "ahead."
6. Difference is thereby tamed: it is not real divergence but lag.

The political stakes are immediate. If difference is lag, there is no alternative future to argue for — only the question of *how fast* the laggards catch up. Resistance is irrational; the road is fixed; "there is no alternative."

## Why it is also a repression of time

Counter-intuitively, the move is not just an erasure of space. It is *also* a foreclosure of temporality:

> "In these conceptions of singular progress (of whatever hue), temporality itself is not really open. The future is already foretold; inscribed into the story." (68)

A single-trajectory time is not a time of event, novelty, or politics. It is a script. Massey's argument from Part Two — that space and time are co-implicated — pays off here: closing space also closes time. The [[cosmopolitan-time-queue]] is the joint repression.

## Fabian on the denial of coevalness

Massey draws extensively on [[johannes-fabian]]'s *Time and the Other* (1983), which exposes this move in twentieth-century anthropology. By placing "those who are observed" in a different time from the observer, anthropology "sanctioned an ideological process by which relations between the West and its Other . . . were conceived not only as difference, but as distance in space and Time" (Fabian 1983: 147, quoted Massey 2005: 69). Coevalness is the antidote: see [[coevalness]].

## How globalisation inherits the move

Chapter 8 of *For Space* extends the diagnosis: the dominant globalisation narrative repeats the modernity move. Africa is not different from the West, just "developing." The neoliberal rhetoric of inevitability ("globalisation is like gravity") is the temporal convening of space all over again. See [[aspatial-globalisation]], [[globalisation-as-inevitability-rhetoric]].
