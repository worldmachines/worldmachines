# That's Interesting! — Murray S. Davis (1971)

## Citation

Davis, Murray S. "That's Interesting! Towards a Phenomenology of Sociology and a Sociology of Phenomenology." *Philosophy of the Social Sciences* 1 (1971): 309-344.

## Central Argument

A theory is judged great not because it is true but because it is *interesting* — because it denies some specific aspect of its audience's taken-for-granted assumptions (the "assumption-ground"). Davis proposes that all interesting propositions share a single logical structure: "What seems to be X is in reality non-X."

The routine process of scientific evaluation focuses on methodology (Is it true?), but what actually determines whether a theory attracts attention, gains adherents, and endures is whether it effectively disrupts existing belief structures in a way that is neither too minor (obvious) nor too radical (absurd).

## The Index of the Interesting

Davis catalogs 12 logical categories by which propositions can deny assumptions. Each operates on a different dimension of the audience's taken-for-granted world:

| Category | Assumption denied | Move |
|----------|------------------|------|
| **Organization** | What seems disorganized is actually organized (or vice versa) | Pattern ↔ chaos |
| **Composition** | What seems individual is actually holistic (or vice versa) | Part ↔ whole |
| **Abstraction** | What seems a local phenomenon is actually general (or vice versa) | Particular ↔ universal |
| **Generalization** | What seems heterogeneous is actually one species (or vice versa) | Many ↔ one |
| **Stabilization** | What seems stable is actually unstable (or vice versa) | Static ↔ dynamic |
| **Function** | What seems to function as X actually functions as Y | Manifest ↔ latent |
| **Evaluation** | What seems good is actually bad (or vice versa) | Value reversal |
| **Co-relation** | What seems unrelated is actually correlated (or vice versa) | Independent ↔ dependent |
| **Co-existence** | What seems to co-exist cannot (or vice versa) | Compatible ↔ incompatible |
| **Co-variation** | What seems to co-vary positively actually co-varies inversely (or vice versa) | Direct ↔ inverse |
| **Opposition** | What seems opposed is actually similar (or vice versa) | Different ↔ same |
| **Causation** | What seems the cause is actually the effect (or vice versa) | Cause ↔ effect reversal |

## The Assumption-Ground

The concept of the "assumption-ground" — drawn from Garfinkel's ethnomethodology and Schutz's phenomenology — is the web of taken-for-granted beliefs that constitutes an audience's baseline reality. Interesting propositions work by targeting a specific strand of this web: if they leave the whole web intact, they're obvious; if they attempt to destroy the entire web, they're absurd. The sweet spot is a single, targeted denial that forces reconstruction of the surrounding belief structure.

This connects to the Overton window concept but operates at the level of epistemology rather than politics.

## The Rhetorical Spectrum

Davis identifies a spectrum of audience response based on the degree of assumption-denial:

- **"That's obvious"** — The proposition affirms what the audience already believes. No disruption.
- **"That's interesting!"** — The proposition denies one specific assumption while leaving the rest of the belief structure intact. The sweet spot.
- **"That's absurd"** — The proposition denies too many assumptions simultaneously, threatening the audience's entire reality structure. Rejected as crazy.

A proposition that is "interesting" to one audience may be "obvious" to another (who already hold the counter-assumption) or "absurd" to a third (whose assumption-ground is too distant).

## The Sociology of the Interesting

Davis proposes an entirely new field: the study of what makes ideas catch on, spread, and endure. Not truth (the province of methodology), not beauty (the province of aesthetics), but *interestingness* — the dynamic mechanisms by which ideas disrupt and reconstruct belief systems. He argues this field is more important for understanding intellectual history than either philosophy of science or sociology of knowledge.

## Key Quotes

- "It has long been thought that a theorist is considered great because his theories are true, but this is false. A theorist is considered great, not because his theories are true, but because they are interesting."
- "The audience finds a proposition 'interesting' not because it tells them some truth they did not already know, but because it tells them some truth they thought they already knew was wrong."
- "Those who have articulated the grounds of their audience's assumptions have been considered the most interesting theorists."
