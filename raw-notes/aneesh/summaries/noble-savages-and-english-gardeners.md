# Noble Savages and English Gardeners: Kulturkritik from Rousseau to Goethe

**Source:** Franz R. Kempf, *Philosophy and Literature*, Vol. 39 No. 2 (October 2015), pp. 422–442. DOI: 10.1353/phl.2015.0055

## Central Argument

Kulturkritik is not merely "critique of culture" but a **regulative mode of experimental thinking** that self-critically acts out antithetical scenarios in order to find ways to heal from culture *through* culture. The noble savage and the English garden are oxymoronic articulations of a desired reconciliation between nature and culture — but both are paradoxically *products* of the cultural refinement they ostensibly reject.

The key paradox: Kulturkritik wants to heal the wound of self-alienation caused by excessive rationalism, but it can only do so using the very tools of reason. Its ideals are postulates; every synthesis is homeopathic. "Kulturkritik seeks a nontheological redemption that is paradoxical because it disavows the Fall without renouncing the fruit from the tree of knowledge."

## Structure

Kempf proceeds through five numbered sections:
1. The horticulture-politics parallel: French garden = omnipotence of reason; English garden = freedom of nature — but this binary is immediately complicated (Hegel sees the English garden as obstructing "eine in sich seiende Seele")
2. Rousseau's *Discourses* and the rhetoric of the noble savage (*bête* vs. *bourgeois*)
3. Rousseau's rhetorical strategy: "standing between two mirrors" — self-projection and self-reflection simultaneously
4. Schiller's response: the aesthetic drive (*Spieltrieb*) as synthesis of sensuous and formal; art as the cure; the English garden as a calculated ideal
5. Goethe's skepticism toward the English garden — his rejection of its "beautiful semblance" and insistence on *Kunstwahrheit* (artistic truth) over *Naturwirklichkeit* (natural verisimilitude)

## Key Claims

### On Rousseau
- The *bête* (solitary primitive) is an a priori construct, never meant to be historically real — its function is rhetorical contrast to attack the *bourgeois*
- "The Savage lives within himself; the sociable man, always outside of himself" (*CW3*, p. 66)
- The noble savage is not the solitary brute but the inhabitant of nascent society — "at equal distances from the stupidity of the brutes and the fatal enlightenment of Civil man" (*CW3*, pp. 48–49)
- Julie's Elysium garden in *Nouvelle Héloise* is a French garden metamorphosed into an English garden through art: "Nature did it all, but under my direction" — creating only "the illusion of natural wilderness"
- "All this cannot be done without a modicum of illusion" (*CW6*, p. 394)
- The botanizer in *Reveries* achieves oneness with nature through a magnifying glass and Linnaeus's *Systema naturae* — culture heals through culture

### On Schiller
- Identifies overspecialization (not just refinement) as the cause of self-alienation
- The norm is not original nature but "the Greeks of antiquity whose wholeness of being is irretrievably lost"
- The *Spieltrieb* (play drive) sublates sensuous and formal drives, producing "living form" = beauty
- "It is only through beauty that man makes his way to freedom" (*AE*, p. 90)
- The Hohenheim Park near Stuttgart as his horticultural ideal: "a middle way between the rigidity of the French style and the anarchy of the so-called English garden"
- His theory of the sublime (1801) paradoxically *generates* more culture: in the sublime, man becomes "conscious of his independence from nature" — what the sublime produces *is* culture
- His admission: art that educates toward healing is "aesthetic semblance" — "a beauty that is only an illusion, and a freedom that exists only in fiction"

### On Goethe
- Engages Rousseau's paradox through "Wiederholte Spiegelungen" (repeated mirrorings) — a *Steigerung* (heightening/intensification) rather than dialectical synthesis
- Rejects the English garden on aesthetic grounds: it "perpetuates the current bad habit of wanting to be unrestrained and lawless" (*SW18*, pp. 760–62); it "makes do with semblance because it turns reality into fantasia"
- In *Elective Affinities* (1809): the English garden project ends in death for all parties — Nature (Ottilie, Eduard, Otto) and Culture (Charlotte, Captain) both die
- Goethe's model is Claude Lorrain: paintings with "the highest truth, but no trace of actuality" — *Kunstwahrheit* over surface naturalism
- *Faust*'s synthesis: his English garden vision is spoken "in anticipation" and is "a product of magic" — an illusion
- "Sorge" (Care/Cura) from Hyginus's *Fabulae*: Care as creator, gardener, cultivator — the *ur*-gardener who shaped homo from humus
- Nature and culture in Goethe are "interlocked in a dynamic upward movement of irreducible polar opposites" — never blended, always *hinan* (upward/onward)

## Key Quotes

> "The Savage lives within himself; the sociable man, always outside of himself." — Rousseau, *CW3*, p. 66

> "Nature did it all, but under my direction, and there is nothing here that I have not designed." — Julie (*CW6*, p. 388)

> "It is only through beauty that man makes his way to freedom." — Schiller, *AE*, p. 90

> "The more pretentious, the more seductive this material … the more the art that forces it back and asserts its own kind of dominion over [it]." — Schiller, *AE*, p. 151

> "Kulturkritik is more than a critique of culture. It is a thought experiment, or a mode of experimental thinking that enables the acting out of antithetical scenarios." — Kempf, p. 430

## Intellectual Lineage

- Rousseau → Schiller (Rousseau "initiated processes culminating in the French Revolution"; Schiller's Kulturkritik is "an answer to the French Revolution gone awry")
- Rousseau → Goethe (Goethe writes to his sister in 1767: "Don't laugh about this seemingly foolish philosophy...")
- Schiller vs. Rousseau: man in natural state is for Schiller a "troglodyte," not innocent; reflection *begins* humanity rather than corrupting it
- Goethe vs. Schiller: Schiller talks about an effect, Goethe about an essence; the beautiful semblance Schiller advances is, for Goethe, insufficient — it masks "instrumentalization of nature and culture for the sake of the beautiful effect"
- Precursors/analogs named: Diogenes, Freud, Adorno, *The Matrix*, Frank Lloyd Wright's Fallingwater, Tom Stoppard's *Arcadia*

## What's Missing

- No engagement with colonialism's actual violence (the "noble savage" framing viewed through imperial eyes — Kempf notes this but doesn't pursue it)
- No treatment of women's relationship to Kulturkritik (Julie as gardener, Charlotte as nature/culture casualty — both subordinate)
- The question of whether the English garden's "demise" in Goethe is also the demise of Kulturkritik's utopian project is raised but left open
