# How to Make Sense of AI (Chin, 2026)

**Source**: commoncog.com, published March 2026. Part 1 of a sensemaking series; Part 2 is [[How Experts Sensemake]].

## Central Argument

Effective AI sensemaking is a special case of [[Outcome Orientation]] and [[Fast Adaptation Under Uncertainty]]. The correct stance is not to predict AI's trajectory but to maintain clarity about your own outcomes and act experimentally. The method: consume only **detailed field reports of use**, not analysis, forecasts, or opinion — and filter those reports through **four questions of uncertainty**.

## The Method

**Step 1 — Ignore**: All opinions, predictions, scenario essays, forecasts, "situational awareness" updates. Regardless of author prestige.

**Step 2 — Attend**: Only detailed field reports of actual AI use. Judged by: adequacy of detail, credibility of author, context of use, stated goals. If a field report contains embedded speculation, read only the report portion.

**Step 3 — Apply the Four Questions of Uncertainty** (attributed to [[Vaughn Tan]], *Not Knowing* series, 2023–24):
1. What **new outcomes** are now possible?
2. What **actions** can I take in response?
3. What are the **relative values** of these outcomes to me, given my situation?
4. What are the **causal relationships** driving these outcomes?

## Why Ignore Analysis?

- Technological impacts are uneven and unpredictable; analyst context differs from your context
- Attention economy incentivizes fear-priming content
- Much "analysis" is self-soothing — comforting writers/readers rather than informing action
- Reading opinions has real opportunity cost: time not spent on personal experimentation

## The Outcome Orientation Frame

> "You must know what to ignore. Attention is a limited resource."

The method operationalizes [[Outcome Orientation]]: before consuming any content, ask "What outcome am I trying to achieve here?" This alone reportedly resolves information overload for consistent practitioners.

## Fast Adaptation as Superior Strategy

Forecasting is cognitively expensive and unreliable. The better stance: set up organizational and personal structures for rapid experimentation and adaptation. Advantage accrues to those who sensemake faster and act in response to present conditions — not those who predict correctly.

> "You don't have to predict the future if you can see the present moment clearly."

Key requirements for fast adaptation:
- Willingness to experiment
- Information-sharing infrastructure (Slack channels, curated threads)
- Organizational agility to change direction on new data

## Historical Precedent: Technological Revolutions

Chin's reading program (1.5 years) covered 10+ technological bubbles via *Engines That Move Markets* (Alisdair Nairn), *The Shock of the Old* (Edgerton), biographies of Rockefeller/Morgan/Livermore. Core finding: mass technology adoption produces no sustained competitive advantage. Sustained advantage comes from coupling technology to *new organizational forms* (e.g., Walmart's decentralized logistics, not just barcode scanners).

## What's Missing

- The method presupposes access to high-quality field reports — not uniformly available across domains
- "Four questions" framework lacks guidance on weighting when multiple outcomes are simultaneously plausible
- No treatment of how sensemaking should change once you've moved from exploration to commitment
