# Summary: *Onto-Cartography* Ch. 7 — "Gravity" (Bryant 2014, pp. 184–232)

## Why "Gravity" Rather Than "Power"

Chapter 7 opens with a physical-philosophical genealogy. Bryant traces the concept of gravity from Lucretius through Newton to Einstein to construct his own analogue. Lucretius held that material interaction requires material contact — nothing acts at a distance. Newtonian gravity was therefore scandalous to materialists: it appeared to be force exerted across a vacuum, a kind of occultism. Einstein resolved the scandal by showing that gravity is not a force at all but a *curvature of space-time* produced by mass, a topology along which other bodies travel. Bodies don't attract one another; they travel the curved paths mass creates.

Bryant imports this Einsteinian move into social ontology. In the humanities and social sciences, "power" and "force" occupy the same explanatory role Newtonian force occupied in physics — they are invoked to explain phenomena but provide only tautological grounds. Citing Hegel's *Science of Logic*, Bryant notes that explaining unemployment by "social forces" merely repeats in different words what was to be explained, providing an "occult" explanation rather than a material one (pp. 187–88).

"Power" carries a second liability: **anthropocentrism**. It draws analytical attention to narratives, signs, discourses, and institutions — the plane of expression — while ignoring the plane of content: rivers, ocean currents, disease epidemiology, mountain ranges, fossil fuel reserves. As theorists from Braudel to Latour to Bennett have shown, physical geography and material resources substantially organize human social arrangements in ways that operate regardless of any ideology (p. 188).

The rhetorical advantage of "gravity" is that it is an **all-purpose term** — operative in a deep-ocean volcanic vent as much as a government agency, straddling the social and the natural without privileging the cultural. Bryant's concept of gravity "includes all of the discursivist and practice-based elements of concepts of power proposed by theorists such as Foucault and Bourdieu, while also helping us to recognize the power that things themselves exercise — by virtue of what they *are*, not by virtue of how we symbolize or represent them" (p. 194).

## Gravity as Machinic Mediation

In the onto-cartographical sense, gravity refers to "the way in which one machine influences the movement and becoming of other machines, as well as the interactions possible between machines" (p. 188). One machine bends the space-time movement and becoming of another. Unlike Einstein's gravity, which pertains only to mass and physical trajectory, Bryant's gravity encompasses: physical motion, discursive fields produced by signs, the topography of technologies, chemical fields, and social institutions.

The Brazil nut tree (*Bertholletia excelsa*) provides Bryant's paradigm case (pp. 194–96). The tree requires a complex ecology of media to reproduce: bees of a specific type to pollinate its flowers (which require nearby orchids to attract the bees), and agouti rodents to crack its super-hard seed shells and bury the seeds. Bees, orchids, and agouti each exert gravity on the Brazil nut tree — defining where it can and cannot grow, shaping its movements and becomings. The tree in turn exerts gravity on humans: it draws indigenous peoples into the forest, creating paths of encounter with jaguars and vipers, generating territorial conflicts, contributing $44 million annually to South American economies. The tree functions as what Lacan called a **"quilting point"** — a node that draws other machines together into an assemblage (p. 195).

Gravitational relations can be **symmetrical** or **asymmetrical**. Symmetrical: both machines are dependent on one another for their existence (mutual parasitism or mutualism). Asymmetrical: one is captured in the orbit of the other without the reverse being true. The asymmetrical case is more politically interesting. Bryant analyzes the owner/worker relation as "asymmetrically symmetrical" — both depend on each other, but owners are more dependent on workers than workers on owners, since surplus value is produced by workers, not owners. An ideology-machine (capital) distorts this asymmetry, making the more powerful machine appear indispensable (pp. 196–97).

## The Six Object-Types

Depending on how a machine mediates or is mediated, it occupies one of **six positions** within an assemblage. These are relational, not intrinsic — a machine can pass from one type to another as relations shift, and the same machine can be different types in different assemblages (pp. 197–98).

### 1. Dark Objects (pp. 198–202)
Objects so thoroughly withdrawn that they produce no local manifestations and exercise no gravitational effects on other machines in a given assemblage. An absolute dark object would manifest to no entity anywhere; relative dark objects are dark for some assemblages while active in others. Examples: ultraviolet light (invisible to humans), neutrinos (non-interacting with most matter), the Dead Sea Scrolls (hidden for centuries, dark to religious assemblages until discovered). Dark objects haunt every assemblage as its unactualized potential — "every machine harbors within it the capacity to locally manifest itself in different ways under different sets of worldly, ecological, or environmental conditions" (p. 201). The dark-object concept also licenses the *clinamen* — Lucretius's swerve — as a reminder that machines may possess powers that no current mapping registers.

### 2. Bright Objects (pp. 202–205)
A machine that **gravitationally overcodes** the local manifestations, movements, and becomings of other machines in an assemblage. The Sun is Bryant's paradigm: it organizes the orbit of all planets, governs atmospheric and biological processes, and is the ultimate source of the energy embedded in fossil fuels. "Within the frame of reference of the Earth, all politics is ultimately, in one way or another, solar politics" (p. 202). Rice is a bright object for agrarian communities, organizing labor rhythms, body postures, feast and famine cycles, social relations. Coal-company scrip is a perfect micro-example: companies issued currency redeemable only at company stores, making workers entirely dependent on the company for food, clothing, and shelter, trapping them as satellites in the company's orbit (p. 204). **Oil is perhaps the brightest object of contemporary life**: "There is nothing on the planet that is not somehow caught in its gravitational pull... In one way or another, all life on the planet is currently structured by paths created by oil" (p. 204). Bright objects capture other machines as *satellites*. The field of choices is pre-delineated by the bright object — satellites choose among options the bright object makes available, not among all conceivable options.

### 3. Satellites (p. 203)
Machines captured in the orbit of bright objects. Their local manifestations, movements, and becomings are substantially organized by the bright object. The relationship between young children and parents is the satellite–bright-object relationship: parents don't determine what the child becomes, but they substantially organize the paths along which the child moves and develops. The child navigates a topography of parental desires, regrets, neuroses, and values — "warps and depressions in the world that the child navigates as it develops" (p. 203).

### 4. Dim Objects (pp. 205–207)
Objects that exist in assemblages but exercise very little gravity of their own — "the part of no part," in Rancière's formulation. They appear in the world with little voice. Examples: slaves, the homeless, women prior to suffrage, non-dominant religious groups, illegal immigrants. Dim objects are "the site of politics" (p. 207): Rancière's politics is that moment when dim objects rise to challenge the gravitational configuration of a world. Bryant extends this to non-humans — the 2010 BP oil spill rendered entire ecosystems suddenly visible as dim objects that had been structuring livelihoods without recognition.

### 5. Black Holes (p. 207–208)
Objects whose gravitational bending is so great that nothing can escape. Bryant cautiously uses this metaphor for institutions from which exit is practically impossible: terminal illnesses, overwhelming addictions, certain prisons, perhaps capitalism itself. "As Žižek somewhere says, it is easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism" (p. 208).

### 6. Rogue Objects (pp. 208–210)
Machines unattached to any particular gravitational field that wander in and out of assemblages, reconfiguring gravitational relations between existing entities when they appear. Like rogue planets, they arrive from elsewhere or erupt from within a world and cannot be anticipated from the existing gravitational topology. Examples: the bubonic plague, Hurricane Katrina, the Occupy movement, the Internet. Rogue objects have two essential features: they appear from nowhere (from another world or from unregistered potential within a world), and they *reconfigure* gravitational relations between machines already in the assemblage. Bryant engages Badiou's theory of the event here, accepting the basic structure but refusing Badiou's restriction of events to the human domains of science, love, politics, and art.

## The Political Dimension: Infrastructure Before Ideology

The political stakes of Bryant's framework emerge most sharply in his treatment of bright objects and the critique of discursive political thought. Most political theory concentrates on ideology — beliefs, significations, normative frameworks, discourses. This corresponds, in Bryant's typology, to a concern with *incorporeal* bright objects (signs, laws, norms). Bryant does not deny these exercise gravity. His argument is that the **heavy gravity of material infrastructure** is more determinative, less attended to, and harder to dislodge.

The company-scrip example is the sharpest instance. Scrip is simultaneously an incorporeal machine (a currency-form) and a material machine (a physical token controlling access to food and shelter). The political analysis of scrip that focuses only on "ideology" — on how it constructs workers as dependents — misses that scrip exercises its power primarily by controlling what food you can eat, whether your children are warm, and whether you have any viable exit. The scrip does not need anyone to believe in it ideologically; it works by organizing material flows.

The strategic implication is explicit: **the question is one of building alternatives** (p. 205). When we understand that satellites are trapped not by false consciousness but by the absence of alternative gravitational fields, the political strategy shifts from ideology-critique to infrastructure-construction. You cannot liberate workers from company scrip by convincing them scrip is contingent; you liberate them by creating access to federal currency. The coal company's power weakens not because its ideology is exposed but because alternative economic machines become available.

Onto-cartography thus offers a framework for what might be called "infrastructural politics" — political intervention at the level of which machines are built and which are dismantled, rather than at the level of which beliefs are contested.

## Subjects, Quasi-Objects, and Catalysis (pp. 211–226)

The chapter's third major movement critiques the philosophical tradition's treatment of the subject as a fixed type of being. Bryant argues that "subject" names a **transitory functional role** rather than an intrinsic feature of a being. Drawing on Serres and Latour, he develops the concept of the **quasi-object**: a machine that, through its circulation in an assemblage, draws other machines together and reconfigures their relations.

Serres's soccer ball is Bryant's paradigm. "[T]he ball is played, and the teams place themselves in relation to it, not vice versa. As a quasi object, the ball is the true subject of the game" (Serres and Latour 1995: 108). As the ball moves, players continuously reconfigure their spatial relations and roles. The ball *subjects* them — draws them into particular configurations. When the ball is in the garage after the game, it ceases to be a subject.

Subjects/quasi-objects have five features (p. 225): (1) they define a functional role, not a type of being; (2) anything can function as a subject — human or non-human; (3) the role is transitory; (4) subjects are catalysts assembling machines into relations; (5) they are *dynamic* quilting points that move and continuously reconfigure relations.

This connects to a critique of what Bryant calls the "privilege of the gaze" in the philosophical tradition — from Aristotle's privileging of sight in the *Metaphysics* to Descartes's meditating subject, the tradition has treated knowledge as a matter of passive specular observation rather than active intervention (pp. 212–13). By contrast, a machine-oriented ontology holds that powers are discovered only by *acting* upon machines to trigger their operations — not by looking.

## Events as Catalytic Becomings (pp. 227–232)

The chapter closes with a theory of **events**. A becoming is when a machine gains or loses powers or capacities. An event is a becoming of such scale that it sets nearly all the beings in an assemblage into becoming — sending "ripples of gravity throughout the rest of an assemblage." Events are "like supernovas" (p. 230). The Cambrian explosion is the paradigm, but so are the Industrial Revolution, the printing press, the Internet, climate change. Events unfold processually, not instantaneously; they are "furtive and quiet, winding their way through assemblages until one day we wake up and notice that everything is different" (p. 230). Bryant's concept differs from Badiou's primarily in refusing to restrict events to the human domain and in refusing to grant them inherent ethical or political privilege.
