# Tuan (1991): Language and the Making of Place: A Narrative-Descriptive Approach

**Source**: Yi-Fu Tuan, "Language and the Making of Place: A Narrative-Descriptive Approach," *Annals of the Association of American Geographers* 81(4): 684–696.
**PDF**: `raw_sources/Tuan - 1991 - Language and the Making of Place A Narrative-Desc.pdf`

## Central Argument

Tuan argues that language is not merely a vehicle for describing places that exist independently of it — language is *constitutive* of place. Without speech, humans cannot begin to formulate ideas about place, discuss them, or translate them into action. Words have the power to render objects "formerly invisible" visible, to direct attention, to organize insignificant entities into significant composite wholes, and — in the most decisive cases — to call places into being outright. The Hebraic-Christian frame is foundational here: "God said let there be light, and there was light." Speech is "a component of the total force that transforms nature into a human place" (p. 685).

This argument extends from pre-literate hunter-gatherers to sophisticated literary cultures; from the naming of topographical features by pioneers to the inscription of colophons on Chinese scrolls. In every case, the mechanism is the same: words do not label a pre-existing place — they create the perceptual presence that makes a place real.

## Three Approaches to Linguistic Place-Construction

Tuan surveys the field and identifies three methodological stances:

1. **Linguistic/grammatical** — focuses on what the structure of language (grammar, metaphor) reveals about how a culture organizes spatial objects. Starting point: the language system itself.
2. **Sociolinguistic** — focuses on the use and effectiveness of speech in particular social contexts; linked to European philosophers and critical theorists (Foucault, Olsson) concerned with language, epistemology, and power.
3. **Narrative-descriptive** (Tuan's chosen approach) — draws on the first two, but absorbs them into its story line without pausing for theoretical overviews or analytical detail. Theories "hover supportively in the background while the complex phenomena themselves occupy the front stage." It is the approach of cultural geographers, historians, and anthropologists; its merit is being more inclusive and faithful to the complexities of actual experience, even at the cost of being less analytically penetrating.

## Major Sections

### Words, Myths, and Song
Hunter-gatherers live in a "deeply humanized world" not because they have materially transformed it but because they have named it. Insiders see "homeplace" where outsiders see nature. Myths connect observable landscape features (a tree, a rock) to the human socioeconomic order; storytelling "converts mere objects 'out there' into real presences." The extreme case: Australian Aboriginal songlines, where Dreamtime ancestors literally sang the world into being. Place is wherever an ancestor stopped to act, not a resource locality.

### Explorers and Pioneers
Place emerged for European settlers "with the first swing of the pioneer's ax" — but before that, with the first ritual act of possession (Columbus, banners unfurled at Guanahani). Explorers used naming to convert undifferentiated space into usable territory; names gave features a "certain character," making them navigable landmarks. Paul Carter's *The Road to Botany Bay* is central: naming is the act by which exploration becomes directed rather than merely random movement. "Mount Prospect" becomes a virtual place before the settler can stand on it.

### Names and Naming
"Naming is power — the creative power to call something into being, to render the invisible visible, to impart a certain character to things" (p. 688). Key cases: the "Mississippi River" (Algonquian origin, adopted from French explorers) whose name *created* the vast hydrological system as a unified reality; "Asia" as a European conjuration that gradually became a permanent reality even for Asians; Adam's naming of the animals in Genesis as the prototype of all taxonomic power.

### Maintenance and Enhancement of Place — Oral and Informal
Places decay if not retold. "Mount Misery" will fade from consciousness if the name is not passed on, if stories are not "continuingly elaborated." The Wind in the Willows dialogue between Rat and Mole is Tuan's key illustration: Rat verbally recreates Mole's house for him, transforming it from a source of shame to a source of pride — "the jolliest place I ever was in." Warm conversation brightens a room; malicious speech casts a sinister light. Language is the maintenance infrastructure of place.

### Maintenance and Enhancement — Literary Works
In literate societies, written texts supplant storytelling as the primary place-maintenance technology. Updike's description of London (quoted at length, pp. 690–691) makes the city more vivid to some readers than the actual physical buildings. "221B Baker Street is more vividly present to some Londoners than are the apartment homes of their maiden aunts" (p. 690). Literary works sustain place-meaning beyond the visit and beyond the lifespan of any individual.

### Chinese Garden and Landscape
Chinese art is Tuan's most extreme case of language completing the physical. Words (short poems, inscriptions, colophons on scrolls) are an integral component of landscape art — not labels for what is already present, but what *calls the objects into being*. "The Chinese connoisseurs have not actually said that words call objects into being, wake up rocks and flowers, imbuing them with life and meaning, and yet something of that belief is there" (p. 691). Without the written word, the garden is visually incomplete; the visual alone does not rise to the full potential of the scene.

### Geographers and the Making of Place
Geographers are themselves place-makers through their words. The explorer-geographer's naming of a topographic feature is the strongest case. But academic geographers also create perceptual presence: Abler et al.'s monograph on the Twin Cities enhanced the visibility of that place; Harvey's account of Sacré-Coeur gave it a sombre, contested character it did not have for casual tourists. The geographer's effect is "perceptual" — words draw attention to things, making aspects of reality hitherto invisible become visible.

### Making and Unmaking — The Moral Dimension
If language builds, it also destroys. It takes little skill to defame or burn down; it takes "full documentation and a high degree of eloquence to elevate a person's reputation." The creation of "Asia" as a name may be a poor place — "a monster." Language is "never morally neutral." Tuan ends by arguing that geographers, as world-makers through words, are under obligation to "speak well" — for speaking and building are "part of the same uniquely human, world-making process" (p. 694).

5. **On the moral dimension of world-making** (p. 694): "If we are under obligation to build well, we are also under obligation to speak well, for the two are part of the same uniquely human, world-making process."

## Relation to *Space and Place* (1977)

*Space and Place* established the phenomenological distinction (space = formless/disorienting; place = bounded/meaningful). This 1991 article extends that framework by specifying *mechanism*: it is language — speech, naming, narrative — that does the work of converting space into place. The earlier book was largely experiential (how places *feel*); this article is more active (how places are *made*). Together they form a complete account: place is phenomenologically constituted *and* linguistically enacted.
