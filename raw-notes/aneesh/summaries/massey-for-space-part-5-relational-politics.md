# Massey, *For Space* (2005) — Part Five: A Relational Politics of the Spatial

Source: Doreen Massey, *For Space*, London: SAGE, 2005, Part Five (chapters 13–15), pp. 148–195. See also [[doreen-massey]].

## Argument of the part

The book's political climax. Part Five argues that the recognition of space as **the sphere of multiplicity** (Parts 2–4) is also a recognition that politics is **constitutively spatial**. Three theses:

1. **Place is the event of throwntogetherness** (Ch. 13): a place is an unchosen meeting of trajectories that *requires* negotiation. The political stakes are immediate and unavoidable. See [[throwntogetherness]], [[the-politics-of-the-event-of-place]].
2. **There are no rules of space and place** (Ch. 14): no universal "topographic" principle (openness is good; closure is bad; the local is authentic) survives political pressure. Each situation must be assessed on its own [[power-geometry]] grounds. See [[no-rules-of-space-and-place]], [[spatial-fetishism]].
3. **Time-spaces are made and contested** (Ch. 15): every place, every workplace, every home is the ongoing production of relational closures and openings. A relational politics of place is both about throwntogetherness *and* about a politics of connectivity beyond the local. See [[making-and-contesting-time-spaces]], [[politics-of-connectivity]].

## Chapter map

### Ch. 13 — Throwntogetherness: the politics of the event of place (pp. 149–162)

Opens with the **Hamburg "oldest immigrant" boulder** vignette (see [[hamburgs-oldest-immigrant]]). A glacial erratic discovered in 1999 in the Elbe is taken up by Ulla Neumann, Hamburg's commissioner for immigrant affairs, and adopted as the city's emblem on the eve of the 2000 reform of German citizenship law. The poster (figure 13.1) shows the gateway-rock and asks: how long must you have been here to be local?

Massey reads the poster as the **political artefact** corresponding to her ontology. A place is a [[dynamic-simultaneity]] of trajectories — some human, some nonhuman, some old, some recent — and the political question is what we make of the negotiation. "Place as an ever-shifting constellation of trajectories poses the question of our [[throwntogetherness]]" (Massey 2005: 151).

The chapter then extends through public space (the politics of pigeons in Trafalgar Square, the privatisation of urban squares, the necessity of "negotiated exclusion" rather than empty-pluralism romanticism), the London-as-world-city case (the City's dominance produces poverty and exclusion across the rest of the city; "London is successful **and partly as a result** there are great areas of poverty"), and ends with a reading of Los Angeles and Amazonia as human-nonhuman negotiations of place. See [[the-politics-of-the-event-of-place]].

### Ch. 14 — There are no rules of space and place (pp. 163–176)

The political-philosophical thesis of the part. Massey moves through three extended examples to demonstrate that **no general spatial principle** (openness is virtuous; closure is reactionary; the local is authentic; the global is liberating) yields the right political answer in every case:

- **The Deni of Amazonia / Greenpeace boundary-demarcation campaign**: the Deni want a boundary; "local" closure here is anti-colonial and progressive.
- **Hamburg / EU immigration**: the European left wants openness; "local" closure here is reactionary.
- **Isle of Dogs (London) / BNP electoral victory in 1993**: a working-class area drowning under the City's overflow; here "local" voice is both racist and a real-grievance speaking; both are present, both are political.
- **José Bové / Confederation paysanne / McDonald's-Millau**: against US hormone-beef but not anti-American; pro-local but not nationalist; an attempt to articulate a non-fetishised localism.
- **Derrida on hospitality**: the unconditional Law of hospitality and the conditional laws of right-to-hospitality must be held in tension; neither alone is the answer.

Massey's conclusion: "Spatial rules (topographic categories such as openness, closure, claims for an authenticity of place) are inadequate grounds for either struggle. Once again, there can be no such *a priori* politics." (167) See [[no-rules-of-space-and-place]], [[spatial-fetishism]].

### Ch. 15 — Making and contesting time-spaces (pp. 177–195)

The closing constructive chapter. Massey's empirical case study of high-tech laboratories versus their researchers' homes (in the Cambridge area) demonstrates that **every** time-space is a relational production with terms of openness and closure that are continuously negotiated. The lab is "open" to global research connections but closed to children, domesticity, and non-conforming uses. The home is "closed" to the world but porous to his work and to consumer flows. Neither is simply open or closed; each is **selectively** so, and the selection is political.

The chapter then extends to the politics of **connectivity** — local struggles linked through "constructed equivalences" (Laclau & Mouffe) rather than through accumulation of bounded particularities. Local struggles are themselves **relational achievements**, not internally-given identities. The Confederation paysanne's network of small farmers across Mali, Brazil, and France is Massey's example. See [[making-and-contesting-time-spaces]], [[politics-of-connectivity]].

## Compiled positive thesis

- **Politics is constitutively spatial.** It is not the politics-of-space; it is that politics happens *because* space brings trajectories into unchosen co-presence.
- **Every place is an event** requiring negotiation; the negotiation is the politics.
- **No spatial principle is universally valid.** Openness, closure, the local, the global — none can be hailed in advance as the right move.
- **Specificity is irreducible.** "We come to each place with the necessity, the responsibility, to examine anew and to invent." (169)
- **Relationality extends across scales.** Local struggles are global; global struggles are local; the "constructed equivalence" is the political technology that links them.

## Cross-source resonances

- **Bryant's machinic politics** — the machinic ontology of *Onto-Cartography* shares the anti-essentialism and the "every place is constituted by what touches it" claim. Bryant's "regimes of attraction" closely parallel Massey's time-space negotiations.
- **The wiki's `the-architecture-of-resistance` / `the-shelter-as-epistemic-engine` / `nakatomi-space` cluster** — these all share Massey's claim that built space is political because it manages the terms of throwntogetherness.
- **Berlin's pluralism** — there are many incompatible goods, and politics is the negotiation; no single principle yields the answer. Massey's "no rules of space and place" is the spatial corollary.
- **Mouffe's agonistic democracy** — the explicit philosophical source for Massey's account of place as the site of constitutive antagonism.
- **Latour on the parliament of things** — Massey's insistence that human-nonhuman negotiation is part of the politics of place echoes Latour.

## Contradictions / Conflicting Data

Massey explicitly criticises:
- **Heidegger** on place as too rooted, too unopen to external relations (183).
- **Hardt & Negri** (*Empire*, 2001) on "smooth space" — they fall into the same bipolarity (smooth/striated) they try to escape, and end up romanticising openness in a way that mirrors the opposite romanticisation of place.
- **Deleuze & Guattari** on the desert/nomad imagery — Caren Kaplan's critique (1996) that the "spaces of alterity" are Western projections that fail to recognise their own subject position; Massey endorses (172–173).
- **Carter et al. 1993** and the broader tradition that says "place is space to which meaning has been ascribed" — for Massey this perpetuates the abstract-space/concrete-place binary she rejects.
