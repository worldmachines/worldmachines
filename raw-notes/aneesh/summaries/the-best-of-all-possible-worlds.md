# The Best of All Possible Worlds: On Gambas, Gardening and Panglossian Optimism

**Source:** Florian Lohse, *Notefields* (Substack), January 20, 2026. https://notefields.substack.com/p/the-best-of-all-possible-worlds

## Central Argument

The essay is a contribution to [[venkatesh-rao]]'s 2026 Contraptions Book Club reading of *Candide*. It uses three nested scenes — a dinner argument, a refugee chef's story, a nocturnal garden encounter — to dramatize the tension between:
- **Panglossian optimism**: the view that suffering at any scale is acceptable as a "stepping stone" on humanity's trajectory toward its destiny
- **Garden ethics**: the view that meaningful work requires illegible, emplaced, local care — untransferable, un-aggregatable, not legible to machines

The essay does not resolve this tension so much as let both sides speak and then escape through a literal thicket.

## Three Scenes

### I: The Portuguese Dinner
"Horto" (a colleague from Porto) argues that Elon Musk, Trumpism, child labor in chip supply chains, and authoritarianism are all "slightly darker patches on a poster from a Science Fiction movie" — necessary turbulence on humanity's path to the stars. The argument is the Panglossian paradigm: zoom out enough and any local suffering becomes a stepping stone. Lohse is frustrated but finds no clean counter-argument before the chef arrives.

Horto's worldview references:
- Gould & Lewontin's "Spandrels of San Marco and the Panglossian Paradigm" (1979): the critique of adaptationist/functionalist teleology in evolutionary biology — the same logical structure as "everything happens for a reason" applied to history
- Moore's Law as teleological spine: "we are approximately doubling our capacities every two years, that means any kind of remaining local suffering is equally halved every two years"

### II: José's Story
The chef José Antunes's life is a sequence of brutal accidents that produced good outcomes: caught in the Angolan civil war (1974), he escaped by chewing through barbed wire; a Benedictine monk's random bible-throw in a refugee crowd put him on a freighter to Brazil; he inherited a restaurant he couldn't sell due to a sulphur well under the basement. The story is an ironic anti-Panglossian parable: José's life has the *shape* of Panglossian fortune (disaster → next chapter → eventual stability) without the teleological grace. Things happened, not because they were necessary for a larger plan, but through contingency, luck, and violence.

José's grandmother's aphorism: "The complications of life are many, but greater is the power of constant misunderstandings."

### III: The Thicket Garden
After missing the last train, Lohse rides a scooter, hits a root, and lands in an urban thicket where a man lives who has tended a small secret garden: walnut tree, a converted puddle-pond, frogs, fish, cucumbers, carrots. The Gardener was previously a professional "mover of frameworks and ideas" — a legible, viral, algorithmic thinker:

> "The more people I reached, the thinner my work became. By the end I couldn't recognize my own thoughts when they came back to me. They'd been processed through so many other minds and machines, averaged out into something smooth and easy to pass along."

In the thicket, nothing "moves like that." The walnuts fall and stay. The frogs came because the pond was right. The place cannot be explained to someone not standing in it — and that is "exactly what keeps it alive."

The Gardener's closing: "You would have to find your own thicket first."

## Key Concepts

**The Panglossian Paradigm** (via Gould & Lewontin): The tendency to explain every feature of a system as optimally designed for its function; in history, the tendency to retroactively justify suffering as necessary progress. Named for Dr. Pangloss in Voltaire's *Candide*.

**The Thicket**: Lohse's term (explicitly crediting "Aneesh Sathe's ideas on thickets as alternative places for digital expression, in particular his essay 'The Kernel and the Arc'") for a space of emplaced, illegible, non-scalable care. Contrast to the legible, viral, machine-readable work of the attention economy.

**Zoom-Level Ethics**: Horto's argument requires a particular zoom level (species / civilizational) to seem plausible; from inside the barbed wire, it does not apply. The essay stages this as an irreducible tension, not a solvable problem.

## Intellectual Lineage

- **Voltaire's *Candide*** (1759): the source text for the book club reading; Candide's famous conclusion "il faut cultiver notre jardin" as the original endorsement of local garden ethics over speculative optimism
- **Gould & Lewontin, "The Spandrels of San Marco"** (1979): the Panglossian Paradigm as a critique of adaptationist just-so stories — structurally parallel to Horto's techno-optimism
- **Aneesh Sathe, "The Kernel and the Arc"**: thickets as spaces of alternative digital expression (directly cited)
- **Venkatesh Rao, 2026 Contraptions Book Club**: the institutional context

## What's Missing

- The essay doesn't directly engage *Candide*'s text — it uses the occasion but treats Voltaire's argument as background
- Horto's position is never quite refuted; the thicket ending sidesteps rather than answers it
- The connection between Panglossian optimism and specific power structures (who benefits from zoom-out reasoning?) is touched but not developed
