# Tuan — *Space and Place*, Chapters 4–6 (1977/2002)

Source: Yi-Fu Tuan, *Space and Place: The Perspective of Experience* (Minneapolis: University of Minnesota Press, 1977; repr. 2002), pp. 34–84.

See also: [[tuan-2002-space-and-place-ch1-3]] for Chapters 1–3.

---

## Chapter 4: Body, Personal Relations, and Spatial Values (pp. 34–50)

Tuan's central argument here is that **spatial values are not discovered but projected** — the human body, with its asymmetries and postures, is the original measuring rod and meaning-source of all organized space. The chapter works outward from posture to cosmology.

**Upright vs. prone.** When a six-month-old infant sits up, its horizon widens and a new social orientation begins. The standing posture is "assertive, solemn, and aloof"; the prone is submissive. The word *stand* is root for status, stature, statute, estate, institute — all implying achievement and order (p. 37). Every morning human beings re-enact this postural triumph against gravity.

**The vertical axis.** "High" and "low" are strongly charged words in most languages. Superior derives from a Latin word for height; the Sanskrit *brahman* from a term meaning height. Social status is designated high or low rather than great or small. God dwells in heaven — among Rabbinical literature, in Islamic tradition, and in Christian iconography. Architecture encodes this: important buildings are elevated on platforms; tall monuments command greater esteem; residential elevation correlates with prestige (p. 38). Tuan traces the principle through Peking's urban form, where the ceremonial avenue is read as height ("one always goes *up* to Peking"), Mecca as the earth's highest point, and nationalistic claims about local peaks.

**Front/back axis.** Frontal space is visual, vivid, illuminated, future-oriented; rear space is dark, profane, past. The face commands respect; lesser beings hover in the shadow behind superiors. In China the ruler faces south and receives yang (light, male, life), while his back region is yin (dark, feminine, profane). Tuan extends the principle to rooms, buildings, and cities — the traditional Chinese city clearly separated ceremonial south-front from commercial north-back (p. 41).

**Right/left.** In nearly all cultures the right signifies sacred power, the principle of effective activity, the good; the left is profane, impure, feeble. Cosmologically, "the right represents what is high, the upper-world, the sky; the left is connected with the underworld and the earth" (p. 43). The Chinese exception is revealing: because the ruler faces south, his *left* is east (rising sun, yang, male), overriding the biological right-bias with a cosmological one.

**Body as measure.** "Man is the measure. In a literal sense, the human body is the measure of direction, location, and distance" (p. 44). Folk measures of length — finger, span, cubit, fathom, yard, mile, furlong — are bodily. Spatial prepositions are anthropocentric: Merleau-Ponty's observation that "when I say an object is *on* a table, I always mentally put myself either in the table or in the object" (p. 45). Many African and Pacific languages derive spatial prepositions directly from body-part nouns.

**Distance and personal relations.** Distance from self organizes demonstrative pronouns and personal pronouns in close tandem — in Tlingit, four distinct terms grade nearness outward from speaker. The chapter closes by noting that "close" and "distant" fuse geographical and interpersonal meanings: intimacy and proximity reinforce each other; social distance may invert geographical distance (the valet lives close but is not close) (p. 50).

> **Key passage**: "Upright and prone: these positions yield two contrary worlds… The move from the supine horizontal to the seated perpendicular is already 'more than a postural triumph. It is a widening horizon, a new social orientation.'" (p. 37, citing Gesell and Amatruda)

> **Key passage**: "'High' and 'low,' the two poles of the vertical axis, are strongly charged words in most languages. Whatever is superior or excellent is elevated, associated with a sense of physical height." (p. 37)

## Chapter 5: Spaciousness and Crowding (pp. 51–66)

The guiding distinction: **ample space is not always experienced as spaciousness; high density does not necessarily mean crowding**. Both feelings depend on conditions that resist simple generalization. Spaciousness is antithetical to crowding, but neither maps cleanly onto geometric measures.

**Spaciousness and freedom.** "Spaciousness is closely associated with the sense of being free. Freedom implies space; it means having the power and enough room in which to act" (p. 52). The capacity to move is foundational — the infant, the prisoner, and the bedridden all lack it. Energetic children and athletes enjoy expansiveness that desk-bound workers can barely imagine. Eric Nesterinko, the hockey player: "I took off my camel-hair coat. I was just in a suit jacket, on my skates. And I flew. Nobody was there. I was as free as a bird... I have a feeling this is the innate desire of man" (p. 52).

**Space as symbol.** "Space lies open; it suggests the future and invites action." On the negative side, openness is also exposure and vulnerability — "bad" etymologically carries the meaning "open." Space has no fixed pattern of established human meaning; "it is like a blank sheet on which meaning may be imposed. Enclosed and humanized space is place" (p. 54). Agoraphobics and claustrophobics represent pathological fixation on each pole. Healthy beings need both: "Human lives are a dialectical movement between shelter and venture, attachment and freedom" (p. 54).

**Cultural encoding of open space.** Americans learned to read the open plains of the West as opportunity and freedom; Russian peasants experienced the same type of landscape as oppressive vastness that drained the will. Gorky: "As far as the eye can see stretches an endless plain, and in the midst of it stands an insignificant wretched little man, cast away upon this dreary earth to labor like a galley slave" (p. 56). The forest too can signify either boundless space (one lost in it) or claustrophobic clutter. Spaciousness is as much a cultural appraisal as a geometric fact.

**Crowding as social, not geometric.** A shy pianist practicing in the corner of a large room: one observer enters, and the pianist immediately feels spatial constraint (p. 59). Inanimate objects seldom produce this effect; "people crowd us; people rather than things are likely to restrict our freedom and deprive us of space." Crowding is the awareness that one is observed and that one's behavior is constrained by unwanted social demand. A rich man surrounded by servants is not crowded: their low status makes them "invisible — part of the woodwork." Crowding is a function of social relation, not density.

**The Bushman counter-case.** !Kung Bushmen in the Kalahari live in camps where average space per person is only 188 square feet — far below American Public Health Association standards. Yet they show no symptoms of biological stress. Space is arranged to maximize contact, not minimize it; proximity is desired (p. 62). Working-class families in England similarly tolerate high residential density and even prefer the constant human background noise. The cost of successful adaptation to crowding, Tuan notes, is the atrophying of deep inwardness: "Privacy and solitude are necessary for sustained reflection and a hard look at self" (p. 65).

**Space as resource and spiritual attribute.** In the Hebraic tradition, words for spaciousness mean both physical size and psychological/spiritual qualities — escape from danger, freedom from constraint. "He brought me forth into a broad place; he delivers me, because he delighted in me" (Psalm 18:19). On the spiritual plane, space connotes deliverance and salvation (p. 58).

> **Key passage**: "Spaciousness is closely associated with the sense of being free… An immobile person will have difficulty mastering even primitive ideas of abstract space, for such ideas develop out of movement — out of the direct experiencing of space through movement." (p. 52)

> **Key passage**: "Space lies open; it suggests the future and invites action… Enclosed and humanized space is place. Compared to space, place is a calm center of established values." (p. 54)

## Chapter 6: Spatial Ability, Knowledge, and Place (pp. 67–84)

The chapter traces the developmental and cultural arc from **bodily spatial skill** (motor performance, kinesthesia) through **spatial knowledge** (conscious, transferable, articulable) to the **cognitive map** (a schematic representation of spatial relations that exceeds any individual's direct experience). The movement from skill to knowledge is not linear: skill does not require knowledge, knowledge can impede skill, and the two are mutually enhancing when well integrated.

**Skill without knowledge.** A professional typist's fingers fly over the keyboard, yet she cannot visualize where each key is. Bicycle riding requires balance and proprioception, but a physicist's diagram of the forces involved is not required. "Self-conscious knowledge can even stand in the way of perfecting a skill" (p. 68). Spatial ability becomes spatial knowledge only when movements can be envisaged, analyzed, and communicated.

**Route vs. survey knowing.** Brown's maze experiments are Tuan's central empirical case. Subjects learn the maze as a sequence of movements — a succession of kinesthetic patterns tied to recognized landmarks (a rough spot, a tilting board, a long straight stretch). They acquire route knowledge. But when asked to reproduce the maze in a drawing, they fail badly: "I have to be *there* before I can tell you" (p. 72). The drawn map departs so far from the actual course that it cannot be used as a map (Fig. 6). Survey knowledge — knowing where places are in relation to each other from an imagined vantage — is a different, more abstract cognitive achievement. Most people operating in familiar environments rely on route knowledge supplemented by a sketchy and imprecise survey image.

**When space becomes place.** "When space feels thoroughly familiar to us, it has become place" (p. 73). The transition happens through accumulated kinesthetic experience and the emotional attachment to recognized landmarks. Small children and people with cognitive limitations can identify individual shops and residences but cannot integrate large space into a spatial scheme; they feel disoriented whenever they step outside the small area of habitual contact (p. 74).

**Cognitive maps and their cultural determinants.** Tuan surveys variation across cultures in spatial skill and the conditions that produce sophisticated geographical knowledge. The contrast between the Temne of Sierra Leone and Eskimos of Arctic Canada is his main case (following John Berry). Eskimos possess a spatial-geometric vocabulary comparable to Western technical vocabulary and excel at map-making, mechanical spatial reasoning, and navigation in an environment stripped of landmarks. The Temne have meager spatial-geometric vocabulary but a rich visual environment. The key variable is not environment alone but the **social-ecological demand for spatial knowledge**: Eskimos must navigate vast, featureless, hostile terrain alone; Temne farmers rarely need to orient in unfamiliar space because their cohesive group and benign local environment constitute their "place" (pp. 78–79).

**Pacific Islander navigation as synthesis.** Puluwatan navigators of Micronesia represent the richest integration of route and survey knowledge. They learn routes as embodied kinesthetic patterns (the feel of wave swells, the color of water over reefs) but also command an abstract conceptual framework (the *etak* system, which organizes star bearings relative to reference islands across hundreds of miles of open ocean). Thomas Gladwin: "The Puluwatans picture themselves and their island in their part of the ocean much as we might locate ourselves upon a road map" (p. 82). The ocean is not empty, threatening space but a network of known routes and places.

> **Key passage**: "When space feels thoroughly familiar to us, it has become place. Kinesthetic and perceptual experience as well as the ability to form concepts are required for the change if the space is large." (p. 73)

> **Key passage**: "I have to be *there* before I can tell you." — maze subject, on the limits of route knowledge abstracted from performance (p. 72)
