# Books Are Dwellings

**Source**: Aneesh Sathe, *Books Are Dwellings* (2025-10-05) — https://aneeshsathe.com/books-are-dwellings/
**Author**: [[Aneesh Sathe]]

## Central Argument

"Reading differently" (scrolling, social media, threads) is not reading at all — it is "letting highway billboards flash through my mind." Books are dwellings: the difference between a book and a post is not length but devotion. A post demands little effort; a book demands devotion, and that difference shows in what both reader and writer bring. A book club amplifies this: each book becomes a room you walk through with others, and discussion creates doorways between rooms.

## Key Claims

1. **The "reading differently" thesis is false** — Sathe explicitly reverses his prior position. Social media, blogs, and chats are not reading; they are billboard-scanning.
2. **The distinction is effort and devotion, not length** — "A post demands little effort; a book demands devotion." This is the load-bearing claim.
3. **Books are places** — the spatial/architectural metaphor: books as dwellings, rooms, places to inhabit rather than consume.
4. **Reading attentiveness is selective** — "those who read books are those that really want to read." Books filter for investment.
5. **Book club as connective architecture** — "each book becomes a room I walk through with others — and when we meet to talk, it feels like discovering a doorway between those rooms."
6. **The Contraptions book club** as a concrete recovery device (echoed in [[Better Company Than Caesar]]).

## Spatial Metaphors

The essay's central innovation is architectural: books as *dwellings*, rooms, places. This inverts the usual temporal metaphor (reading *takes* time) with a spatial one (reading *creates* space). This connects to:
- Bachelard's *Poetics of Space* (unmentioned but resonant)
- The "room" metaphor for intellectual community
- The [[Cloister Web]] concept (semi-private creative rooms)

## What's Missing

- No engagement with audiobooks — do they qualify as "dwelling"?
- The "highway billboard" framing condemns all digital reading, but long-form digital essays (like Sathe's own) go untheorized
- No account of *bad* books — can a poorly-written book still be a dwelling?
