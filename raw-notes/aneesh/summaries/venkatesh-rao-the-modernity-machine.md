# The Modernity Machine (Rao, Jan 2025)

**Source**: Contraptions newsletter, January 26, 2025  
**Author**: [[venkatesh-rao]]

## Central Argument

The birth of modernity should be dated to the 13th century, not the 16th. Rao argues for *psychological modernity* — the emergence of a recognizable modern cognitive mode — appearing globally and in parallel between 1200 and 1600. This is distinct from, and precedes, the European *Westernization* narrative that locates modernity in the Renaissance and Enlightenment.

The key reframe: the 16th-century scientific and philosophical revolutions were "decoupled leaps that irrupted into *already modern* societies," not the forces that modernized them in the first place.

## The Westernization vs. Modernization Distinction

Non-Western populations became technologically modern through science and technology acting on a landscape of *regional early modernities* that had already taken shape 1200-1600. Westernization (philosophical, cultural, aesthetic) has been largely cosmetic — restricted to "suits and ties." Science and technology have achieved near-100% penetration. The religious map of the Old World has been largely unchanged since 1200.

> "People I consider as authentic representatives of cultures worldwide are *culturally distinguishable modernists* rather than traditionalists."

## The Modernity Machine as Societal Apparatus

The [[Modernity Machine]] is a civilization-scale contraption assembled from six evolving relationships among four social classes: **monarch, oligarchy, technocracy, workers**. Each relationship underwent a pivotal secular shift 1200-1600.

### The Six Relationships of Modernity

1. **Monarch-Oligarchy**: Magna Carta (1215) as archetype; reining in divine-right monarchy. Venice's hereditary Doge system as parallel early instance.
2. **Monarch-Technocracy**: Philip IV's strike against the Templars (1307). Established that political power can arise from *codified knowledge*, not just land. Also established highly portable technocratic loyalties — setting the pattern for mercenary companies and condottieri.
3. **Monarch-Workers**: Assize of Arms (1252) arming English yeomen with longbows; extends military preparedness to the working class. Battle of Crécy (1346) as landmark event for English modernity's birth.
4. **Oligarchy-Technocracy**: Rise of the clergy's financial influence through indulgences. John Wycliffe (1330-1384) critiques this a century before Gutenberg. The printing press initially *accelerated* religious corruption before enabling reformation.
5. **Oligarchy-Workers**: Black Death as defining moment — suddenly scarce labor gains extraordinary bargaining power, terminal decline of serfdom in Western Europe.
6. **Technocracy-Workers**: Rise of guild-organized workers; Battle of Crécy (1346) as archetype when longbow infantry defeated knight-dominated cavalry. This relationship "grew to be the most important one."

## The Santa Claus Test

An informal heuristic for psychological modernity: can you talk to people from a given era as rough cognitive peers? Rao concludes Venice by the late 13th century passes (psychological modernity), while Constantinople of the same era does not. Applied also to Muhammad bin Tughlaq (1325-1351) in India — a recognizably modern ruler despite "madness."

## Animating the Configuration

The secular trend across all six relationships: *the previously weaker party gained significant and persistent new agency*. Power shifted noisily but steadily downward in the four-layer hierarchy. Reactionary moves (Philip IV vs. Templars) scored temporary reversals but the long-term trajectory held.

## The 2025 Mirror

Rao applies the 1200-1600 European template to the contemporary American situation:
- Trump's DEI strikes ~ Philip IV vs. Templars (temporary reactionary reversal of a longer secular trend)
- "Fall of Woke" ~ corruption-driven collapse of 13th-century technocracy (priestly indulgences → social justice indulgences)
- AI ~ longbow distribution to workers: centuries of accumulated technocratic knowledge gifted to the working class in one fell swoop
- Crypto ~ early printing press for indulgences: the religious shitcoin phase before the real revolution

## What's Missing / What the Author Doesn't Address

- The argument for psychological modernity remains informal; the "Santa Claus test" is a vivid heuristic but not a falsifiable criterion
- The six-relationship framework is asserted, not derived — why exactly these six, and not others?
- The non-European cases (India, China, Ottoman Empire) are gestured at in footnotes rather than given full treatment
- No engagement with James Scott's *Seeing Like a State* or similar work on legibility and state modernization
