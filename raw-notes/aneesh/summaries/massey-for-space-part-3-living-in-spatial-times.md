# Massey, *For Space* (2005) — Part Three: Living in Spatial Times?

Source: Doreen Massey, *For Space*, London: SAGE, 2005, Part Three (chapters 6–10), pp. 62–101. See also [[doreen-massey]].

## Argument of the part

Part Three is the most explicitly political section of the book up to this point. Massey argues that **modernity** and **neoliberal globalisation** share a structural move: both convert coexisting spatial difference into a single temporal sequence — others are not different, they are merely "behind." She names the resulting figure the [[cosmopolitan-time-queue]]. Drawing on [[johannes-fabian]], she argues for **coevalness** — radical contemporaneity — as the political-ontological antidote, and ends with elements for an alternative imagination that refuses to tame the spatial.

## Chapter map

### Ch. 6 — Spatialising the history of modernity (pp. 62–71)

The signature diagnosis. Modernity converts geography into history. Different places are read as different stages: Western Europe is "advanced," other parts of the world "behind." This temporal convening is the engine of progress-narratives (liberal, Marxist, modernisation-theoretic), all of which deny coevalness. The convening also *forecloses temporality itself*: the future is already foretold by the queue.

> "Coexisting heterogeneity is rendered as (reduced to) place in the historical queue." (Massey 2005: 68–69)

The chapter draws heavily on [[johannes-fabian]]'s *Time and the Other* (1983) — anthropology's "denial of coevalness" places its object in a different *time* from the observer's, which "is what needs to be overcome." See [[spatialising-modernity]], [[coevalness]].

The chapter also critiques the "premodern unity of space and place / postmodern space of flows" periodisation (Giddens) using empirical work showing that pre-1500 societies were already interconnected (Wolf 1982; Gupta & Ferguson 1992; Oakes 1993). See [[billiard-ball-geography]].

### Ch. 7 — Instantaneity/depthlessness (pp. 71–78)

The postmodern double-bind: depthless instantaneity celebrated by some (Jameson's "depthlessness" critique, the cyberspace utopians) is in fact a *different* form of spatial closure — a flat present without depth or trajectory. Massey reads this as another taming, not a liberation. See [[instantaneity-depthlessness]].

### Ch. 8 — Aspatial globalisation (pp. 78–89)

The neoliberal globalisation discourse "isn't spatialised": it represents the world as a unified frictionless market (free trade ideology), then **simultaneously** mobilises the opposite imagination (defensible borders, anti-migration rhetoric) when convenient. The doubleness is not a bug but the operating principle:

> "Capital, the rich, the skilled . . . can move easily about the world . . . Meanwhile the poor and the unskilled from the so-called margins of this world are both instructed to open up their borders and welcome the West's invasion in whatever form it comes, and told to stay where they are." (86)

The "globalisation as gravity / inevitability" rhetoric ([[globalisation-as-inevitability-rhetoric]]) is the **temporal convening of space all over again**: places "ahead" produce globalisation; places "behind" must adjust or perish. Resistance is futile because the future is fixed. See [[aspatial-globalisation]].

### Ch. 9 — (Contrary to popular opinion) Space cannot be annihilated by time (pp. 89–97)

Critique of the "time has annihilated space" trope (Marx, Harvey, cyberspace utopians). Space is more than distance; speed-up reduces distance but does not abolish multiplicity, and **multiplicity is the heart of space**. The chapter also develops the political point that "happenstance juxtaposition of previously unrelated trajectories" — the unchosen neighbour, the corner-turning encounter with alterity — is one of the productive characteristics of material space, and that gated communities, science parks, and digital tribalism are all attempts to **purify space** of this productive friction. See [[space-cannot-be-annihilated-by-time]], [[happenstance-juxtaposition]].

### Ch. 10 — Elements for alternatives (pp. 97–101)

A short closing chapter assembling Massey's positive proposals:

1. **Space as the sphere of heterogeneity** (positionality as the minimum order of differentiation).
2. **Space as the sphere of relations, negotiations, practices of engagement** — "the dimension which poses the question of the social, and thus of the political."
3. **Space as the sphere of coevalness** — radical contemporaneity, against the [[cosmopolitan-time-queue]].

The chapter introduces (or makes explicit) the term [[power-geometry]] — Massey's signature contribution to political geography — to name the differential power to negotiate flows and mobilities. Globalisation is not the smoothing of space; it is the production of new power-geometries.

## Compiled positive thesis

- The radical contemporaneity of mankind is a project (Fabian 1983: xi).
- Recognising spatiality involves recognising coevalness — the existence of trajectories with at least some autonomy from each other.
- The spatial is the realm of the configuration of potentially dissonant (or concordant) narratives.
- Places are foci of meeting and non-meeting, integral to the generation of novelty — not locations of pre-given coherence.

## Cross-source resonances

- **Rao's "World Machines" / "Modernity Machine" / "Divergence Machine" cluster** — the [[cosmopolitan-time-queue]] is a near-perfect partner to the "history machine" framing of modernity as a sense-making engine that converts spatial coexistence into temporal sequence. See [[cosmopolitan-time-queue-and-world-machines]] for the synthesis.
- **Berlin, *Against the Current*** — Berlin's polemic against monism (one true answer, one historical direction) is the philosophical-political ground for Massey's critique. Berlin would endorse coevalness as a pluralist commitment.
- **Bryant, *Onto-Cartography*** — Bryant's "pluralism of worlds" (no single all-containing world) is the ontological version of Massey's coevalness.
- **The wiki's `the-viscous-frontier`** — Massey's argument that the future is open *only if* space is open (and thus that universal narratives close down politics) is the philosophical ground for the friction-as-political-resource thesis.

## Contradictions / Conflicting Data

Massey explicitly critiques:
- **Giddens 1990** on the supposed "premodern unity of space and place" — empirically wrong (Wolf 1982, Oakes 1993).
- **Marx's "annihilation of space by time"** as a slogan that misreads what is being reduced (time) for what is being expanded (space-as-multiplicity).
- **Bauman 2000** on the "irrelevance of space" — same error.
- **The "margins have arrived at the centre" trope** — geographically inaccurate (most of the margins have *not* arrived; they have been excluded).
