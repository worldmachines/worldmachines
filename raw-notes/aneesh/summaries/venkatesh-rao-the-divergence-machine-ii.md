# The Divergence Machine II: Progress as a Non-Stationary Argument (Rao, Feb 2026)

**Source**: Contraptions newsletter, February 22, 2026  
**Author**: [[venkatesh-rao]]

## Central Argument

Capital-P Progress in the Modernity Machine era was a "Fiat Progress" — uncontroversial, yoked to fiat idealism, underwritten by the state, governed by established moral rubrics. Good times were rewards for good behavior.

The Divergence Machine era (1600-2000) planted seeds for a fundamentally different understanding of Progress: the **[[Argument of Progress]]**. This is Progress understood as a dynamic, pluralist, *non-stationary* discourse — a partially cooperative mutual exploration of novelty on an *expanding* frontier, not a debate with winners and losers.

The two March book-club reads — *The Courtier and the Heretic* (Leibniz vs. Spinoza) and *The Infidel and the Professor* (Hume and Adam Smith) — frame this as an intellectual genealogy: Spinoza → Hume → Smith, with Leibniz as the brilliant reactionary who made the first great error.

## Fiat Progress vs. the Argument of Progress

| Fiat Progress | Argument of Progress |
|---------------|----------------------|
| Emerged in 1200s, institutionalized by 1600 | Seeded 1600-2000, mainstream only now |
| Fixed interpretive tradition (f is received) | New sense-making required (f must be constructed) |
| Debatable in good/bad terms | Not a debate — a mutual exploration |
| Reality scope is constant (x is fixed) | Reality scope expands within lifetimes |
| Rooted in metaphysics | Rooted in history — and fast-moving history |

> "The Argument of Progress is the data, *and* the process of making sense of it, through the discovery or construction of new patterns of thought."

## The y=f(x) Framework

Rao introduces a mathematical mnemonic:
- **x** = the rapidly expanding scope of stuff you have to make sense of — "the world" in "world view"
- **f** = the sense-making process / argument-as-process
- **y** = the resulting understanding of reality / value

In Fiat Progress: x is constant, f is received tradition, y can only be sacred or profane by an existing ontology.

In the Argument of Progress: x expands rapidly (Galileo's Jupiter moons, explorations of the Americas, telescopes, microscopes), requiring construction of *new* f, yielding genuinely new y. Value emerges from a *new process* of making sense of new data, not from moral reasoning from first principles.

## Progress is Not a Game

The Argument of Progress is not zero-sum debate. The mistake of treating it as a game with winners and losers (optimists vs. pessimists) is a reduction to Fiat Progress scaffolding:
- Whig history, Leibnizean optimism, Collison-Cowen Progress Studies, Thielean determinate optimism, a16z-ish American Dynamism — all Fiat Progress narratives in disguise
- "The fundamental divergentist move is to simply accept the dread, and avoid premature commitments, choosing instead to live in ontological doubt while the nature of reality shakes itself out in your mound."

Is it an *infinite* game in the Carse sense? Closer, but not quite — the divergence machine variant is about *continuing the game* rather than winning it, but also about accepting genuine ontological dread rather than converting it into optimism/pessimism.

## Leibniz: The First Great Error

[[gottfried-wilhelm-leibniz]] (1646-1716) was the first to reduce the Argument of Progress to a contest between optimism and pessimism. He was "fundamentally a philosophical reactionary, trying to rescue the Aristotelian philosophy of classical antiquity and the flavors of Christianity that rested on it, from the onslaught of the phenomenology of modernity."

Voltaire's *Candide* (January read) mercilessly parodies Leibniz as Pangloss — and correctly so. Leibniz was also a legitimate genius (calculus, physics, hydraulic engineering, monadic philosophy) but his philosophical project was a brave and futile attempt to protect antiquity from the onslaught that Spinoza had helped unleash.

> "He was a tragic, rather than farcical figure."

## Spinoza: The First Divergentist Move

[[baruch-spinoza]] (1632-1677) was "a ghost haunting the fledgling divergence machine rather than the recently matured modernity machine." He laid the foundations for the separation of religious and secular traditions of thought and institutionalism.

A non-trivial biographical detail: he was a *lens grinder*, participating in the scientific revolution in optics that was expanding the human sensorium to include both the microscopic and the telescopic. He was the more evolved human of the two.

Spinoza made the divergentist move: accepting ontological doubt as a *state of being*, rather than resolving it prematurely into optimism or pessimism. His heresy from the Portuguese Jewish community in Amsterdam was not merely intellectual — Amsterdam was a site of early religious pluralism, and the young print industry there allowed a life's work to become a legacy.

## The Leibniz-Spinoza Encounter as Non-Stationary Argument

The intellectual entanglement between the two is neither a debate nor a dialectic. They were "two important centers in a polycentric narrative with a gradually moving 'average' state." Spinoza was on the "hot" side (history moving in his direction); Leibniz was on the "cold" side. The sum was greater than the parts — but not in a positive-sum game sense. A *non-stationary* argument: not moving on a static metaphysical canvas, but on an *expanding reality canvas*.

> "The evolving argument *invented new ways to talk about new things worth talking about.*"

## Hume and Smith: The Argument Matures

[[david-hume]] (1711-1776) and [[Adam Smith]] (1723-1790) are intellectual descendants of Spinoza; Leibniz (as philosopher) had already faded into irrelevance a generation later, partly because of Voltaire's *Candide*.

- **Hume**: Went much further than most, "setting aside complex theories of causation in favor of near-pure phenomenology." Only willing to treat "the log files of reality" as reality. "He's the original philosopher of the AI age, offering the first of what has now become an endless series of 'bitter lessons' delivered by the Argument of Progress." The Kronecker parallel: "God created the integers, everything else is the work of man."
- **Adam Smith**: Applied the same orientation to economics — the "invisible hand" inaugurates a discipline *about* noisy, messy reality data, with only weak emergent constitutive laws. The opposite of divine design.
- **Unified theory**: "Shit happens, but it's not entirely unpredictable and disorderly. There are laws, but they're just handy, contingent heuristics, not 'reality' itself."

Both continued the fundamentally naturalist and empiricist tendency opened by Spinoza. Darwin completes the arc: by making divergence and variety a *load-bearing feature of how reality itself operated*, Darwin "put an end to the fetish for secular canonicity that marks world views before him."

## Progress and Divergence: The Connection

The Argument of Progress in the Divergence Machine is fundamentally a *plural* phenomenon. Through the 17th and 18th centuries, a parade of mostly European thinkers constructed a pluralist tradition that *constituted* a divergent Argument of Progress.

By the 20th century, this tradition turned global — but it had not gone mainstream even by 2019. Covid + AI made the Argument of Progress a mainstream thing: "It is no longer possible to operate by a Fiat Progress narrative with a straight face."

> "What started to become important around Spinoza's time, was not to *agree* on the nature of reality, but to continue to *participate* in it, without hindering the ability of *others* to participate in it. To *approach* rather than *retreat from* a changing human condition."

## Key Quotes

> "Tolerance and pluralism were born of efforts to make sense of an expanding reality. Morality slowly came to rest not on theology but on the existence of an expanding frontier."
