# What Do Platforms Really Do?

**Source**: [aneeshsathe.com, 2025-07-19](https://aneeshsathe.com/what-do-platforms-really-do/)
**Author**: [[Aneesh Sathe]]
**Key Reference**: [[David S. Landes]], "What Do Bosses Really Do?" (1986), *Journal of Economic History*

## Central Argument

Digital platforms — Substack, YouTube, TikTok, etc. — are not neutral infrastructure or background IT administrators. They are the reincarnation of the historical "boss" figure Landes described: the merchant-entrepreneur who organized production, connected producers to markets, and absorbed (or shifted) the risks of doing so. Platforms execute these same functions through [[Algorithmic Management]] rather than physical overseers, while deploying the rhetoric of creator empowerment to obscure a classic [[Putting-Out System]] logic.

## Key Claims

1. **Platforms as factory owners**: like 18th-century factory owners who simplified craft tasks to hire cheaper labor, platforms disaggregate creative production — paying viral stars well and niche creators little — "purchasing exactly that precise quantity which is necessary for each process."
2. **[[Algorithmic Management]] replaces overseers**: platforms abstract away problems factory owners faced (embezzlement, shirking) through incentive algorithms. They don't need to care whether you produce; they profit only when you do.
3. **[[Putting-Out System]] logic persists**: the merchant-manufacturer shifted capital expenditure onto the worker (plant and equipment); platforms shift creative risk onto the creator. If a creator fails, only the creator loses. The platform participates only in upside.
4. **[[Creator-as-Hero Rhetoric]]**: Substack's "writers are the heroes of culture" framing is identified as economically shrewd obscurantism — it masks the power asymmetry and risk asymmetry of the platform relationship.
5. **Monetization destroys hobby space**: by framing writing as a financial transaction, platforms colonize the [[Mental Space for Hobbies]], distorting incentive structures toward engagement-maximizing rather than meaning-making.

## Intellectual Lineage

- **[[David S. Landes]]** — "What Do Bosses Really Do?" (1986): the essay's foundational frame. Landes argued bosses were essential historical actors, not exploiters to be eliminated.
- **[[Putting-Out System]]** (proto-industrial): merchant-manufacturers who owned materials and controlled distribution, outsourcing production to home workers.
- **[[Guilds]]**: the old-media analogue — controlled production and pricing, now replaced by platforms that impose no supply constraints.
- **[[Substack]]**: named as an example of platform rhetoric (creator-hero framing) at the moment of a "substantial fundraise."

## Key Quotations

> "The platform's rhetoric of the creator-as-hero is a shrewd economic arrangement."

> "While appearing to be mere background IT admins, they are, in fact, the central organizers of production."

> "By casting the creator as the hero, they obscure their own power and shift the immense risks of creative work onto the individual."

## What's Missing

- No engagement with platform labor organizing (e.g., YouTube union discussions, Twitch streamer walkouts)
- The essay focuses on content platforms; doesn't address gig-labor platforms (Uber, Fiverr) where the analysis would be sharper
- No treatment of how [[Network Effects]] entrench platform power beyond the boss/worker framing
- Doesn't address whether worker-owned alternatives (co-ops, fediverse) could dissolve the asymmetry
