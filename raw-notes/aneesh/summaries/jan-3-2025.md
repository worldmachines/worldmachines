## Sections

### 1. Blogs as the Last Good Internet

The best blogs should provoke a sense of totality — "I don't even know where to look, or where to begin." This was the author's reaction to Ribbonfarm (~15 years ago). The claim: as AI-generated content degrades the open web, personal blogs become the refuge for genuine human intellectual exchange. The emphasis on *high-ownership personal media* as distinct from platform-mediated content.

### 2. How to Run a Link Blog Well

[[Simon Willison]]'s link blogging model as the template:
- Always name the creator (useful for future search)
- Quote a key paragraph to help readers decide
- Add original commentary so that reading *both* post and source is better than either alone

> "My goal with any link blog post is that if you read both my post and the source material you'll have an enhanced experience over if you read just the source material itself."

The author notes the meta-irony of posting this as a link post.

### 3. AI Widens the Gap It Was Supposed to Close

Paper by **Eric So et al.** on AI in financial decision-making. Key finding: AI summaries improve decision quality *across the board*, but only when summary complexity is matched to reader sophistication. The problem:

- **Advanced summary**: "operating margins declined due to higher input costs caused by supply chain disruptions"
- **Simplified summary**: "reduction in profits"

The gap in signal leads to a gap in decision quality. Conclusion from the paper: *"AI widens the knowledge-driven disparities between sophisticated and unsophisticated participants."*

The author frames this through [[Jargon as Compression]]: experts who can precisely scope a question extract more precise output. The model can't compensate for what the user doesn't know to ask.

### 4. Probability as Useful Fiction

[[David Spiegelhalter]] in *Nature*: numerical probability is not an objective feature of the world — it's a construction from judgment and assumption. Exceptions: statistical mechanics, Mendelian genetics. Everywhere else — weather, economics, risk — probability expresses personal or collective uncertainty.

The escape hatch: **de Finetti's 1931 exchangeability theorem**. If you judge a sequence of events as *exchangeable* (order of observations doesn't affect beliefs about future ones), this is mathematically equivalent to acting as if driven by objective chance. You can operate *as if* probabilities are real without needing them to be ontologically real.

> "Probability is a fiction, but the right fiction to operate with."
