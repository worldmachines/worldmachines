## Chapter 7 — Mythical Space and Place (pp. 85–100)

Tuan opens by distinguishing two kinds of mythical space. The first is **diffuse mythical space**: the hazy, symbolically charged field that surrounds any known empirical world. Every culture has it — the region beyond immediate experience that is known only "indirectly through symbolic means" (p. 87). Facts require contexts, and "contexts invariably grow fuzzy and mythical around the edges" (p. 88). This ambient mythic field is not error to be corrected; it is structurally necessary to the coherence of practical knowledge.

The second kind is **oriented cosmological space**: a more deliberately articulated world-view in which man stands at the center of a cosmic frame oriented to the cardinal points. This is the *homo microcosmus* in spatial form — the human body writ large as cosmos, or the cosmos organized around the human center. Tuan identifies two overlapping schemata that cultures use to relate self to universe: (a) the body as image of the cosmos (microcosmic correspondence — earth as bones, water as blood, clouds as breath); and (b) man at the center of a cardinally-oriented spatial frame. Both schemata respond to the same underlying need: to feel at home in the universe, to replace alien indifference with resonance and address.

Oriented mythical space shares several structural features across cultures. It is **anthropocentric**: it places human beings at the center. It **imputes personality to space**, thus transforming abstract space into valued place. It is "almost infinitely divisible" — from the known world to the single shelter, each smaller unit can be an image of the cosmos (p. 91). The Saulteaux Indians of Manitoba orient themselves to four winds understood as cardinal places — "directions are primarily places — 'homes' — rather than courses of movement in space" (p. 92). The Chinese cosmos, exemplified by a Han dynasty roof tile, encapsulates the whole cosmic order: four animals at four directions, human desire expressed at center in characters for "long life" and "happiness" (p. 93).

Tuan surveys the Pueblo Indian world view (the quadripartite cardinal grid with the kiva as microcosm), the Chinese cosmological order (resonance rather than mechanical causation — change one phenomenon and all associated ones follow "like a multiple echo," p. 97), and European medieval cosmology (Isidore of Seville's quaternary divisions, the *orbis terrarum* maps with Jerusalem at center).

The chapter closes with a crucial claim: mythical space is both an intellectual construct and "a response of feeling and imagination to fundamental human needs" (p. 99). It "ignores the logic of exclusion and contradiction" — a cosmos can have many centers — and can hold the part as an image of the whole without requiring the part to be the whole in miniature. The final bridge to Chapter 8: "Architectural space — a house, a temple, or a city — is a microcosm possessing a lucidity that natural features lack. Architecture continues the line of human effort to heighten awareness by creating a tangible world that articulates experiences, those deeply felt as well as those that can be verbalized, individual as well as collective" (p. 100).

**Key quotations:**
> "Facts require contexts in order to have meaning, and contexts invariably grow fuzzy and mythical around the edges." (p. 88)

> "Oriented mythical space... It imputes personality to space, thus transforming space in effect into place." (p. 91)

> "Directions are primarily places — 'homes' — rather than courses of movement in space. The idea of space is subordinated to the idea of the location of significant places." (p. 92)

> "Mythical space is an intellectual construct. It can be very elaborate. Mythical space is also a response of feeling and imagination to fundamental human needs." (p. 99)

## Chapter 8 — Architectural Space and Awareness (pp. 101–117)

Tuan's central thesis is that **architecture teaches**. This is not a metaphor. The built environment functions like language: "Without words feeling reaches a momentary peak and quickly dissipates.... The built environment, like language, has the power to define and refine sensibility. It can sharpen and enlarge consciousness. Without architecture feelings about space must remain diffuse and fleeting" (p. 107).

The chapter begins by contrasting primitive builder and master architect not to privilege the latter but to illuminate the spectrum of awareness that construction requires. Both must decide where to build, what materials to use, what form to impose. But the modern architect's consciousness is "almost painfully stretched to accommodate all the possible forms that occur to him" (p. 103). Nonliterate and peasant societies show a paradox: their shelters change little over time, yet there may be **greater awareness of built form** among their builders, because everyone builds their own house, construction is repeated throughout a life, and building is a ceremonial-religious act — "To build is a religious act, the establishment of a world in the midst of primeval disorder" (p. 104).

Tuan then catalogs three ways architecture educates awareness:

1. **Inside/outside articulation**: Architecture heightens the pre-given bodily sense of interior versus exterior, private versus public. "Architectural space — even a simple hut surrounded by cleared ground — can define such sensations and render them vivid" (p. 102). The evolution from round semisubterranean hut to rectangular courtyard house traces the progressive sharpening of this contrast.

2. **Verticality and scale**: Monumental architecture "enlarges mankind's consciousness of space, heightened people's awareness of the vertical and the horizontal, of mass and volume" (p. 108). The grain elevator on the Nebraska plain is for Wright Morris not vanity but ontology: "It's a problem of being. Of knowing you are there" (p. 110).

3. **The illuminated interior as cosmos**: The Pantheon's oculus — single light source flooding a hemispherical drum — attains "sublime simplicity" and allows a worshipper to apprehend spatial vastness by entering it rather than contemplating it from without. Tuan compares the Mongolian yurt (smoke hole as "eye of heaven") with the Pantheon: both use the dome-and-oculus structure to image the sky, yet the yurt is intimate and smoke-stained while the Pantheon is monumental (pp. 110–111).

The Nias village (Indonesia) as cosmic diagram, the Atoni house of Indonesian Timor as a map of cosmic and social order (upper/male/spiritual vs. lower/female/secular — see Fig. 13), and the Pygmy camp (social space at center, sacred space at the forest periphery) all demonstrate that settlement is not merely shelter but **concretized world-view**. "In nonliterate and traditional communities the social, economic, and religious forms of life are often well integrated. Space and location that rank high socially are also likely to have religious significance" (p. 112).

The chapter ends with the modern diminishment: as literacy replaces architecture as the primary vector of symbolic transmission, buildings lose their function as cosmic texts. "The house is no longer a text encoding the rules of behavior and even a whole world view that can be transmitted down the generations" (p. 116). The Gothic cathedral provoked no divergent critical opinions among its medieval builders — it was simply the forecourt to paradise, needing no literary explication. The modern skyscraper, by contrast, generates irreconcilable readings precisely because the *consensus gentium* it once represented no longer exists.

**Key quotations:**
> "The built environment, like language, has the power to define and refine sensibility. It can sharpen and enlarge consciousness. Without architecture feelings about space must remain diffuse and fleeting." (p. 107)

> "To build is a religious act, the establishment of a world in the midst of primeval disorder." (p. 104)

> "Architecture 'teaches.' A planned city, a monument, or even a simple dwelling can be a symbol of the cosmos. In the absence of books and formal instruction, architecture is a key to comprehending reality." (p. 102)

> "The designed environment serves an educational purpose. In some societies the building is the primary text for handing down a tradition, for presenting a view of reality." (p. 112)

## Chapter 9 — Time in Experiential Space (pp. 118–135)

Chapter 9 makes explicit what the previous chapters implied: space and time are not separable dimensions of experience but mutually constitutive aspects of a single orientation. "The experience of space and time is largely subconscious. We have a sense of space because we can move and of time because, as biological beings, we undergo recurrent phases of tension and ease" (p. 118). Language betrays the fusion: architectural space is called "frozen music" (spatialized time); the passage of time is described as "length"; time is even "volume" — one has a "big time" (p. 118).

Tuan examines how environmental structure shapes spatio-temporal consciousness. The Congo Pygmies, enveloped in dense forest, have no horizon, cannot survey distance, and therefore have a restricted and shallow concept of both space and time — "neither the genealogical past nor the future holds much interest" (p. 120). The Hopi of the American Southwest, living on a semi-arid plateau with panoramic views and sharply etched landmarks, elaborate a complex dual-realm cosmology: **manifested** (objective, horizontal, historical) vs. **manifesting** (subjective, vertical, timeless-mythical). Distance in Hopi thought belongs to the objective realm but shades into the subjective as it increases — "the greater the distance the greater the lapse of time" (p. 121). Remote space is ancient time; nearby space is the present.

The mythic encoding of distant space as past time is nearly universal. "Long ago and far away" opens legends and fairy tales not by coincidence but by cognitive logic: for the Hopi, what happens in a distant village can only be known here later (p. 122). Taoist timeless paradises, European Isles of the Blest, and modern vacation resorts all carry the same psycho-spatial logic: the remote is the atemporal, the prelapsarian.

Tuan then dissects the time-structure of ordinary purposive movement. **Historical time and oriented space** arise together from intentional action: "here is now, there is then" (p. 129). Going to work in the morning is forward movement through space and time; returning home traces steps backward in space and forward in subjective familiarity — home as image of the past, of origins. Plans project a spatial goal into temporal future; habit dissolves the structure into unconscious routine. Music and dance interrupt this directional structure by canceling purposive orientation — soldiers march toward death but military music makes each step feel like a stride into "open and undifferentiated space" (p. 128–129).

The chapter closes by mapping three kinds of mythic time onto spatial form. Cosmogonic time (the story of origins, linear and one-directional) tends to be suppressed or weakly symbolized in spatially prominent cosmologies; astronomical time (cyclical, repetitive) maps naturally onto symmetrical sacred space; human time (also one-directional, biased toward the future) distorts cosmic symmetry. The Chinese City of Man — ideally symmetrical, cosmically oriented — is in actuality asymmetrical, with a privileged axis southward from the imperial palace: "the traditional city, though strongly influenced by cosmic ideas in basic design, is nonetheless... the City of Man. It mirrors the asymmetry of human time and life" (p. 135).

**Key quotations:**
> "Space is historical if it has direction or a privileged perspective. Maps are ahistorical, landscape paintings are historical." (p. 122)

> "The intention to go to a place creates historical time: the place is a goal in the future." (p. 130)

> "Human time, like the human body, is asymmetrical: one's back is to the past, one's face to the future. Living is a perpetual stepping forward." (p. 132)
