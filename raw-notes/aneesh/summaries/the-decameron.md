# The Decameron

*The Decameron* (Italian *Decamerone*, "ten days' work") is a collection of one hundred prose tales written by [[giovanni-boccaccio]] around 1349–1353, in the Tuscan vernacular. It is built as a **frame narrative**: a single fiction (a storytelling company in retreat) contains the hundred embedded tales, ten told on each of ten days. The work stands at the threshold of the European Renaissance, and is read in this wiki both as a literary monument and as an unusually precise pre-modern instance of the World Machines pattern of withdrawal into a curated refuge during an interregnum (see the synthesis [[the-brigata-as-thicket]]).

## The Frame: plague, flight, and the brigata

The frame is set in motion by catastrophe. The First Day opens with the celebrated description of the **1348 plague** in Florence — an anatomy of social collapse in which "the reverend authority of the laws, both human and divine, was all in a manner dissolved and fallen into decay," because the ministers and executors charged with enforcing them are dead. Kinship, burial rite, law, custom, and religious observance all break down; "brother forsook brother… nay, fathers and mothers refused to visit or tend their very children."

Against this dissolution, seven young women and three young men — the **brigata** — meet by chance in the church of Santa Maria Novella and resolve to withdraw together to a country estate two miles outside the walls. Pampinea's founding speech frames the flight not as abandonment of the social but as the construction of a better-regulated alternative. In the country the company constitutes itself as a **rotating monarchy**: a king or queen is elected for each day, a full household of servants is ordered, the canonical hours are repurposed as a daily schedule, and a single explicit rule is imposed — no news from the stricken city is to be brought inside. Each day's sovereign sets the theme for the next day's stories. The frame thus functions as a small, deliberately designed society in which storytelling is the central civic activity.

## The ten Days and their themes

- **Proem & Author's Introduction** — Boccaccio dedicates the book, in his own voice, to lovesick ladies as the readers least able to seek relief elsewhere, and recasts storytelling as therapeutic consolation and an ethical debt of gratitude.
- **Day One** (Pampinea; free topic) — wit and ready speech; sacred fraud and the permissiveness of divine grace (Ser Ciappelletto, Abraham the Jew, the three-rings parable of Melchizedek).
- **Day Two** (Filomena; *those who attain joy after adversity*) — Fortune as the operating system of the world; shipwreck, exile, disguise, and improbable restoration (Andreuccio of Perugia, Madam Beritola, Alatiel, the Ginevra/"Sicurano" tale).
- **Day Three** (Neifile; *those who win or recover a much-desired thing by industry*) — wit as the master virtue, set against sustained anticlerical satire; the walled Eden-garden as setting (Masetto, Gillette de Narbonne, Alibech).
- **Day Four** (Filostrato; *loves that ended unhappily*) — tragic love crushed by paternal, fraternal, and social authority, prefaced by Boccaccio's "green geese" defence of writing about women and desire (Tancred and Ghismonda, Lisabetta's pot of basil, the eaten heart).
- **Day Five** (Fiammetta; *loves that, after trials, reached happiness*) — love as a civilizing and capture-resistant force, vindicated by persistence and recognition (Cimon, Nastagio degli Onesti, Federigo and his falcon).
- **Day Six** (Elisa; *those who escaped loss or shame by a ready word*) — rhetoric itself as the subject: the well-timed sentence as a survival technology that undoes rank (Cisti the baker, Madam Filippa of Prato, Guido Cavalcanti, Fra Cipolla).
- **Day Seven** (Dioneo; *tricks wives have played on their husbands*) — female wit sovereign in the household; surveillance defeated by opacity (Peronella and the vat, the jealous husband as confessor, the enchanted pear-tree).
- **Day Eight** (Lauretta; *tricks played in every direction*) — trickery as a social art, including the collection's most morally uncomfortable tale, the scholar Rinieri's near-lethal revenge on the widow Elena.
- **Day Nine** (Emilia; free topic) — a deliberate miscellany of female resourcefulness, clerical hypocrisy, and the credulity of fools (the abbess and the priest's breeches; the Calandrino hoaxes).
- **Day Ten** (Pamfilo; *deeds of magnificence and magnanimity*) — the culminating ethical Day, escalating bids of generosity, friendship, and erotic renunciation, closing on the deeply ambiguous test of Griselda's patience, which Dioneo himself calls a "monstrous folly."
- **Conclusion of the Author** — Boccaccio steps outside the frame to answer anticipated charges of licentiousness, partiality toward women, uneven quality, length, and satire of the friars.

## The major arc

Read as a whole, the hundred tales trace a deliberate movement. The early Days are governed by **Fortune** — an impersonal force that strips identity, property, and rank, then sometimes restores them (Day Two is its purest expression). The middle Days shift authority from Fortune to **human wit**: ingenuity (Day Three), ready speech (Day Six), and trickery (Days Seven–Nine) become the instruments by which characters move through, around, and against the institutions and surveillance that would constrain them. The final Day turns from cleverness to **virtue** — magnificence, friendship, renunciation, and patient endurance — as if the cycle, having exhausted the comedy of survival, arrives at a considered account of human excellence. Threaded throughout is a sustained **anticlerical critique** (most pointed in Day Three's Tedaldo sermon and the friar-tales) and a recurring interest in **women's agency under structural constraint**, from Alatiel's bare survival to Ghismonda's defiant speech on nobility as earned rather than inherited.

The frame has its own arc, distinct from the tales. The brigata is **formed** in the interregnum of the plague, **maintained** through ten days of rule-governed storytelling, and finally — on the morning after Day Ten — **deliberately dissolved** by Pamfilo, who reasons that the company has become known to neighbours and that its commodity (its insulated, controlled interior) would be spoiled if it continued. They return to Florence, the plague still awaiting them. The refuge is constituted with a closure date; it does not pretend to resolve the crisis, only to hold a way of being open inside it.

## Storytelling as world-maintenance and Boccaccio's defence

The *Decameron* repeatedly argues for its own activity. The Proem casts stories as medicine for the enclosed and the lovesick. Within the frame, Dioneo's defence of the licentious Day-Seven theme states the book's poetics aloud: "the judges have forsaken the tribunals… the laws, as well Divine as human, are silent, and full licence is conceded unto every one for the preservation of his life." Storytelling is positioned as the one ordering activity available when ordinary order has failed — a form of world-maintenance carried out from inside the refuge.

The **Author's Conclusion** completes the defence. Its central axiom relocates moral responsibility from the text to its reader: "Corrupt mind never understood word healthily… so those which are not altogether seemly avail not to contaminate the well-disposed, any more than mire can sully the rays of the sun." The tales, Boccaccio insists, were told "amidst gardens, in a place of pleasance," to listeners of mature wit; their licence is coextensive with the garden's borders. Remove the context — the brigata, the refuge — and the argument changes. The defence is thus inseparable from the frame: the book defends itself by appeal to the bounded, curated space in which its stories were told.

## Reception and significance

*The Decameron* is a foundational work of Italian prose and a model for later frame-collections; Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales* is its closest English analogue, and individual tales were carried forward across European literature (Keats's *Isabella, or the Pot of Basil* from IV.5; Petrarch's Latin translation of the Griselda tale, which Chaucer adapted as the Clerk's Tale). In this wiki's framework, the *Decameron* is cited as one of the **early catalogs of proliferating voices** that mark the [[modernity-machine]]'s emergence — "social life becomes polyphonic" — and its frame supplies the historical case study for the synthesis [[the-brigata-as-thicket]].
