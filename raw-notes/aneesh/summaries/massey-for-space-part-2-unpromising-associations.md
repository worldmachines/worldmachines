# Massey, *For Space* (2005) — Part Two: Unpromising Associations

Source: Doreen Massey, *For Space*, London: SAGE, 2005, Part Two (chapters 2–5), pp. 17–59. See also [[doreen-massey]].

## Argument of the part

Part Two is the **diagnostic** half of the book. Massey reads four influential twentieth-century traditions — Bergsonian process philosophy, Lévi-Straussian structuralism, the post-structuralism of Laclau and de Certeau, and Derridean deconstruction — and shows that each of them, in different ways, has saddled "space" with connotations of stasis, closure, and representational deadness. Her wager is that **what time has gained, space has paid for**: every effort to rescue temporality from determinism has been bought at the price of relegating space to the realm of "the dead, the fixed, the undialectical, the immobile" (Foucault 1980, quoted at Massey 2005: 49).

The constructive proposal that arrives in Chapter 5 is the book's central thesis: space is **the dimension of dynamic simultaneity** — the sphere of coexisting, ongoing, mutually-affecting trajectories. Without space there is no multiplicity (no others to interact with); without multiplicity there is no time (since time is the change produced by interaction). Cf. [[space-as-the-dimension-of-multiplicity]], [[dynamic-simultaneity]].

## Chapter map

### Ch. 2 — Space/representation (pp. 20–30)

Target: the implicit equation **space = representation = stabilisation = death-of-life**. Massey reads Bergson, de Certeau, and Laclau as all collapsing two distinct things — (i) the claim that representation fixes the flow of life, and (ii) the claim that what representation produces is space. She concedes (i) is partially defensible; she rejects (ii) as unwarranted. The map is not the territory; the "tracing" is not "space." What is conceptualised in any representation is not time alone but **time-space**: society is "both temporal and spatial" (Massey 2005: 27). To equate space with representation is to "rob space of those characteristics of freedom (Bergson), dislocation (Laclau) and surprise (de Certeau) which are essential to open it up to the political" (28). See [[space-as-representation-critique]].

A subordinate thread argues that this hegemonic conflation is itself **historically produced**: it descends from Bergson's battle with classical physics. Bergson needed time to be open and indeterministic; classical mechanics insisted on time-reversibility; so Bergson built an opposition in which everything that science did to time — pinning down, holding still — was relabelled "space." Massey notes (via Prigogine and Stengers 1984) that the science Bergson was fighting no longer exists, and so the polemical motive for that move has dissolved. The chapter is also a sustained warning against humanities **"physics envy"** and against treating natural science as a court of final corroboration.

### Ch. 3 — The prison-house of synchrony (pp. 36–48)

Target: structuralism (Lévi-Strauss, Saussure, the langue/parole distinction) and its post-structuralist legacy (Althusser, Laclau, de Certeau). Structuralism, originally an emancipatory attempt to escape narrativising "primitive" societies as Western Europe's past, achieved this by treating each society as a coherent **structure** to be analysed synchronically. But synchrony was then translated as **space**, on the bare assumption that whatever is not-temporal must be spatial. The result was a conceptualisation of space as **a-temporal, internally interlocked, closed**, lacking "real change," and thus politically inert. See [[prison-house-of-synchrony]].

Massey traces this through Lévi-Strauss's "ruse" of substituting diachrony for history (Fabian 1983 cited at 38); through Althusser's critique of Hegel's "essential section" (the cross-section in which every part expresses the whole); through Laclau's *New Reflections* (1990), where "spatiality" is reserved for **causal closure** rather than for any positive description of the world; and through de Certeau's strategy/tactic distinction (1984), where "strategy" is space (the proper, the fixed) and "tactic" is time (the mobile, the resistant). In each case the move to open up temporality leaves space conceptually intact as the prison-house. See [[coupure-and-causal-closure]], [[space-as-event]].

### Ch. 4 — The horizontalities of deconstruction (pp. 49–54)

Target: Derridean deconstruction. Massey grants that Derrida — unlike Laclau and de Certeau — gives spacing **explicit positive attention**: *différance* names both deferral (temporal) and differentiation (spatial); spacing is "a productive, positive, generative force" (Derrida 1972/1987, fn 42). And yet, she argues, the **practice** of deconstruction has been pulled toward the differential side of *différance* and toward a **textual** imagination of the world. The world-as-text image privileges a horizontal, two-dimensional surface to be deconstructed; it leaves "too little recognition of the multiple trajectories of which that 'horizontality' is the momentary, passing, result" (51).

A second worry: Derrida's account of spacing as **negative** — spacing as "the movement of setting aside," as that which "interrupts every self-identity" — generates difference from a presumed One rather than recognising **positive multiplicity** from the outset. She cites a long exchange between Derrida and his interlocutor Houdebine in which Houdebine struggles to make exactly this point: heterogeneity is not exhausted by spacing-as-expulsion; spacing also names the *position* of a positive something (Derrida 1972/1987: 81 ff). She aligns her own constructive thesis with the **Spinoza–Bergson–Deleuze** line of philosophical affirmation. See [[horizontalities-of-deconstruction]], [[positive-versus-negative-multiplicity]].

### Ch. 5 — The life in space (pp. 55–59)

The constructive payoff. Massey gathers the thesis:

> "On this reading neither time nor space is reducible to the other; they are distinct. They are, however, co-implicated. On the side of space, there is the integral temporality of a dynamic simultaneity. On the side of time, there is the necessary production of change through practices of interrelation." (Massey 2005: 55)

Bergson asked "what is the role of time?" and answered: time prevents everything from being given at once. Massey adds the dual: the role of **space** is to provide the condition for the relations that generate time. "We cannot 'become', in other words, without others." (56) See [[the-co-implication-of-space-and-time]].

The chapter closes by linking the conceptualisation of space to subjectivity. Via Grosz, Irigaray, and Gillian Rose, Massey reads the **interiorisation of time** and the **exteriorisation of space** as a gendered move (time-masculine-interior; space-feminine-exterior), and argues for a "spatialisation of being" — an outward-looking, relational self that does not gain authenticity by withdrawing behind its frontiers. The political stake is explicit: "if time is to be open then space must be open too" (59).

## Key positive claims compiled from Part Two

1. Space is **not** representation, **not** synchrony-as-causal-closure, **not** the horizontality of the text. ([[space-as-representation-critique]], [[prison-house-of-synchrony]], [[horizontalities-of-deconstruction]])
2. Space is a **discrete multiplicity** whose elements are themselves imbued with temporality — a **dynamic simultaneity** rather than a static contemporaneity. ([[dynamic-simultaneity]])
3. Space is **always under construction**: an open ongoing production, the product of relations-between rather than a pre-given container. ([[space-as-always-under-construction]])
4. Without space, no multiplicity; without multiplicity, no genuine others; without others, no change; therefore no time. ([[space-as-the-dimension-of-multiplicity]])
5. The political stakes are direct: closed conceptions of space underwrite the singular universal, the one-history, the one-voice. Opening space conceptually is a prerequisite for opening the political.

## Cross-source resonances

- **Bryant, *Onto-Cartography*** — Massey's "space as relational product" and Bryant's "topological field of machine-paths" are near-identical theses arrived at from different traditions. Both reject the container/Euclidean image; both treat space as plural, multiple, and produced. See [[doreen-massey]], the existing `spatial-epistemology-the-master-thread`.
- **Tuan, *Space and Place*** — Tuan's phenomenology of place (pause, attachment, the body) sits in a complementary register; Massey's critique of "space as representation" would mostly endorse Tuan's experiential method while pushing back against any view that places are bounded essences (which Tuan does *not* hold, in fact).
- **Bergson and Sensemaking** — Massey's reading of Bergson's "duration prevents everything being given at once" connects to the wiki's existing Klein/Weick sensemaking material via the question of *temporal openness* in cognition.
- **World Machines / Divergence Machine cluster** — the critique of structuralist temporal-erasure-of-space resonates with Rao's "history machine" framing (modernity converting spatial coexistence into temporal sequence) but Massey's account is more philosophically rigorous and explicitly anti-teleological.

## Key quoted passages

- "There is no case at all for the second proposition: that there is an equivalence between space and representation." (27)
- "Synchrony is not con-temporality, but a-temporality." (Osborne, cited 37)
- "The very life, and certainly the politics, are taken out of it." (30)
- "Politics and space are antinomic terms. Politics only exist insofar as the spatial eludes us." (Laclau, quoted 44) — Massey reads this as a *symptom* of the diagnosis, not as a true claim.
- "We cannot 'become' . . . without others. And it is space that provides the necessary condition for that possibility." (56)
- "If time is to be open then space must be open too." (59)

## Contradictions / Conflicting Data

None internal to Part Two, but Massey is explicitly contesting **Laclau** (whose work the wiki has not yet engaged independently — see [[ernesto-laclau]]) and **de Certeau**, whose "tactics of the weak" is widely admired in cultural studies. Massey's verdict: the tactics/strategies framework is theoretically generative but conceptually traps space in the role of static "given."
