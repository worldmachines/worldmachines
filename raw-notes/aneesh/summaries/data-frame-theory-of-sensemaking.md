# A Data-Frame Theory of Sensemaking — Klein, Phillips, Rall & Peluso (2007)

**Authors**: Gary Klein, Jennifer K. Phillips, Erica L. Rall, Deborah A. Peluso (Klein Associates Inc.)
**Published in**: *Expertise Out of Context* (Proceedings of the Sixth International Conference on Naturalistic Decision Making), 2007. Chapter 6, pp. 113–155.

## Central Argument

Sensemaking is **not** the passive accumulation and processing of data into inferences. It is the deliberate effort to understand events by simultaneously fitting data into frames and fitting frames around data. The relationship is bidirectional and constitutive: the frame defines what counts as data, and data elicit and refine the frame. Neither comes first.

This is a departure from information-processing models in which data arrive, are processed, and produce understanding. Klein et al. insist that "data" are constructed, not given — filtered, abstracted, and distorted by the frames that make them perceptible.

## The Nine Assertions (Table 6.1)

1. Sensemaking is the process of fitting data into a frame and fitting a frame around the data.
2. The "data" are inferred using the frame, rather than being perceptual primitives.
3. The frame is inferred from a few key anchors.
4. The inferences used in sensemaking rely on abductive reasoning as well as logical deduction.
5. Sensemaking usually ceases when the data and frame are brought into congruence.
6. Experts reason the same way as novices, but have a richer repertoire of frames.
7. Sensemaking is used to achieve a functional understanding — what to do in a situation — as well as an abstract understanding.
8. People primarily rely on just-in-time mental models.
9. Sensemaking takes different forms, each with its own dynamics.

## The Seven Forms of Sensemaking (Figure 6.1)

1. **Connecting data and frame** — basic pattern recognition; the data-frame match
2. **Elaborating the frame** — extending the frame as more data arrives, filling slots
3. **Questioning the frame** — when inconsistent data violates expectations; "fundamental surprise" (Lanir, 1991)
4. **Preserving the frame** — explaining away inconsistent data; can lead to [[Fixation Error]]
5. **Reframing** — replacing the current frame; requires simultaneous replacement of anchors
6. **Seeking a frame** — when no frame is available; searching for anchors from fragmentary data
7. **Comparing frames** — simultaneous elaboration of competing frames using "logical competitor sets" (LCS)

## Key Theoretical Claims

### Data Are Constructed
Data elements are not transparent representations of reality. Different observers (fireground commander, arson investigator, insurance agent) construct different data from the same fire scene. Feltovich, Spiro & Coulson (1997) catalog systematic distortions people make when abstracting: treating dynamic processes as static, treating simultaneous processes as sequential, converging on single interpretations.

### Anchors Drive Frame Selection
People select frames from a limited set of 3–4 key anchors. An inaccurate anchor early in a sensemaking sequence corrupts subsequent interpretation more severely than a late anchor. Weather forecasters studied by Debra Jones relied on only 2–3 anchors.

### Abductive Reasoning Dominates
Experts and novices alike reason abductively — inferring the best explanation given observed data — rather than deductively. NICU nurses exemplify this: "I am seeing these symptoms (which in my experience have been connected with sepsis); therefore perhaps this baby has sepsis." This is technically the fallacy of affirming the consequent, but is an appropriate strategy for uncertain environments.

### Expert-Novice Differences
Experts do not use different reasoning *strategies* than novices. They have richer frames, more routines, greater sensitivity to context, and superior functional orientation (they know what to *do*, not just what is *true*). The mechanism of generating inferences is the same; the quality of the inferences differs because the frames differ.

### Just-in-Time Mental Models
People rarely have comprehensive mental models of complex systems. They assemble JIT mental models from fragmentary causal knowledge on demand. This has training implications: expanding one's repertoire of causal relationships is more valuable than trying to build comprehensive mental models.

## Key Cases

- **Example 1 (Ominous Airplanes)**: Major A.S. reframes post-9/11 overflight reports from terrorist surveillance to lost student pilots by finding anchors that fit the alternative frame (Al Qaeda tactics manual, visual flight rules training, nuclear plant landmarks for navigation).
- **Example 2 (Reconnaissance Team)**: Brigadier general elaborates a richer frame than a sergeant by deploying experience-based expectations about tank company organization.
- **Example 3 (Helicopter Accident)**: Captain B. cycles through successive frames as each anchor is replaced by a better one.
- **Example 4 (Laundry Chute Fire)**: Captain L. questions his frame when flames appear on the fourth-floor landing — reframes from "basement fire" to "heavily involved building" in real time.
- **Example 5 (NICU)**: Nurse simultaneously tracks "making a good recovery" and "developing sepsis" as competing frames using body language cues that override normal vital signs.
- **Example 6 (Flying Blind)**: Solo pilot elaborates, questions, preserves, then reframes using the Ohio River as a dominant new anchor.

## Applications

### Decision Support Systems
- More data does not improve sensemaking past a threshold (~8–10 data elements); confidence continues rising while accuracy plateaus (Oskamp, 1965; Lanir, 1991 on Yom Kippur War)
- DSS should support abductive inference, not just logical inference
- The DIKW (data-information-knowledge-wisdom) hierarchy is misleading; it reifies the passive information-processing model
- DSS should help track available anchors, since people use so few

### Training
- Avoid training delayed commitment to frames — early commitment enables expectancy generation and anomaly detection
- "Diagnostic vagabonding" (continual reframing without commitment) is as dysfunctional as fixation
- Train expanding frame repertoire and causal relationship repertoire, not generic sensemaking skills
- Process feedback requires a good mental model to interpret; outcome feedback is insufficient

## Intellectual Context

- Extends [[Karl Weick]]'s sensemaking theory (Weick 1995) with a cognitive mechanism
- Related to but distinct from [[Situation Awareness]] (Endsley 1995): sensemaking is a *process* of constructing data and meaning, not a *state* of knowledge
- Synthesizes frames (Goffman 1974; Minsky 1975), scripts (Schank & Abelson 1977), and schemata (Bartlett 1932; Neisser 1976; Piaget)
- Connected to [[Recognition-Primed Decision]] model (Klein 1998): Level 1 RPD is the unconscious data-frame match; Level 2 RPD involves deliberate sensemaking
- Draws on [[Abductive Reasoning]] (Peirce 1903; Josephson & Josephson 1994)
- Macrocognitive framework: see [[Macrocognition]]
