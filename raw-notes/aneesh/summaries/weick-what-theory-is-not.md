# What Theory Is Not, Theorizing Is

**Source:** Weick, Karl E. "What Theory Is Not, Theorizing Is." *Administrative Science Quarterly* 40 (1995): 385–390.

## Central Argument

Products of theorizing rarely emerge as full-blown theories. Most "theory" in organizational studies is approximation. The five elements that Sutton and Staw identified as "not theory" — references, data, lists, diagrams, hypotheses — are not simply failures; they are components of an *ongoing theorizing process*. The key evaluative question is not "is this a theory?" but "is this an interim struggle moving toward theory?"

## Three Senses of Approximation

1. **Merton's four forms** of theoretical approximation:
   - General orientations (variables named but not interrelated)
   - Analysis of concepts (clarified but not connected)
   - Post-factum interpretation (ad hoc hypotheses from single observation)
   - Empirical generalization (isolated two-variable proposition)

2. **Theory as a continuum, not dichotomy** (Runkel & Runkel, 1984): The word "theory" belongs to the same family as guess, speculation, supposition, conjecture, hypothesis, model. Social scientists should "use theory whenever they are theorizing" — not reserve it for ultimate triumph. "Modesty is all very well, but leaning over too far backward removes a good word from currency."

3. **Slippage in construction**: Theorists start with a nonlinear vision and must convert it to linear propositions. This conversion inevitably loses accuracy. What looks like "not theory" may be a clumsy attempt to disassemble a gestalt — more practice and more nuanced language yields more of the originating insight.

## On the Five "Not Theory" Elements

### References
Ceremonial citations that merely *point* to theories are not theory. But authors who *pinpoint and paraphrase* key ideas from references are doing theoretical work — which takes space that editors and reviewers need to tolerate.

### Data
Data alone are not theory. But Starbuck (1993) argues: the best theorists, like the best doctors, may prescribe directly from symptoms without the intermediary of diagnosis/theory. Theory comes *last*, summarizing observed treatment-symptom relations. The Mann Gulch paper: Weick took the disaster as "symptoms," ran thought-trial "treatments" to find which concepts made a difference, and theory emerged afterward. "Some people who seem to confuse data with theory may simply be midway through this process."

### Lists
Farther from theory than stories, but a list carries tacit theoretical messages: items on it are more crucial than items off it; causation is simultaneous rather than sequential; items toward top matter more. "Relatively small amounts of tweaking and articulation could make it an explanation."

### Diagrams
More explicit than lists about sequence, determinacy, and pathways of influence. Staw and Ross (1987) diagram on escalation of commitment implicitly shows that cross-level escalation is more costly than within-level — a proposition implicit in the *form* of representation, not stated in the text.

### Hypotheses
Stand-alone hypotheses ("empirical generalizations" in Merton) lack only connections with propositions and concepts to become theories. "Such connecting may necessitate little more than raising the level of abstraction of key terms."

## Process vs. Product Tradeoff

The key question: **is this an interim struggle that is moving from one text to another, with its past and future made clear?** If yes — revise and resubmit. If no (diagrams preceded this paper, diagrams will follow) — the theorizing is less robust.

The ultimate tradeoff: between *theorizing* (process, doing) and *theory* (product, freezing). ASQ's own Notice to Contributors: "Ungrounded theory is no more helpful than atheoretical data" — already encodes this tradeoff.

## Key Quotations

> "Theory belongs to the family of words that includes guess, speculation, supposition, conjecture, proposition, hypothesis, conception, explanation, model." (Runkel & Runkel, via Weick)

> "The process of theorizing consists of activities like abstracting, generalizing, relating, selecting, explaining, synthesizing, and idealizing."

> "A text that looks like 'not theory' may simply be a clumsy attempt to disassemble a gestalt into linear propositions."

## Implications for the WMP

Weick's framework is directly useful for the World Machines Project. The WM framework is explicitly described by Rao as "sensemaking triage and shared conceptual language, not a Grand Unified Theory" — precisely Weick's "interim struggle." The WMP is doing disciplined theorizing, not pretending to have a theory. The Weick paper provides philosophical grounding for this epistemic posture.
