# Isaiah Berlin — Against the Current: Essays in the History of Ideas

**Source**: Isaiah Berlin, *Against the Current: Essays in the History of Ideas* (1979; Princeton University Press 2015 edition)
**Author**: [[Isaiah Berlin]]
**Type**: Book (essay collection)

## Note on Source

The raw source for this entry is the full text of the opening essay "The Counter-Enlightenment" (32 pp.), which serves as the programmatic introduction to the collection. The other essays are named but only partially available; this summary prioritizes the direct text.

## Structure of the Collection

The book collects Berlin's essays on thinkers who ran "against the current" of mainstream Enlightenment rationalism. The opening essay, "The Counter-Enlightenment," provides the intellectual map for the whole. Other major essays include:

- "The Originality of Machiavelli"
- "The Naïveté of Verdi"
- "Georges Sorel"
- "Joseph de Maistre and the Origins of Fascism"
- "Vico and the Ideal of Enlightenment"
- "Herder and the Enlightenment"
- "Nationalism: Past Neglect and Present Power"

## Central Argument of "The Counter-Enlightenment" (pp. 1–32)

The essay identifies and maps the intellectual tradition that challenged the three interlocking pillars of Enlightenment rationalism:

1. **Monism**: one correct answer to all genuine questions, discovered by reason
2. **Universalism**: a single rational framework valid for all cultures and all times
3. **Progressivism**: history as the march of reason toward human perfection

Berlin's argument is both historical and normative: these thinkers identified real problems, and their insights — especially about the irreducibility of different human ends — deserve serious engagement rather than dismissal.

### The Enlightenment Position (as Target)

The *philosophes* — Voltaire, d'Alembert, Condorcet, Helvétius — held that:
- Natural law governs inanimate and animate nature alike
- Human nature is fundamentally the same in all times and places
- Local and historical variations are unimportant compared to the universal core
- The methods of Newtonian physics can be applied to ethics and politics with similar success
- Progress toward rational civilization is achievable once irrationality is removed

This was "the central dogma of the entire Enlightenment" (p. 4). What was attacked was precisely this: that ultimate laws governing human life are real, knowable, and universal.

### The Sceptical Tradition (Background)

Berlin notes that skepticism about universal moral truth goes back to Greek sophists (Protagoras, Antiphon, Critias), and was powerfully revived by:
- **Montaigne**, **Cornelius Agrippa**, **Charron** — 16th-century sceptics
- **Bodin**, **Montesquieu** — sociological relativism based on travel literature and natural geography
- **David Hume** — dissolved the logical link between fact and value, weakening all claims to universal moral science

Nevertheless, these sceptics retained a common conviction that ultimate human ends were broadly similar. The *true* counter-Enlightenment went further.

## Key Thinkers Covered (from "The Counter-Enlightenment" essay)

### [[Giambattista Vico]] (pp. 5–8)

Vico's revolutionary move: denied that there are timeless natural law truths knowable to any rational person at any time. Instead:
- *Verum ipsum factum*: we can truly know only what we have made — hence human history is more intelligible to us than nature (which God made)
- Human cultures are not steps on a single scale of progress but incommensurable wholes, each with its own style (*Stileinheit*)
- Myths and poetry are not primitive errors corrected by reason — they are authentic collective expressions of a civilization's vision
- The Homeric poems are an unsurpassable achievement, but they could only spring from a brutal, heroic society — no later civilization can simply reproduce them

Vico "laid the foundations at once of comparative cultural anthropology and of comparative historical linguistics, aesthetics, jurisprudence" (p. 7).

### [[Johann Georg Hamann]] (pp. 8–13)

Hamann ("the Magus of the North") was Vico's most ferocious counterpart in the German tradition:
- Pietist background: faith, not reason, as the basic organ of acquaintance with reality
- All truth is particular, never general; reason is merely an instrument for classifying and arranging data — it cannot grasp reality as such
- "Individuum est ineffabile" (Goethe, in the spirit of Hamann): the individual is inexpressible; no concatenation of concepts can capture the fullness of individual experience
- Language is not a tool for expressing pre-formed thought — it is the direct expression of historical life; each language, each profession, each community has its own irreducible idiom
- "Passion alone gives to abstractions hands, feet, wings" — the Enlightenment strips passion and makes dead abstractions
- His attack on Frederick the Great's rationalist-French cultural imposition in Prussia is prototype of every later attack on cultural imperialism

Hamann is the founding figure of a line running through Herder, Jacobi, Möser, Young, Burke — and ultimately to Romanticism.

### [[Johann Gottfried Herder]] (pp. 13–16)

Herder, Hamann's disciple, constructed a coherent system out of his teacher's isolated flashes:
- *Einfühlung* ('feeling into') is the method of cultural understanding — imaginative empathy, entering the Schwerpunkt (centre of gravity) of another culture
- Each culture has its own *Schwerpunkt*, its own constellation of values that cannot be judged from outside
- Herder is no nationalist: "different cultures could and should flourish fruitfully side by side like so many peaceful flowers in the great human garden" (p. 15)
- But the seeds of nationalism are unmistakably present — cultural individuality easily becomes political demand for statehood
- Condemned the Romans for crushing native civilizations, the Church for forcible baptism of the Balts, British missionaries for destroying Asian cultures
- The tree of knowledge kills the tree of life: "uniformity is death"

**Herder on cosmopolitanism**: "To belong to a given community, to be connected with its members by indissoluble and impalpable ties of common language, historical memory, habit, tradition and feeling, is a basic human need no less natural than that for food or drink" (p. 16).

### The *Sturm und Drang* (pp. 11–13, 18–19)

The literary movement Hamann and Herder inspired, fed also by Rousseau's early writings:
- Lenz, Klinger, Gerstenberg, Leisewitz — outbursts against the rational ordering of life
- Schiller's *Die Räuber* (1781): the hero Karl Moor's tragic revolt against rational reform; knowledge is not enough
- Goethe's reaction against Holbach's *Système de la nature* as "Cimmerian, corpse-like"
- The Romantic pronouncement: "To dissect is to murder" — the seamless whole of reality misrepresented by spatial, static metaphors of mathematics

### [[Joseph de Maistre]] (pp. 27–32)

The darkest strand of counter-Enlightenment:
- Men are by nature evil, self-destructive, full of conflicting drives; society requires authoritarian control
- "The most reliable guides to nature" are history and zoology — both reveal "a field of unceasing slaughter"
- The Enlightenment conception of natural benevolence is "shallow and false"
- Social order in the end rests on the executioner — the terror of the scaffold is what holds civilization together
- Nations are born and die like individuals; they have "a common soul" — an organic mysticism that explicitly anticipates 20th-century racism and fascism
- *Bâtonocratie*: rule by the stick — only force is truly sovereign

Berlin: Maistre's doctrines, combined with Romantic heroism and the historic/unhistoric nation distinction, "duly inspired nationalism, imperialism, and finally, in their most violent and pathological form, Fascist and totalitarian doctrines in the twentieth century" (p. 32).

### Edmund Burke and Justus Möser (pp. 17–18)

- **Burke**: founded his attack on the French revolutionaries on appeal to the myriad historical strands binding human beings — contrasted with the utilitarian model of society as a trading company held together by contracts
- **Möser**: first historical sociologist; argued that each age has its own *Zeitstil* and *Volksstil*; societies must be understood by total impression, not by isolating elements analytically; the variety of local custom (a German village law contradicting a neighboring one) is precisely what preserves freedom from uniformity

### Schelling, Romanticism, and the Descendants (pp. 22–26)

- **Schelling**: the universe as the self-development of a primal, non-rational force graspable only by intuitive men of genius — poets, philosophers, statesmen
- This produced the contrast between critical analytic intellect (Enlightenment) and primary imagination (Romanticism): a commonplace in Fichte, Hegel, Wordsworth, Coleridge, Goethe, Carlyle, Schopenhauer
- The Romantics: values are not found but made, not discovered but created; the creative self generates its own ends
- The "creative self" variously identified with: transcendent cosmic spirit, individual mortal flesh (Byron, Hugo), super-personal organism (nation, class, race, history) — this last giving Romanticism its most dangerous political form
- **Novalis**, **Tieck**: the universe as perpetual activity of spirit; the man of genius as most conscious agent of the world's self-development

### Kant as Inadvertent Ancestor (pp. 19–21)

Berlin identifies the paradox: it was the "profoundly rational, exact, unromantic Kant" who, through his moral doctrine of autonomy, fed the very Romanticism he would have abhorred. By insisting on moral freedom — that only those who are true authors of their own acts can be praised or blamed — Kant created a cult of moral autonomy that Romantic individualists radicalized into unbridled self-assertion. Kant blamed Rousseau's doctrine of the will; the Romantics radicalized both.

## Berlin's Framework

The Counter-Enlightenment is not a unified movement. It runs from:
- Vico and Herder's empathetic pluralism (which Berlin endorses as philosophically serious)
- Through Hamann's radical fideism (which Berlin finds intellectually interesting but ultimately irrational)
- To Maistre's authoritarian organicism (which Berlin takes seriously as a diagnostician, not as a prescriber)
- To full Fascism and totalitarianism (the pathological endpoint)

Berlin's intervention: extract the valid pluralist insights from this tradition without endorsing the political conclusions they were used to justify. The counter-Enlightenment thinkers were often right in their diagnosis (the Enlightenment does suppress cultural particularity and genuine value conflict) but wrong or catastrophic in their prescriptions (authority, tradition, violence, organic mysticism).

## What's Missing / Contentious

- The essay is largely a map, not a close argument — it moves rapidly through figures without sustained close reading
- Berlin has been accused of domesticating dangerous thinkers, making them safe for liberal pluralism when their actual politics were far more radical or reactionary
- The slide from Herder's peaceful cultural pluralism to political nationalism is noted but not explained — why did this particular insight produce these particular politics?
- The Machiavelli reading (absent from this essay but central to the collection) has been contested
- Berlin's own pluralism inherits the counter-Enlightenment's conservative tilt: treating tragic value conflicts as permanent forecloses historically contingent social solutions

## Key Quotations

- On the Enlightenment's core dogma: "that these laws were real, and could be known, whether with certainty, or only probability, remained the central dogma of the entire Enlightenment" (p. 4)
- On Vico: "the notion of a single style that pervades all the activities and manifestations of societies of human beings at a particular stage of development" (p. 6)
- On Hamann: "'Passion alone,' said Hamann, 'gives to abstractions hands, feet, wings'" (p. 10)
- On Herder's cosmopolitanism critique: "Cosmopolitanism is the shedding of all that makes one most human, most oneself" (p. 16)
- On Maistre: "all social order in the end rests upon one man, the executioner" (p. 30)
- On the endpoint: doctrines that "duly inspired nationalism, imperialism, and finally, in their most violent and pathological form, Fascist and totalitarian doctrines in the twentieth century" (p. 32)
