# Summary: Onto-Cartography Ch. 5 — The Structure of Worlds

Bryant, Levi R. *Onto-Cartography: An Ontology of Machines and Media*. Edinburgh University Press, 2014. Chapter 5, "The Structure of Worlds," pp. 111–139.

---

## 1. What a World Is Not

Bryant opens by clearing the field of rival conceptions. A world is not:

- **A planet** (planets are machines *within* worlds, not worlds themselves)
- **A network of signs** — Heidegger's account of Dasein's structural openness to world via meaning confuses access to a world with the world itself. The world as Heidegger sees it from "everydayness" is how a particular machine (Dasein) encounters world, not what world is in itself.
- **A compossible system of sense** (contra Deleuze): sense or incorporeal machines are *components* of worlds, not their condition.
- **A transcendental** (contra Badiou): the transcendental is a sieve through which a social machine encounters a world, not the world as such.
- **The horizon of lived experience for the cogito** (contra Husserl): world is indifferent to our existence. Sartre got this right — worlds are in-themselves.
- A subjective construct of *any* kind.

The positive formulation, italicized in the text: "*A world is an ecology of loosely coupled machines linked by machines without any of these machines totalizing world.*" (p. 114)

---

## 2. Pluralism: There Are Many Worlds

With Deleuze and Badiou, Bryant begins from a plurality of worlds rather than one universe. Two reasons:

**First, machines are external to their relations.** No machine relates to all others. It is possible that some machines are completely unrelated to any other — he calls these "dark objects." Since worlds are nothing more than their component machines, and since machines can be unrelated, there can be multiple discrete assemblages.

**Second, there is no action at a distance.** For machines to interact they must be capable of touching in some way — directly (locality) or indirectly (through a mediating third machine). This introduces a triadic structure: p → q → r, where machine p affects machine r only through the medium of machine q. Interactions require media, and media take *time* to travel across space. A supernova millions of light-years away going off right now is, for all practical purposes, in a different world from ours — the light will not reach us within any human-relevant timespan. The rate at which mediators travel places constraints on both the size of worlds and the possibility of shared worldhood.

> "Where two entities are unable to interact we should conclude that they belong to two different worlds." (p. 119)

---

## 3. The Ontological Flatness of Worlds

Worlds are not *geometrically* flat but *ontologically* flat. No machine totalizes or conditions all others from a transcendent or hierarchical position. There is no God, no language, no Platonic form, no *cogito* that sits above the plane and determines all others. As Ian Bogost is cited: "all things equally exist, yet they do not exist equally" (p. 115). Machines are unequal among themselves in all kinds of ways — some exercise greater power and play more significant roles — but there is no ontological great chain of being. A God, if one exists, is a machine like any other: affected by other machines, contending with their resistances and tendencies.

The flatness also entails *anarchy*: there is no ultimate governing principle within a world, only immanent planes of machines affecting and being affected by one another. "Hence all ecologies are anarchic." (p. 116)

---

## 4. Worlds as Dynamic, Fuzzy, Historical

Worlds are not fixed structures or enumerable sets. Bryant invokes Timothy Morton's "mesh" — a dynamic and ever-changing result of machine interactions, fuzzy without clearly fixed boundaries. With each interaction between machines composing a world, the configuration changes. Events like revolutions, hurricanes, and stock-market crashes send ripples throughout all machines in a world. The diachronic dimension: machines have a *history*, they are produced, and things could be otherwise — a politically consequential claim.

Crucially, **worlds differ from machines**: a machine's elements are structurally coupled such that their separation entails destruction of the machine. A world's component machines are *separable* (external relations). Trees can be uprooted from one world to another; rocks can be blown off Mars and land on Earth. This is why both political revolutions and climate catastrophe are possible — relations between machines can be severed and reconfigured.

> "A political revolution is the complete transformation of social relations organizing a world. Such revolutions would not be possible unless relations between machines in a world could be severed and reconfigured." (p. 123)

---

## 5. Worlds Are Not Coterminous with Human Society

This is Bryant's anti-correlationist move at the world-scale. We are in a world — but the world is not *our* world, or even a human world. Bee worlds, factory worlds, river-system worlds, stellar worlds are all real ecologies of machines. The text makes the point negatively by showing what keeps worlds apart: since interaction requires material mediation, entities incapable of materially mediating each other's operations cannot belong to the same world. This is independent of whether humans observe, classify, or narrate those worlds.

Incorporation of incorporeal (semiotic) machines — language, law, classifications — does not make world dependent on human signification. On Neptune there is nothing but content (corporeal machines); there is no plane of expression. Planes of expression appear to be more widespread than human worlds alone — cetaceans, cephalopods, wolf packs, bees all develop forms of expression — but they are not constitutive of world as such.

---

## 6. Content and Expression Within Worlds

A major section draws on Deleuze and Guattari's reworking of Hjelmslev to distinguish two semi-autonomous planes present in many (not all) worlds:

- **Plane of content**: corporeal machines — bodies, actions, passions, intermingling of material entities.
- **Plane of expression**: incorporeal machines — semiotic transformations, signs, norms, classifications. Expression changes not what a machine *is* corporeally but how other machines *relate* to it. Lacan's two doors (Hommes / Dames) illustrate: the signs do not change the material constitution of the doors, but entirely restructure which bodies may pass through which door. "Incorporeal transformations transform not the thing itself, but rather how other machines relate to the machine or thing." (p. 129)

The two planes are real, autonomous, and non-reducible to each other. Content is not infrastructure and expression is not superstructure — both can outpace the other, as Bryant shows with the World War II case: corporeal transformations (women in factories) preceded expressive transformations (gender norms) by a generation.

Incorporation of signifiers, laws, judgments, and classifications are incorporeal transformations in this sense. Marriage, citizenship, racial categorization, guilty verdicts — all change social being without changing corporeal constitution.

Bryant uses this to criticize Luhmann: societies are not composed *solely* of communications. Technological media, roads, rivers, power lines, tornadoes, resources all play as significant a role in social form as communications do. "Communications are only one element among others in social assemblages." (p. 122)

## 7. Key Quotable Passages

1. **On what a world is** (p. 114): "*A world is an ecology of loosely coupled machines linked by machines without any of these machines totalizing world.*"

2. **On flatness and plurality** (p. 115–116): "There is no ultimate ground within a world for machines. There are only immanent planes of machines affecting and being affected by one another without a supplementary dimension that structures all their interactions. An ecology is a network of machines without a single governing principle. Hence all ecologies are anarchic."

3. **On action at a distance** (p. 119): "Where two entities are unable to interact we should conclude that they belong to two different worlds."

4. **On political revolution as world-restructuring** (p. 123): "A political revolution is the complete transformation of social relations organizing a world. Such revolutions would not be possible unless relations between machines in a world could be severed and reconfigured."

5. **On incorporeal transformations** (p. 129): "*Incorporeal transformations transform not the thing itself, but rather how other machines relate to the machine or thing.*"

6. **On Luhmann's error** (p. 122): "Communications are only one element among others in social assemblages."

7. **On ontological fetishism** (p. 118): "Onto-cartography, in part, seeks to overcome this ontological fetishism through the investigation of how worlds preside over the local manifestations of machines."

---

## 8. Chapter Argument in One Movement

Bryant moves from definitional exclusion (clearing away phenomenological, semiotic, and transcendental accounts of "world") to a positive account (world = flat, plural, anarchic ecology of loosely coupled machines) to internal structure (content/expression, synchronic/diachronic articulation) to political implication (things can be otherwise; onto-cartography maps what must be changed). The chapter is the ontological ground for the rest of the book's social-political applications.
