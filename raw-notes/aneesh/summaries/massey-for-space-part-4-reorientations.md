# Massey, *For Space*, Part Four: Reorientations (2005)

**Chapters**: 11 — "Slices through space"; 12 — "The elusiveness of place"
**Source**: Doreen Massey, *For Space*, Sage Publications 2005, pp. 103–145
**Author entity**: [[doreen-massey]]

---

## Context within the Book

Part Four follows Parts Two and Three, which were primarily critical: dismantling the reduction of space to representation, time, or a static surface. Part Four is constructive. Having cleared the ground, Massey builds her positive account of what space is and what place is — accounts that she presents as philosophically and politically inseparable.

---

## Chapter 11: "Slices through Space"

### The Map as Problem

Massey opens with her relationship to maps — their seductive ability to "carry you away" — and then turns them into a symptom of the very conceptual error she is fighting. Western maps present space as "a flat surface, a continuous surface. Space as the completed product. As a coherent closed system" (Massey 2005: 105). In the manner of structuralist synchrony, the map shows an order-in-things: static, instantly interconnected, surveyed from nowhere.

The postcolonial and feminist critiques of maps (Harley, Huggan, Rabasa) are noted and accepted so far as they go, but Massey finds them limited: deconstructing the map through the figure of the **palimpsest** still relies on a surface-imagination. Palimpsest suggests layers of suppressed pasts, but leaves intact the idea that space is fundamentally horizontal and stratified. It cannot adequately convey the **coevalness** — the simultaneous existence of multiple active trajectories — that Massey wants to restore.

### The Chance of Space

Space, on Massey's alternative account, is "the sphere of a dynamic simultaneity, constantly disconnected by new arrivals, constantly waiting to be determined (and therefore always undetermined) by the construction of new relations" (Massey 2005: 107). The key figure is **trajectories** rather than surfaces. What the map presents as a completed horizontal is, when properly imagined, a cross-section through multiple ongoing stories.

This reframing introduces the concept of **the chance of space**: the element of surprise, encounter, and unexpected juxtaposition that space necessarily carries when understood relationally. The accidental neighbour is Massey's figure for this: spatial configuration brings otherwise unconnected narratives into contact. Against a pure celebration of randomness (the situationist flâneur), Massey argues for a mixture — "labyrinthine clarity" in Aldo van Eyck's phrase — that is neither deterministic closure nor pure chaos. Architecture (Tschumi's Parc de la Villette project) reaches for this, but Massey criticises its remaining reliance on superimposed flat structures rather than on intertwined open-ended trajectories.

### Travelling Imaginations

The long middle section of Chapter 11 develops the thesis through travel examples: commuting from London to Milton Keynes; Hernán Cortés cresting the pass above Tenochtitlán; migrants imagining "home." Each shows how the dominant imagination of travel-as-crossing-a-surface conceals the more accurate account: one moves between and through ongoing stories. When Cortés descends upon Tenochtitlán, he is not "conquering space" — he is encountering an *other history* that has its own geographies and trajectories.

The **nostalgia** case is especially sharp. Going home means — on the dominant spatial imaginary — going back in time: the northern home is held in the amber of the moment one left it. But places change without the migrant. Massey's own trip home to south Lancashire, and the vignette of the chocolate cake her mother had changed to a new recipe, dramatises this: the claim to return-to-the-past robs others of their continuing histories. "You can't go back in space-time" (Massey 2005: 124).

The Raymond Williams passage — a woman in her pinny bending over a drain, frozen for the train passenger in an eternal instant — is Massey's sharpest image: the dominant spatial imaginary immobilises others, holds them still for "our" purposes. Thinking space as a sphere of multiple trajectories "brings the woman in the pinny to life" (Massey 2005: 118–119).

## Chapter 12: "The Elusiveness of Place"

### Migrant Rocks: The Lake District Argument

Chapter 12 opens with a one-paragraph summary of Massey's place-theory: "if space is rather a simultaneity of stories-so-far, then places are collections of those stories, articulations within the wider power geometries of space" (Massey 2005: 130). A friend persistently objects: but what about the mountains? Skiddaw seems immovable, timeless.

Massey reads the local geology. Skiddaw's rocks were laid down in a sea in the southern hemisphere approximately 500 million years ago. They have since drifted across the equator, been folded, eroded, covered, and only rose above sea level about 10 million years ago. Continental drift continues. The whole of northwest Britain is still rising after the removal of ice. The conclusion: **immigrant rocks** — "just passing through here, like my sister and me only rather more slowly, and changing all the while" (Massey 2005: 136). The geological move extends the constructive argument beyond human trajectories. Even apparently "timeless" nature is a trajectory-in-process. There is no stable ground — not even under the mountains.

### The Event of Place

> "what is special about place is precisely that throwntogetherness, the unavoidable challenge of negotiating a here-and-now (itself drawing on a history and a geography of thens and theres)" (Massey 2005: 140)

Place is an **event**: a coming-together of trajectories at a conjuncture. This is:

- **Open** (no fixed boundary)
- **Internally multiple** (not intrinsically coherent)
- **Not capturable as an essential cross-section** — the trajectories that constitute it have different temporalities
- **Necessitating invention** — since there is no pre-given community or identity, throwntogetherness demands negotiation

Massey draws on Latour to insist this includes nonhuman trajectories: Skiddaw and weekend tourism are both "histories/trajectories whose co-formation participates in the event of Keswick" (Massey 2005: 138).

The science parks boxed section (pp. 143–145) applies the concept concretely: science parks are "places as temporary constellations where the repercussions of a multiplicity of histories have been woven together" (Massey 2005: 144). Their apparent coherence conceals the interweaving of class struggles, gender politics, and legitimation of elite knowledge-production. "The containment is impossible."

## Key Thinkers Engaged

- **Harley, Huggan, Rabasa** — postcolonial critique of mapping; accepted but extended
- **Michel de Certeau** — critiqued for time/space binary embedded in his travel accounts
- **Tschumi** — architecture that reaches toward relational space but stays within horizontal superimposition
- **Eric Wolf** — "a people without history"; aligns with Massey's critique of spatialising the Other
- **Aldo van Eyck** — "labyrinthine clarity" as design-analogue to relational openness
- **Arif Dirlik** — right to bring ecology in, wrong to use nature as a source of fixity
- **Bruno Latour** — licenses the extension of place-as-event to nonhuman trajectories
- **Isabelle Stengers / Ilya Prigogine** — complexity theory; Massey borrows Zeitgeist but insists on spatial specificity
- **Wendy Wheeler** — nostalgia and postmodernity; needs reworking because it robs places of history

## Key Quotations

- "If you really were to take a slice through time it would be full of holes, of disconnections, of tentative half-formed first encounters." (Massey 2005: 107)
- "Loose ends and ongoing stories." (Massey 2005: 107)
- "In space as I want to imagine it, you just might [drive off the edge of the known world]." (Massey 2005: 111)
- "Immigrant rocks: the rocks of Skiddaw are immigrant rocks, just passing through here, like my sister and me only rather more slowly." (Massey 2005: 136)
- "All the essences become events" (Latour, cited Massey 2005: 139)
- "Place as event. [...] the potential event of place remains. The containment is impossible." (Massey 2005: 145)
