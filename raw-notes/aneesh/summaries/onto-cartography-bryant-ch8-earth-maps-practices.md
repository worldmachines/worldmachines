# Onto-Cartography Ch 8: Earth, Maps, and Practices — Summary

## Overview

Chapter 8 is the book's payoff: the pivot from ontology to politics. Having spent seven chapters establishing the machine ontology and its attendant vocabulary (bright objects, gravity, flows, local manifestations, virtual proper being), Bryant now asks: what is this framework *for*? The answer is a practice of geophilosophy — a planetary-scale materialism that provides a three-part toolkit for progressive political intervention: cartography, deconstruction, and terraformation. The chapter is also Bryant's most sustained engagement with the question of *scale*: the Earth, not the subject, not discourse, not capital-as-ghost, is the ultimate frame.

---

## 1. The Earth as Onto-Cartographic Frame

Bryant opens by settling his ontological bets: emergence materialism against part-materialism (pp. 234–236), flat/horizontal ontology against vertical/transcendence ontology (pp. 237–239). The Earth — the plane of immanence — is the non-transcendent horizon within which all machines operate. An ontology of immanence commits to "solely to the existence of earthly worlds, rejecting any transcendent beings" (p. 237). God, Platonic forms, the transcendental subject, linguistic idealism — all are vertical machines that overcode and therefore distort political analysis. Geophilosophy's first move is to insist that *there is only nature*, that culture is itself "a formation of nature" (p. 252), and that the nature/culture distinction is the primary impediment to adequate political and ecological thought.

Bryant draws on Jared Diamond (*Guns, Germs, and Steel*) to illustrate what geophilosophy enables: Diamond's explanation of why wealth and power were distributed as they are in the modern world — climate, latitude, domesticable animals, disease epidemiology — is precisely the kind of analysis that the nature/culture divide renders illegible (pp. 253–255). Social theory restricted to signs, ideologies, and discourse cannot ask Diamond's questions, and therefore cannot see the material machines that make certain forms of domination possible or inevitable.

The developmental systems theorists (DST) — Susan Oyama, Richard Lewontin, Kim Sterelny — provide Bryant's biological model for flat, horizontal causality: the organism is not a blueprint unfolding from DNA, but a developmental system in which DNA, RNA, protein, structural maturation, function, activity, experience, organism, and environment all interact bidirectionally (pp. 242–248). This DST model applies to social assemblages: social formations are not expressions of an underlying sovereign cause (capitalism-as-ghost, patriarchy-as-structure) but historically contingent developmental systems in which human and non-human machines mutually overdetermine one another.

## 2. Cartography: The Four Maps

The chapter's most practically detailed section (pp. 257–266) enumerates four map types, each serving a distinct political function:

**Topographical maps** are snapshots of assemblages at a point in time. They inventory the machines that compose an ecology (human and non-human, corporeal and incorporeal), map hierarchical relations between them, identify bright objects and their satellites, trace flows of energy and matter, and map the material outputs and waste of machine operations. Bryant's key injunction: "avoid abstractions!" (p. 257). "Capitalism," "patriarchy," "racism" are shorthand for assemblages — they are things to be *explained*, not explanatory agencies. The cartographer asks how *this* assemblage is put together, not how to overcome the ghost.

**Genetic maps** chart the historical genesis of how a world came to be. Marx's *Capital*, Braudel, Darwin, DeLanda are all exemplary genetic cartographers (p. 263). Two functions: first, a Husserlian epoché on the present — by showing worlds where clocks, for example, were absent, the genetic map defamiliarizes our own and makes visible the machinic agencies we otherwise overlook; second, it reveals *contingency*: "things do not have to be this way and that they can be otherwise" (p. 264). Genetic cartography is not just historical curiosity — it is directed at the future, opening a space of political imagination.

**Vector maps** chart the directions along which worlds are currently unfolding based on gravitational tendencies in the present (pp. 264–266). James Hansen's climate projections and Marx's vector of capitalism toward wage depression are exemplary. Vector maps give activists and theorists a glimpse of the future, allowing them to determine *where* to intervene in the present rather than merely responding to immediate demands. Without vector maps, activists fight for goals that inadvertently reinforce existing gravitational structure — the example of teachers fighting for "educational excellence" without understanding that standardized testing feeds a capitalist ecology of assessment companies (p. 265).

**Modal maps** chart possible futures — futures that *could* exist if particular deconstructions or terraformations were enacted (pp. 266–267). Where vector maps say "here is where we are heading," modal maps say "here is a world we could build if we subtract or add these machines." Modal maps are the domain of activists, generals, and engineers. They synthesize topographical and vector analyses into actionable strategies. The environmental activist's modal map: given a vector toward ecological collapse, what changes to the assemblage — which machines removed, which added — would produce a survivable future?

## 3. Deconstruction: Severing Relations

Deconstruction (pp. 267–273) is the practice of severing relations — both semiotic/expressive and corporeal/material — that sustain oppressive assemblages.

*Expressive deconstruction* operates on signifying machines: Judith Butler's *Gender Trouble* shows that gender identities are not natural kinds but performative scripts; Althusser's analysis of ideological state apparatuses shows institutions serving functions other than their apparent ones. Both challenge the legitimacy of existing semiotic assemblages.

*Material (corporeal) deconstruction* is Bryant's distinctive contribution. It severs energetic and physical dependencies — not discourses. The person locked into fossil fuel dependence not by false consciousness but by material necessity (no money for solar panels, construction job requiring large vehicles, food shipped from afar) is not freed by ideology critique but by material deconstruction of the energy dependencies that constrain them (pp. 270–271). Bryant's exemplar is **rolling jubilee**: the Occupy Wall Street practice of purchasing debt from creditors for pennies on the dollar and then forgiving it. Debt functions as a gravitational mechanism — a bright object that organizes life, diminishes spatial mobility, suppresses activism, funnels income to creditors. Rolling jubilee literally severs this gravitational relation, opening possibilities for movement, education, and political organization that debt foreclosed (pp. 271–273).

Caution: material deconstruction can have unintended consequences. The cane toad introduced into Northern Australia to prey on insects that were ravaging sugar cane was wildly successful at eliminating the insects — and equally destructive to the broader ecosystem, lacking natural predators (p. 273). Good modal maps must account for contingency as much as for desired outcomes.

## 4. Terraformation: Building Worlds

Terraformation (pp. 273–279) is the construction of new assemblages — adding machines to the world to open new paths of movement and becoming. Where deconstruction subtracts, terraformation adds. Bryant's example is **LETS (Local Economic Transaction Systems)**: a mutual credit system in which participants earn credits for services and redeem them for others' services, with the sum of gains and losses always zero (pp. 276–278). LETS is a terraformation because it constructs an alternative energetic and economic ecology that allows people to meet needs outside the gravitational field of capitalist wage-labor. Crucially, LETS does not confront capitalism head-on; it constructs an alternative assemblage that sidesteps capitalist gravity, a "flight in place" (p. 278).

Critical theory's failure, in Bryant's diagnosis, is that it stops at deconstruction:

> Phase 1: Radical Critique  
> Phase 2: ?  
> Phase 3: Revolutionary Society! (p. 274)

Without terraformation — without a picture of what alternatives look like and how to build them — even sound critiques leave people trapped in the gravitational web of assemblages they recognize as unjust. One aim of geophilosophy is to encourage more thought and work devoted to terraformation (p. 275).

## 5. The Eco-Political Conclusion

Bryant's closing synthesis (pp. 279–286) defines the threefold aim of onto-cartography:

**First**, to multiply points of social and political intervention by attending to non-human machines. Continental theory has been dominated by "the discursive and first-person experience of subjects" (p. 279). Onto-cartography supplements, not replaces, discourse analysis by attending to how things — clocks, debt, fossil fuels, mountains — exercise gravity over human assemblages.

**Second**, to comprehend social assemblages as ecologies rather than as domains distinct from nature. This requires developing a "thermopolitics" and "thermoethics" — a political thought attentive to work and energy, which are "almost entirely absent from the history of philosophy" (p. 280). As climate change intensifies, understanding social assemblages in terms of energy flows and material waste becomes not optional but urgent.

**Third**, a plea for the concrete. The move against reified abstractions — "capitalism," "patriarchy," "racism" as sovereign ghosts — in favor of attending to *specific assemblages* and *specific machines* (p. 282). Marx and Foucault are the exemplary cartographical politicians: their maps are maps of *particular* social ecologies under *particular* historical conditions, not timeless templates. "The paradoxical danger of cartographical analyses is that they can blind us to the new forms social ecologies are taking. We forget that the map is not the territory" (p. 285).

Bryant acknowledges what Marx and Foucault cannot do: their human exceptionalism — the assumption that the project is ultimately about *human* emancipation — forecloses the broader ecological engagement that climate collapse demands. The framework needed must "synthesize the findings of the linguistic turn, Marxist thought, Foucaultian thought, media theorists such as McLuhan, Kittler, and Ong, as well as the post-humanist thought of the ecologists, the new materialists, the actor-network theorists, and the work of thinkers such as Diamond and Braudel" (p. 286). Onto-cartography is Bryant's attempt to provide this framework.

## Quotable Passages

1. "The first and most important principle of onto-cartography lies in the dictum: 'avoid abstractions!' Big umbrella terms such as 'capitalism,' 'patriarchy,' 'heterosexism,' 'religion,' 'society,' 'racism,' 'ontotheology,' 'culture,' and so on do more to obscure than explain." (p. 257)

2. "These terms are all shorthand for *assemblages*. They are generic terms for extremely complex relations between machines in worlds. It is those *assemblages*, the machines that compose those assemblages, that cause things, not these terms." (p. 258)

3. "The first step in producing any sort of change or new assemblages lies in having a good map." (p. 257)

4. "Geophilosophy is composed of three interrelated practices: cartography, deconstruction, and terraformation. These practices are undertaken not simply for the sake of understanding worlds, but more fundamentally for the sake of producing more just, equitable, and sustainable worlds." (p. 256–257)

5. "This is the basic thesis of onto-cartography and geophilosophy. If we are to understand why assemblages take the form they take, we must investigate how corporeal and incorporeal, human and non-human, inorganic, organic, and social machines intersect and interact with one another." (p. 255)

6. "The paradoxical danger of cartographical analyses is that they can blind us to the new forms social ecologies are taking. We forget that the map is not the territory and that, in any event, territories change." (p. 285)

7. "It is astonishing that the concepts of work and energy are almost entirely absent from the history of philosophy... energy — whether or not it is available, whether or not one has it — is one of the primary mechanisms by which power (in the political sense) or gravity exercises itself within social assemblages." (p. 280)

8. "Rather than fighting the existing assemblage, one simply flees it and sets up camp elsewhere. As we see in the case of LETS, this flight need not entail movement from one node to another in space. Rather it can be a flight in place, right there within the same geographical region of the other assemblage." (p. 278)

## Tensions and Self-Criticisms Bryant Acknowledges

Bryant is aware that onto-cartography faces structural limits:

- **The map is not the territory**: Cartographical analyses can become obstacles, blinding the cartographer to new forms a social ecology is taking by over-applying inherited maps (p. 285). Marx and Foucault are both exemplary cartographers and cautionary tales.
- **Unintended consequences of deconstruction**: The cane toad problem — severing one relation can cascade unpredictably through the assemblage (p. 273).
- **Partial knowledge of complex systems**: Good topographical maps require identifying the right machines, and the "central actors of these assemblages will be surprising" (p. 259). Crude maps miss non-human agencies.
- **Human exceptionalism in heritage frameworks**: Even the best available Marxist and Foucaultian analyses remain too anthropocentric to be fully adequate to ecological politics (p. 285).
