# On Being Good vs. Knowing Good: Perspectives on AI

**Source**: [aneeshsathe.com, 2024-01-22](https://aneeshsathe.com/being-good-vs-knowing-good-ai/)
**Author**: [[Aneesh Sathe]]

## Central Argument

LLMs exhibit confidence but lack *taste* — the capacity to distinguish good from bad, to guide toward optimal outcomes, to know when to question the goal itself. This is not a fixable bug but a structural consequence of how they are built: without real-world experience, there is no mechanism for taste to develop. Sathe frames expertise as having two components — knowledge (which AI can approximate) and taste (which it cannot). The practical implication: AI is excellent for eliminating tedious tasks but unsuitable as a genuine thinking partner or advisor.

## Key Claims

1. **The tool/advisor distinction** — AI as spreadsheet eliminates tedious work while preserving the human's judgment about what to do with the output. AI as advisor requires judgment the system does not have.
2. **Taste is experience-dependent** — just as arithmetic precedes calculators and calculators precede spreadsheets, each tool layer assumes the human has internalized the prior layer. LLMs skip this ladder.
3. **Nick Cave's intuition is correct for the wrong reasons** — Cave objects to AI as a shortcut to creativity on artistic/spiritual grounds. Sathe agrees, but locates the problem epistemically: shortcuts bypass the formation of judgment.
4. **Healthcare as the stress test** — in mission-critical fields, the stakes of misapplied AI are highest. Sathe calls for balancing exploration and regulation: over-regulation locks out beneficial innovation; under-regulation exposes vulnerable populations.
5. **Selective environment drives quality** — users who can recognize what is good will raise the floor. The analogy is clinical quality improvement driven by discerning practitioners.
