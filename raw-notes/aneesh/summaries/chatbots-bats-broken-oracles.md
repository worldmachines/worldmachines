# Chatbots, Bats & Broken Oracles

**Source**: https://aneeshsathe.com/chatbots-bats-broken-oracles/
**Author**: [[Aneesh Sathe]]
**Date**: 2025-07-05

## Central Argument

LLMs represent a qualitative shift in human-computer interaction: for the first time, computers can serve as Socratic interlocutors. Previous computing solved *problems* (which dissolve on solution); LLMs create a surface for pursuing *questions* (which open into quests for understanding). The ability to hallucinate, paradoxically, is an asset in this context — it creates exploratory surface area.

## Key Distinctions

### Problems vs. Questions (after [[Agnes Callard]])
- **Problems**: Barriers blocking a goal. Solved by technology; dissolve once solved. (Recipe → congee problem disappears.)
- **Questions**: Demands for a new understanding of the world. No established method; begin a *quest*. Science's perpetual dissatisfaction with existing answers is the stance that pursues questions.

### Pre-answered Questions
- Questions that arrive already answered — we hold them as settled without having examined them.
- Example: "What is it to be a good scientist?" — the scientist rushes to provide standard answers without interrogating them.
- Socratic dialogue forces articulation and thereby destabilizes the pre-answer.

## The Turing Test Detour

- [[Alan Turing]] reframed "What is thinking?" (a question) into "Can we detect thought?" (a problem) — a characteristic move of reducing questions to problems.
- LLMs pass the Turing test but we remain unconvinced — evidence that the original *question* of thinking was not dissolved by the test.

## The Bat Analogy (after [[Thomas Nagel]])
- Nagel's "What is it like to be a bat?" exemplifies Socratic inquiry on an unsolvable problem: you generate crude approaches (clicking in dark rooms), which create surface area for criticism and further inquiry.
- The crude approach is better than nothing if the goal is inquiry rather than solution.
- Thinking = a social quest for better answers to questions that already appear answered.

## AI as Socratic Partner

Conditions that make LLMs viable as Socratic co-pilots:
1. **Humanness from training data** — shared enough worldview to provide normative mirror (unlike a bat).
2. **Infinite patience** — no internal urge to win.
3. **Knowledge access** — growing reference databases for grounding; hallucination acceptable until then.
4. **Conversational affordance** — back-and-forth in natural language enables genuine question-posing.

## Key Metaphor
> "Each word we put in ties down the AI to specific vantage points to help us explore."

This links directly to the [[Space and Place]] framework developed in the companion essays: words create *places* in the pure space of possibility.

## What's Missing / Not Addressed
- No treatment of power asymmetries in AI-as-Socrates (who curates the "established knowledge"?)
- Does not address whether Socratic dialogue requires genuine disagreement or merely the *appearance* of it.
- The analogy to Nagel's bat is evocative but understates how much Nagel's argument depends on first-person phenomenal consciousness — which LLMs arguably lack.
