# Massey, *For Space* — Part One: Setting the Scene

**Source**: Doreen Massey, *For Space* (London: Sage, 2005), pp. 1–35
**Chunk**: Part One — Setting the Scene (~11k words)

## Overview

Part One is both a diagnostic and a manifesto. Massey opens with three "ruminations" — concrete political-spatial failures — then in Chapter 1 distils three propositions that will govern the rest of the book. The Part closes by previewing her philosophical adversaries (Bergson, structuralism, deconstruction) and making clear that the project is essentially *political*: a badly imagined space generates badly imagined politics.

---

## The Three Ruminations (pp. 1–8)

### Rumination 1: The Cortés/Tenochtitlán Encounter (pp. 2–5)

Massey opens with the November 1519 meeting of Hernán Cortés's army and Moctezuma's Aztec city, narrated from both sides. The Aztecs worked with a cosmological time-space of "place-moments" (*lugares momentos*): direction, time, historical cycle were fused. Cortés carried an incipient Western imaginary of space as a surface to be traversed and conquered.

Her critical move: **the "voyages of discovery" narrative immobilises the Aztecs.** By treating space as a surface on which Cortés moves and Tenochtitlán lies still, the story strips the Aztecs of their own histories and trajectories:

> "So easily this way of imagining space can lead us to conceive of other places, peoples, cultures simply as phenomena 'on' this surface. It is not an innocent manoeuvre, for by this means they are deprived of histories. Immobilised, they await Cortes' (or our, or global capital's) arrival. They lie there, on space, in place, without their own trajectories." (Massey 2005: 4)

The Aztec cosmology — Townsend (1992) and Soustelle (1956) — recognised only "concrete complexes of space and time, heterogeneous and singular sites and events... 'place-moments'" (Massey 2005: 6). The *Codex Xolotl* represents space and time together as itineraries of footsteps, not Euclidean grids. Massey reads this as a proto-resource for her own thesis: already thinking space as a product of trajectories, not a container.

### Rumination 2: Globalisation as Reduction of Space to Time (pp. 4–5)

Clinton's 1998 claim that resistance to neoliberal globalisation is as futile as resisting gravity is Massey's exemplary case of a spatial sleight-of-hand: the rhetoric of inevitability converts geography into history, space into time. Countries like Mozambique and Nicaragua are rendered not as contemporaries with distinct trajectories but as earlier stages of a single historical queue.

> "The proposition turns geography into history, space into time... That cosmology of 'only one narrative' obliterates the multiplicities, the contemporaneous heterogeneities of space. It reduces simultaneous coexistence to place in the historical queue." (Massey 2005: 5)

If difference is temporal (they are behind), resistance is illegitimate (you cannot fight the future). This is the political stakes of spatial imagination.

### Rumination 3: The Ambiguity of Place (pp. 5–7)

"Local place" has become a political fetish: celebrated by some as the sphere of authentic community, rejected by others as the locus of nationalist exclusion. Both readings share the assumption that place is closed, coherent, bounded against the exterior. Massey refuses both and the implicit space/place binary they reproduce. She asks: what if place too were reconceived as relational and open?

## The Three Propositions (Chapter 1, pp. 9–15)

See [[the-three-propositions]] for the full manifesto. In brief:

1. **Space as Product of Interrelations** — Space is not a pre-given container; it is constituted through practices of relation. See [[space-as-relational-product]].
2. **Space as Coexisting Heterogeneity** — Space is the sphere of contemporaneous plurality. "Without space, no multiplicity; without multiplicity, no space." See [[coexisting-heterogeneity]].
3. **Space Always Under Construction** — Space is never finished or closed; it is "a simultaneity of stories-so-far." See [[space-as-always-under-construction]], [[simultaneity-of-stories-so-far]].

## The Diagnostic Frame: Implicit Imaginations of Space (pp. 7–8, 13–15)

The unifying diagnostic: **implicit imaginations of space have political effects**. The voyages-of-discovery cosmology, the neoliberal reduction of space to time, the nationalist place-fetish — all operate through *unthought* spatial conceits embedded in practice. The book's project is to make these explicit and propose an alternative:

> "What is needed, I think, is to uproot 'space' from that constellation of concepts in which it has so unquestioningly so often been embedded (stasis; closure; representation) and to settle it among another set of ideas (heterogeneity; relationality; coevalness... liveliness indeed) where it releases a more challenging political landscape." (Massey 2005: 13)

## The Bergson Thread (Part Two opener, pp. 17–25)

Part Two opens with Bergson, treated as a structural enemy of space despite being an ally on time. Bergson's strategy of defending the openness of duration required characterising space as its negative other: static, discrete, the result of "spatialising" the living temporal flow. Massey's counter: the slide from *spatialisation* (an activity) to *space* (a dimension) is illegitimate. Bergson's own later recognition of duration in external things points toward exactly what Massey is arguing:

> "It is what I am calling space as the dimension of multiple trajectories, a simultaneity of stories-so-far. Space as the dimension of a multiplicity of durations." (Massey 2005: 24)

Laclau performs an analogous move: spatialisation = hegemonisation = ideological closure. De Certeau too. Each effectively puts space permanently on the wrong side of politics.
