# Better Company Than Caesar

**Source**: Aneesh Sathe, *Better Company Than Caesar* (2025-08-11) — https://aneeshsathe.com/better-company-than-caesar/
**Author**: [[Aneesh Sathe]]

## Central Argument

Two impulses toward polymathism exist and they are morally distinct. The first — Caesar's — is performative: it seeks to be *seen as* accomplished in a field outside one's competence. The second — the essay's recommended alternative — is curiosity-driven and directed inward: the pleasure of knowing and doing well, indifferent to reputation. Montaigne (and through him Bertrand Russell) is invoked to distinguish the mania of the mover-and-shaker from the regenerative joy of genuine intellectual range. Curiosity, not ego-extension, is "better company than Caesar."

## Key Claims

1. **Montaigne's observation is ancient and recurring** — Caesar wanted to be known as an engineer, not a general. Dionysus the Elder wanted to be a poet, not a commander. The mania transcends era.
2. **The pattern is alive in modern elites** — CEOs and investors who publicly posture as physicists or engineers, "rather bad" at the field they court.
3. **The key distinction: being seen vs. wanting to know** — Sathe draws a line between performative polymathism and genuine curiosity. The former is an ego operation; the latter is a joy operation.
4. **Burnout reversed by widening attention** — personal testimony: end-of-2023 burnout broken not by rest but by the ability to "focus my mind on reading new things." The Contraptions book club as recovery mechanism.
5. **Russell's formula** — "The secret of happiness is this: let your interests be as wide as possible, and let your reactions to the things and persons that interest you be as far as possible friendly rather than hostile."
6. **Popova's "unselfing"** — Maria Popova on Bertrand Russell: happiness requires dissolution into something beyond the ego, "wakefulness to an aspect of the world other than myself."

## Intellectual Lineage

- [[Michel de Montaigne]] → personal essay as the form for interrogating one's own impulses
- [[Bertrand Russell]] → *The Conquest of Happiness* (1930) as guide to curiosity-based flourishing
- [[Maria Popova]] → "unselfing" as a name for the mechanism

## Historical Figures as Negative Examples

- **Caesar** — wanted to be known as an engineer; the essay's negative exemplar
- **Dionysus the Elder** — military leader who pursued poetry despite little skill
- **Periander** — doctor who abandoned his medical reputation for bad poetry (cited by Archidamus)

## What's Missing

- No engagement with whether the distinction holds under social pressure — is purely inward curiosity actually possible for public figures?
- The burnout-recovery narrative is asserted rather than analyzed; the mechanism linking reading to recovery is not theorized
- No critique of Montaigne's own position: was his essayism also partly performative?
