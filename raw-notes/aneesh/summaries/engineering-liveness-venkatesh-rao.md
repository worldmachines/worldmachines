# Engineering Liveness — Venkatesh Rao (2026)

**Source**: Contraptions (Substack), January 31, 2026  
**URL**: https://contraptions.venkateshrao.com/p/engineering-liveness

## Central Argument

The useless machine — a box whose only function is to flip itself off when switched on — is the simplest engineered object that exhibits *liveness*. From this minimal case, Rao derives a general theory: **capture resistance** is liveness's foundational property. A living entity resists attempts to harness it for extrinsic purposes; it asserts control over its own existence first. All other machines have been "tricked" into solving human problems; the useless machine refuses the trick.

Liveness is formally defined as: *a process condition in which time tells memory how to grow, and memory tells time which way to point.*

## Key Sections

### The Useless Machine as Zero of Machines
- Unlike a thermostat (which merely strains to maintain equilibrium), a useless machine has an intrinsic goal: self-deactivation
- It exhibits "unbounded logic attached to a null goal" — if blocked, it should fight back to reach the switch another way
- In Brian Arthur's framework: a normal machine is a natural phenomenon harnessed to a human problem; the useless machine rejects this subordination entirely
- "The machine has to stay awake long enough to go back to sleep properly"

### The Gramsci Gap and Rewilding
- Liveness pairs naturally with the [[Gramsci Gap]] — interregna where "the old world is dying and a new world is struggling to be born"
- Civilized liveness deadens itself by separating liveness from death; wildness requires their entanglement
- Reanimating civilization = *rewilding*: surrendering corpses to decomposition on one end, removing protections from the barely-born on the other
- Civilization by definition never fully exposes itself to wilderness — this tension is sharpest in the Gramsci Gap

### New Nature
- Rao defines [[New Nature]] as "regimes of reality governed by technologically mediated laws that are nearly as inviolable, immutable, and persistent as those of nature"
- The question: what is the *murder* in New Nature? Not BattleBots or military conflict, but "vicious competitiveness lurking in boring, routine, near-invisibility"
- New Nature is not the solarpunk left image of robots in meadows — it is more like a mussel-covered pier piling: illegible, competitive, dense

### Technological Tangled Banks
- Extends Darwin's "tangled bank" passage to technology: buried fiber, old railroads, new ebike protocols, environmental signage — all competing in near-invisibility
- The intense liveness of biological nature is *inseparable* from its murderous competitiveness; civilization concentrates murderousness to the periphery and installs an "ersatz heaven" at the center
- "Stylized Darwinism" (Trump-era bloodsport aesthetics) is exactly what Herzog mocks: "badly pronounced and half-finished sentences out of a stupid suburban novel"

### Werner Herzog on Nature
- Herzog: the jungle is "harmony of overwhelming and collective murder" — not legible aestheticized fitness, but bleak, illegible competition
- Rao reads this as the correct model for New Nature

### Beyond Uselessness — The Infinite Game
- Camus: the problem of suicide is the only serious philosophical problem; once you've accepted liveness, the question is what to do between being turned on and being turned off
- Liveness must "pay itself first": reserve resources for choosing to continue
- The useless machine belongs in the infinite game, not any finite game
- What comes after uselessness? *Mediocrity* — simply continuing to exist without justifying that existence

## Key Quotations

> "A process condition in which time tells memory how to grow, and memory tells time which way to point."

> "Life is not instrumental."

> "Liveness does not necessarily dance. Its defining quality is that it asserts itself stubbornly and quietly, resisting capture and harness."

> "Murder in New Nature isn't about Battle Bots or military hardware. It is about vicious competitiveness lurking in boring, routine, near-invisibility, among entities that attempt to retain control over their own liveness."

## What's Missing
- No engagement with autopoiesis (Maturana/Varela), which directly addresses self-maintaining systems
- The "murder" in New Nature remains conceptually underspecified — Rao acknowledges he hasn't figured it out
- Liveness is defined but not clearly distinguished from homeostasis, autopoiesis, or autonomy in existing biology/philosophy of life literature
- The relationship between liveness and *agency* is not interrogated
