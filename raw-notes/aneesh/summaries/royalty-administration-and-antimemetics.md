# Royalty, Administration, and Antimemetics

**Source**: Aneesh Sathe, *Royalty, Administration, and Antimemetics* (2025-07-12) — https://aneeshsathe.com/royalty-administration-and-antimemetics/

## Central Argument

Royalty is inherently *memetic* — it projects power through visible, memorable acts (architecture, ceremony, divine legitimacy). The administrative machinery that actually runs empires is *antimemetic* — high-impact but designed to be invisible, unattributable, and hard to hold accountable. This diffusion of responsibility protects administrators from popular wrath and ensures the king remains the lightning rod.

## Key Concept: Antimemetics in Governance

An [[Antimeme]] is an idea with high real-world impact but low spreadability — it resists being remembered, attributed, or spread. Administrative structures are structurally antimemetic:
- Diffuse responsibility across many officials
- Use formal titles that obscure actual power ("merely recorded imperial decisions")
- Operate within legal/religious interpretation rather than visible legislation

## Historical Case Studies

### Mughal Empire
The Mughal administrative structure was a matrix organization: provincial officials (bakhshi, sadr as-sudr, finance minister) reported to central government rather than to the provincial governor (subahdar). This dual-reporting structure diffused responsibility and made the locus of decision-making opaque to outsiders. See [[Mughal Administration]].

### Ming Dynasty — Hongwu Emperor
After abolishing the Central Secretariat to consolidate personal power, the Hongwu Emperor was overwhelmed by correspondence and appointed "grand secretaries" who officially only "recorded imperial decisions." In practice, they wielded enormous power with complete deniability. See [[Ming Dynasty Grand Secretaries]].

### Al-Andalus, Ottomans, Safavids, Mughals — The Ulama
The [[Ulama]] shaped legal systems and public morality by *interpreting* royal decrees and applying them as daily law. This interpretive authority, operating within the bureaucracy rather than above it, was profoundly antimemetic: influence without attribution.

## The Defenestration of Prague as Frame
The essay opens with the [[Defenestration of Prague]] (1618): royal representatives thrown from windows for the mismanagement of others. The image captures how memetic visibility makes rulers scapegoatable regardless of actual culpability. The administrators who caused the suffering remain invisible; the visible figures take the fall.

## Yes, Minister as Modern Analog
The dialogue between Hacker and Sir Humphrey illustrates modern administrative antimemetics: civil servants retire into "obscurity" while wielding real power, receiving index-linked pensions, and escaping accountability ("if you screw things up, I get the boot"). See [[Yes, Minister]].

## What the Author Doesn't Address

- Whether digital-age transparency tools (FOIA, leaks, social media) have disrupted the antimemetic equilibrium.
- The tension between efficient bureaucracy and democratic accountability.
- How antimemetic structures relate to modern corporate governance (e.g., board vs. management diffusion of responsibility).
