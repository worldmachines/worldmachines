# The Best Game Ever Made

**Source**: Aneesh Sathe, *The Best Game Ever Made* (2025-07-13) — https://aneeshsathe.com/the-best-game-ever-made/

## Central Argument

Dwarf Fortress is not primarily a game in the conventional sense — it is a complete world simulation designed to generate emergent stories. Its value lies not in winning (there is no win state) but in the texture of life and catastrophe it enables. The essay frames DF as an act of sustained human audacity: built over 20 years, without institutional support, by brothers who prioritized simulation depth over commercial viability.

## Key Claims

- DF is the most complex game ever created, simulating everything from tree growth rates to individual body parts (enabling, famously, cats that get drunk).
- The design philosophy is story-generation first: "losing is fun" because failure produces the most interesting narratives.
- DF is in MoMA's permanent collection, and its acquisition changed how MoMA approaches software preservation.
- Tarn Adams built DF while completing a PhD, supported initially only by donations, eventually going full-time.
- The Steam release (with publisher Bay 12 Games partnering with Kitfox Games) made the brothers "overnight millionaires" — "that night was 20 years long."
- The game was estimated at ~44% completion by Tarn Adams. The percentage likely hasn't changed as new ideas continue to expand the target.

## Thematic Frames

### Complexity from Simple Origins
DF began as a sequence of simple experiments by two brothers who made games as kids. No external funding, no commercial aim. The goal was always simulation depth so the game could "tell great stories." See [[Emergent Complexity]].

### The No-Win Condition as Design Philosophy
DF has no victory state. "Losing is FUN!" This inverts conventional game design and reframes failure as generative rather than terminal. See [[Failure as Generativity]].

### Community as Collaborative Infrastructure
Fans contributed art, music, and utility software. Many are now full-time team members. The community around DF exemplifies organic open-source collaboration without formal coordination. See [[Community-Built Creative Works]].

### Audacity as a Recurring Theme
The essay returns twice to the phrase: "To play Dwarf Fortress is to experience audacity." The audacity is not heroic bravado but sustained, unglamorous commitment to a vision across two decades of normal human hardship.

## What the Author Doesn't Address

- Whether DF's design philosophy is reproducible or whether it required an unusually specific personal situation (brothers, no dependents initially, academic environment).
- The tension between "44% complete" and shipping a v1.0 — when is creative completionism a virtue vs. a pathology?
- DF's inaccessibility (ASCII graphics, brutal learning curve) as a feature rather than a bug.
