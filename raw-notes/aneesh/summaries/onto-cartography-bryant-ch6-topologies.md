# Summary: Bryant, *Onto-Cartography* Ch. 6 — "Topologies of Space and Time" (pp. 140–183)

## Chapter Arc

Chapter 6 is the most expansive chapter of *Onto-Cartography*, making good on the book's central methodological claim: that mapping worlds requires not Cartesian geometry but topology — the science of nearness, distance, and connectivity as *produced* by relations between machines. Bryant opens by stressing that space and time are inseparable ("spatio-temporality"), each always having the dimension of the other, and closes with a theory of overdetermination that explains why no local manifestation of a machine can be attributed to a single cause.

The chapter unfolds across four large movements: (1) ontology of topological space; (2) the temporal topology of machines; (3) overdetermination; (4) two ethical prescriptions for onto-cartography. A section on "geophilosophy" — worlds as having their own irreducible geographies — threads through all of them.

---

## I. Space as Topology, Not Container

Bryant opens from Lucretius and Michel Serres: machines are vortexes, dynamic stabilities arising from flow rather than fixed substances. Stability is the puzzle, not motion. Space is not an empty container (the Newtonian picture) but a *milieu of paths and flows* produced by machines interacting with one another.

The crucial distinction is **Newtonian vs. topological space**:

- **Newtonian space**: a homogeneous, pre-existent container with invariant metric distances. Austin, TX is necessarily closer to Cairo than it is to New York by this measure because there is "a constant metric that grids all space" (p. 144).
- **Topological space**: space *arises from* machines. "Space is conceived of as a network of paths between machines or nodes produced by machines" (p. 144). Distance is not metric but relational: it is measured in the number of paths, nodes, and mediating machines one must traverse to reach another.

Bryant illustrates with three network typologies (Figure 6.1): centralized, decentralized, and distributed — each producing different proximity and distance relations among the same set of nodes, regardless of their Euclidean positions.

> "In a topological conception of space, matters are very different. Where a Newtonian conception of space conceives space as a *pre-existent* container in which machines are housed, a topological conception of space treats space as *arising from* machines." (p. 144)

The Atlantic City casino example is central: casinos were built on the premise that metric proximity to low-income neighborhoods would create economic spillover. But topologically, the casinos constructed a self-enclosed network — visitors drove directly to the casino, spent money within it, and left. The surrounding city was *topologically remote* despite being metrically adjacent. Metric proximity was insufficient for economic relation.

Bryant draws on Doreen Massey's *For Space* (2005) as an ally: space is "the product of interrelations," "the sphere of the possibility of the existence of multiplicity," and "always in the process of being made — never finished, never closed" (Massey 2005: 9, p. 147).

Topological fields are milieus of both movement and becoming. A machine placed in a new topological node will encounter different inputs, different structural couplings, different semiotic machines — and will become differently as a result. Kim Stanley Robinson's *Red Mars* illustrates this: colonists' children grow taller and differently muscled because they develop in Mars's lower gravity, a different topological-machinic field (p. 150).

> "It is high time for critical and emancipatory theory to become geographical." (p. 157)

## II. Paths as Natural, Technological, and Expressive

Paths are not simply physical roads. They include semiotic machines: the "Men's"/"Women's" signs on Lacan's doors segregate bodies into two different spaces; passports open or close national paths; the DSM-IV generates treatment paths through naming and categorization; national borders constitute expressive machines that keep metrically proximate people topologically distant. Animals marking territory similarly structure paths for other animals.

Feedback (positive and negative) and structural coupling explain why topological distributions persist. Negative feedback maintains organizations (thermostats, predator-prey equilibria, wealth attracting wealth). Positive feedback spins organizations into disequilibrium — which Serres, pace Bryant, shows can generate new order rather than simply disorder. Crucially, Bryant insists these are *descriptive not normative* categories: "harmony" is not necessarily good.

## III. Temporal Topology — Multiple Times

Onto-cartography rejects Kant's unitary time just as it rejects Newtonian unitary space. *Every machine has its own temporality*: "time is the rate at which a machine can engage in operations" (p. 158). There is a plurality of heterogeneous times that never unify.

Three qualifications on Kantian temporality:
1. Onto-cartography *pluralizes* temporalities across all machines, not just rational subjects.
2. Time is not one container but many; different times do not necessarily add up to a whole.
3. Time is also the *duration* of a machine's outer existence — subatomic particles vs. galaxies.

Machines are selectively open to temporal flows at specific *rates*. Humans perceive at ~60 Hz; houseflies at ~200 Hz; honeybees at ~300 Hz (Mineault 2011, p. 159). The fly registers motion the human cannot; information, as Bateson defines it, is "a difference which makes a difference" — and whether something can function as information depends entirely on the rate at which the machine can register it.

> "The thesis of onto-cartography is that time is the rate at which a machine can engage in operations." (p. 158)

The **politics of temporal mismatch** runs throughout. Climate change happens at a geological rate that human perceptual and institutional machines cannot register as information. US political institutions — representatives on two-year terms, senators on six-year terms, presidents on four-year terms — are temporally coupled to the news media machine, which selects only *deviation from the norm* as information, thus favoring controversy over long-term planning. The two temporal structures reinforce each other to produce systematic incapacity for slow-process response.

The Braudel example is equally sharp: wheat has one harvest per year and structures European societies around intense planting/harvest periods with long fallow intervals — necessitating food preservation technologies and enabling family-farm agriculture. Rice has two to three harvests per year and is labor-intensive, pushing societies toward collective farming and stratification around a priestly/noble class managing rice reserves (p. 162). Agricultural grain temporality organizes social structure.

> "The rate at which a machine can register inputs is also its capacity for encountering events in its environment as information." (p. 159)

### Memory and Topological Time

Memory introduces topological folding of time itself. Serres's crumpled handkerchief: points metrically distant in linear time can become topologically proximate through memory. Freud's depiction of Rome as a psychical entity where all historical phases coexist illustrates this: the past can leap over intervening events and directly influence the present (p. 163).

Heinz von Foerster's distinction between **trivial machines** (fixed input-output) and **non-trivial machines** (output transforms the machine's structure such that the same input produces different output later) is crucial. Mnemonic, non-trivial machines can draw on remote events to respond to the present. A revolutionary "closer" in communication with Roman Republicans than with her contemporaries; a Sartrean professor temporally inhabiting the 1950s even while physically present in the 2020s.

> "Time is not smooth and distributed in the same way throughout a world, but is rather lumpy, discontinuous, and heterogeneous." (p. 166)

GPS satellites illustrate temporal relativity: satellites run faster than ground-based vehicles because they are further from Earth's mass. Without compensating for this temporal differential, GPS would accumulate ~10km error per day. Time literally arises from the mass and speed of machines (p. 167–168).

## IV. Fatigue and the Politics of Time

A pivotal section grounds political theory in temporal topology. *Fatigue* — both as finite operational capacity and as physical/cognitive exhaustion — is a real ontological feature of non-trivial machines. Operations always require energy. Organization never comes for free.

Bourdieu's *skholè* — leisure or free time as the condition of possibility of "pure thought" — reveals the class basis of academic and critical theory: the social scientist forgets that their practice requires temporal freedom that is structurally unavailable to most people. The oppressed tolerate oppressive conditions not primarily because they believe in a legitimating ideology, but because they are energetically captured by temporal necessity: work, childcare, debt, basic survival (p. 173–174).

> "If time is a central site of the political, then this is because time is the possibility of carrying out operations. A life that is saturated by the exigency of demands . . . finds it tremendously difficult to explore the possibility and construction of other operations and social relations. It is a life trapped in a web of existing relations of power." (p. 174)

Latour's speed-bump example shows how non-human machines redistribute temporal-energetic constraints: a speed bump achieves speed reduction as reliably as a moral injunction, without relying on belief (p. 172–173).

Creating time — *opening time* — is therefore a project central to any emancipatory politics.

## V. Overdetermination

The chapter closes with an extended theory of overdetermination, drawn from Freud (*Interpretation of Dreams*) rather than Althusser. A phenomenon is overdetermined not when it has one cause but when it arises from a *variety* of causes interacting conjunctively. Bryant distinguishes this sharply from Deleuze/Guattari's "overcoding" (territorialization on a single sovereign agency).

The corn example: a grape's qualities result from its genetics *and* soil conditions *and* weather *and* incorporeal farming practices *and* insect ecology — and, and, and. Traffic patterns arise from laws, road layouts, driver dispositions, vehicle states, and random events. Bhaskar's "generative mechanisms" (powers that can be dormant or out of phase with events) are aligned with Bryant's machines: their local manifestations in open systems are always overdetermined by conjunctions with other machines.

Jane Bennett's assemblages: "uneven topographies, because some of the points at which the various affects and bodies cross paths are more heavily trafficked than others, and so power is not distributed equally across the surface" (Bennett 2010: 24, p. 180).

Two ethical prescriptions follow:
1. *Never reduce a machine to its relations* — every machine carries an excess capable of breaking with its circumstances (p. 181).
2. *Never ignore the role that relations play in local manifestations* — individualism is the ideology of reactionaries, attributing to machine-essence what is a by-product of relational context (p. 182–183).

Lucretius: relations are contingent and external. They can be severed. "All subsequent revolutionary thought has consisted in arguing the former, while all reactionary thought has argued for the internality of relations, for the relational or 'natural' being of machines." (p. 182)

## Key Thinkers Engaged

- **Doreen Massey** — *For Space* (2005): space as product of interrelations, always under construction
- **Michel Serres** (with Latour) — vortexes, crumpled handkerchief of time, topology of nearness and rifts
- **Fernand Braudel** — *Structures of Everyday Life*: grain temporalities structuring social organization
- **Heinz von Foerster** — trivial vs. non-trivial machines
- **Roy Bhaskar** — generative mechanisms, open vs. closed systems, overdetermination
- **Jane Bennett** — assemblages with uneven topographies
- **Bourdieu** — *skholè*, scholastic disposition, time as class privilege
- **Deleuze** — repetition, mnemonic machines, holographic relation to past
- **Freud** — overdetermination, topological time as crumpled Rome
- **Kim Stanley Robinson** — *Red Mars*: topological field shaping biological becoming
- **China Miéville** — *The City & The City*: two cities occupying one Euclidean space, topologically separate
