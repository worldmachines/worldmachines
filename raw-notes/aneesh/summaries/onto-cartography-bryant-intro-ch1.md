# Onto-Cartography: Introduction & Chapter 1 — Summary

**Source**: Levi R. Bryant, *Onto-Cartography: An Ontology of Machines and Media* (Edinburgh University Press, 2014), pp. 1–36.
**Sections covered**: Introduction: "For a Renewal of Materialism" (pp. 1–14); Ch. 1: "Towards a Post-Human Media Ecology" (pp. 15–36).

---

## I. The Polemic: Against Discursive Materialism

Bryant opens as a convert describing his apostasy. Prior to 2006, he was a committed discursivist — trained in Žižek, Lacan, Derrida, Adorno, and the French post-structuralists — persuaded that social relations are structured by language and the diacritical differences of signs. The "materialist turn" in theory, he argues, produced a paradox: it evaporated matter. The word "materialism" became a *terme d'art* meaning only that phenomena are "historical, socially constructed, involves cultural practices, and is contingent" — nothing about fossil fuels, automobiles, sunlight on the earth's albedo, or the role bees play in agriculture (Bryant 2014, p. 4).

The analytic and political consequences were serious. Discursivist social theory could explain how fashion exercises power through symbolic capital, but it could not explain how turnstiles in subways, mountain ranges, and ocean currents organize social relations. An "entire domain of power became invisible," foreclosing strategic interventions in the material conditions of life. Most urgently, discursivism made it nearly impossible to theorize climate change — a problem that requires thinking about the real physical efficacy of fossil fuels, pollutants, and atmospheric systems, not merely their cultural representation (Bryant 2014, p. 4).

Bryant traces the genealogy of this collapse to a misreading of Marx. Marx was right that commodity fetishism conceals social relations (Bryant 2014, p. 2, citing Marx 1990: 165, 128). But his successors drew the wrong conclusion: things are *only* crystallizations of the social, rather than beings with their own powers. The result was a curious anti-dialecticism that failed to recognize "the way in which non-human, non-signifying agencies structure social relations as much as the discursive" (Bryant 2014, p. 4).

The conversion event was playing *SimCity 4* in November 2006. Bryant found that the signifier was not the sole agency structuring social relations in the game. Whether a commercial district grows is a function of available energy from the power plant — not a signifying or cultural difference. Whether people vote you out of office because of traffic congestion is not the result of a signifier (Bryant 2014, p. 5). He had "an encounter with real materiality, with physical stuff, with things, and encountered the differences they make" (Bryant 2014, p. 6).

> "With the 'materialist' turn in theory, matter somehow completely evaporated and we were instead left with nothing but language, culture, and discursivity." (Bryant 2014, p. 2)

> "The idea of removing 'turnstiles' as one way of producing change and emancipation wasn't even on the radar." (Bryant 2014, p. 4)

## II. What Bryant Means by "Matter"

Bryant adopts an unabashedly naïve materialism: the world is composed entirely of "stuff" and "things." He does not try to resolve what matter *in-itself* is — that is a question for physics and chemistry. He does not claim matter is one type of thing; rather, there appear to be many different types. Ideas and concepts have their materiality too: they exist only in brains, on paper, in computer data banks, and can only be transmitted through physical media. "I call these entities 'machines' to emphasize the manner in which entities dynamically *operate* on inputs producing outputs." (Bryant 2014, p. 6)

## III. Onto-Cartography Defined

"Onto" from Greek *onto*, meaning "thing"; "cartography" meaning "map." Onto-cartography is Bryant's name for "a map of relations between machines that analyzes how these assemblages organize the movement, development, and becoming other machines in a world" (Bryant 2014, p. 7).

The central thesis: "social relations or ecologies take the form they take due to the gravity — my term for 'power' — physical and discursive machines exercise on elements that inhabit assemblages, worlds, or ecologies" (Bryant 2014, p. 7).

Bryant positions onto-cartography as an expansion rather than refutation of critical theory: drawing on Whitehead's observation that "the chief error in philosophy is overstatement" (Whitehead 1978: 7), he argues that discursive theories are not false but overstated. The goal is a framework "robust enough to integrate the discoveries of the critical theories, while also making room for a non-reductive account of the role played by physical media in the structuration of social relations" (Bryant 2014, p. 8).

Geography is described as "the queen of the social sciences" precisely because it least dematerializes the world — recognizing that social relations are always embedded in a particular space or place, that communication takes time and requires media to travel, and that geographical features play a role in the form social relations take. "Social and political philosophy needs to become more geographical." (Bryant 2014, p. 7)

## IV. Chapter 1: Post-Human Media Ecology and the Machine Concept

### The Machine as Universal Ontological Category

Bryant's opening for Ch. 1 is ontologically maximalist: "We didn't have to await Archimedes for the invention of the machine, for being has never consisted of anything but machines" (Bryant 2014, p. 15). Every entity — material or immaterial, corporeal or incorporeal — is a machine. "Entity," "object," "existent," "substance," "body," "thing" are all synonyms of "machine."

The term is chosen for two reasons. First, it captures that entities are fundamentally *operational* — beings that do, operate, act. Second, it sidesteps the subject/object binary that has dominated four hundred years of philosophy. As the OED puts it, a machine is "a body regarded as functioning as an independent body."

### Clearing Three Prejudices

**1. All Machines Are Rigid**: The rigid machine (automobile, cell phone, lamp) is only a sub-species. Butterflies live much of their lives as caterpillars; living planets undergo distinct climatic phases; children, otters, and organizations learn and change. Moreover, not all machines are material: a national constitution, a recipe, a novel are machines without being material assemblages of fixed parts (Bryant 2014, pp. 15–16).

**2. All Machines Are Designed**: Bryan traces this to hylomorphism — the model inherited from Aristotle (and dominating philosophy ever since) in which an artisan first conceives a form in mind and then imposes it on passive matter. But attending to actual fabrication reveals a *negotiation*: "the production of any artifact is much closer to a *negotiation* than the simple imposition of a form upon a passive matter" (Bryant 2014, p. 19). Drawing on Sartre's *Critique of Dialectical Reason*, Bryant shows that the steam engine imposed imperatives on its designers (encouraging large industrial plants due to labor requirements), and that the clock, once invented, restructured all of social life in ways no inventor intended. "The designer of the train is no less designed by the train than she designs the train." (Bryant 2014, p. 20) Most machines — trees, copper atoms, living planets — were designed by no one; they *emerged* from out of other machines (following DeLanda).

**3. All Machines Have a Purpose or Use**: Entities as diverse as neutrinos, black holes, seeds, and capybaras are machines but do not have intrinsic purposes. Rather, all machines are *pluripotent*: they possess a range of possible becomings. Rocks can become paperweights, doorstops, weapons, boiling stones. History is better described in terms of biological *exaptation* — a trait taking on a different function than it originally served — than design (Bryant 2014, pp. 23–24). Machines do not *have* a purpose; they *take on* a purpose when structurally coupled to other machines.

### Machine Typology

A first taxonomy emerges in Ch. 1:
- **Corporeal machines**: made of matter, occupying discrete time and place. Subatomic particles, rocks, grass, human bodies, institutions, refrigerators.
- **Incorporeal machines**: defined by iterability, potential eternity, and multi-instantiability. Recipes, scores of music, equations, theories, novels, cultural identities. They require corporeal bodies to exist, but are not reducible to any single corporeal body. A novel is the same novel whether on paper, in memory, or in a data bank.
- Within both: gradations from **rigid** (fixed operations, cannot learn: mathematical equations, rocks) to **plastic** (modifiable by flows: organisms, novels, cultural identities).
- Corporeal machines further divide into: **inanimate** (change only through external causes or internal processes; entropy-destined), **animate** (engage in operations to preserve their organization — a cat's wound heals), **cognitive** (capable of directing their own action — dolphins, institutions, some technologies).

"Dormant" incorporeal machines are those that still exist through their corporeal inscriptions but are no longer active or remembered — the *Dead Sea Scrolls*, Lucretius's *De Rerum Natura* before its fifteenth-century rediscovery. Like Deleuze and Guattari's tick, they await a warm-blooded entity to activate them (Bryant 2014, pp. 27–28).

### Post-Human Media Ecology

The chapter's second movement redefines *media* through McLuhan. Bryant quotes McLuhan: "Whether . . . light is being used for brain surgery or night baseball is a matter of indifference . . . 'the medium is the message' because it is the medium that shapes and controls the scale and form of human association and action." (McLuhan 1994: 8–9, cited at Bryant 2014, p. 22.)

Media — which Bryant equates with machines — are formative of human action in ways that don't simply issue from human intentions. But Bryant extends McLuhan in two directions:

1. **Beyond sense-organs**: McLuhan treats media as extensions of human organs and faculties. Bryant argues the category is broader: "*A machine functions as a medium for another machine not only when it amplifies or extends a sense-organ, but also whenever it modifies the activity or becoming of any other machine*" (Bryant 2014, p. 33). Vitamin B modifies moods; cigarettes modify lung cell self-maintenance; the temperature of a crocodile's nest determines the sex of eggs.

2. **Beyond humans**: McLuhan's media theory is implicitly anthropocentric. Bryant extends it post-humanly: media relations hold between *any* two machines, regardless of whether humans are involved. Electric lights are no less a medium for insects and lizards than for humans. The stoker in Kafka's *Amerika* is a medium for the steam engine, not the reverse (Bryant 2014, pp. 33–34).

The materiality of the medium — not its content — is what matters. Writing vs. speech as legal media: writing gives laws a permanence and spatial reach that speech cannot, allowing the formation of larger kingdoms and shared identity among people who have never met (Bryant 2014, pp. 31–32).

> "Based on the foregoing, we are now in a position to give our first definition of onto-cartography. In its initial formulation, onto-cartography is the investigation of structural couplings between machines and how they modify the becomings, activities, movements, and ways in which the coupled machines relate to the world about them. It is a mapping (cartography) of these couplings between machines (onta) and their vectors of becoming, movement, and activity." (Bryant 2014, p. 35)

## V. Key Interlocutors in These Sections

- **Žižek / Lacan / Derrida / Adorno**: the discursivist camp Bryant is leaving
- **Jane Bennett** (*Vibrant Matter*, 2010): affirmed — "things have their power as well"
- **Marshall McLuhan**: crucial positive source on media materiality; Bryant extends but also corrects him
- **Jean-Paul Sartre** (*Critique of Dialectical Reason*): illustrates machine imperatives on designers
- **Maturana and Varela**: *structural coupling* as a biological concept adopted ontologically
- **Manuel DeLanda**: emergence without design — machines emerge from other machines
- **Ian Bogost**: "alien phenomenology" introduced via footnote; will recur in Ch. 3
- **Graham Harman**: "dormant objects" concept adopted into Bryant's framework
- **Walter Ong** and **Andy Clark**: on how material media (writing, paper) shape what incorporeal machines are possible
