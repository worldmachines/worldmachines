# On Protocols, Wagons, and Associated Acrobatics

**Source**: [On Protocols, Wagons, and Associated Acrobatics](https://aneeshsathe.com/on-protocols-wagons-and-associated-acrobatics/)
**Date**: 2025-07-27
**Author**: [[Aneesh Sathe]]

## Central Argument

The essay argues that the protocol of creative work is not just showing up consistently but *getting back on* after falling off. More fundamentally, the author extracts from [[Robert Peake]]'s [[Summer of Protocols]] talk the idea that the poet (and by extension, any creative worker) is continuously constructing the self through iterative cycles of observation and synthesis. The wagon is not a destination—it is the vehicle of continuous self-construction.

## Key Claims

1. **Falling off is inevitable** — life interrupts habit; the question is only whether you return.
2. **The writing protocol as self-construction**: observation of inner/outer environment → synthesis into writing → writing feeds back into the self → sum of all iterations = life's work + constructed self.
3. **Pointing toward maximal interestingness** is the navigational heuristic when the destination is uncertain.
4. **Building the wagon matters as much as reaching a destination** — process has intrinsic value independent of outcome.
5. Scrivener's "30 days of use" (non-consecutive) trial embodies the same philosophy: the protocol accommodates life.

## Quotable Passages

> "Building wagons is as important as going somewhere with potential for something new, even if the path is uncertain."

> "Pointing in the direction of maximal interestingness."

> "The sum of all noticing and synthesis is your life's work."

## What the Essay Doesn't Address

- How to design environments that make falling off less likely
- The social/relational dimensions of creative practice (accountability, audience)
- Whether "maximal interestingness" is sufficient as a compass, or whether it can lead to dilettantism
