# My Grandfather Had Lore, My Father Had Google, I Have ChatGPT, My Son Will Have Lore

**Source**: [aneeshsathe.com, 2024-01-17](https://aneeshsathe.com/my-grandfather-had-lore/)
**Author**: [[Aneesh Sathe]]

## Central Argument

The arc of information access follows a cyclical pattern analogous to the Dubai camel proverb: from lore (oral expertise, pre-modern) → Google (search, early internet) → ChatGPT (generative AI, 2020s) → possibly back to lore. The mechanism driving regression: Google's search quality has measurably declined while LLMs like ChatGPT produce confident falsehoods without citations or confidence scores, eroding the reliability of mass information access.

## Key Claims

1. **Search degradation is documented** — researchers have confirmed Google results are worsening. This is not aesthetic complaint; it undermines a critical infrastructure for decision-making.
2. **LLMs hallucinate by design** — unlike search, which links to primary sources, generative models produce fluent fabrications with no confidence scoring and no citation trail. The xkcd "citation needed" joke has become existential.
3. **Explainability as the only viable standard** — if AI systems cannot expose their reasoning chain, users cannot apply their own judgment when answers seem wrong. Non-explainable AI is unsafe in any mission-critical domain.
4. **The Perplexity bet** — Sathe acknowledges Perplexity AI as attempting to restore citations to LLM answers, but is skeptical that citation grafting onto generative architecture solves the underlying trust problem.
5. **The fallback** — if both search and LLMs fail, formal education and expert lore become the only epistemically defensible sources. This would be a step backward in democratizing knowledge access.
