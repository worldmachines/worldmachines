# Why 'Cost Disease' Is the Secret Force Behind America's Toxic Solitude

**Source**: Alex Mayyasi via Derek Thompson's Substack, April 7, 2026
**URL**: https://open.substack.com/pub/derekthompson/p/why-cost-disease-is-the-secret-force
**Author**: [[Alex Mayyasi]]

## Central Argument

The epidemic of American loneliness has a structural economic cause that technology- and values-based narratives miss: [[Baumol's Cost Disease]]. Because social experiences are labor-intensive and non-scalable, they become progressively more expensive in a growing economy — while digital, solitude-inducing products benefit from exponential productivity gains and become cheaper. The profit motive therefore systematically favors anti-social businesses. Addressing this requires active policy intervention.

## Key Claims

1. **Two-tier economy**: The economy bifurcates into scalable sectors (electronics, streaming, food delivery) that get cheaper over time, and labor-intensive sectors (restaurants, concerts, childcare, theater) that get more expensive. Social life sits almost entirely in the latter.

2. **Baumol's original observation**: Economist William Baumol (1960s) investigated the economics of performing arts for the JFK administration. His insight: you cannot automate a string quartet. As wages rise economy-wide due to productivity growth elsewhere, labor-intensive industries must pay more to retain workers — but cannot pass productivity gains to customers. Result: perpetual relative price inflation.

3. **Anti-social businesses scale, social businesses don't**: Netflix can serve a global user base from one content investment. A sit-down restaurant cannot. When restaurants *do* achieve efficiency gains, it's by becoming *less social* — kiosk ordering, uncomfortable seating designed to increase table turnover.

4. **Facebook as cost-disease casualty**: Facebook's evolution from social network to algorithmically-curated media feed illustrates cost disease in action. User-generated social content didn't scale efficiently, so the platform pivoted to professional content from anywhere — becoming anti-social to achieve productivity gains.

5. **The policy cure**: Baumol himself identified the solution — progressive taxation in a growing economy can subsidize cost-disease sectors. The piece asks whether this logic extends beyond health care and education to include bowling alleys, dive bars, and restaurants: institutions of social life.

## Key Quotation

> "Because of cost disease, most of the world's most powerful corporations and most driven entrepreneurs are well capitalized and incentivized to increase our solitude, while purveyors of long nights with friends are outliers who succeed despite strong headwinds."

## What's Missing

- No engagement with how urban density and housing costs compound the problem — cities make social life possible but are themselves cost-disease victims
- Doesn't distinguish between different types of solitude (chosen vs. imposed) or different valences of "social" (surface interaction vs. meaningful connection)
- The proposed subsidies for "pro-social businesses" raise difficult definitional questions — who decides which businesses are sufficiently social?
- No engagement with international comparisons: why do some societies (Japan, Italy, France) appear to sustain stronger social infrastructure despite similar economic dynamics?
