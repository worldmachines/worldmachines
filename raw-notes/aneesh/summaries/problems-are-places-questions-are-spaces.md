# Problems are Places, Questions are Spaces

**Source**: Aneesh Sathe, *Problems are Places, Questions are Spaces* (2025-07-14) — https://aneeshsathe.com/problems-are-places-questions-are-spaces/

## Central Argument

Agnes Callard's distinction between **problems** (tractable, solvable, disappear when solved) and **Socratic questions** (open-ended quests that leave you richer when answered, never truly closed) maps cleanly onto the spatial philosophy of **places** (bounded, ordered, meaningful) and **spaces** (pure potential, defined negatively by the places that surround them).

The author prefers the space/place lens because it extends beyond the human mind to encompass territory, physical experience, and scientific experimentation — a broader stage than the cognitive Q&P framing.

## Key Distinctions

### Problems = Places
- Both are **tractable**: draw a boundary, name the thing, apply a recipe/algorithm/ritual
- Problems disappear when solved; places are demarcated and then treated as stable building blocks
- The moment you say "here" you have a place; the moment you say "do this" you have packaged a problem

### Questions = Spaces
- Both require **orientation before action**: find north before plotting a march
- Questions are additive: solving them leaves you with something (the solution + new territory)
- Solved problems become stable places that orient further exploration of the question
- A question is "like the horizon you constantly seek"
- Spaces are defined and explored by the places that surround them — identity, orientation, and memory of a space are stored in its places

### The Key Asymmetry
A question contains problems: en route to answering a question, you solve sub-problems that act as waypoints. But solving all the problems never fully closes the question — it just opens a larger horizon.

## Intellectual Lineage

- **Agnes Callard**, *Open Socrates* — source of the Q&P distinction
- **Previous essay**: *We Need Homes in the Delta Quadrant* (Sathe) — earlier exploration of space/place philosophy
- **Implicit influence**: Phenomenological geography (Tuan, Lefebvre) on place as "an ordered world of meaning"

## The Scientist's Preference
The author notes that the space/place framing is "more satisfying" scientifically because it allows exploration even when knowledge isn't tied down by facts — you can experiment in a space without having established its complete conceptual cartography.

## What the Author Doesn't Address

- Whether the Q&P distinction maps onto the S&P distinction perfectly or only partially (are there questions that close? places that remain open?)
- The full argument of *Open Socrates* — the essay engages only with this one distinction from Callard
- Whether the "horizon" metaphor is a feature or a limitation — does it suggest that questions are indefinitely deferred rather than genuinely progressive?
