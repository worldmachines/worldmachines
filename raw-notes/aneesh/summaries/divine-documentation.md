# Divine Documentation

**Source:** https://aneeshsathe.com/divine-documentation/  
**Author:** Aneesh Sathe  
**Date:** 2025-07-16

## Central Argument

Good documentation is an act of deep empathy: reaching into the future to equip the reader with everything they need. The essay traces a journey from button-mashing teenager to documentation advocate, and extends the insight to writing prose — words are buttons, and learning their grammar requires the same humble submission to the manual.

## The Button-Pressing vs. Reading Split

Two postures toward unfamiliar technology:
1. **Hypothesis-driven button pressing** — fast, discovery-oriented, but brittle; relies on *shared pattern languages* already internalized
2. **Reading the manual** — slower, but creates new understanding of the problem, not just solutions

Both are valid at different stages. Button-pressing spikes interest; documentation deepens mastery. The [[Hacky Mindset]] is the productive middle ground, but documentation is where it graduates.

## Documentation as Empathy

> "Writing good documentation is hard. It is an exercise in deep empathy with my user."

Key reframings:
- **Documentation as time travel**: reaching into the future to help an unknown reader
- **The future user is often past-me**: the self-service compact — past-you being kind to now-you
- **Socratic interlocutor vs. documentation**: cross-referencing [[Socratic Method]], an expert interlocutor is "weight training"; good documentation is "a kindly spirit ancestor parting the mist"

## The Anti-Library and Staged Learning

[[Virginia Tufte]]'s *Artful Sentences* is placed in the [[Anti-Library]] — books not yet ready to be read. The essay demonstrates a principled reading sequence: [[Zinsser]]'s *Writing to Learn* → *On Writing Well* → [[Strunk and White]] (for *how*) → Tufte (for mechanics). This is a meta-documentation of one's own learning path.

## Words as Buttons

The pivot: "words are buttons." This connects the documentation essay to the [[Hack Hacky Hacker]] piece — the same experimental, tactile relationship the author has with technology is now applied to language. But the lesson drawn here is that buttons without grammar are unreliable.
