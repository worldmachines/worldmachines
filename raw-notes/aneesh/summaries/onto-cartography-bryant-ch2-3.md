# Onto-Cartography Ch 2–3: What Is a Machine? / Alien Phenomenology

See also: [[onto-cartography-bryant-intro-ch1]], [[levi-bryant]], [[onto-cartography]]

## Chapter 2: What Is a Machine? (pp. 37–53)

### The Operational Turn

Bryant's central move in Ch 2 is ontological: shift from asking *what a thing is* (its properties, qualities, essence) to asking *what a thing does* (its operations, inputs, outputs). Building on Ian Bogost, he defines an operation as "a basic process that takes one or more inputs and performs a transformation on it" (Bogost 2006: 7, cited p. 38). Bryant adds that every operation produces an output. *A machine is a system of operations that perform transformations on inputs thereby producing outputs* (p. 38).

This is not merely a redescription. Within a machinic ontology, **every entity is a machine** — trees, frogs, recipes, scientific papers, corporations, mathematical theorems. The question we must always ask of any machine is not "what are its properties?" but "what does it *do*?" (p. 38–39). Machines are not expressive or representational but *productive*: they are factories, not theaters (p. 40).

### Virtual Proper Being and Local Manifestation

Bryant introduces a fundamental split within any machine between its **virtual proper being** (the system of operations it is *capable of* — its powers) and its **local manifestations** (the outputs it actually produces in a given context). This distinction, drawing on George Molnar's account of powers, carries five features: powers are *directed* toward a manifestation; *independent* of their exercise; characterized by *actuality* (real even when dormant); *intrinsic* to the machine; and *objective* (independent of observation) (pp. 40–42).

Bryant reserves "power" for a capacity, and "operation" for the actual exercise of that capacity in producing a manifestation (p. 42). Manifestations come in three forms:
- *Qualitative*: transformations of a machine's qualities (color, shape, texture)
- *Agentive*: transformations in a machine's activity or behavior
- *Material*: outputs that depart from the machine and circulate in the world (the diploma, the oxygen, the spoken sentence) (pp. 43–44)

A key consequence: qualities are not properties the machine *has* intrinsically but *activities* resulting from operations. Lucretius on atoms — they have no color, but their arrangements produce color — is cited as an early machinic intuition (p. 43).

### Powers are Plastic, Not Fixed

Machine powers are not stable essences; they wax and wane through couplings and environmental conditions. Catherine Malabou: "the brain of a pianist is not strictly identical to that of a mathematician, a mechanic, or a graphic artist" (Malabou 2008: 7, cited p. 45). Minds are extended beyond the skull into couplings with world-machines (Andy Clark's "extended mind hypothesis," p. 45). The metamorphosis of a caterpillar into a butterfly, the brittleness of cold steel — all demonstrate that powers are shaped by the history of couplings a machine undergoes.

### Rigid vs. Plastic Machines

All machines are characterized by *plasticity* to some degree — the plurality of virtual manifestations they can produce. Bryant positions machines on a spectrum:

- **Absolutely rigid machines** (ideal): impermeable to inputs; perform the same operations regardless of what flows through them. The Pythagorean theorem is the limiting case — equally true for all triangles regardless of material, color, or size (p. 51).
- **Absolutely plastic machines** (ideal): completely transformed by every encounter. Approached by certain subatomic particles that become something else entirely in collision (p. 51).
- Between these poles: industrial factories, bureaucracies, and dogmatic theologies approach rigidity; organic bodies, great works of art, and anarchic political movements approach plasticity.

A **great work of art** is paradigmatically plastic because it is *pluripotent*: it resonates in a variety of ways given different historical and cultural milieus, producing novel local manifestations as a result (p. 52). Kafka's *The Trial* can be read psychoanalytically, politically, theologically, as a critique of bureaucracy, and so on (p. 52). Pluripotency is not infinite plasticity — a rock cannot become a butterfly — but it is a machine's capacity to produce genuinely different outputs across contexts of coupling.

### Binary Machines and Trans-Corporeality

All machines are *binary machines*: they require coupling with other machines to perform operations. Drawing on Deleuze and Guattari, Bryant notes that one machine always provides a *flow* that becomes the input upon which a second machine operates. However, Bryant modifies D&G: machines are not *always* coupled; what matters is the *variability* of couplings (p. 47). Relation, following Deleuze, is external to its terms (p. 48).

The inputs that flow through a machine are never formless — they are themselves machines with their own powers. Consequently, *there is no structureless matter*; all matter is formatted by its own operations (p. 49). This generates *reciprocal determination*: flows modify the machine that operates on them, just as the machine modifies the flows. Jane Bennett's example of omega-3 fatty acids modifying not just cellular metabolism but the behavioral dispositions of the body that metabolizes them (Bennett 2010: 41, cited p. 50). This mutual shaping is what Stacy Alaimo calls **trans-corporeality** — the inseparability of bodies from their material-environmental milieu (pp. 48–49).

## Chapter 3: Alien Phenomenology (pp. 54–74)

### Structural Openness and Operational Closure

Every machine is *selectively* open to the world — it specifies the flows to which it is structurally open. Mantis shrimp see eleven or twelve primary colors and perceive polarized, infrared, and ultraviolet light entirely invisible to humans (p. 54). The bureaucracy-machine is open primarily to *forms* — structured documents that reduce human communication to pre-defined parameters (pp. 55–56).

This selective structural openness is paired with **operational closure**: a machine never encounters a flow *as it is*, but always transforms that flow according to its own internal operations. The sonar of a submarine encounters a shark as a set of size, shape, and velocity data — "oblivious" to all the shark is for itself (p. 57). This is what Kant observed about cognition — objects must conform to the conditions of our cognition, not the reverse (Kant, Critique, Bxvi, cited p. 57). Bryant radicalizes Kant: *what Kant says about cognitive machines holds for all machines* (p. 57). Rocks, capybara, insurance companies — none encounters things as they are in themselves. All machines relate behind "firewalls" — the operations of each machine (p. 58, crediting Harman).

Niklas Luhmann: machines "cannot see what [they] cannot see" (Luhmann 2002a: 129, cited p. 59). The lived experience of a machine is indistinguishable from the world itself, because each machine only has access to operationally transformed flows in its internal world (p. 60). This generates the perils of **epistemological closure** and **confirmation bias** in cognitive and social machines (pp. 60–61).

### Alien Phenomenology Defined

The term "alien phenomenology" comes from Ian Bogost (*Alien Phenomenology*, 2012). As Bryant formulates it, alien phenomenology is the practice of investigating how *non-human* entities experience the world — their structural openness, their operational closure, their local manifestations. It is what Luhmann calls "second-order observation": observing not how an entity appears *to us* but how the world appears *to that entity* (Luhmann 2002b). It parallels Jakob von Uexküll's *ethology* — asking what the world is like for other animals (pp. 62–63).

Bryant's version is explicitly **post-vitalistic**: it does not claim that all beings possess consciousness, awareness, or intentionality. Rather, "the claim is that all machines, regardless of whether they're living, are selectively open to particular flows and operate on these flows in ways unique to that type of machine" (p. 64). Alien phenomenology requires bracketing (*epoché*) our own human aims to ask what *the machine's* aims or goals might be.

### Examples of Non-Human Phenomenologies

- **Mantis shrimp**: open to a far wider electromagnetic spectrum than humans; the alien phenomenologist asks how these flows function in mating and predator-detection (pp. 54, 63–64).
- **The bat**: following Nagel's famous puzzle, we cannot directly experience sonar-navigation, but we can make inferences from knowledge of biology and the physics of sound (pp. 62–63).
- **Bureaucratic-machines**: structurally open to forms; operationally closed around criteria of categorical fit — blind to the singularity of human circumstances (pp. 55–56, 63).
- **Insurance-company machines**: a submitted form takes on the functional value of profit/loss analysis, not health-coverage (pp. 58–59).
- **Plants** (Michael Pollan's *The Botany of Desire*): apples, potatoes, tulips, and marijuana have "seduced" humans to maximize their reproductive fitness through blind evolutionary processes — a reversal of the assumed human-plant hierarchy (pp. 65–67).
- **Cows and livestock**: from an evolutionary perspective, bovine agents have enlisted humans as instruments of their reproductive success — clearing forests, eliminating predators, fencing pastures — while exercising selective pressure on human populations through disease and diet (pp. 67–68).

### Three Stakes of Alien Phenomenology

**1. Analytic**: mapping relations between machines requires knowing what flows each machine is open to and how it operates on them. Without this, onto-cartography is blind (p. 69).

**2. Ethical**: our narcissistic tendency to encounter non-human others only in terms of their utility for our aims generates enormous cruelty. Alien phenomenology opens more compassionate forms of relation — Cesar Millan thinking like a dog, not a human, to restructure the dog's environment and pack relations (pp. 70–71); Temple Grandin adopting cow phenomenology to reform slaughterhouse practices (p. 65). Colonial genocide, sexism, and ableism are all failures of alien phenomenology (p. 71).

**3. Political** — "Thermodynamic politics": Institutions are alien minds operating by their own structural openness and operational closure. Addressing a corporation through ethical persuasion is "like trying to explain calculus to a cat" (p. 73) — the corporation is simply not structurally open to moral flows. Boycotts and strikes work because they target the flows *to which corporations are actually open* — profit and loss, energy and production (pp. 72–73). Bryant calls this **thermodynamic politics**: engaging with a machine in terms of its own operational language. This contrasts with **semiotic politics** — which assumes that institutions are open to the same communicative flows as humans, a confusion that vitiates much protest politics (pp. 73–74).

## Key Quotable Passages

1. "*A machine is a system of operations that perform transformations on inputs thereby producing outputs.*" (p. 38)

2. "Within a machinic ontology entities are understood as machines. Being is composed of machines all the way down. The first question to ask of any machine is not 'what are its properties?', but rather 'what does it *do*?'" (pp. 38–39)

3. "With Spinoza, we can thus say that we do not know what a machine can do (Spinoza 2002: 280–1). Contrary to that ontological vision that comprehends entities in terms of predicates inhering in a subject, we can only discover the being of a machine, its powers, through acting upon it and varying its relations to other machines to discern what local manifestations and becomings arise as a consequence." (p. 46)

4. "What Kant says here about cognitive-machines holds for all machines. Rocks, neutrinos, insurance companies, and capybara no more encounter things as they are in themselves, than our own minds..." (p. 57–58)

5. "Machines 'cannot see what [they] cannot see' (Luhmann 2002a: 129). The flows to which one machine are open are invisible to another machine..." (p. 59)

6. "Alien phenomenology is not making a panpsychist claim to the effect that all beings, animate and inanimate, possess consciousness, awareness, perception, and cognition. Rather, the claim is that all machines, regardless of whether they're living, are selectively open to particular flows and operate on these flows in ways unique to that type of machine." (p. 64)

7. "Persuading a corporation through ethical appeals is about as effective as trying to explain calculus to a cat." (p. 73)
