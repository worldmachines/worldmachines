# Shitty Castles: On the Art of Digging Fake Moats

**Source:** Florian Lohse, *Notefields* (Substack), March 25, 2026. https://notefields.substack.com/p/shitty-castles

## Central Argument

People — individuals, institutions, and civilizations — regularly construct elaborate systems they know are flawed or temporary. This is not necessarily hypocrisy or self-deception; it can be a rational pragmatic strategy to:
1. Maintain economic/political security while a paradigm shifts around you (Leibniz)
2. Demonstrate power and attract resources through the act of costly construction itself (Chambord)
3. Signal strategic direction to investors when existing value propositions are weakening (OpenAI/Jony Ive acquisition)

The essay proposes reading "shitty castles" not as moral failures but as **temporal bridges** — structures that are "good enough to keep yourself sane until you have a better idea."

## Three Case Studies

### Leibniz's Monad Theory
- In 1676, Leibniz visits Spinoza, who had argued that God = Nature (Deus sive Natura) — a radical secular move
- Leibniz was "impressed and unsettled" but publicly positioned himself as Spinoza's opponent
- His Monad theory (each monad contains its own immortal soul, answerable to a personal God) is widely suspected to be insufficient: Lessing thought Leibniz was "a Spinozist by heart"; Bertrand Russell: "Leibniz fell into Spinozism whenever he allowed himself to be logical"
- Leibniz's own letters reveal he was primarily motivated by securing economic/political patronage
- Yet the Monads may have been a pragmatic fence rather than a sincere metaphysical position: a way to remain aligned with religious patrons during a period of radical intellectual transition

### Château de Chambord (1519–)
- François I began building Chambord despite just being defeated at Pavia by gunpowder units that would destroy such walls instantly
- The castle's official explanation: it was never meant to be a real fortress but "an architectural citation of bygone times" — nostalgia for chivalry
- But it *worked* as power: Charles V visited in 1539 and called it "a synthesis of all that human effort can achieve"
- Cost signal: moving 220,000 tons of stone with 1,800 workers is *itself* a demonstration of power

### OpenAI / Jony Ive Acquisition (2025)
- OpenAI spent $6.5 billion acquiring Jony Ive's "io" design company to build an "AI-native personal gadget"
- The AI gadget space has produced no successful products; the acquisition seems "expensive, complicated and somehow bound to fail from the beginning"
- But: the markets needed a strategic signal to justify a $500B valuation based only on "a chatbot, an API and the buttery voice of Sam Altman"
- Costly signaling through an implausible but impressive acquisition may have been the functional goal

## The Moat as Lifecycle Model

The essay closes with a taxonomy of moats through history:
- **13th century moat**: honest functional value (defense)
- **16th century moat** (Chambord): performative value as demonstration of power
- **19th century moat** (Neuschwanstein): purely ornamental, to please an eccentric patron
- **Monty Python's "Holy Grail"** swamp castle: satirical ridicule

This suggests a general pattern for ideas and frameworks: **functional → performative → ornamental → ridicule**. The more functional flaws you can find in a system, the more likely you're facing "a dead facade, not a working fortification."

## Connection to Venkatesh Rao

Lohse explicitly cites Rao's concept of "energetic zombies" from [[The Divergence Machine]] — ideas from the past that still channel enormous energy while carrying little of the world's "evolutionary intelligence." Leibniz's Monads, Chambord, and possibly OpenAI's hardware pivot may be "zombie ideas" in their time. But the challenge is: how do you tell a zombie from something worth engaging with?

## Key Quotes

> "Why would you build a castle that is not a castle? To use the power you still have to create something fancy that is good enough to keep yourself sane until you have a better idea."

> "Leibniz fell into Spinozism whenever he allowed himself to be logical; in his published works, accordingly, he took care to be illogical." — Bertrand Russell (quoted)

> "There is nothing, I think, upon which I have brooded more earnestly over the course of my life... than the problem of assuring my security in the future." — Leibniz to Arnault (quoted)

## Self-Implication

Lohse turns the argument on himself: "I think with some of my past writing I have been digging fake moats for myself. I wrote some fancy essays while deep inside I knew they would not withstand the contact with reality." The essay endorses a Spinozist alternative — staying with discomfort and uncertainty rather than building defensive structures — while acknowledging this is "hard to pull off."
