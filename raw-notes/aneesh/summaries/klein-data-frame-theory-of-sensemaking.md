# A Data-Frame Theory of Sensemaking (Klein, Phillips, Rall, Peluso, 2007)

**Source**: Proceedings of the Sixth International Conference on Naturalistic Decision Making (pp. 113–155). Funded by the US Army Research Institute for Behavioral and Social Sciences.

## Central Argument

Sensemaking — "the deliberate effort to understand events" — is not passive information processing. It is a recursive, bidirectional relationship between *data* and *frames* in which each constitutes the other. The Data-Frame Theory provides a descriptive account of this process across expert domains, grounded in naturalistic fieldwork.

## Eight Functions of Sensemaking

1. Problem detection (e.g., weather forecasters scanning for threatening conditions)
2. Connecting the dots / making discoveries (e.g., general inferring tank formation from one sighting)
3. Forming explanations (e.g., physician diagnosis)
4. Anticipatory thinking (preventing future accidents)
5. Projecting future states (preparation)
6. Finding levers / how to think and act (recasting a purchasing decision as specific tradeoffs)
7. Seeing relationships (using a map)
8. Problem identification (student figuring out which solution strategy applies)

## Core Definition

> "Sensemaking is the process of fitting data into a frame and fitting a frame around the data."

Frames are **explanatory structures** that define entities by describing their relationship to other entities. Forms include: stories, maps, scripts, plans, and mental models.

- The frame *defines, connects, and filters* data
- The data *elicit and help construct* the frame

## The Data-Frame Symbiosis

- Frames determine what *counts* as data in the first place — data is always *constructed*, not passively received
- Initial frames are anchored on ≤3–4 data elements
- An anchor used for one frame is difficult to redeploy in an alternative frame (anchor lock-in)
- Sensemaking ceases when data and frame are brought into congruence — no key unexplained data, no key ambiguous frame elements

## Relationship to Prior Concepts

The paper explicitly situates frames as similar to:
- [[Cognitive Chunks]] — organized units of knowledge in long-term memory (Sweller & Cooper, 1985)
- Schema acquisition in learning research
- Weick's sensemaking (frames as "extracted cues" + retrospective narrative)

> Weick (1995): sensemaking is "a continuous alternation between particulars [data] and explanations [frames]"

## Expert-Novice Differences (Key Findings)

Experts and novices use identical sensemaking processes. What differs:
- **Richer repertoire of ready-made frames** — allows faster initial frame construction
- **Richer mental models** = denser causal understanding of the domain, generating more specific expectancies
- **Faster expectancy violation detection** — quicker to notice when reality diverges from prediction

Mental models are defined narrowly as **causal understandings of how things work** — a *kind of* frame, alongside scripts, maps, and stories.

## The Confirmation Bias Reframe

What appears as confirmation bias in lab studies is, in naturalistic settings, rational frame-guided information seeking. Expert skilled decision makers do *not* use "keep an open mind" strategies — they commit to frames early and use specific expectancies to self-correct faster. This claim is positioned as a *falsifiable* assertion of the theory.

## Decision Support System Implications

Conventional data→insight pipeline is wrong. Good decision support:
- Tracks anchors (used and discarded) rather than maximizing data throughput
- Externalizes anchor categorization to support alternative frame construction
- Tests quality by measuring time to detect planted anomalies ("garden path" scenarios)
