# How Experts Sensemake (Chin, 2026)

**Source**: commoncog.com, published April 2026. Part 2 of a sensemaking series; Part 1 is [[How to Make Sense of AI]].

## Central Argument

The [[Data-Frame Theory of Sensemaking]] provides the best current account of how humans make sense of ambiguous situations. Critically: experts and novices use *identical* sensemaking strategies, but experts have (a) richer causal mental models, (b) more frame fragments, (c) better-developed expectancies, and (d) superior strategies for avoiding [[Frame Fixation]]. This framework directly challenges [[Confirmation Bias]] as a useful explanatory concept.

## The Four Cycles

1. **Basic Data-Frame Cycle** — Frame and data are mutually constitutive: frames select what counts as data; data anchors and extends frames. Frames are constructed from ≤3–4 anchors.
2. **Elaboration Cycle** — Once committed to a frame, the practitioner seeks to fill gaps via active data gathering.
3. **Preservation Cycle** — When anomalous data appears, one option is to dismiss it and reinforce the current frame.
4. **Reframing Cycle** — Abandoning the current frame when anomalies accumulate; involves holding ≤2–3 competing frames in parallel.

## Key Expert-Novice Differences

| Dimension | Novice | Expert |
|---|---|---|
| Mental models | Thin causal maps | Dense, domain-specific causal relationships |
| Frame repertoire | Few ready-made frames | Rich repertoire plus fragment library |
| Expectancies | Vague | Specific, violable |
| Self-correction speed | Slow | Fast (earlier expectancy violation detection) |
| Sensemaking orientation | Abstract understanding | Functional, anticipatory (for action) |
| Frame fixation recovery | Slow, long | Faster, expert-specific strategies |

## The Confirmation Bias Challenge

The article argues that what looks like confirmation bias is actually rational frame-guided data seeking. Klein et al's claim: **in naturalistic settings, "keep an open mind" strategies consistently underperform frame-first strategies**. The [[Naturalistic Decision Making]] (NDM) research program is positioned against the [[Heuristics and Biases]] tradition (Kahneman/Tversky), which builds on lab studies with novices on toy problems.

## Expert Strategies for Frame Fixation

- **Medicine**: "logical competitor set" — grouping diseases with identical symptom presentations to prevent anchor lock-in
- **Dynamic domains** (business, investing, war): commit early but monitor vigilantly for anomalies; easier to construct alternative frames with confirming evidence than to search for disconfirming evidence

## Implications for Decision Support Systems

The conventional data→information→insight→decision pipeline is wrong. The right model: **frame construction is the high-order bit**. Decision support should track anchors and possible-anchors, not maximize data throughput. More data without better frames degrades analyst performance.

## The Walmart Fragment

A long case study on Walmart's PC-era competitive advantage is provided to illustrate how fragments work: the case embeds the mental model "winning through technology requires coupling it to a new organizational structure, not just deploying it." Having this fragment ready primes readers to notice analogous patterns in AI adoption.

## Contradictions / Missing

- The article does not address the difference between sensemaking under time pressure vs. deliberate inquiry — the four cycles may have very different dynamics.
- No treatment of *group* or *organizational* sensemaking (Weick's territory) — only individual cognition.
- The claim that confirmation bias is not a bias in naturalistic settings glosses over cases where experts have persistent, costly biases (medical overdiagnosis, etc.).
