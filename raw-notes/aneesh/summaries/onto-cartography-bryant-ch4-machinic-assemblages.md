# Summary: Bryant, *Onto-Cartography* Ch 4 — Machinic Assemblages and Entropy (pp. 75–108)

## Overview

Chapter 4 is the heart of Bryant's ontological machinery. It does three things in sequence: (1) refines the machine concept by distinguishing endo- from exo-relations, thereby establishing what it means for a new machine to emerge from a coupling; (2) develops an assemblage theory keyed to powers/affects rather than qualities, drawing heavily on Deleuze and Guattari and on Andy Clark's extended mind thesis; and (3) introduces entropy as the central problem that machines must solve simply to persist — giving the book its thermodynamic grounding.

## Endo-Relations and Emergence

Bryant opens by distinguishing two types of relations a machine has. *Exo-relations* are external connections to other discrete machines that can be severed without dissolving the machine — a television remains a television when unplugged from the wall. *Endo-relations* are internal relations between the machines that compose a given machine; these constitute that machine's being. An assemblage constitutes a genuinely new machine when the endo-relations between its parts generate powers or capacities not present in any individual part.

The H₂O example is paradigmatic: hydrogen and oxygen are individually combustible, yet water puts out fires. These emergent properties are not magical — they depend entirely on the powers of the parts and what happens when they are coupled in this way. Bryant explicitly disavows a "bulwark against reductionism" reading; the emergent properties of water require hydrogen and oxygen, they just also require the specific form of their coupling. The parts must be related *this way* for water's powers to emerge.

This generates the key claim: "an assemblage constitutes a new machine or unit when it is able to act on other entities in the world in ways that its parts cannot." (p. 77) Assemblages thus proliferate entities rather than reducing them; being is composed of more types of beings than common sense admits.

## Machinic Genesis: Reversible and Irreversible

Bryant distinguishes reversible from irreversible machinic genesis. Reversible: the coupled machines can be disaggregated while retaining their prior powers (an automobile can be disassembled; H₂O can be electrolyzed back to its elements). Irreversible: the machines that compose the assemblage are themselves destroyed or transformed in the process — as when the body metabolizes food, transforming and destroying the foodstuffs so that the body cannot be decomposed back into the corn it has consumed.

## Assemblages and Individuation by Power: The D&G Connection

Bryant takes the Spinozist/Deleuzian question — what can a body do? — as the key to individuation. Against classification by genus and species (qualitative resemblance), entities should be categorized by their *affects*: passive affects (ways a machine is selectively open to the world) and active affects (capacities to produce differences in the world). A racehorse differs more from a workhorse than a workhorse from an ox — not anatomically, but in what each can do and what can be done to it.

The man–horse–stirrup–lance assemblage is Bryant's most developed example of genuinely new power through coupling. Prior to the stirrup, the horse-mounted soldier could not transmit the force of the horse through his weapons; Newton's third law threw him from the saddle. The stirrup changes everything: "The lance and the sword came into being in the Bronze Age only by virtue of the man–horse assemblage, which caused a lengthening of the dagger and pike." (Deleuze and Guattari 1987: 399, quoted p. 82) A new machine — man-horse-stirrup-lance — comes into being, with powers none of the parts individually possess.

Bryant extends this to Andy Clark's *extended mind* thesis: beliefs, memories, and even affects can be distributed across physical media external to the brain. The paper on which we do arithmetic is not merely a convenience — it is a genuine part of the cognitive assemblage. Notebooks, smartphones, internet databases are components of mind-assemblages with different powers than bare biological brains. "Cognition that takes place with pencil, paper and Arabic numerals is *different* from cognition that takes place purely inside the head." (p. 87) This has political implications: assemblages coupled to different media possess different powers, so "political humanism" that treats all humans as equal fails to register genuine inequalities produced by differential media-coupling.

## Machinic Problems

Because a machine's parts are themselves machines with their own structural openness and operational closure, they are never perfectly harmonized into the whole. Every machine is "simultaneously a unit and a crowd or herd of cats." Parts continue to pursue their own trajectories, introducing tension, friction, and the permanent threat of dissolution. Latour: "[n]one of the actants mobilized to secure an alliance stops acting on its own behalf." (Latour 1988: 197, quoted p. 79) Evolution itself is a *bricoleur*, jerry-rigging new functions from pre-existing structures that served different purposes — hence the infant-killing pelvic geometry of *homo sapiens* women. Every machine is jerry-rigged; no machine ever quite totalizes its parts.

## Entropy and Negentropic Operations

The chapter's second half introduces entropy not in the thermodynamic sense (heat death / loss of available energy) but in the information-theoretic and biological sense: *probability distribution among elements*. A system is *highly entropic* if any element has an equal probability of appearing anywhere in the system; it is *lowly entropic* if the distribution of elements is highly organized and differentiated. A *negentropic* system actively engages in operations to *maintain* a state of low entropy — to prevent drift toward disorganization.

Bryant's key claim: the vast majority of corporeal machines and many incorporeal machines face the perpetual threat of entropy. A machine is an improbability — a low entropy state — and improbabilities naturally tend to dissolve. Most machines must therefore engage in constant operations to reproduce their organization across time. "Reproduction is a continuous problem for systems with temporalized complexity... [systems must be] securing the constant renewal of system elements — or, more briefly, not with static but with dynamic stability." (Luhmann 1995: 49, quoted p. 96)

Bryant distinguishes two entropy-resistance strategies: (1) inorganic machines resist entropy through physical *forces* (molecular bonding, gravity) without engaging in active operations — rocks don't regrow chipped-off pieces; (2) organic, cognitive, and social machines engage in active *operations* that reproduce parts and the relations between parts. An organic body perpetually loses cells and perpetually reproduces them, maintaining not merely the parts but the organizational pattern of their interrelations.

## Cities as the Extended Example

Bryant devotes extended analysis to the city as a social machine to show entropy/negentropy at work. A city is a low entropy system: its stratification into classes, occupations, ethnic and religious groups represents a highly *improbable* distribution of persons. This differentiation is continuously produced and reproduced by:
- *Incorporeal machines*: educational institutions, law, media, churches, linguistic norms, gender roles — which both form bodies in particular ways and function as regulatory feedback mechanisms punishing deviance
- *Corporeal machines*: roads, pipes, fiber optic cables, building stock, transit infrastructure — which connect and segregate populations, channeling the energy and matter flows the city needs

Above all, cities require ongoing flows of *energy and matter* from outside: food, fuel, building materials, people. Cities are necessarily selectively open to an exterior from which they draw inputs. Hurricane Katrina illustrates this with "stunning clarity": cut off from flows of energy and communication, New Orleans rapidly became highly entropic — social differentiation collapsed, inferences from one element to another became impossible, the negentropic structure fell apart not because people stopped believing in norms but because the material flows sustaining the negentropic operations ceased.

## Entropy, Plasticity, and Politics

Bryant offers a crucial normative caution: entropy is not evil and low entropy is not good. Highly negentropic social systems can be deeply oppressive — the case of Ritchie's character in *Summer of Sam*, whose deviation from neighborhood norms subjected him to violence, illustrates Foucault/Althusser's "power" as negentropic regulatory operations normalizing bodies. Political emancipation often requires *introducing entropy* into a social machine — disrupting the negentropic mechanisms that enforce oppressive differentiations. But some entropy reserve (*plasticity*) is also necessary for adaptation: a machine with entropy so low its selective openness is fixed cannot respond to novel inputs and will be destroyed by changes in its environment (as social machines now face from climate change).

## Conclusion Bryant Draws

"Machine and entropy are two central concepts of onto-cartography. The first concept reminds us that things, objects, entities, or substances are processes composed of operations acting on inputs producing outputs... By contrast, the concept of entropy reminds us that machines require *work* if they are to continue their existence throughout time." (p. 106) The task of onto-cartography is to map those negentropic operations — both to improve or sustain machines we want to persist, and to devise strategies for demolishing machines we wish to end.

## Quotable Passages

1. "An assemblage constitutes a new machine or unit when it is able to act on other entities in the world in ways that its parts cannot." (p. 77)

2. "Every machine is simultaneously a unit and a crowd or herd of cats. Put differently, no machine ever manages to totalize or master its parts. Rather, parts continue to possess a life of their own. As Latour dramatically puts it, '[n]one of the actants mobilized to secure an alliance stops acting on its own behalf...'" (p. 79)

3. "Every machine is jerry-rigged by the anonymous Macgyvers of nature and culture." (p. 80)

4. "Cognition that takes place with pencil, paper and Arabic numerals is *different* from cognition that takes place purely inside the head. Put more dramatically, for Clark these are two different types of minds." (p. 87)

5. "A negentropic system is a system that engages in *operations* that stave off — at least for a time — transition to a high entropic state. A negentropic system engages in operations to reduce and exclude noise, thereby maintaining the improbability of its organization." (p. 95)

6. "Cities only exist in and through the operations that perpetuate them. In this respect, and as is the case with all machines, a city is more a verb than a noun." (p. 103)

7. "The concept of entropy reminds us that machines require *work* if they are to continue their existence throughout time... Far from being static lumps that just sit there, machines must instead engage in constant operations to continue their existence from moment to moment. Where those operations cease, the machine also ceases." (p. 107)
