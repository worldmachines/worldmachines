# The Modernity Machine II (Rao, Jun 2025)

**Source**: Contraptions newsletter, June 28, 2025  
**Author**: [[venkatesh-rao]]

## Central Argument

A mid-year update to the modernity machine thesis, 6 books into the 2025 Contraptions book club. The key refinement: the world-machine periodization (1200-1600) holds up under global horizontal reading, and the ~1600 boundary is defensible as the moment when the completed modernity machine was "powered on" worldwide — after which the *postmodernity machine* (here called the [[divergence-machine]]) began to be sketched out.

## The Two Reading Principles

1. **World as Machine**: Mechanistic, engineering perspective. Reverse-engineer how the world works. Actively seek machine metaphors, decenterings of the human. (Example: centering horses rather than humans in the steppe nomad story.)
2. **[[Horizontal History]]**: Read geographically global but time-boxed, rather than following vertical streams of identity (individuals → dynasties → kingdoms). Deliberately cross-cultural comparison within a temporal band.

## The Machine Turn-On Problem

World machines take ~400 years to assemble, but "turning on" takes no more than a generation. The modernity machine assembled 1200-1600; it turned on ~1600-1630. The postmodernity/divergence machine assembled 1600-2000; it turned on "in the last decade" (i.e., ~2015-2025).

The 1600 boundary is a *machinic* periodization cut — it integrates political, cultural, and technological into a single paradigm, producing a more elegant periodization than:
- Fukuyama's political cut at Battle of Jena (~1813)
- Technology-focused cut at the 1890s (artisan era → scaled engineering / interchangeable parts)
- Culture-focused cut at late-18th century romanticism

## The Horizontal History Demonstration: Battle of Talikota (1565)

The Battle of Talikota in India in 1565 is Rao's showcase example of horizontal history. Often mythologized as a Hindu-Muslim war (Vijayanagara vs. Deccan Sultanates), the battle actually featured:
- Artillery tactics on *both* sides (not just one)
- Two Muslim generals *in the Vijayanagara army* who defected mid-battle
- A confused blend of medieval pitched-battle modes and artillery-catalyzed maneuver
- Cosmopolitan mercenary politicking beneath the religious overstory

This rhymes with the Thirty Years War in Europe: the overstory is religious, the understory is professionalized, mercenary, and largely non-religious. The Sufi, Bhakti, and Sikh reform movements were already spreading through the Indian fabric by this time — exactly as Protestant reformation was spreading through Europe. The printing press arrived in India (Goa, with the Portuguese) in 1556.

## The "Everything Everywhere, All at Once" Character of Modernity

The simultaneous global arrival of modernity is systematically underestimated. The essential 20% of the modernity machine — delivering 80% of the disruption — was installed relatively quickly across the globe. Europe's post-1600 divergence is a story about the *postmodernity machine*, not the modernity machine; it has very different dynamics.

## Moving Parts That Got Turned On (~1600-1630)
- Matured print revolution
- Scientific sensibility
- Legalistic (not vibe-based) religious pluralism
- Final eclipse of steppe nomad military dominance
- First full-ish world mapping, all major sailing routes established
- Americas as a modernity workroom
- Africa's (dark) integration into the world machine

## Moving Parts That Got Turned Off
- Pre-gunpowder military technologies
- Scribal cultures
- Eastern Mediterranean-focused myopic European mindset
- Chronologically nonsensical senses of history
- Mythology and literalist religion as load-bearing structural components
- The Silk Road (effective obsolescence)
- Chivalric cultural modes (Don Quixote, 1605, as global marker)

## Key Quotes

> "The essential 20% of the modernity machine, which delivered 80% of the disruption and re-building of the societal fabric, was installed relatively quickly."

> "Europe did diverge from the rest of the world after 1600, a story about the postmodernity machine rather than the modernity machine, but a persuasive case can be made that that is a distinct story with very different dynamics."

## What's Missing

- No explicit account of *why* 400 years is the assembly time for world machines — what determines that tempo?
- The Islamic Reformation parallel (Sufi, Bhakti movements) is gestured at but not analyzed at the same depth as the European story
- The thesis of print as the key differentiator between Indian and European post-1600 trajectories is asserted briefly but not argued
