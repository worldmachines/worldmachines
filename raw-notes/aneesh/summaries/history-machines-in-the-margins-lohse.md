# History Machines in the Margins — Florian Lohse (2025)

**Source**: Notefields (Substack), October 30, 2025  
**Author**: Florian Lohse

## Central Argument

Grand historical paradigms — modernity, postmodernity — are not suddenly emerging monoliths. They are **history machines**: assemblages of components that develop independently and distributedly across long timescales, until conditions align and the machine lurches into motion, causing "shock and awe." This extends Rao's Contraptions Book Club framing from Venkatesh Rao.

The essay is also a meditation on provincial identity: the author, a German visiting Singapore for the first time, finds in the cosmopolitan airport an echo of a Chinese ambassador's experience walking through the Majapahit port of Canggu in the 14th century — both outsiders in a sophisticated, foreign, bustling world.

## The Majapahit Case Study

### Basic Facts
- Majapahit: a Hindu-Buddhist maritime empire centered on Java, Sumatra, and Bali, at its peak in the 14th century
- Cosmopolitan and religiously pluralistic: Hindu, Buddhist, Muslim, and animist communities coexisted
- Had advanced naval technology: nine-decked "jong" ships possibly carrying up to 1,000 people — larger than Portuguese ships of the 15th century
- The Portuguese apparently destroyed Javanese charts, cartographic records, and navigation logs by Royal Decree of November 13, 1504

### The Disappearing Archive
- "Not a single indigenous specimen is known to have been preserved" of Javanese cartography — which "presumably explains why pre-Portuguese Indonesian cartography has never been the subject of any specific study"
- The deliberate erasure of Majapahit's technological record is itself a historical fact worth noting — winners shape the historiographical record

### The Afterlife
- Majapahit disappeared quickly in the 15th century but had a rich afterlife:
  1. Structural leftovers were material for Dutch colonialists
  2. Its royal legacy legitimized later kingdoms and the state of Indonesia (4th largest population; largest Muslim country)
  3. The royal class fled to Bali, taking the royal library, costumes, and religious artifacts — "Bali became the reincarnation of Majapahit"

### The Chronicler in the Margins
- An "oddball court official" documenting daily life in 12th-century Majapahit was considered irrelevant at publication — his notes are now the primary historical source
- Lohse's key provocation: the future is already present, "barely recognized if not for some dull notes in the margins"

## The History Machine Framework

From Rao's book club framing:
- Modernity may have started not in the 15th century but around the 12th century
- The Islamic and Mongol phases of globalization, not European exploration, are the primary vectors
- Barbara Tuchman's *A Distant Mirror* (on the Black Death) as catalyst for this framing
- Rao's thesis: "a specific European experience of modernization, rather than a prototype for a global one" — the Enlightenment made the *European* mind, not a universal one

## Key Quotation

> "I like the idea that the future is already around, barely recognized if not for some dull notes in the margins."

> "It is not always singularly great men driving the course of history. Instead there are lots of independent ideas, concepts and theories developing at the same time but distributed across continents, forming an increasingly powerful apparatus that eventually will start moving, rattling everybody suddenly awake to a new paradigm."

## What's Missing
- The essay is deliberately thin — a personal reflection, not a systematic argument
- No engagement with postcolonial historiography (Dipesh Chakrabarty's "provincializing Europe" is the obvious missing interlocutor)
- The claim that modernity began in the 12th century is only asserted via Rao, not argued directly
- No theory of *why* history machines accelerate or stall
