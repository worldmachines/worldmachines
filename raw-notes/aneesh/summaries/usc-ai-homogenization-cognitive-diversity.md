# AI May Be Making Us Think and Write More Alike

**Source**: Julia Grimmett, USC Dornsife News, March 11, 2026
**URL**: https://dornsife.usc.edu/news/stories/ai-may-be-making-us-think-and-write-more-alike/
**Lead Researcher**: Morteza Dehghani (USC); first author Zhivar Sourati
**Published in**: *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* (Cell Press), March 11, 2026

## Central Argument

LLMs are homogenizing human cognition at scale. Because billions of people now use a handful of models for writing, reasoning, and ideation, individual cognitive differences are being flattened toward the statistical center of LLM training data — which itself overrepresents Western, educated, industrialized, rich, and democratic (WEIRD) societies. The researchers argue this reduces both individual creativity and group-level collective intelligence, and recommend deliberate diversification of LLM training data.

## Key Claims

1. **Homogenization mechanism**: LLMs capture and reproduce statistical regularities in training data. When users refine their writing through LLMs, their output converges toward this statistical center — losing stylistic individuality and creative ownership.

2. **Indirect exposure effect**: Even non-users are affected. Social conformity pressure means that if enough people around you express ideas in LLM-inflected ways, your own expression shifts toward that norm.

3. **Individual vs. group cognition divergence**: Paradoxically, *individuals* generate more ideas and more detailed ideas with LLM assistance — but *groups* produce fewer and less creative ideas when members all use LLMs. This is because LLMs reduce the variance that makes group brainstorming productive.

4. **Reasoning monoculture**: LLMs favor chain-of-thought (linear, step-by-step) reasoning. This suppresses intuitive and abstract reasoning styles — which are sometimes *more efficient* for certain problem types.

5. **Agency shift**: Users defer to model-suggested continuations rather than crafting their own responses — gradually transferring cognitive agency from person to model.

6. **WEIRD bias in training data**: LLM outputs "mirror a narrow and skewed slice of human experience" — amplifying dominant languages and ideologies while marginalizing others.

## Key Quotation

> "The concern is not just that LLMs shape how people write or speak, but that they subtly redefine what counts as credible speech, correct perspective, or even good reasoning."
> — Zhivar Sourati

## Proposed Solution

Intentional incorporation of diversity in language, perspectives, and reasoning into LLM training sets — grounded in *actual* human global diversity (not random variation). Also: adjusting how individuals interact with LLMs to preserve cognitive distinctiveness.

## What's Missing

- No mechanism for how "diverse training data" would actually work at scale — who curates it, and by what standard?
- The paper focuses on text-based tasks; doesn't address whether the homogenization effect persists in visual, embodied, or creative domains
- No engagement with whether homogenization has *any* benefits — coordination, reduced miscommunication, accessibility for non-native speakers
- Doesn't address the possibility that pre-LLM writing was already heavily homogenized by mass media, education systems, and internet filter bubbles
