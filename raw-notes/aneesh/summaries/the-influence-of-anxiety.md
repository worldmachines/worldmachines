# The Influence of Anxiety (The Point, ~2022–2024)

**Source**: *The Point* magazine. Author not named in the source text.

## Central Argument

Harold Bloom is systematically misread by both his academic critics (who dismiss him) and his popular admirers (who sentimentalize him). He is best understood as a **mystical thinker** in the tradition of Kabbalah and visionary Romanticism — not a literary historian or critical theorist in any conventional sense. His [[Anxiety of Influence]] theory is both necessary as a proving ground and genuinely paralyzing if taken as a stopping point rather than a passageway.

## Bloom's Core Theory: The Anxiety of Influence (1973)

Every serious writer is shaped by literary **precursors** — but not through benevolent transmission. The relationship is one of **sublimated agon** (conflict, competition for literary immortality). Key mechanisms:

- Strong poets cannot simply "imitate" or "learn from" precursors — they must **misread** them (creatively distort, revise, reinterpret)
- The accumulation of literary history becomes a crushing burden — **belatedness**
- The only viable response: strategic creative misreading that transforms the inherited material enough to justify new existence

## The Six Revisionary Ratios (Background)

Bloom's *Anxiety* describes six moves strong poets make against precursors (clinamen, tessera, kenosis, daemonization, askesis, apophrades). The essay does not name these explicitly but gestures toward them as "strategies of evasion."

## Bloom's Intellectual Genealogy

- Strongly influenced by [[Giambattista Vico]]'s historical cycles (Aristocratic → Democratic → Chaotic age) — used structurally in *The Western Canon*
- Affinities with [[Sigmund Freud]]'s dynamics of sublimation, repression, and the unconscious
- Root influences: Romantic poets (Blake, Wordsworth, Keats), William Shakespeare as the ultimate "sect of one"
- Self-positioned against "school of resentment": poststructuralism, deconstruction, feminist theory, New Historicism, multiculturalism

## The Western Canon (1994) as Mystical Sermon

Bloom's canon argument is less about culture wars than about **the canon as mechanism of memory** — against the forgetting that culture produces. The criterion for inclusion: does the work continue to influence writers? Do artists elect works into the canon by reading and being shaped by them?

Key passages:
> "Nothing is so essential to the Western Canon as its principles of selectivity, which are elitist only to the extent that they are founded upon severely artistic criteria."

> "Aesthetic criticism returns us to the autonomy of imaginative literature and the sovereignty of the solitary soul, the reader not as person in society but as the deep self, our ultimate inwardness."

## The Paralysis Problem

The essay's central biographical thread: Bloom's theory, experienced personally, transfers anxiety from the texts to the reader/writer. For a young writer, the theory becomes:
- A proof of essential belatedness
- A picture of literary history as endless evasion and self-deception
- A block to originality, since every "original" impulse can be reduced to further evasion

The essay links this to broader contemporary cultural paralysis: **mass media overproduction** as compensatory overactivity — the symptom of paralyzing anxiety about influence.

## The Resolution: Through, Not Around

The essay's conclusion: anxiety cannot be transcended intellectually but can be metabolized experientially. The prescription:
1. Allegorize the anxiety — make it part of the work itself
2. Name it, learn from it, let it pass through
3. Hold Bloom's pessimism as a proving ground, not a final verdict
4. The greatest writers Bloom admired all shared: fierce dedication to their own voice

## Bloom's Academic Reception

The essay describes Bloom's institutional trajectory: brilliant Yale Romanticist → public intellectual via *The Anxiety of Influence* → figurehead of canon debates after *The Western Canon* → mass-market author of *Shakespeare: The Invention of the Human*, *Genius*, *Best Poems*. Academic colleagues never forgave the public audience.

## Contradictions / Missing

- The essay does not engage with feminist and postcolonial critiques of Bloom's canon on their own terms — it dismisses them as symptoms of anxiety
- No account of how Bloom's theory handles collective or folk traditions vs. individual authorship
- The "through not around" resolution is gesturally described but not operationalized
