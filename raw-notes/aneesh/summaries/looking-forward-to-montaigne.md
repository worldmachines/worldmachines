# Looking Forward to Montaigne

**Source**: [Looking Forward to Montaigne](https://aneeshsathe.com/looking-forward-to-montaigne/)
**Date**: 2025-07-31
**Author**: [[Aneesh Sathe]]

## Central Argument

[[Byung-Chul Han]] argues in *The Crisis of Narration* that storytelling has become storyselling — narrative commodified to create consumers. The author juxtaposes this with [[Michel de Montaigne]], who invented the personal essay and wrote in a frank, meandering way that paradoxically makes him feel more alive than contemporary influencers. The tentative thesis: Montaigne-style personal writing — small-scale, honest, non-teleological — could be the antidote to Han's doom. We may not need a grand shared narrative; knowing about a neighbor's garden bug problem might restore the sense of "liveness" that social media has stolen.

## Key Claims

1. **Storyselling vs. storytelling** (Han): narrative has been colonized by commerce; stories now manufacture consumers rather than create shared reality.
2. **Montaigne as counter-example**: 500 years dead, more real than any influencer — his frank meandering resists commodification by design.
3. **The old internet as reference point**: Montaigne's writing "reminds of the old internet" — a lost mode of authentic, personal publishing.
4. **Granular liveness**: small personal stories (garden bugs, daily life) create a sense of presence and shared humanity that grand narratives do not.
5. **The mediocre lens**: "having the average, mediocre, lens to life will take us through these dark days" — a defense of ordinariness.
6. **Epistemic humility**: quoting Montaigne's "que sais-je?" ("what do I know?") — provisional thinking as a virtue.

## What the Essay Doesn't Address

- Han's full argument (the author hasn't read *The Crisis of Narration* directly)
- Whether Montaigne's style is actually teachable/scalable, or whether it requires his particular position (aristocrat, retired, not selling anything)
- The question of audience: Montaigne wrote for a coterie; the internet requires publishing to strangers
